The Anchor

S&P futures rise on Alphabet's Google Cloud beat — GDP, PCE, and Brent $126 make Thursday the week's most consequential data session.

Wednesday's session closed essentially flat — S&P 500 at 7,135.95 (▼ 0.04%), Nasdaq at 24,673.24 (▲ 0.04%), Dow at 48,861.81 (▼ 0.57%) — as markets held their positions through the FOMC decision and waited for the Big Tech earnings after the close. What arrived after 4 PM was a split verdict: Alphabet and Amazon beat decisively (GOOGL +8.4% AH, AMZN +2%), while Meta fell 9% after hours on user growth disappointment and Iran-war WhatsApp impact, and Microsoft fell 2% despite Azure beating guidance. On balance, S&P 500 futures are pointing to a higher open Thursday as investors read the Alphabet result as the stronger AI-demand signal. The Dow's Wednesday decline of 280 points was driven by the Russell 2000's 1.15% drop — small-caps continuing to underperform as higher interest rates and oil prices disproportionately affect smaller companies with floating-rate debt and tighter energy cost margins.

The overnight Brent spike to $126.41 is the session's biggest wildcard. Oil's move — driven by Trump's "just give up" statement on Iran's Day 62 — could pull equity futures lower when US markets open if it sustains above $120. But as Brent has pared gains back toward $116 in overnight trading, its immediate equity impact is being absorbed rather than amplified. The more consequential data for Thursday's session arrives in two stages: the morning data block (US Q1 GDP advance estimate, March PCE, Initial Jobless Claims), and Apple's after-close earnings. The March PCE print is the single most-watched inflation data point of the month — it is the first PCE capturing a full month with Brent averaging above $100, and any surprise above the 2.6% consensus core estimate would test the morning's equity gains and potentially push the 10-year yield above its current 4.42%.

Big Tech · Results · Split ▲ Futures GOOGL +8.4% · AMZN +2% · META -9% · MSFT -2% · net positive for futures

Big Tech earnings produce a split outcome — Alphabet and Amazon beat, Meta and Microsoft slide — but futures read it as net positive for AI.

Wednesday night's earnings produced a precise split that the market is reading as a positive net signal. Alphabet's Google Cloud +63% to $20 billion — against a $460 billion backlog — and Amazon's AWS beat of $37.59 billion versus $36.64 billion estimate together demonstrate that enterprise AI infrastructure demand is robust. GOOGL surged 8.4% in after-hours; AMZN rose 2%. The negative side: Meta fell 9% after hours on user growth disappointment and Iran-war WhatsApp disruption; Microsoft fell 2% despite Azure accelerating to 40% as investors focused on full-year capex raised to $190 billion and capacity-constraint language. The market's interpretation — reflected in futures pointing higher — is that Alphabet and Amazon's strong results outweigh Meta and Microsoft's after-hours decline, because the positive results are driven by AI infrastructure demand (a bullish structural signal) while the negative results are driven by geopolitical disruption (a temporary external factor) and capex guidance (a supply-building investment, not a demand shortfall). Eli Lilly also reported Thursday morning before the open: beat, stock up 7%, full-year guidance raised to $82–$85 billion — adding a pharmaceutical sector positive to the morning's mixed macro backdrop.

FOMC · Powell · Stays On Board 3.5–3.75% Hold · 8-4 dissent · Powell remains as Governor · Miran preferred cut · Warsh incoming

Fed holds at 3.5%–3.75% — Powell's extraordinary announcement: he stays on the Board of Governors, citing Trump's legal attacks. Historic 8-4 dissent.

The Federal Reserve held its benchmark funds rate in the 3.5%–3.75% range on Wednesday — the consensus outcome — but the meeting's defining moment was Chair Jerome Powell's announcement that he will remain on the Federal Reserve Board of Governors after Kevin Warsh takes over as chair. Powell cited Trump's "legal attacks" as having left him "no choice" but to stay, in a statement that simultaneously validates the independence concerns surrounding the Warsh nomination and creates a structurally unprecedented central bank leadership arrangement: a sitting chairman who becomes a governor, retaining a vote on monetary policy under his successor. The FOMC dissent split 8-4 — the most divided vote since October 1992. Stephen Miran preferred to lower rates by 25 basis points; Hammack, Kashkari, and Logan opposed the inclusion of an easing bias in the statement. For markets, the immediate rate impact is nil — a hold was 100% priced. The Powell-stays development matters for its implication that the transition to Warsh will be more contentious and more institutionally complex than a clean handoff, potentially creating Fed communication ambiguity through Q2 as markets try to interpret which signals come from Warsh and which from the broader board Powell now anchors.

Brent $126 · Oil · Escalation $126.41 Four-year high · Trump "give up" · pared to $116 · US gas 2022 high · WTI $103.58

Brent briefly hits $126.41 — four-year high — as Trump tells Iran to "give up." WTI crosses $103; US gas at highest since 2022.

Brent crude's overnight surge to $126.41 per barrel — before paring gains back toward $116 — is the energy market's most significant single-session move since the Hormuz closure began. The catalyst: Trump's declaration that the US naval blockade is a "success" and his direct call for Iran to "just give up," the conflict's most unambiguous statement of US non-willingness to negotiate on Iranian terms. WTI rose above $103, crossing the $100 threshold for the first time since the ceasefire was declared and marking the third consecutive day of WTI gains. US retail gasoline prices hit their highest since 2022 on the combined effects of Brent's surge and the refinery margin pressure from rising crude input costs. For equity markets, the Brent spike's impact depends on whether it sustains above $120 or reverts to the $114–$116 range: above $120 sustained, stagflation concerns re-emerge and pressure consumer discretionary and transportation stocks; at $116, the market absorbs it as a geopolitical premium. The overnight paring of gains suggests the market expects the latter — but the spike demonstrated that a sustained $120+ scenario is possible if Trump's "give up" framing escalates Iran's response rather than accelerating a diplomatic resolution.

ECB · Hold · June Hike Possible 2.0% Deposit rate hold · markets price 3 ECB hikes · first June possible · Lagarde "layer cake"

ECB holds deposit rate at 2.0% — but markets now price three hikes in 2026, first possible June, as Lagarde cites "layer cake of shocks."

The European Central Bank held its deposit rate at 2.0% at its Thursday meeting, but the market signal embedded in the decision is tighter than the hold implies. Markets are now fully pricing three ECB rate hikes in 2026, with the first potentially arriving as soon as June — a reversal of the easing expectations that characterized Q1 2026 market positioning. ECB President Lagarde cited a "layer cake of shocks" from the Iran conflict, the UAE's OPEC departure adding structural uncertainty to energy markets, and the absence of any Hormuz diplomatic resolution, as the factors preventing any near-term easing bias. The ECB's pivot from an expected cutter to a potential hiker has direct implications for euro-area sovereign bond markets: German Bund yields are rising alongside US Treasuries in a synchronous global tightening signal. For the US 10-year Treasury — currently at 4.42% — the ECB's hawkish trajectory reduces the "European divergence" argument for Treasury demand (if Europe is also tightening, there is less reason for capital to flow from Europe into US bonds for yield pickup). The US 10-year could test 4.5% if March PCE prints above consensus today, against a backdrop of ECB potential hikes and a Fed holding with a divided board.

US Q1 GDP advance estimate and March PCE release today — the first full oil-above-$100 month hits core inflation as Apple reports after close.

Thursday's data calendar is the most consequential single-session macro release of the month. The US Q1 2026 GDP advance estimate — the first look at first-quarter economic growth — lands this morning, with the Atlanta Fed's GDPNow model projecting 1.2% annualized growth as of April 29. A print at or below 1.2% would be the softest quarterly US GDP since the mild contraction in Q4 2024, potentially re-opening the "stagflation" framing that markets have been reluctant to adopt explicitly: high inflation (Brent above $100, PCE elevated), low growth, no Fed cuts in sight. March PCE — the Fed's preferred inflation measure — is the data point with the most direct policy impact: it is the first monthly read capturing a period when Brent averaged above $100 throughout, and any print above the 2.6% core consensus would force a reassessment of both the Fed's hold timeline and the ECB's hike trajectory. Apple's after-close Q1 earnings — approximately $95 billion in revenue estimated — will cap Thursday's session with the final Mag-7 report of the season; iPhone AI-driven upgrade cycle commentary will determine whether the AAPL stock, which has been essentially unchanged this week, breaks meaningfully in either direction. Eli Lilly's pre-market beat (+7%, guidance raised to $82–$85 billion full-year) has already provided a healthcare sector positive that offsets some of the pharma weight from Merck reporting later.

Gold holds at $4,638 as Treasury yields rise to 4.42% — diverging real-yield dynamics ahead of PCE and the ECB's tightening pivot.

Gold's modest 0.26% rise to $4,638 — despite the Brent spike to $126 and the ECB's hawkish hold — reflects the diverging forces acting on safe-haven assets on Thursday morning. The 10-year Treasury yield's 6-basis-point rise to 4.42% is creating an incremental headwind for non-yielding gold, as higher real yields raise the opportunity cost of holding the metal. Yet gold is rising rather than falling because the ECB's potential hike trajectory is weakening the dollar — DXY fell 0.17% to 99.65 — and dollar weakness has been a stronger gold driver than yield levels for most of 2026's trading sessions. The Brent spike adds an additional tail: investors uncertain about Hormuz's resolution are holding gold as a macro hedge against scenarios where the conflict escalates beyond the current Day 62 stalemate. The 10-year Treasury's move to 4.42% is itself a function of two simultaneous pressures: the Fed's 8-4 dissent vote (three governors opposed easing bias, suggesting upside rate risk), and the ECB's hawkish hold (reducing European demand for US Treasuries as a yield pickup play). If March PCE prints above 2.6% today, the 10-year could test 4.5% — a level that would begin to meaningfully pressure equity valuations at current multiples.

Quick hits

  • The FOMC's 8-4 dissent is historically significant for what it reveals about internal Fed dynamics. The last 4-member dissent was October 1992. Three of the four dissents on Wednesday were hawkish (opposing easing bias language), while one was dovish (Miran preferring a cut). The split reveals a Federal Reserve simultaneously fighting internal pressure from below (Miran's cut preference, likely reflecting real-economy concern about growth softening) and from above (the hawkish governors responding to Brent $116+ and the UAE-OPEC structural oil price shift). Warsh enters this divided institution as an incoming chair who, per his confirmation hearings, will not be Trump's "sock puppet" — but will face an institutionally divided board where the consensus range for appropriate policy spans more than 50 basis points.
  • The Atlanta Fed's GDPNow model at 1.2% for Q1 2026 — if confirmed by today's advance GDP estimate — would mark the fourth consecutive quarter of GDP growth below 2.0%, a pattern that typically signals either a structural growth slowdown or the early stages of a demand-side compression from sustained high energy costs. Each $10 increment in Brent above $90 reduces US household real purchasing power by approximately 0.1–0.15% of annual disposable income; at $116 average Brent, the Q1 drag on consumer spending is approximately 2.6–3.9% of annualized disposable income — a significant headwind to the "resilient consumer" thesis.
  • Meta's 9% after-hours decline is significant in a market context where the stock had zero Sell ratings entering earnings. Against a zero-Sell consensus, any meaningful after-hours decline triggers covering activity: analysts who previously maintained neutral/buy ratings are now forced to revise, and the absence of any pre-positioned bearish view means the post-earnings price discovery process happens entirely in the after-hours session without the short-side cushion that normally dampens single-session moves. The asymmetric setup — which was identified as a risk entering earnings — has now produced exactly the tail event it implied was possible.
  • Apple's after-close report today is the last of the Mag-7 season, and it arrives in a uniquely isolated position: Alphabet beat, Meta missed, Microsoft and Amazon produced mixed signals, and now Apple reports with the AI-upgrade cycle question still unresolved. iPhone's AI-driven upgrade cycle — dependent on Apple Intelligence features that began rolling out in early 2026 — will be the primary earnings commentary focus. If Apple's iPhone unit sales are tracking the AI upgrade thesis (accelerating from the prior cycle's trough), the result validates a consumer-facing AI monetization story that complements Alphabet and Amazon's enterprise AI infrastructure beats.
  • Gold at $4,638 with a DXY at 99.65 is following the dollar-gold inverse correlation that has been the most reliable single-variable price driver for gold in 2026. Dollar weakness (DXY declining from 99.82 to 99.65) provides the structural support for gold even as rising yields create a headwind. The key threshold: if the March PCE print today forces dollar strength (as investors price fewer Fed cuts and more tightening), gold could give back Thursday's modest gains quickly. Conversely, if GDP comes in well below 1.2% and PCE is in-line, the stagflation signal strengthens — and stagflation is historically the environment where gold performs best relative to both equities and bonds.
  • The Russell 2000's 1.15% decline Wednesday — significantly worse than the large-cap S&P 500's 0.04% drop — reflects the small-cap sector's disproportionate exposure to the current macro environment: floating-rate debt (more expensive in a higher-for-longer regime), energy cost sensitivity (more direct pass-through from Brent to operating margins for manufacturing small-caps), and geopolitical supply chain disruption (smaller companies have fewer resources to reroute logistics around Hormuz). The Russell's persistent underperformance in April is a signal that the current macro headwinds are concentrating economic damage in the parts of the economy that Wall Street's large-cap indices underweight.
