Markets · Deep Dive · Markets closed Sunday — Friday close values · Iran crisis overhangs Monday open

Markets closed Sunday after a Friday in which the Nasdaq recorded its fifth consecutive losing session — its longest streak since February — and now face a Monday open overloaded with new risk: the IRGC drone strike on a Hormuz cargo ship, US retaliatory strikes, and the IRGC's counter-attack against US-linked targets in the early hours of June 28 collectively reintroduce an Iran risk premium that WTI and Brent had deflated since the MOU was signed.

Friday's settlement prices — WTI at $70.34, Brent at $73.74 — were set before the full Iran escalation timeline became clear. Monday's open will price, for the first time in conventional markets, a ceasefire that is now openly exchanging military strikes between the US and Iran. Gold at $3,962 — which did not spike on Friday's tech selloff — is expected to attract a safe-haven bid. The 10-year Treasury at 4.45% will see competing pressures: flight-to-quality buying vs. the inflationary implications of a potential oil supply shock. The week also brings the end of Q2 and H1 2026, June non-farm payrolls on Friday July 3, and the largest portfolio rebalancing event of the year — in an environment now materially more uncertain than it was when the FOMC last met.

The Anchor

Nasdaq five straight losses. S&P 7,325. WTI $70.34. Gold $3,962. UST 10Y 4.45%. Iran ceasefire crisis emerges. Week ahead: Q2 end + June NFP. Monday's open prices a weekend where the Hormuz supply assurance — the deal's most durable market anchor — became the deal's most active military flashpoint.

The Iran Hormuz escalation changes the market's Iran calculus in a specific and measurable way. The MOU's primary market benefit was the 60-day sanctions waiver that kept Iranian crude flowing through Hormuz, holding WTI at disinflationary levels and providing the energy-side argument for a September Fed rate hold. That argument depended on the Strait remaining open and Iranian supply flowing without interruption. An IRGC drone hitting a cargo ship, US strikes on Iranian missile and radar sites, and the IRGC firing missiles and drones at US-linked targets overnight — with Kuwait and Bahrain reporting incoming projectiles — is not a diplomatic friction point. It is active military exchange at the world's most important energy chokepoint. Monday's oil open will re-price the risk premium that had been deflating since June 17; the question is not whether WTI opens higher, but by how much, and whether the spike is a single-session event or the beginning of a sustained re-rating of Hormuz risk.

The Friday close picture, separate from the Iran escalation, was already challenged. The Nasdaq's fifth consecutive losing session drove the index 0.70% lower to 25,181, and consumer sentiment at 48.9 — near historic lows and unchanged between the preliminary and final June readings — signals a consumer already under meaningful price pressure before any oil supply shock arrives. Citi's tech sector downgrade Friday — citing the difficulty of seeing "how everyone in the AI/Tech path wins" — added an analyst community signal to the selloff narrative. The week ahead compounds these headwinds with Q2 and first-half rebalancing pressure: tech-heavy portfolios must mechanically sell the sector that has underperformed to return to benchmark weight, adding to already-negative fund flows. June non-farm payrolls arrive Friday July 3 into a holiday-shortened trading session before a long July 4 weekend, concentrating a highly market-moving data release into a single low-liquidity day.

The Iran Hormuz ceasefire breakdown is the defining macro event for Monday's market open. The sequence: an IRGC drone struck a Singapore-flagged cargo ship in the Strait of Hormuz on Friday, June 26; US Central Command struck Iranian missile and drone storage facilities and coastal radar installations in retaliation, calling it a "powerful response" to Iran's "dangerous behavior"; and the IRGC fired missiles and drones at US-linked targets in the early hours of Sunday, June 28, between 2 and 3 a.m., with Kuwait and Bahrain reporting incoming projectiles and condemning the attacks as "heinous." This sequence — from drone attack to US strikes to IRGC counter-strikes — constitutes active military exchange at the Strait of Hormuz, not diplomatic protest. WTI at $70.34 and Brent at $73.74 are Friday settlement prices set before this escalation was fully priced; Monday's oil open will re-rate Hormuz risk for the first time in conventional market hours.

The Iran risk premium's return creates competing pressures across asset classes. Oil: higher, as Hormuz supply assurance is now credibly threatened. Gold: higher, as geopolitical safe-haven buying returns (gold did not rally on Friday's tech selloff, which was a valuation story, not a geopolitical one; the Iran escalation is different). US Treasuries: ambiguous, with flight-to-quality buying competing against the inflationary implications of an oil supply shock that would raise the CPI above the trajectory Federal Reserve September projections currently incorporate. Equities: energy sector benefits from oil re-rating; tech and consumer discretionary face additional pressure from a market that already closed Friday with five consecutive Nasdaq losses and consumer sentiment at 48.9. The severity of Monday's moves will depend on whether markets read the Hormuz exchange as a limited retaliatory cycle that the MOU framework contains, or as the beginning of a sustained escalation that puts the 60-day ceasefire window at risk.

The week of June 29 through July 3 was already the most complex calendar week of Q2 before the Iran escalation emerged: the simultaneous end of June, the second quarter, and the first half of 2026 triggers institutional portfolio rebalancing at triple scale, with tech-heavy portfolios facing mechanical selling pressure after the Nasdaq's five consecutive losses drove the sector further below benchmark weight. June non-farm payrolls arrive Friday July 3 in a holiday-shortened trading session ahead of the July 4 weekend — concentrating the year's most FOMC-critical employment data into a single low-liquidity day. Early forecasts suggest jobs growth eased meaningfully from May's 172,000, which would strengthen the argument for a September Fed rate hold. The Iran oil shock now lands into that calendar as a third variable, and it complicates the Fed calculus in both directions: a soft jobs print pointing toward a September hold could be offset by a Hormuz-driven CPI spike that revives the hiking argument.

The FOMC's September meeting will incorporate the June CPI print (arriving mid-July), the July CPI print (arriving mid-August), and the July non-farm payrolls before it meets. Nine members projected a rate hike at the June dot plot, primarily on the grounds that labor market tightness and AI infrastructure cost inflation were keeping inflation above target. The June jobs data on Friday is the first major input into whether that projection changes. If jobs slow and oil remains contained, the September hold case strengthens. If the Hormuz escalation pushes oil back toward pre-deal levels ($84), the disinflationary energy argument that has been the hold case's most durable pillar is materially weakened — and the rate path for September, July CPI, and the end of 2026 shifts accordingly. The week-ahead calendar is the first convergence of all these variables into a single five-day trading window.

WTI crude at $70.34 and Brent at $73.74 are Friday settlement prices established before the Iran Hormuz escalation fully played out Friday afternoon and overnight. The values reflect the prior market consensus: Iranian tankers flowing under the 60-day sanctions waiver, Hormuz open, WTI at a 16% discount to its pre-MOU baseline of roughly $84. That consensus is now under direct challenge. An IRGC drone attack on a cargo ship, followed by US military strikes on Iranian missile and radar infrastructure, followed by IRGC counter-strikes against US-linked targets — with Kuwait and Bahrain reporting incoming projectiles — is not consistent with the "Hormuz protest but tankers flowing" posture that markets had been pricing through MOU Day 10. Monday's oil open will re-price this risk, and the direction is higher; the magnitude depends on whether the overnight IRGC counter-strike is the end of a retaliatory cycle or the beginning of a sustained escalation.

Gold at $3,962 and Treasuries at 4.45% tell different stories about Friday's risk environment. Gold did not rally on the tech selloff — a finding from edition 082 that suggested markets read Friday's equity losses as a valuation correction within a growth-positive environment, not a systemic risk event. The Iran overnight escalation is a categorically different risk type: it is geopolitical and supply-side, not valuation and demand-side, and gold historically rallies on Middle East military escalations in a way it does not rally on equity multiple compression. The Treasury 4.45% is the output of a risk-off bond rally from the tech selloff; Monday could see additional flight-to-quality bond buying as Iran risk reprices, or it could see those gains partially reversed if oil spikes and the inflationary scenario — which is bond-bearish, not bond-bullish — becomes the dominant market narrative. The sequencing of Monday's asset moves will clarify which scenario markets believe they are in.

Quick hits

  • June non-farm payrolls arriving on July 3 — in a holiday-shortened session before a long weekend — create a specific structural problem: a weak print lands into a market that cannot fully process it before a four-day weekend, concentrating the initial move into a single illiquid session. Institutional investors who need to reposition in response to the data may not be able to do so at scale before the holiday, meaning the full price discovery process extends into the week of July 7. If the Iran crisis is still escalating at that point, the July 7 open could see both the NFP reaction and the Iran risk premium repricing interact simultaneously.
  • Energy sector equities benefit from the Iran oil spike in a way that tech and consumer discretionary do not. The Dow — which has a larger energy and industrial weighting than the Nasdaq — held up better than the Nasdaq all last week, closing Friday up 0.10% while the Nasdaq fell 0.70%. If the Iran escalation drives oil higher on Monday, the Dow/Nasdaq divergence that characterized last week's action may widen further: a rotation from tech/growth into energy/value consistent with the rising-oil, rising-inflation scenario. Watch the energy-to-tech spread as a real-time read on whether markets believe the Hormuz risk is transient or persistent.
