Markets · Deep Dive

US equity markets bounced strongly Thursday — Nasdaq +1.91% to 26,517, S&P +1.08% to 7,500, Russell 2000 +2.10% to 2,979 — suggesting markets viewed Wednesday's post-FOMC selloff as excessive, then closed for Juneteenth with WTI at $76.60 and the 10-year yield at 4.53%, as Monday's open inherits the nuclear talks postponement unpriced.

Thursday's recovery is the market saying Wednesday overshot. The logic: a dot plot showing 9 of 18 FOMC members projecting a rate hike is hawkish, but it is not a majority, not a rate action, and it is based on inflation data that predates the oil deal's full supply signal. The Nasdaq's 1.91% recovery — with the Russell 2000 leading at 2.10% — reflects a market that repriced quickly to the hawkish signal on Wednesday, then concluded the move was technically excessive relative to what a non-majority dot plot actually implies for rate policy. The Russell 2000 outperforming large caps is the most telling detail: small-cap equities are the most interest-rate-sensitive sector in the market, and their 2.10% recovery day after a hawkish FOMC signals that small-cap investors believe this is a disclosed distribution, not the beginning of a sustained tightening cycle. WTI's rebound to $76.60 from $75.01 complicates the narrative: eighty mines in the Hormuz main channel mean the supply increase from the MOU is arriving more slowly than the immediate post-deal $75 price implied. Slower oil disinflation means slower CPI relief means less reason for Warsh to revise the hawkish dot plot by September. The Juneteenth holiday closes the market before Monday's nuclear talks postponement can be priced into equities — investors head into the weekend with Thursday's recovery intact but two unresolved variables ahead.

The Anchor

Nasdaq +1.91%. Russell 2000 +2.10%. Thursday was the market deciding Wednesday overreacted. Then Juneteenth closed everything. Monday opens with nuclear talks postponed — a variable Thursday's buyers never priced.

Thursday's recovery is best read as a technical correction of Wednesday's excess rather than a fundamental reversal of the hawkish FOMC signal. The dot plot is still hawkish: 9 of 18 members project a rate hike, and that distribution remains the disclosed FOMC view. What Thursday's buyers concluded is that a non-majority projection from a first-meeting chair, based on inflation data that predates the oil deal's supply effect, warranted repricing from the rate-cut pricing of Tuesday — but not the 1.34% Nasdaq decline that Wednesday produced. The resulting recovery brought indices roughly to a rate-neutral pricing between Wednesday's pre-FOMC open and Wednesday's post-FOMC close. That is approximately the right price for a non-majority hawkish signal.

The nuclear talks postponement arrived Thursday night after market close. Equity markets have not priced it. Vance's delayed Switzerland trip — driven by Iran's demand for Lebanon ceasefire guarantees — adds geopolitical uncertainty to a set of markets that on Thursday had resolved to price the MOU's completion as a durable positive. If the nuclear talks delay is resolved over the weekend (Israel provides a modified Lebanon arrangement, Vance reschedules for next week), Monday's equity market may open without significant additional pressure. If the delay extends through next week or signals a structural breakdown in the MOU's second stage, the geopolitical premium that markets priced into Thursday's recovery gets partially priced back out on Monday.

All major US equity indices rebounded Thursday from Wednesday's FOMC-driven selloff: the Nasdaq gained 1.91% to 26,517, the S&P 500 rose 1.08% to 7,500, the Dow added 0.31% to 51,650, and the Russell 2000 surged 2.10% to 2,979. The Russell 2000's market-leading recovery is the session's most significant data point. Small-cap equities are the most interest-rate-sensitive sector in the market — they carry more floating-rate debt, are more dependent on credit conditions, and are more exposed to domestic economic activity than large multinationals. A 2.10% Russell advance the day after a hawkish FOMC suggests small-cap investors concluded the hawkish projection represents disclosed opinion, not impending policy action.

Thursday's recovery positioned indices at roughly the rate-neutral valuation between Wednesday's pre-FOMC open (pricing rate cuts) and Wednesday's post-FOMC close (pricing rate hikes). That is the correct valuation for a dot plot in which 9 of 18 members project a hike but 9 do not — a 50/50 distribution that should price somewhere between the two extreme scenarios. The market found that price in one session. FOMC members giving speeches next week will either push the distribution more hawkish (more members signal agreement with the hike projectors) or soften it (hike projectors walk back their language) — and the equity market will need to reprice again when those signals arrive.

WTI crude rebounded to $76.60 on Thursday — up 2.12% from Wednesday's $75.01 — as markets recalibrated the Hormuz reopening timeline. The Strait's main central channel retains approximately 80 mines requiring clearance; commercial vessels transiting Thursday used the northern route through Iranian waters and the southern route through Omani waters, which are fully open. At least 10 vessels transited Thursday via these alternative routes. The alternative routes have limited throughput relative to the main channel, meaning the supply increase from Hormuz reopening is real but slower than the post-deal $75 oil price trajectory assumed.

The oil rebound is modestly hawkish for the FOMC's September calculus. The disinflationary signal the Fed needs to revise its June rate-hike projection depends on WTI at or below $75 feeding into Q3 CPI data. If WTI averages $76-77 through late June while mine clearance progresses, the magnitude of oil's disinflationary contribution to Q3 CPI will be smaller than the immediate post-deal price implied. Smaller oil disinflation means less data-based reason for Warsh to revise the hawkish dot plot at September's FOMC. The $76 rebound narrows — but does not eliminate — the margin for the oil-beats-FOMC Q3 thesis.

Gold fell to approximately $4,228 Thursday, down 1.12% from Wednesday's $4,276, as the dollar tested yearly highs following Warsh's hawkish FOMC signal, according to Kitco's Thursday market report. The 10-year Treasury yield reached 4.53% — 7 basis points above the pre-FOMC level of 4.46% — as bond markets continued digesting the rate-hike projection that 9 of 18 FOMC members submitted. Both the dollar and yield moves mechanically pressure gold: a stronger dollar makes gold more expensive in other currencies (reducing demand), while higher real rates increase the opportunity cost of holding a non-yielding asset.

Gold's recovery path depends on the same oil-disinflation-to-FOMC-revision sequence that frames the broader rate narrative. If Q3 CPI data shows the WTI post-deal supply surge producing meaningful headline disinflation, and if the September FOMC revises the hawkish dot plot toward neutral, the dollar and real-rate pressures on gold reverse. Friday's Juneteenth closure pauses the repricing. Bond markets reopening Monday alongside any nuclear talks developments will provide the next directional signal for both the 10-year yield and the dollar — the two variables that most directly set gold's near-term floor.

Juneteenth closes NYSE, NASDAQ, CME, and US bond markets Friday — a federal holiday that pauses equity price discovery for 24 hours precisely as a new geopolitical variable enters the picture. VP Vance's postponement of his Switzerland nuclear talks trip, announced Thursday evening after US market close, has not yet been priced by equity markets. When markets reopen Monday, they will incorporate the delay as a new factor alongside whatever weekend developments arrive regarding Lebanon ceasefire negotiations and the Hormuz mine-clearance timeline. Thursday's recovery — Nasdaq +1.91%, S&P +1.08% — enters the weekend as the equity market's current pricing of the post-FOMC, post-MOU environment.

Monday's open will be a test of whether Thursday's recovery was sustainable or whether the nuclear talks postponement adds sufficient uncertainty to erode it. The weekend is the window for diplomatic resolution: if the US and Israel reach a Lebanon arrangement that allows Iran to participate in nuclear talks, Vance can rebook Switzerland for early next week and the 60-day window resumes. If no arrangement is reached by Monday morning, the equity market faces pricing the question of how long the nuclear talks delay lasts — and whether a delay of weeks risks the entire second stage of the MOU framework.

Quick hits

  • The Russell 2000 outperforming the Nasdaq on Thursday's recovery — 2.10% vs. 1.91% — is a nuanced signal about how markets read the Warsh hawkish dot plot. Small caps carry more floating-rate debt than large caps, so a genuine expectation of rate hikes should disproportionately pressure small caps (higher interest expense, tighter credit conditions for smaller borrowers). The fact that small caps led the recovery instead of lagging it tells you that Thursday's buyers concluded the rate-hike projection is priced as a theoretical disclosure, not a near-term policy path. If FOMC members in speeches next week reinforce the hawkish signal — moving toward majority hike projectors — small caps will underperform again as floating-rate debt exposure becomes a live concern rather than a theoretical one.
  • The key data event for the markets' Q3 narrative is now the July CPI print, not any FOMC meeting. The June FOMC has happened; the September FOMC is the next decision point. Between now and September, the data that will revise the hawkish dot plot is CPI — specifically, whether WTI's post-deal price decline (from $84 to $76) shows up in headline CPI. The July CPI (reporting June data, released in mid-July) will be the first data print to partially include the post-deal oil move. If June's CPI shows even a 0.1-0.2% relief at the headline from oil, the argument for September rate-hike projection revision gets its first data point. Markets will start pricing the September FOMC's potential revision weeks before it occurs — the July CPI date is the next major market event to watch after Monday's open.
