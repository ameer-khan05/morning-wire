The oil relief bid's ceiling is the Houthi Saudi port blockade. The five-country ceasefire proposal addresses the Iran-US conflict and Hormuz; it does not address Houthi operations against Saudi energy infrastructure and Red Sea shipping, which is a separate military campaign the Houthis can continue regardless of any Iran-US ceasefire agreement. If the ceasefire is reached and Hormuz reopens while Houthis maintain a Saudi port blockade, Gulf energy disruption persists from a different source. War-risk insurance premiums, which remain structurally elevated from the tanker kills in prior weeks, will reprice for that scenario rather than a full Gulf normalization. Oil at $87.52 Brent reflects a partial-relief scenario: Hormuz ceasefire possible, Houthi operations continuing. Full relief — both theaters resolved — is a different and lower oil price environment that Tuesday's numbers do not yet reflect.

Quick hits

  • The +52% Q2 EPS growth pace from the 10% of S&P 500 companies that had reported through last Friday is the most important macro data point for understanding tonight's Alphabet earnings in context. Fifty-two percent EPS growth at the index level means the revenue and margin environment is fundamentally strong in Q2 — which means Alphabet's earnings need to clear a high bar to outperform expectations, not just a baseline one. Alphabet's Q2 consensus of +21% revenue growth and +26% EPS growth would be consistent with a +52% index environment if Google Cloud AI growth is accelerating and Search AI monetization is holding. The risk is not that Alphabet misses the consensus — it is that it beats the consensus but the forward guidance on capex-to-revenue conversion disappoints.
  • The Houthi Saudi port blockade is an underreported escalation with direct market implications. Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping — which began in December 2023 and have continued through the Iran conflict — already added 20–30% to shipping time and cost for Asia-Europe trade routes that go around the Cape of Good Hope instead of through the Red Sea. A declared Saudi port blockade extends that disruption from transit shipping to destination shipping for Saudi Arabia's energy exports. Saudi Aramco's crude oil export capacity is one of the variables keeping Brent below $100 despite Hormuz disruption; if Saudi port blockade materializes as actual interference with tanker loading at Saudi terminals, the oil price ceiling lifts. War-risk insurance underwriters will price that scenario tomorrow if it is not already in their models.
