Second, Spirit Airlines grounded all operations on Saturday, becoming the first major US airline to cease operations in 25 years. Spirit's shutdown is a direct economic consequence of the Iran-driven WTI crude oil regime at $104 per barrel: jet fuel costs at current WTI levels have eliminated Spirit's thin-margin business model, which depended on low fuel prices to sustain fares competitive against legacy carriers. Spirit's shutdown is not an isolated event; it is the first measurable corporate casualty of the oil regime, and the market will watch for sector contagion at Monday's open.

Berkshire · Abel · First Meeting $11.3B Abel chairs for first time · Buffett (95) front row · Q1 $11.3B vs $9.6B · smaller crowd · folksy era ends

Berkshire Hathaway's first annual meeting under Greg Abel — Buffett (95) in the front row as an observer. Q1 operating earnings doubled to $11.3 billion from $9.6 billion. A smaller crowd marks the first meeting in over 60 years without Buffett in the chair.

Berkshire Hathaway's annual meeting in Omaha on Saturday was the first since 1965 not chaired by Warren Buffett. Greg Abel, who became CEO in January 2026 when Buffett stepped down from executive leadership, presided over detailed business discussions that CBS News described as replacing "the folksy wisdom and jokes that were a staple of the Berkshire Hathaway annual meeting for decades." Buffett attended as a director, seated in the front row, escorted to his chair to a standing ovation from a noticeably smaller crowd at CHI Health Center than prior years — a practical indicator that the meeting's prior attendance was driven by the desire to see Buffett specifically, not Berkshire generically. The financial results are not in question: Q1 2026 operating earnings of $11.3 billion — up from $9.6 billion in Q1 2025, a 17.7% increase — confirm that the business transition has not disrupted Berkshire's fundamentals. Warren Buffett, now 95, sat in the front row and received applause; Tim Cook attended the morning session and was acknowledged by Buffett. The crowd was smaller, the tone was more formal, the numbers were stronger. The Buffett era at Berkshire's annual meeting has ended its particular form of financial theater — what replaces it is a traditional institutional investor meeting, distinguished only by the performance of the underlying business.

Spirit Airlines · Shutdown · 25 Years Grounded First major US airline shutdown in 25 years · WTI $104 · jet fuel regime · ultra-low-cost model broken · sector contagion watch

Spirit Airlines grounds all operations — the first major US airline shutdown in 25 years. Jet fuel at WTI $104 has destroyed Spirit's ultra-low-cost margin model. The market will watch for sector contagion in other fuel-intensive, thin-margin carriers at Monday's open.

Spirit Airlines grounded all operations on Saturday, becoming the first major US airline to cease operations entirely since the post-9/11 collapse of Midway Airlines in 2001. Spirit's shutdown is a direct and specific consequence of the Iran-conflict oil price regime: Spirit's ultra-low-cost carrier model depends on jet fuel costs being sufficiently low to enable fares below legacy carriers while maintaining positive operating margins. WTI crude at $104 per barrel — sustained for more than two months by the Hormuz blockade — has eliminated that margin equation. Spirit's $1.1 billion in 2025 revenue, already under pressure from post-pandemic competition, cannot absorb fuel costs at current levels while maintaining the price competitiveness that is the sole reason its customers fly with it. The shutdown's market implications extend beyond Spirit itself: American, Frontier, and other thin-margin or heavily fuel-exposed carriers will face scrutiny at Monday's open about whether their fuel hedging provides sufficient protection at WTI $104 for the duration of the Iran conflict. The legacy carriers — Delta, United, American — have fuel hedging programs and revenue diversification that provide a buffer; ultra-low-cost carriers with minimal hedging are the most exposed segment. Spirit is the first to break; the question for Monday is whether it is also the last.

Quick hits

  • The Nasdaq 25,000 milestone — crossed and confirmed at Friday's 25,114.44 close — is the first five-digit round number the index has crossed since its 2021 highs. The milestone will attract significant media attention and retail investor attention at Monday's open, potentially providing a psychological momentum boost to the index. The 25,000 level was achieved in a week where Microsoft confirmed $37 billion in AI annual revenue run rate and Apple posted a record quarter — which means the milestone is analytically grounded in genuine earnings performance rather than multiple expansion alone. The VIX at 16.40 suggests the options market is not worried about a 25,000 reversal; the Iran dual-track and Lebanon expiry are the risks that could test that complacency in the 11 days ahead.
  • Spirit Airlines' shutdown creates a specific legal and operational situation for the roughly 4 million passengers who hold Spirit tickets for future travel. Unlike a bankruptcy reorganization (which keeps an airline operating while restructuring), a complete operations shutdown means those passengers receive neither their travel nor their ticket money from Spirit directly — they are unsecured creditors in a company with no ongoing revenue. The DOT's passenger protection protocols cover some passengers via credit card chargebacks; others may have travel insurance. The practical disruption — roughly 300 daily Spirit flights cancelled immediately — creates demand pressure on competing low-cost carriers including Frontier, Southwest, and Allegiant, potentially supporting their yields in the near term even as the industry faces the fuel cost pressure that destroyed Spirit.
  • Berkshire's Q1 operating earnings of $11.3 billion — doubled from the prior year — deserve attention beyond the Buffett transition narrative. The doubling reflects specific business performance: Berkshire's insurance operations (GEICO, Berkshire Hathaway Reinsurance) have recovered from the prior year's catastrophe losses; BNSF (railroad) has benefited from freight volume recovery; and the energy business (Berkshire Hathaway Energy) has generated strong utility returns. The $11.3 billion figure does not include investment gains from Berkshire's equity portfolio (which would add tens of billions in the period Apple appreciated), as operating earnings exclude realized and unrealized investment income. Greg Abel's challenge is not the first quarter's performance — it is sustaining the capital allocation discipline that Buffett's reputation enabled: the ability to deploy the approximately $175 billion in cash Berkshire holds into high-quality acquisitions at reasonable prices, without the Buffett premium that counterparties historically offered.
  • The April jobs report due May 8 has a specific new dimension that the March report did not: Spirit Airlines' shutdown. Spirit employed approximately 11,000 people directly; its ground operations, catering contractors, and third-party logistics partners add additional headcount to the employment impact. Whether the Spirit layoffs land in the April (April 30 cutoff) or May report depends on the exact timing of the shutdown notice, but in either case, they represent the first measurable aviation-sector employment contraction of the oil-price-driven conflict regime. Warsh will be managing a fed funds decision framework against a jobs report that may show the first direct evidence of conflict-economy labor market deterioration — exactly the stagflation-adjacent scenario that creates the most difficult Fed posture.
  • Gold at $4,571 — unchanged from Friday's close, as markets are closed — represents approximately $550 above the pre-Iran conflict price level from late February 2026. That $550 per ounce risk premium, sustained for 65 days, has generated approximately $8.6 billion in total gold market value creation above pre-conflict levels across the approximately 15,600 tonnes of above-ground investment gold. The premium is being sustained by two concurrent drivers: the oil-driven inflation premium (UST 10Y at 4.52%, but gold is also a real-asset hedge) and the safe-haven demand from 65 days of active conflict. Trump's "can't imagine" statement on Iran's 14-point plan sustains both drivers into Monday; a genuine diplomatic breakthrough on Hormuz access would compress both drivers simultaneously and push gold toward $4,200-4,300.
  • The Dow's Friday close at 49,499.27 — 501 points below 50,000 — sets up the 50,000 psychological milestone as a potential first-week-of-May market story, conditional on the Iran dual-track and Spirit contagion not repricing the conflict risk premium into the equity market at Monday's open. The 50,000 narrative will attract retail and media attention if the Dow approaches the level without a negative macro catalyst intervening. The irony is that Meta's ~17% decline over two sessions — the primary driver keeping the Dow below 50,000 — was caused by AI spending guidance, not by the Iran conflict that has been the dominant macro theme for 65 days. The Dow's proximity to 50,000 is therefore being blocked by the AI capex narrative, not the geopolitical one.
