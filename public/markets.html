Markets · Deep Dive

US stock futures rebounded Thursday — S&P 500 +0.83% to 7,327.57, Dow +0.80% to 48,789, Nasdaq +1.05% — reversing Wednesday's sharp losses after CENTCOM announced the latest Iran strikes complete and traders positioned for the possibility of resumed negotiations. Oracle fell more than 10% in extended trading after announcing a $20 billion equity and debt raise for AI infrastructure expansion.

The week's market structure is a clean case study in geopolitical risk trading. Monday's Apache downing began the Iran escalation. Tuesday's US strikes and Trump ultimatum drove selling. Wednesday's 49-Tomahawk strike package plus Iran's counter-strikes plus the Hormuz closure declaration sent the S&P down 1.62%, the Dow down 1.87%, and the Nasdaq down 1.98% — the worst single-day decline since April. Thursday's CENTCOM "strikes complete" announcement reversed the acute risk-off positioning: futures climbed across all four major US indices, oil pulled back from its conflict peak, and gold fell as defensive positioning unwound. The Iran conflict has not resolved; it has paused. Markets are pricing the pause as a 70-80% probability of resumed talks, with the remaining 20-30% in continued conflict.

The Anchor

Wednesday was the worst day for US equities since April. Thursday is the recovery. The driver of both is the same: Iran. The resolution trade, when it arrives, will be one of the largest single-session relief rallies in recent memory.

Wednesday's decline — S&P -1.62%, Dow -1.87% (approximately 900 points), Nasdaq -1.98% — came as US forces struck Iran, Iran declared the Strait of Hormuz closed, and the IRGC fired on US military bases in Bahrain and Kuwait. The simultaneity of those three events in a single trading session produced the most intense Iran-driven market move since the conflict began February 28. Thursday's recovery tracks the CENTCOM "strikes complete" announcement: the same logic that sent markets down on escalation sends them up on the suggestion of closure. The recovery is partial — markets remain well below May 31's record levels (S&P 7,580 vs. 7,327; Dow 51,032 vs. 48,789) — because the conflict has not resolved. But the "strikes complete" framing is the market's best current signal that the ceiling on this round of escalation has been reached.

Oracle's after-hours decline of more than 10% on the $20 billion equity and debt raise is a separate and illustrative data point. Oracle has been one of the defining AI infrastructure stocks of 2026, rising substantially as the Stargate partnership with OpenAI and SoftBank has translated into concrete data center construction and AI workload contracts. The $20 billion raise is a capital-markets signal that Oracle's AI infrastructure buildout requires more external funding than its operating cash flow and existing balance sheet can provide — a growth story at a scale that exceeds internal funding. The market's -10% reaction is rational: equity dilution is a near-term negative for existing shareholders even when the underlying strategy is sound. It does not change the AI infrastructure demand thesis; it prices the cost of fulfilling it.

US stock futures climbed Thursday across all four major indices — S&P 500 +0.83% to 7,327.57, Dow +0.80% to 48,789, Nasdaq +1.05% to 24,665, Russell 2000 +0.90% to 2,864 — reversing Wednesday's sharp declines after CENTCOM announced its latest round of Iran strikes complete, raising hopes that negotiations could resume. Wednesday's session had seen the Nasdaq fall 1.98% — its worst day since April 2025 — as the confluence of US strikes, Iran's Hormuz closure declaration, and IRGC retaliatory strikes on US Gulf bases produced peak conflict-week trading volatility.

The S&P 500's gap from May 31's record 7,580 to Thursday's 7,327 — a 253-point or 3.3% decline — represents the Iran risk premium that markets are holding above fundamental equity value. The same AI infrastructure investment cycle (Nvidia, Dell, Snowflake earnings confirmed at extraordinary growth rates) and geopolitical backdrop (US military and tech dominance intact) that drove the May 31 record remain in place. What changed is the Iran conflict's intensity, which raised the hurdle rate for risk assets by compressing the probability of near-term rate cuts and adding war-premium uncertainty to commodity inputs and supply chains. Resolution removes that premium.

Oracle fell more than 10% in extended trading after revealing plans to raise an additional $20 billion through equity and debt offerings to fund its artificial intelligence infrastructure expansion — the largest single AI-related capital raise by an established enterprise technology company in 2026. Oracle is a core infrastructure partner in the Stargate joint venture with OpenAI and SoftBank, which is building $500 billion in US AI infrastructure over four years, and the $20 billion raise signals that the data center construction and AI workload deployment pace requires capital beyond Oracle's operating cash flow capacity.

The market's reaction — a 10%+ after-hours decline — reflects equity dilution math rather than a strategic judgment against the AI infrastructure thesis. Oracle has been among the defining AI infrastructure stocks of 2026, and the $20 billion raise is a signal that its AI buildout is proceeding at a pace that requires external capital. The negative near-term price reaction is the cost of that growth. Longer term, if Oracle's AI infrastructure position translates to multi-year cloud contract revenues — as the Stargate partnership structure is designed to produce — the equity raised today at current prices may look cheap relative to future earnings. The market's Thursday-morning recovery in broader indices was not Oracle-driven; Oracle remained pressured while chip and AI-platform stocks led the recovery.

Oil prices pulled back from their conflict-week peak on Thursday — WTI toward $89.20 (-2.45%) and Brent toward $93.85 (-1.82%) — as CENTCOM's "strikes complete" announcement raised hopes of Hormuz normalization, even as Iran's closure declaration and CENTCOM's denial left the strait's status disputed in real time. Both benchmarks remain elevated above May 31's WTI $87.20 close, reflecting the ongoing Iran war premium. Gold fell 2.36% to $4,185 as risk appetite returned and defensive positioning unwound. The UST 10-year yield rose to 4.53% (+8 bp), as war-premium inflation concerns offset the safety bid.

The Hormuz status dispute is the most market-consequential open question: Iran declared it closed to all traffic; CENTCOM said commercial ships are continuing to transit normally. Tanker transit data from Lloyd's and MarineTraffic will be the authoritative tiebreaker. If tankers confirm CENTCOM's version — normal transit continuing — WTI could fall further toward $86-87. If Hormuz transit slows meaningfully in the next 24 hours, WTI could spike above $93-95. The oil market is pricing approximately 30-35% Hormuz disruption probability at current levels, consistent with the conflict's intensity but discounted by CENTCOM's denial and the historic resilience of Hormuz traffic even during conflict periods.

The gap between May 31's market records (S&P 7,580, Dow 51,032, Nasdaq 26,972) and Thursday's recovery levels (S&P 7,327, Dow 48,789, Nasdaq 24,665) is the Iran risk premium embedded in current equity prices — 253 points for the S&P, 2,243 points for the Dow, approximately 2,300 points for the Nasdaq. When the Iran conflict resolves — deal signed, Hormuz confirmed open, sanctions lifted or suspended — those gaps close in a single-session relief rally that market participants are already positioning around the edges of. No fundamental in the AI investment thesis or US corporate earnings cycle has changed since May 31; only the geopolitical variable has changed.

The rate-cut repricing component adds additional fuel to the resolution trade. Iran's conflict has kept commodity prices elevated, maintained Hormuz uncertainty as an inflationary input to global supply chains, and prevented the Fed from pivoting toward cuts that the AI-driven equity rally would otherwise have priced by now. Resolution would allow the Fed to signal cuts more confidently, compressing the UST 10-year yield from 4.53% back toward 4.20-4.30%, which adds a bond-proxy tailwind to equities beyond the direct relief rally. The total return from a deal-announcement session — direct index recovery plus rate-cut repricing — could exceed 5-7% for the S&P in a single day.

Quick hits

  • The Russell 2000's +0.90% Thursday recovery (+2,864) is notable because small-cap stocks are rate-sensitive and the week's geopolitical events have been modestly negative for rate-cut expectations. The Russell's recovery implies that traders are not adding significant rate-hike probability on the back of Iran — they are treating the conflict's inflation channel as temporary and transient rather than structurally persistent. If they are right, the Russell's recovery from its conflict lows will be as fast as its decline.
  • Gold's 2.36% fall to $4,185 on a day when Iran is actively firing on US bases and Hormuz is formally declared closed is striking. It suggests the market is reading CENTCOM's "strikes complete" language as a genuine bound on this round of escalation, and that traders are pricing significant probability of resumed talks within days. If that reading is wrong — if the strikes restart or Hormuz genuinely disrupts — gold will recover quickly and new highs above $4,300 are possible within the week.
  • The Anthropic and OpenAI IPO filings, and Oracle's $20 billion AI infrastructure raise, together represent the AI economy's capital market moment: private-market valuations becoming public; infrastructure buildout outpacing self-funding capacity; and the AI investment thesis moving from "speculative" to "capital-markets material." The Iran conflict is the single variable separating current equity levels from new records. When it resolves, the AI capital market story resumes with three concurrent major events (two IPO pricings and an Oracle recovery) as the immediate catalysts.
