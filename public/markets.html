Markets · Deep Dive

Stocks surged and oil crashed Monday as Trump's Sunday night announcement that the Iran-US war is "complete" removed the largest geopolitical risk premium from global markets since the conflict began. The S&P 500 climbed 1.49% to 7,542 — within 38 points of its May 31 all-time record of 7,580 — while Nasdaq gained 2.38% to 25,460 and WTI crude crashed 5.74% to $79.46, its two-month low. UST 10Y yields fell 13 bp to 4.34% as the rate-cut repricing began.

Monday's market session is the "signed deal → record attempt" scenario that Friday's close was positioned toward — with the key difference that Sunday's announcement arrived before Monday's open, giving markets the full session to process the resolution rather than reacting to an intraday headline. The S&P's 1.49% gain from 7,431 lands at 7,542, 38 points from its May 31 all-time record of 7,580 — a gap that represents roughly a half-day of trading at Monday's pace. The Nasdaq's 2.38% outperformance versus the S&P reflects the tech-heavy index's AI component, which benefits disproportionately from the rate-cut repricing: lower long-term rates raise the discounted present value of AI company cash flows more than economically cyclical stocks. WTI at $79.46 is the five-letter word for the session: a 5.74% single-day crash that begins the oil deflation sequence — $79 WTI → CPI passthrough → Fed cut path → rate-cut confirmation — that analysts have projected as the second-order market tailwind from the Iran deal since negotiations began.

The Anchor

S&P 7,542. WTI $79.46. Nasdaq 25,460. UST 10Y 4.34%. The record is 38 points away. The rate-cut repricing that oil normalization enables has just begun. The second act of Monday's trade is the bond market.

The equity trade is the first act, and it is unambiguous: stocks surged across all major indices as markets processed the Iran deal announcement without the uncertainty discount that has been embedded in valuations since February 28. The S&P's 38-point gap to its May 31 record of 7,580 is the residual risk premium that remains to be resolved — likely this week or next as the formal June 19 signing occurs and markets confirm the deal's finality. The Nasdaq's outperformance (+2.38% vs S&P's +1.49%) reflects a dual catalyst: geopolitical risk reduction (general risk-on) and rate-cut repricing (AI company cash flows most sensitive to long-duration discount rate movements). The Dow (+1.20%) and Russell 2000 (+0.79%) are lagging, consistent with their heavier exposure to industrials and small caps whose valuations are less rate-sensitive.

The bond trade is the second act, and it is the one that cascades into the rest of 2026. UST 10Y at 4.34% (down 13 bp from Friday's 4.47%) is the opening reprice — not the destination. Analysts expect the 10Y to trade toward 4.10-4.20% over weeks as WTI at $79.46 or lower shows up in headline CPI, removing the oil-driven inflation overlay that has kept the Fed on hold. The difference between 4.47% and 4.10% is a 37-basis-point repricing that multiplied across the duration of investment-grade credit, mortgage rates, and risk asset valuation models translates into a significant secondary stimulus for housing, corporate borrowing, and equity multiples.

The S&P 500 climbed 1.49% to 7,542 Monday — just 38 points from its May 31 all-time record of 7,580 — as markets processed Trump's Sunday night announcement that the US-Iran war is "complete" in the fullest single-session risk-on move since the conflict's diplomatic breakthrough began in late May. Nasdaq led at +2.38% to 25,460, driven by the AI sector's dual benefit from geopolitical risk removal and rate-cut repricing that raises the net present value of long-duration tech cash flows. The Dow gained 1.20% to 51,816; the Russell 2000 added 0.79% to 2,946.

The 38-point gap from the S&P to its record is the residual uncertainty premium from three sources: the deal not yet formally signed (June 19 ceremony), the Hormuz reopening still in the mine-removal phase, and Israel's announcement that it will not withdraw from Lebanon creating an unresolved conflict axis. As each resolves — Geneva signing, Hormuz oil flow confirmation, Lebanon situation assessed — the gap narrows. The baseline scenario has the S&P at or above 7,580 before month-end as the June 19 signing acts as the final confirmation catalyst.

WTI Crashes 5.74% to $79.46 · Brent $83.17 · Hormuz Reopening Priced In · Sub-$80 WTI $79.46 WTI $79.46 ▼5.74% from $84.29 Fri close · two-month low · Brent $83.17 ▼4.70% from $87.27 · Hormuz mine removal: begins · US naval blockade: authorized to end · oil price path: WTI $79 → $75-76 over weeks as Iranian + Hormuz oil resumes · CPI passthrough: 1-2 months · oil-driven inflation overlay: removed → Fed cut path cleared · OPEC+ response: possible production cut to support prices · Iran oil: currently sanctioned but deal terms include potential sanction relief discussions

WTI crude crashed 5.74% to $79.46 Monday — its lowest level in two months and the first sub-$80 WTI print since before the Iran conflict began — as the Hormuz reopening priced in immediately on Trump's Sunday night deal announcement. Brent fell 4.70% to $83.17. The oil move is among the largest single-day declines in the conflict period, as the Strait of Hormuz risk premium — which had kept WTI elevated relative to pre-conflict fundamentals — began unwinding without market friction that a surprise announcement would have produced. Both the timing and the magnitude are consistent with a market that had positioned for the deal's inevitability and is now completing the unwind.

The oil price path from $79.46 is downward as the Hormuz reopening progresses: mine removal operations take weeks, after which Iranian oil export capacity resumes, and broader OPEC+ production flexibility becomes relevant. Analysts project WTI toward $75-76 over the coming weeks as the supply picture clarifies. The CPI passthrough from $79 WTI to headline inflation requires 1-2 months but, once it appears, gives the Fed the data cover to execute a rate cut. OPEC+ may respond with production support to counteract the price decline; that complication is months away and does not change the near-term oil deflation trajectory.

UST 10Y yields fell 13 basis points to 4.34% Monday — the opening move in what analysts expect to be a significant rate-cut repricing as WTI crude at $79.46 begins the inflation normalization sequence that clears the Fed's path to cut. The 10Y at 4.34% is the starting point rather than the destination: the repricing is expected to continue toward 4.10-4.20% over weeks as oil's CPI passthrough materializes and Federal Reserve officials shift their language from hold to cut. Gold declined modestly to $4,075 (-0.41%) as risk-off demand eased, though the decline was smaller than might be expected, reflecting that some investors retained hedges against remaining conflict risks including Israel-Lebanon.

The rate-cut repricing cascades through the economy in ways that equity markets are only beginning to price. Mortgage rates — which track the 10Y with a spread — will fall as yields decline, improving housing affordability after two years of the most restrictive rates since 2006. Corporate borrowing costs will ease, most significantly for rate-sensitive sectors including real estate, utilities, and small caps. Equity multiples expand mechanically as the discount rate applied to future cash flows falls — a secondary tailwind for every company in the S&P 500 on top of the geopolitical risk-off unwinding that drove Monday's primary move.

The June 19 ceremony is the second stage of the two-stage market resolution trade: Monday's session processes the announcement; Friday's Geneva signing provides the confirmation that formal treaty-level commitment has been made. The two-stage structure means market pricing is not complete at Monday's close — the full resolution trade includes the Geneva signing reaction.

The June 19 signing also starts the 60-day clock on the subsequent negotiations covering sanctions relief, Iran's nuclear program, and the broader conflict settlement terms. Those negotiations will dominate the July-August geopolitical landscape and have their own market implications: successful progress on Iranian sanctions relief opens the door for legitimate Iranian oil exports that add further to the Hormuz supply normalization; failure to agree on nuclear terms within 60 days creates a risk that the deal collapses and oil markets re-price the conflict premium. The 60-day negotiating window is the medium-term variable that the June 19 signing ceremony launches.

Quick hits

  • The Nasdaq's 2.38% outperformance versus the S&P's 1.49% is the AI premium: tech companies with long-duration cash flow projections benefit disproportionately from the rate-cut repricing because their valuation is most sensitive to the discount rate applied to distant earnings. Every 25 bp decline in the 10Y adds approximately 2-3% to the P/E multiple justified for a high-growth tech company with 10-year earning projections. The Nasdaq's outperformance will likely extend as the 10Y continues repricing from 4.34% toward 4.10-4.20% — making the tech-heavy index the primary beneficiary of the deal's second-order market effects.
  • Gold at $4,075 (-0.41%) is a more interesting signal than its modest decline suggests. Gold has historically fallen 2-5% on major geopolitical resolution events as risk-off demand unwinds. Monday's 0.41% decline suggests that a meaningful proportion of gold holders are retaining positions — likely reflecting uncertainty about Israel's refusal to withdraw from Lebanon, the 60-day negotiation window's uncertainty, and the structural gold demand from central banks that has persisted through the conflict. Gold at $4,075 is pricing a deal that is real but not fully settled — an accurate assessment of the June 19 → 60-day negotiation sequence ahead.
  • The Russell 2000's 0.79% underperformance versus the S&P (+1.49%) and Nasdaq (+2.38%) reflects small caps' lower sensitivity to the geopolitical risk-premium driver and their higher sensitivity to credit conditions and domestic economic activity — both of which are improving but more slowly than the headline geopolitical news. Small caps will be the last US equity segment to fully reflect the deal's second-order effects (lower rates, easier credit, improved consumer sentiment) because those effects require the rate-cut repricing to cascade into actual Fed action, not just yield movement. The catch-up trade for small caps comes in Q3, not Q2.
