Markets · Deep Dive

Fed Chair Warsh held rates at his inaugural FOMC but delivered the hawkish shock the market did not expect: 9 of 18 members project a rate hike by year-end — scrapping the rate-cut consensus that markets had built from the Iran deal's oil signal — sending the Dow down 0.98% to 51,493, the S&P 500 down 1.21% to 7,420, and the Nasdaq down 1.34% to 26,022, while Gold fell 1.79% to $4,276 and Intel surged 8.5% premarket Thursday on an Apple chip deal.

Warsh's first FOMC meeting delivered what inflation hawks had hoped for and rate-cut expecters had not priced: a dot plot that showed 9 of 18 FOMC members projecting a rate hike by year-end, with the committee citing a firming job market and inflation at its highest in three years — pushed higher in part by energy prices from the Middle East conflict. The hawkish pivot comes at the moment when the oil market was beginning to price a significant supply increase from the US-Iran MOU's Hormuz reopening and Iranian export sanctions waiver, creating a simultaneous macro crosscurrent: WTI is declining (disinflationary) while Warsh's FOMC is projecting rate hikes (inflationary signal). The resolution of this tension will define markets in Q3. If WTI's decline is sustained below $75 and the Q3 CPI reports show the oil-to-CPI passthrough that the past week's price declines imply, the rate-hike projection may prove to be an FOMC overcorrection that is walked back. If the labor market stays hot and core PCE stays sticky, Warsh's signal will prove prescient and the rate-hike path will accelerate from projection to policy. Neither outcome is resolved this morning. What is clear is that Wednesday's selloff was proportionate to the surprise: a 1.21% S&P decline on a hawkish dot-plot signal from a new chair, on the same day the MOU was signed, tells you that the Fed is the dominant market driver and the geopolitical positive has been largely priced. Thursday's Nasdaq futures advance suggests markets believe Wednesday's move overshot — the partial recovery test begins at open.

The Anchor

Nine of 18. That's how many FOMC members project a rate hike by year-end. Not a cut — a hike. The market had been pricing cuts. Warsh's first meeting rewrote the rate narrative in 45 minutes of press conference. Thursday's Nasdaq futures say the market thinks it overreacted. It might be right.

The FOMC dot plot is not a commitment — it is a projection. Nine of 18 members projecting a rate hike is not a majority (that would require 10), and projections from individual FOMC members carry no binding force. What the dot plot does is reveal the distribution of thinking among FOMC participants, and a distribution in which nine members see a rate hike by year-end is dramatically more hawkish than the market's prior expectation of two or three cuts. The gap between what markets had priced (cuts) and what the dot plot showed (hikes projected by half the committee) explains the magnitude of Wednesday's selloff: the S&P fell 1.21% not because the Fed did something to the economy, but because the Fed's disclosed thinking about the future was much more hawkish than the market had assumed.

The context Warsh's FOMC cited for the hawkish shift is important: the committee highlighted a firming labor market and inflation at its highest in three years. The inflation reference may be partially influenced by the pre-deal energy price environment — WTI was near $84 before the Iran deal, and that level would feed into the CPI data that informed the FOMC's June projections. If WTI sustains at $75 or below through the Q3 CPI data cycle, the inflation concern that pushed the FOMC to the hawkish side will partially resolve — and future dot plots may show the hike projections rolling back. The rate narrative for the next quarter is a race between Warsh's inflation concern and the oil market's post-deal supply surge. Thursday morning's partial market recovery, with Nasdaq futures rising, reflects the market betting that the oil disinflationary signal will win that race.

Fed Chair Kevin Warsh held rates at the Federal Reserve's June FOMC meeting but released a dot plot showing 9 of 18 FOMC members projecting a rate hike by year-end — a hawkish signal that scrapped the rate-cut consensus markets had built since the Iran deal's oil-price decline signaled Q3 disinflationary relief. Warsh cited a firming labor market and inflation at its highest level in three years, pushed in part by energy prices during the Middle East conflict, as the basis for the hawkish distribution. Markets sold off sharply on the projection: the S&P 500 fell 1.21% to 7,420, the Nasdaq fell 1.34% to 26,022, and the Dow fell 0.98% to 51,493. Gold fell 1.79% to $4,276 and the 10-year yield rose 4 basis points to 4.46%.

The key context for the hawkish signal is the timing: Warsh's FOMC was projecting based on data from before the Iran deal's full oil-price signal had appeared in CPI reports. WTI declined from approximately $84 to $75 in the five days between the deal announcement and the FOMC meeting, but that decline will not appear in CPI data for one to two months. The FOMC was therefore looking at an inflation dataset that still reflects the elevated pre-deal energy environment while projecting for a future that includes the post-deal supply surge. If the oil disinflationary signal materializes in Q3 CPI at the magnitude the WTI decline implies — approximately 0.36% of direct headline relief — Warsh's hawkish projection may be revised at the September FOMC. The nine hike projections could become five or six by September. But markets must price the disclosed distribution now, and "nine members see a hike" is a significant repricing event regardless of the Q3 data timeline.

All major US equity indices fell sharply on Wednesday after Warsh's hawkish dot plot, with the Dow shedding 507 points (0.98%) to 51,493, the S&P 500 declining 1.21% to 7,420, and the Nasdaq falling 1.34% to 26,022 as growth and technology stocks bore the brunt of the rate-hike repricing. NovoCure fell 18.8% after a major clinical trial for its tumor-treating fields technology in a new indication failed to meet its primary endpoint. Accenture fell 15.6% following a guidance revision citing lower enterprise technology spending intent in the near term — a data point that is relevant for the broader enterprise AI investment thesis. Defensive sectors outperformed on the day as investors rotated out of rate-sensitive growth names.

The Nasdaq's 1.34% decline is the most policy-sensitive major index move on Wednesday, reflecting that AI and technology names carry high growth-dependent valuations that compress when the rate path shifts from cuts to potential hikes. AI hardware names — which had already seen profit-taking on Tuesday — faced a second consecutive session of pressure as the hawkish FOMC projection added a valuation compression driver on top of the prior session's profit-taking. Nasdaq futures were rising Thursday morning, suggesting the market believes Wednesday's selloff was an overreaction to a non-majority dot-plot signal from a new chair on a data set that predates the oil deal. Whether that recovery is durable will depend on whether further FOMC members push toward the hike posture in speeches this week or walk back the signal.

The Apple deal is a commercial validation of Intel's foundry strategy, which has been the company's primary repositioning effort after years of market-share losses in chip design to AMD and in advanced semiconductor manufacturing to Taiwan Semiconductor. An Apple commitment represents the highest-tier customer validation available in the chip industry — the kind of contract that signals foundry quality is competitive for the most demanding production volumes in consumer electronics.

The Intel-Apple deal illustrates the divergence within the technology sector that will define the next phase of market performance under the hawkish FOMC environment. Growth stocks with high rate-sensitivity — primarily the AI hardware and software names that dominate Nasdaq — face valuation compression from the rate-hike projection. But companies with company-specific positive demand catalysts — new customer wins, major supply agreements, product-cycle inflections — can outperform the macro headwind because their earnings drivers are independent of the rate path. Intel's Apple deal is the archetype: a multi-year supply agreement with a single major customer anchors Intel's revenue forecast regardless of whether the FOMC raises or cuts rates. For investors seeking technology sector exposure in a higher-rate environment, Intel's foundry success repositions it as a capital-intensive, demand-secure manufacturer rather than a growth-multiple-dependent semiconductor designer.

Gold fell 1.79% to $4,276 on Wednesday as the hawkish FOMC signal unwound the inflation-hedge premium that had supported gold's rise through the Iran deal week — gold at $4,315 on Tuesday had reflected both geopolitical uncertainty and the rate-cut path; Warsh's rate-hike projection removes the rate-cut component of that bid. WTI crude continued its post-deal decline to $75.01, down 0.61% on the day and approximately $9 from its pre-deal level near $84, as the MOU's signing and confirmed Hormuz reopening proceedings sustain the supply-side downward pressure on oil prices independent of the Fed. The 10-year Treasury yield rose 4 basis points to 4.46%, reversing Tuesday's 2-basis-point decline as the hawkish dot plot repriced fixed-income markets.

The simultaneous decline of gold and oil on Wednesday tells two different stories. Gold's decline is a rate-expectation response: when the market was pricing rate cuts, gold was attractive as a non-yielding asset whose opportunity cost relative to Treasuries was declining. When the FOMC shifted to projecting rate hikes, gold's opportunity cost relative to higher-yielding fixed income rises, reducing the marginal bid. Oil's decline, by contrast, is a supply story: the MOU's Hormuz reopening and Iranian export sanctions waiver are adding physical supply to a market that was already being repriced by the deal's expected impact. Gold and WTI are both declining, but for completely different reasons — and the WTI decline is more durable, because the supply increase from the MOU is structural. Gold will recover if Warsh's rate-hike projection is walked back in September.

Quick hits

  • The 9/18 dot plot is a near-miss for a rate-hike majority. Ten of 18 FOMC members projecting a hike would have been a majority signal that markets would price as near-certainty of action. Nine of 18 is almost the same thing in practice — it is the modal expectation of the committee split — but markets treat majority thresholds differently than near-majority ones. The one-member difference between nine and ten is economically trivial but psychologically significant for market pricing: "half the FOMC sees a hike" prices differently than "a majority of the FOMC sees a hike." Whether that distinction is meaningful in terms of actual Fed policy depends on which of the nine hike-projectors and nine non-hike-projectors give speeches and signals in the weeks ahead. A single FOMC member moving from non-hike to hike in subsequent communications would shift the distribution to majority and trigger another repricing.
  • Accenture's 15.6% decline on guidance that cited lower enterprise technology spending intent is the most consequential data point for the enterprise AI sector that came out of Wednesday's session — more revealing than the rate-sensitive stock moves. Accenture's consulting revenues are a leading indicator for enterprise technology investment: companies tell Accenture what they plan to spend on technology before they execute those decisions. If Accenture's guidance reflects actual enterprise technology spending pullback — driven by Warsh's hawkish FOMC signal, economic uncertainty, or budget reallocation — AI adoption timelines at large enterprises will be slower than frontier AI companies' ARR growth implied. The Accenture guidance lands in a week when OpenAI is filing for an IPO and SpaceX-xAI has acquired Cursor — the supply side of enterprise AI is expanding while the demand signal from a major consulting bellwether is softening.
  • WTI at $75.01 is already approximately $9 below its pre-deal level near $84. The CPI passthrough from that decline — approximately $0.04 per dollar of WTI decline, implying roughly 0.36% of direct headline relief — will appear in Q3 CPI data. That relief arrives in the FOMC's September meeting in the form of actual data, not projection. If the Fed raised rates in response to inflation that pre-dated the oil deal's supply surge — and the Q3 CPI data shows the surge unwinding — September's FOMC would have the data to reverse course. The market is effectively bet that the oil story beats the FOMC's June projection on a Q3 timeline. Nasdaq futures rising Thursday morning is the market making that bet.
