Markets · Deep Dive

The S&P 500, Nasdaq, and Dow all closed at simultaneous all-time records on Wednesday as Iran peace deal hopes crashed WTI crude 7% to $95.19 and AMD's 19% post-earnings surge powered the technology sector to its strongest conflict-era session.

Wednesday was the single best day for US equities since the Iran-US conflict began February 28: the S&P 500 reached a new record of 7,365.12 (▲ 1.46%), the Nasdaq hit 25,838.94 (▲ 2.02%), and the Dow closed at 49,910.59 (▲ 1.24%), all simultaneously at all-time highs for the first time since before the conflict began. The Russell 2000's 3.84% gain to 2,868 — the strongest performer among the four major indices — reflects small-cap stocks pricing the domestic economic benefit of a Hormuz resolution most aggressively. The two catalysts arrived simultaneously and reinforced each other: Axios reported that the US and Iran are close to a one-page memorandum to end the war, and AMD reported 38% year-over-year Q1 revenue growth with a 19% stock surge. WTI crude's 7% decline to $95.19 — the largest single-day oil drop of the conflict, erasing roughly half of the post-conflict oil premium in a single session — directly improved the Fed's inflation calculus in real time, making rate cuts incrementally more possible. Thursday morning futures are flat to marginally positive at approximately +0.1% as oil finds a modest bid on Trump's parallel warning to bomb Iran if no deal is reached — both the peace deal hopes and the bombing threat are active simultaneously.

The Anchor

Wednesday's simultaneous all-time record close on all four major indices is the conflict period's most powerful market day. The oil-inflation ceiling dropped 7% in a single session. The market is pricing a peace deal before it exists.

Wednesday's record close across all four major US indices is the market pricing the Iran peace deal as if it is already done — which it is not. The Axios report of a one-page memorandum framework triggered the market's reaction; the deal itself requires Iran's formal response, Pakistani mediation, and a final agreement process that Trump has simultaneously described as one-week-timeline and as having "no deadline." The market is therefore buying the optionality of a peace deal, not the certainty of one — the distinction matters because the rally is partially, perhaps substantially, reversible if the deal falls apart. The 7% WTI decline to $95.19 is the key: oil's conflict premium is approximately $20-25 per barrel above its pre-conflict equilibrium of $75-80. Wednesday's decline removed roughly half of that premium. If the deal collapses and oil returns to $110-115, the equity rally partially reverses; if the deal is signed and oil falls toward $80, the rally has substantially more room to run. The market is currently pricing a probability-weighted version of these two scenarios — roughly 60-65% deal probability implied by current levels.

AMD's contribution to Wednesday's rally should be distinguished from the Iran-deal component. AMD's 19% surge on 38% data center revenue growth is an earnings-driven move that is independent of the Iran deal — it would have occurred whether or not the peace deal news landed on the same day. The simultaneous arrival of the Iran deal news and the AMD earnings surge created a compounding effect: the Nasdaq's 2.02% gain required both inputs, as a day with only the Iran deal news and no AMD surge would likely have produced a 1-1.2% Nasdaq gain, and a day with only AMD earnings and no Iran deal news would likely have produced a 1-1.5% Nasdaq gain. The combination produced 2.02% — a simultaneous tailwind in geopolitics and earnings that will be difficult to replicate in subsequent sessions. Thursday's flat futures suggest the market is pausing to assess whether both catalysts have legs.

The specific index performance ordering is informative: the Russell 2000's 3.84% gain was the largest, reflecting small-cap stocks' disproportionate exposure to domestic energy costs and the domestic economy; the Nasdaq's 2.02% gain was amplified by AMD's 19% surge and the broader AI infrastructure earnings confirmation; the Dow's 1.24% and S&P's 1.46% gains reflect the large-cap blend of these forces. The VIX declining 14% to 15.82 is the volatility market's confirmation: options traders are no longer pricing elevated uncertainty for the near-term — specifically, the May 14 Lebanon expiry and the next Iran-UAE exchange risk have been partially discounted by the peace deal proximity signal. The VIX at 15.82 is back to the low-conflict-risk range it occupied during the ceasefire's relatively calm first three weeks; it has no longer priced the Iran-UAE attack of May 4 as the baseline forward scenario. If the peace deal falls apart and oil returns to $115, the VIX spike back above 20 would be the first signal the equity market needs to reprice.

WTI crude's 7% decline to $95.19 is the largest single-day oil price drop of the 69-day conflict — roughly halving the $20-25 per barrel premium that the Hormuz disruption has added above the pre-conflict $75-80 equilibrium. The market is pricing peace deal probability, not peace deal certainty. Oil at $95 is not $75.

WTI at $95.19 after Wednesday's 7% decline represents the oil market pricing an approximately 50-60% probability of a Hormuz reopening within the next 30-60 days — a probability-weighted midpoint between the pre-conflict $75-80 WTI level (Hormuz fully open scenario) and the conflict-era ceiling of $106-115 (Hormuz disrupted scenario). The 7% single-session decline is the oil market's largest conflict-era daily move and signals that the market had been fully pricing Hormuz disruption as the baseline scenario — meaning the peace deal's emergence as credible generated a large and immediate price revision. The move is also logistically grounded: Hormuz handles approximately 21 million barrels per day of oil transit; the disruption premium is not a geopolitical sentiment number but a real physical scarcity premium whose magnitude depends on how much oil can be rerouted around Hormuz via alternative pipelines and ports. The peace deal, if completed, removes the physical disruption; the market's 7% single-session pricing of that removal confirms that the premium was real and the market had been waiting for exactly this signal. Brent's smaller 4.31% decline to $108.23 — relative to WTI's 7.02% — reflects the different physical grade and delivery point dynamics: WTI (US domestic grade) is more sensitive to domestic economic conditions, while Brent (international benchmark) prices the global supply-demand balance with a slightly different sensitivity to Hormuz specifically.

Wednesday's ADP +109,000 private payrolls beat is a positive signal for the BLS Friday print, but the correlation between ADP and BLS has been unreliable in recent months — the two measures have diverged by 30,000-50,000 jobs in three of the past four months. The BLS consensus range of 55,000 to 165,000 is unusually wide for a pre-print estimate distribution, reflecting genuine disagreement among economists on three specific April uncertainties: (1) the oil-economy employment effect — whether $100+ WTI has produced layoffs in energy production, pipeline, trucking, logistics, and airline maintenance that would show up in April's payroll survey; (2) the Spirit Airlines timing — the carrier's 11,000 employees grounded operations May 3, but the April survey reference week (the week containing April 12) likely predates the grounding, pushing those layoffs to the May report; and (3) the conflict's indirect employment effects via consumer confidence and business investment delays. Kevin Warsh has not yet made any public communication as Fed Chair; Friday's jobs report will be the first major data he must interpret publicly, and markets will parse any subsequent Fed commentary as a signal of whether Warsh's stance is more hawkish (inflation priority) or more balanced (inflation plus employment) than Powell's. The Iran deal response and the jobs report may land in the same Friday session — a compressed information environment that creates both upside and downside risk for Thursday's flat futures.

Gold surges 3.14% to $4,700 simultaneously with equities — an unusual co-movement. The UST 10-year yield falls 8 basis points to 4.36% as lower oil reduces the inflation premium in bond prices. The dollar weakens 0.98% to 99.12 DXY. All four asset classes move in the same direction, signaling a single dominant macro driver: the peace deal.

Wednesday's unusual asset co-movement — equities up, bonds up (yields down), gold up, dollar down — is explicable through a single macro framework: the Iran peace deal reduces the oil-inflation premium embedded in all four asset prices simultaneously. Equities gain because lower oil improves corporate margins and consumer spending capacity. Bonds gain (yields fall 8 bps) because lower oil reduces the PCE inflation trajectory that had been keeping yields elevated above 4.4% for three consecutive weeks. Gold gains because the dollar weakens — lower oil reduces the dollar's safe-haven appeal as a global energy-settlement currency, and lower inflation expectations reduce the real-rate headwind to gold (gold does better in lower real-rate environments). The dollar weakens because lower oil reduces global demand for dollar-denominated oil settlement. All four movements are consistent, internally logical, and driven by the same upstream cause. The 8 basis point UST 10Y decline to 4.36% is the most economically significant move of the session: if it sustains and extends toward 4.20% over the next 30-60 days as the peace deal progresses and oil continues to fall, it unlocks a significant improvement in the rate-sensitive parts of the equity market — housing, consumer discretionary, and high-growth tech — that have been suppressed by above-4.4% rates since the conflict began.

Quick hits

  • The Russell 2000's 3.84% gain — the strongest performer among the four major indices — is the most informative single data point about what the market thinks a Hormuz reopening is worth for the domestic US economy. Small-cap companies have disproportionate revenue from and cost exposure to domestic economic activity: their customers are US consumers (more sensitive to oil price through gasoline costs), their supply chains use domestic trucking and logistics (directly exposed to diesel and oil costs), and their debt is disproportionately floating-rate (more sensitive to Fed policy than large-cap's fixed-rate debt). A 3.84% single-session Russell 2000 gain — versus 1.46% for the S&P — implies that the market is pricing: lower gasoline costs improving consumer purchasing power for small-cap consumer goods and services; lower diesel costs improving trucking and logistics margins for domestic supply chains; and higher Fed cut probability (from lower oil-driven inflation) improving the affordability of floating-rate refinancing for small-cap balance sheets. All three effects are downstream of the Iran deal and WTI normalization.
  • Gold's 3.14% gain to $4,700 on a day when equities rallied strongly deserves a macro explanation beyond the standard "gold is a safe haven." The simultaneous gold and equity rally is not a contradiction; it reflects the dollar-weakening mechanism that links both. When the DXY falls 0.98% — as it did Wednesday — dollar-denominated assets including gold become cheaper for non-dollar investors, increasing demand and pushing the price up. Additionally, gold benefits from lower real interest rates: lower oil expectations → lower PCE expectations → lower Fed funds rate expectations → lower real rate (nominal rate minus expected inflation) → higher gold price. The 8 basis point UST yield decline to 4.36% — combined with lower inflation expectations from cheaper oil — reduces the real rate and makes gold relatively more attractive versus interest-bearing assets. Wednesday's gold rally is therefore primarily a dollar-weakening and real-rate story, not a geopolitical fear story — which is different from gold's behavior during the conflict escalation phases, when it rose on flight-to-safety demand. This distinction is important: gold at $4,700 is pricing peace, not war, for the first time since the conflict began.
  • The Fed's position entering Thursday is significantly improved from its position entering Wednesday. Warsh's first month as Fed chair has been characterized by no public communication and a single new data reality: oil is now 7% lower than it was 24 hours ago, and the Iran deal that would lower it further is within sight. If Friday's jobs report shows moderate payrolls growth and WTI stabilizes at or below $95, Warsh has the first credible path to a rate cut that has existed since the conflict began. The market is currently pricing approximately 45-50% probability of a May FOMC cut and approximately 70-75% probability of a June cut — numbers that have moved 10-15 percentage points in the past 48 hours as the peace deal news processed. The Fed's exact FOMC date for May has not been publicly released — Warsh's tenure began April 28, and the standard Fed meeting calendar for May was expected to be in mid-May. The convergence of May FOMC, Lebanon expiry, Iran deal window, and jobs report in the same 10-day window makes this the most consequential macro calendar span of 2026.
  • The DXY declining 0.98% to 99.12 on Wednesday is a significant dollar move for a single session — comparable in magnitude to the dollar's safe-haven surge during the conflict's most intense phases. The decline reflects the mirror image of the flight-to-safety mechanism: as the Iran deal becomes more credible, the dollar's function as the safe-haven reserve currency of a conflict-risk scenario becomes less necessary, and global capital that had accumulated in dollar positions during the conflict period begins to be redirected to non-dollar assets. The DXY below 100 — it closed at 99.12, its lowest since the conflict began February 28 — is the first signal that the dollar's conflict-era premium is being partially returned to the market. The implications extend beyond currency: a weaker dollar improves the earnings of US multinational corporations reporting in dollars (the S&P 500's revenue, roughly 40% of which is international, gets a favorable translation effect), reduces the dollar cost of commodities for non-dollar economies, and supports emerging market currencies that have been under pressure from the dollar's conflict-period safe-haven demand.
  • The Iran deal probability embedded in Wednesday's close deserves quantification. WTI oil's pre-conflict equilibrium was approximately $75-80. The current $95.19 price implies a remaining conflict premium of approximately $15-20 per barrel above equilibrium. If the deal probability were 100% certain, WTI would likely price at $78-82 immediately — the full equilibrium. At $95.19, the market is pricing roughly 50-55% deal probability (the midpoint between $78 full-deal and $115 full-conflict is approximately $96-97, close to current levels). This implies the market believes there is a roughly 45-50% probability the deal falls apart, which is consistent with the complexity of the negotiation: Iran's internal political dynamics under Pezeshkian, the IRGC's posture, the specific terms of the nuclear enrichment moratorium, and Trump's parallel bomb-threat messaging all create genuine uncertainty about whether the one-page memo translates into a signed agreement.
  • AMD's 19% single-session gain and Supermicro's 18% gain produced a combined market cap increase of approximately $200 billion across the two companies — the largest combined single-day AI semiconductor market cap creation of 2026. Nvidia's 2% gain added another $50+ billion. The three companies together added roughly $250 billion in market capitalization on the same day that Iran deal news was processing. The AI data center build-out theme and the geopolitical peace trade are not competing for market attention — they are both providing simultaneous upward pressure on equities through different mechanisms. The durability of Wednesday's rally depends on both holding: the AI spending theme continues to be confirmed by quarterly earnings (next major test: Nvidia's Q1 2026 earnings, expected later in May), and the Iran deal progresses toward signature rather than collapsing on Iran's response to the US framework.
