The oil move's structure explains the Fed path repricing. When Brent rises from $91 to $95.47 in a single session on geopolitical news, markets distinguish between two scenarios: a temporary risk-premium spike that reverses when strikes stop, and a structural supply disruption that persists. The 11th consecutive strike night — with no ceasefire accepted and 26 days to the nuclear deadline — is consistent with a structural disruption rather than a temporary spike. The Houthi Saudi port blockade compounds the supply picture: Saudi Aramco's crude loading infrastructure being threatened simultaneously with Hormuz disruption means the supply constraint has two independent causes that require two independent resolutions. War-risk insurance underwriters are pricing both, which is why the market is treating $95 Brent as persistent rather than spike-and-reverse.

The UST 10-year yield reached 4.63% Wednesday — up 11 basis points and the highest since mid-May — as oil's surge above $85 passed through to inflation expectations and prompted a repricing of the Federal Reserve's September path. CME FedWatch now shows a 24% probability of a July rate hike (up from roughly 10% last week) and a 69% probability of at least 25 basis points by September. The July 29 FOMC meeting remains near-certain to hold; September has become a live decision.

The mechanism connecting oil to the UST 10-year is not direct — the Fed responds to realized inflation, not spot oil. But market participants reprice Treasuries in anticipation of what oil does to future CPI readings. Brent at $95.47 adds roughly 15–20 basis points to expected 12-month inflation relative to Brent at $80. That moves the Fed's reaction function even before CPI reports it. The September FOMC meeting on September 16 is now pricing a coin-flip between hold and 25-basis-point hike, rather than the strong-hold probability it had at $80 Brent last week. Gold at $4,118 benefiting from both geopolitical and inflation-hedge demand simultaneously is unusual — it typically has to choose between the two narratives, since geopolitical premium usually fades when resolved, while inflation premium builds with policy tightening. Both running together means the gold market is pricing an environment where neither geopolitical risk nor inflation risk resolves quickly.

Quick hits

  • The interaction between oil at $95 Brent and September Fed pricing is worth understanding precisely, because it affects the multiple at which investors value every long-duration growth asset — including AI stocks. The Fed's September 16 meeting is now pricing a 69% probability of at least 25 basis points. That is not a certainty of a hike, but it is enough to reprice discounted cash flow models for growth stocks whose value is heavily weighted in future earnings. A growth stock trading at 30× forward earnings is far more sensitive to a 25-basis-point rate increase than a value stock at 12× — meaning the Nasdaq's concentration in high-multiple AI names makes it more vulnerable to oil-driven rate repricing than the S&P 500 as a whole. Alphabet at $190 billion in capex and a premium multiple needs its Cloud AI Q2 number to justify the rate-adjusted valuation tonight.
  • Gold at $4,118 is operating in unusual multi-factor mode. Normal gold demand cycles run on one driver at a time: either inflation hedge (Fed tightening, CPI above target) or geopolitical safe haven (conflict, risk-off) or dollar weakness (dollar selling). When gold is responding to all three simultaneously — as it is now, with Brent driving inflation fears, the Iran conflict sustaining geopolitical premium, and the UST 10-year rising but not high enough to make dollar-denominated bonds a competing haven — the demand structure is unusually broad-based. That is a signal that the resolution of any one driver (oil ceasefire → deflation, Fed holds → inflation fears ease) does not necessarily produce a sharp gold reversal, because the other two drivers remain in place. $4,118 is not a single-factor trade.
