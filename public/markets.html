Brent crude surged to $87.23 (▲4.72%) and WTI to $80.80 (▲3.40%) Tuesday as President Trump announced the reimposition of the US Hormuz blockade starting 4 PM ET Monday and declared that the US would charge a 20% fee on all cargo shipped through the Strait of Hormuz — Bloomberg reporting Trump said the US would be "reimbursed at the rate of 20% on all cargo shipped for any and all costs necessary to do the job of providing safety and security" — while the UN's International Maritime Organization said there was "no legal basis through which to introduce mandatory tolls," and Iran's ports were specifically blockaded while non-Iranian vessels were told they would have "fair and open use" of the Strait, though all would pay the 20% fee.

The 20% Hormuz toll represents a transformation of the oil shock from a military-risk premium to a structural trade-cost premium. A military-risk premium in oil prices reflects the probability of supply disruption — it declines if the military situation de-escalates. A structural trade-cost premium reflects a new cost of doing business through the Strait that applies even under conditions of relative military calm — and it is much harder to remove once established. If the US enforces the 20% toll and major shipping operators begin pricing it into freight rates, the $87 Brent level becomes not a crisis-premium peak but a new baseline elevated by approximately $10–15 of permanent toll-related logistics cost. Wednesday's PPI will be the first data point measuring oil's pass-through into producer prices in the blockade environment, and the oil-inflation interaction Warsh must communicate in testimony today will define the rate path conversation for the next six weeks.

The CPI paradox is the most acute analytical challenge of the week. But that reading will arrive simultaneously with Brent at $87.23, a 20% Hormuz toll, and a conflict trajectory pointing toward further oil price escalation. If Warsh uses the June CPI data as the primary basis for his rate signal at 10 AM, he risks communicating a policy stance that is structurally disconnected from the energy price reality markets are pricing in real time. The 10-year yield at 4.62% — already above Monday's 4.52% before CPI even drops — shows that bond markets are not waiting for the backward-looking data to price the forward oil scenario. Warsh's communication challenge is not which CPI number to cite but how to explain why the Fed's backward-looking mandate produces a different answer than the oil market's forward-looking price.

Quick hits

  • The 20% Hormuz toll's legal contestation by the UN is real but practically irrelevant in the near term. The UN Convention on the Law of the Sea establishes the right of transit passage through international straits — a right that cannot be conditioned on payment under existing international law. The IMO has the right to issue that opinion but no enforcement mechanism. The US, which has not ratified UNCLOS, can ignore the legal objection while the Navy enforces the toll physically. What matters in the short term is not the legal question but the commercial response: will major shipping operators — whose tankers carry the vast majority of Hormuz oil — halt sailings rather than pay a 20% fee, or will they pass the cost through to cargo owners and keep shipping? If operators pass the cost through, the toll becomes a $10–15 per barrel oil-logistics tax embedded in the price of every barrel transiting the Strait. If operators halt sailings, the supply disruption produces the $85–95 scenario Goldman and Citi have been modeling. Either outcome is significantly inflationary — the difference is between gradual and immediate.
  • The bank earnings timing creates a unique financial system stress test. All five major US banks are reporting Q2 earnings on the same day that the US Hormuz blockade takes effect, June CPI drops, and the Fed Chair testifies. Bank executives giving forward guidance on loan quality, energy-sector credit exposure, and geopolitical risk assessment will be commenting in real time on a conflict whose oil-price implications are still unfolding. Goldman Sachs at 6.0% implied move and Citigroup at 5.5% reflect options markets acknowledging that forward guidance uncertainty is unusually high: the question is not whether Q2 earnings were good (consensus expects 20%+ EPS growth) but whether bank executives will guide toward elevated credit provisions for Iran-related economic stress in H2 2026. A cautious forward guidance from any of the five — particularly Citigroup, which has the largest international exposure — would be more market-moving than the Q2 beat itself.
