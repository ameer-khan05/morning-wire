Markets · Deep Dive · Oil War Premium Unwinds · Chip Stocks +3% · Broad Rally · Iran Diplomacy Progress

All four US equity indices rose Friday as Iran diplomacy "positive progress" unwound the war premium that had driven oil to Brent $79.10 and WTI $74.49 through Thursday — Brent falling $2.30 to $76.80 (▼2.91%) and WTI declining to $72.20 (▼3.08%) — while the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index surged 3.06% as SK Hynix's Nasdaq debut held its gains and chip stocks led a broad tech-sector rally that pushed the Nasdaq up 1.3% to 26,207.

Friday's market action is the mirror image of Wednesday's split: instead of the Nasdaq rising while the Dow fell on oil-driven macro pressure, all four indices moved higher on the same oil-driven macro relief — the difference being the direction of crude. Brent's $2.30 decline is the market pricing a reduced probability of sustained Gulf conflict that would keep oil above $79 and push the 10-year Treasury yield toward 5%, the threshold at which discounted cash flow models for high-growth tech companies require multiple compression. At Brent $76.80, the oil-inflation channel is still present — $76.80 is elevated above the pre-exchange levels of $68–69 from before Tuesday's tanker attacks — but it is below the $79–80 level where sustained crude materially affects CPI expectations on the 6-month lag that the Fed uses to evaluate inflation persistence. Gold falling $52 to $4,111 is the safe-haven-demand compression that confirms the market is pricing a shorter conflict duration, consistent with Trump's "very good meetings" characterization. The 10-year yield's 2-basis-point rise to 4.54% — rising on a risk-on day — reflects the rate dynamics of a broad equity rally rather than the oil-inflation concern that drove Wednesday's 7-basis-point yield rise. The PHLX Semiconductor Index's 3.06% gain is the week's final verdict on the AI hardware trade: SK Hynix held its IPO gains, the Samsung paradox was company-specific, and chip stocks are the leading sector in a broadly rising market.

The war premium in oil is coming out — and the same disinflation that eased the Dow's headwind is allowing the chip-stock rally to run without the inflation-channel ceiling it faced this week.

Friday's session produced the broadest equity gains of the week across all four major indices, driven by a single macro variable: Iran's diplomatic de-escalation removing the oil war premium that had compressed the Dow by 1.1% and complicated the rate-cut path on Wednesday. Qatar and Pakistan reporting "positive progress" in Doha talks and Trump hailing "very good meetings in Qatar" sent Brent crude from Thursday's $79.10 down $2.30 to $76.80 — the fastest one-day decline in the oil war premium since the tanker attacks began Tuesday — while WTI fell to $72.20 from $74.49. That oil move reduced the inflation-channel pressure on the 10-year Treasury yield, which ticked up only 2 basis points to 4.54% on a broadly risk-on day rather than the 7-basis-point inflation-concern rise that Thursday's Iran escalation had produced. The S&P 500 gained 0.8% to 7,543.64, the Nasdaq climbed 1.3% to 26,206.89, the Dow added 0.3% to 52,487.41, and the Russell 2000 rose 1.0% to 2,999.

The PHLX Semiconductor Index's 3.06% gain is the session's most strategically significant data point. SK Hynix's Nasdaq debut on Thursday opened 7-fold oversubscribed with shares surging more than 8%, and the question entering Friday was whether those gains would hold through the first full trading session or give back the IPO pop in the pattern of Samsung's sell-on-news dynamic last week. The gains held — and chip stocks broadly rose 3.06% on Friday, meaning the AI hardware trade not only survived Samsung's paradox but accelerated with SK Hynix's confirmation. The PHLX's gain also confirms the thesis that Wednesday's Nasdaq decoupling from the Dow was correctly pricing an AI cycle that is independent of the oil-inflation channel: when oil falls and the macro headwind eases, the AI trade doesn't just hold — it accelerates, because now it has both its own demand cycle and improving macro conditions simultaneously.

Brent crude fell $2.30 to $76.80 on Friday — a 2.91% single-day decline — as Iran's Qatar- and Pakistan-mediated diplomacy reportedly made "positive progress" and military strikes appeared paused, allowing markets to price out a portion of the war premium that had accumulated from pre-exchange levels near $69 through Thursday's peak at $79.10; WTI fell similarly to $72.20 (▼3.08% from Thursday's $74.49). The $2.30 Brent decline is the fastest single-day unwind of the Iran oil premium since the conflict's escalation began Tuesday, and it reduces — without eliminating — the oil-inflation channel that had been complicating the Federal Reserve's September rate-cut path by pushing the 10-year Treasury yield from pre-exchange levels through its current 4.54%. Brent at $76.80 is still elevated above the pre-exchange baseline by approximately $7.80 per barrel, meaning the full war premium requires a formal ceasefire restoration and Strait confirmation to clear.

The partial oil decline's market significance is proportionate to how much of the Thursday-Friday shift is priced as "genuine de-escalation" versus "diplomatic progress that may not hold." If the Doha talks produce a genuine ceasefire restoration that includes Strait of Hormuz reopening and Gulf-state strike cessation, Brent has room to fall toward $70–72 from current $76.80 — which would clear the oil-inflation complication for the Fed almost entirely and restore the September rate-cut probability to its pre-exchange level. If the "positive progress" characterization proves optimistic and strikes resume, Brent's $2.30 Friday decline reverses rapidly and the oil-inflation channel reasserts itself against the Fed's rate path. The market on Friday is pricing the optimistic scenario at approximately 60–65% probability, as implied by the degree of oil decline and gold safe-haven compression.

Gold's $52 decline in a session where Iran is still conducting indirect diplomacy (not a formal ceasefire restoration) reflects the market's calibration of the conflict's expected duration. A $52 decline from $4,163 is a 1.25% safe-haven release — meaningful but not a full reversion to pre-exchange levels. The market is pricing "de-escalation underway, not confirmed" — which is also what the oil market is pricing with Brent at $76.80 rather than $69. Both gold and oil are in partial-de-escalation territory: enough of the war premium has come out to allow equities to rally broadly, but not enough to price a permanent conflict resolution that would require Strait confirmation and nuclear-deal progress. The July 13 CLARITY Act Senate floor consideration and the July 31 FOMC meeting are now the two scheduled catalysts that will determine whether Friday's risk-on move has legs or is a relief rally ahead of renewed uncertainty.

Quick hits

  • The week's equity market sequence — Wednesday AI split (Nasdaq ▲0.2%, Dow ▼1.1%), Thursday futures rise on deal signal, Friday broad rally (all four indices up) — is actually the normal pattern for geopolitical-shock recovery in US equities: initial split as different sectors price the shock differently, then convergence as the diplomatic resolution becomes clearer. The AI sector's specific outperformance during the split phase was unusual, but the convergence toward a broad rally once Iran de-escalation became the dominant narrative was the normal recovery mechanism operating. The question entering next week is whether the AI sector accelerates away from the Dow again if the diplomacy stalls, or whether the next catalyst (CLARITY Act July 13, earnings season) is broad enough to keep all four indices moving together.
  • Gold's behavior across this week's Iran exchange — modest $52 decline on de-escalation — is consistent with the reading that the market held through the entire conflict: this is a serious but containable military exchange, not an existential regional war. Gold rising $18 on escalation and falling $52 on de-escalation in the same week says the conflict premium was never large to begin with, and the de-escalation is priced more confidently than the escalation was. That asymmetry — markets price peace faster than they priced war — is the pattern that suggests the Iran de-escalation trade has more room to run if Doha round 2 makes nuclear progress.
