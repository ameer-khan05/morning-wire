Markets · Deep Dive

Nasdaq fell 0.70% Friday for a fifth consecutive losing session — the longest streak since February — as Apple's MacBook and iPad price hikes triggered a global tech selloff, SoftBank plunged more than 12%, and South Korea's KOSPI triggered a circuit breaker after an 8% decline in Samsung and SK Hynix led chipmakers lower.

The Russell 2000 added 0.10%, supported by the annual Russell Index reconstitution that takes effect after Friday's close, driving significant end-of-week rebalancing. Treasury yields fell 12 basis points to 4.45% as investors rotated into bonds on the risk-off session, reversing some of the yield increase built on Micron's blowout growth signal. WTI crude settled at $70.34 and Brent at $73.74, continuing the oil decline driven by increasing Iranian supply through the Strait of Hormuz. Gold eased to $3,962, remaining below $4,000 as safe-haven demand stayed subdued despite the equity selloff.

The Anchor

Nasdaq ▼0.70%. Apple ▼6%. SoftBank ▼12%. Korean circuit breaker. Five straight losses. Friday's markets are the first session where the AI memory supercycle's inflationary cost — not just its growth benefit — has become the defining market story.

The sequence from Wednesday through Friday encapsulates a complete market narrative arc. Wednesday: Micron reported $41.46 billion in Q3 revenue and guided $50 billion for Q4, validating the AI memory supercycle with numbers beyond any analyst's model. Thursday: the market absorbed those numbers — Nasdaq up 2.1% premarket, then giving back some on Apple's decline and elevated DRAM cost data. Friday: Apple and Microsoft announced consumer electronics price hikes driven by DRAM costs up 98% Q1, triggering a question investors had not previously priced: if AI infrastructure demand is inflating memory costs 98% in a single quarter and those costs are now reaching consumer device prices, what is the ceiling of the supercycle — and what happens to valuations when consumers resist the inflation it generates?

SoftBank's 12%+ plunge is the specific valuation test that Friday's session put to AI infrastructure investors. SoftBank's portfolio is built on the premise that AI infrastructure investment — in Arm Holdings, in data center buildout, in Vision Fund AI companies — will generate returns proportional to the scale of the AI buildout. Apple's price hikes are simultaneously evidence that the buildout is real and large (DRAM prices only rise 98% in a quarter if demand is extraordinary) and evidence that the buildout has a cost that markets may resist (consumer device inflation leading to demand destruction leading to lower AI chip orders). The question SoftBank's 12% decline is asking: at what price level does consumer AI inflation create a demand signal that interrupts the hyperscaler capex cycle?

Nasdaq fell 0.70% Friday to 25,181, marking its fifth consecutive losing session and the longest losing streak since February — a five-day run that began after the AI-driven 2.10% surge on Micron's earnings Wednesday and has progressively unwound the tech premium as rising DRAM costs made the AI infrastructure buildout's consumer cost explicit. The S&P 500 declined 0.44% to 7,325. Apple led the Nasdaq lower with a 6% decline after announcing MacBook and iPad price increases of up to $300 driven by AI data center DRAM demand. SoftBank plunged more than 12% as AI infrastructure valuations faced their broadest reassessment in months. South Korean chipmakers Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix fell sharply, triggering an 8% KOSPI circuit breaker.

The character of the five-day losing streak reveals a market that is not uniformly negative on AI but is specifically repricing the cost side of the AI narrative. Wednesday's 2.1% Nasdaq surge was a response to Micron's demand-side validation: AI infrastructure spending is real and accelerating. Friday's 0.70% decline is a response to the supply-side consequence: that same spending has inflated DRAM costs 98%, forcing Apple and Microsoft to raise consumer device prices and raising the question of whether consumer resistance to AI-driven inflation can interrupt the hyperscaler capex cycle that has driven the entire AI infrastructure investment thesis. The Dow's 0.10% gain — the one positive major index Friday — reflects rotation into industries less directly exposed to the AI memory cost spiral: energy, utilities, financials, and industrials that benefit from a growing economy without paying premium memory prices.

WTI crude held at $70.34 Friday after Thursday's 3.92% settle — keeping the oil market near its lowest level since February as Iranian tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz continued building supply under the 60-day sanctions waiver, even as Iran maintained its diplomatic protest about the Hormuz routing protocol. Brent settled at $73.74. The 60-day waiver, which covers not just Iranian oil production but the full trade support chain including banking, shipping, and maritime insurance, provides supply certainty through August 21 — meaning both the June CPI print (published mid-July) and the July CPI print (published mid-August) will be calculated with Iranian supply flowing at current volumes, at current prices. Both prints arrive before the FOMC's September meeting.

WTI at $70.34 versus the pre-Iran-deal baseline of approximately $84 represents a 16.2% oil price decline that will progressively register in the energy components of June and July CPI. The September FOMC meeting — where nine members had projected a rate hike in the June dot plot — will be assembled with both those CPI readings in hand. The rate hike case those members built was constructed with oil near $84; at $70.34, with the waiver guaranteeing the low-price environment through the June and July reporting periods, the energy-driven component of their inflation case is materially weakened. Gold at $3,962 — below $4,000 and continuing its decline from the Iran-deal-era highs above $4,400 — reflects that the geopolitical risk premium is still deflating, even as Day 9 Hormuz friction introduces marginal uncertainty.

The 10-year Treasury yield fell 12 basis points to 4.45% Friday as investors rotated into bonds during the tech-led equity selloff — a risk-off move that marks the first significant yield decline since the Islamabad MOU ceasefire sent investors into risk assets and drove yields higher. Thursday's yield rise to 4.57% was a growth-driven signal: bonds selling as investors bought Micron-story equities and growth expectations improved. Friday's 12-basis-point reversal is the mirror image: an equity selloff driving bond buying that compresses yields. The directional distinction matters for reading the rate picture. Two different market conditions produce two opposite bond moves; which one defines the medium-term rate trend is determined by whether the AI infrastructure selloff continues or reverses.

The Russell 2000's 0.10% gain — the only major index outside the Dow in positive territory Friday — reflects the mechanics of the annual Russell Index reconstitution rather than fundamental small-cap strength. The reconstitution, which takes effect after Friday's close, involves significant end-of-week capital rebalancing as funds that track Russell indexes must adjust their holdings to match the newly published index composition. Russell's 2026 reconstitution data shows larger leaders and stronger small caps relative to prior years, meaning the flows favoring small-cap additions are outweighing the exits in the reconstituted index. The Dow's 0.10% gain reflects capital rotating from high-multiple tech to lower-multiple cyclical and value names — the Dow's industrial and financial composition makes it the natural destination for growth-to-value rotation in tech-specific selloffs.

Quick hits

  • The five-day Nasdaq losing streak from Wednesday's peak deserves a precise reading: it is not a reversal of the AI infrastructure investment thesis — it is a repricing of the thesis's cost side. Wednesday's 2.1% surge was entirely demand-side: Micron's Q3 numbers confirmed that AI memory demand is real, large, and durable. The subsequent four days of declines are cost-side: if AI memory demand is real, large, and durable enough to push DRAM prices up 98% in a quarter, then the same demand is also generating consumer-facing inflation (Apple/Microsoft price hikes), potential regulatory scrutiny (EU antitrust of AI market concentration), and valuation concerns for AI infrastructure holders (SoftBank). The thesis is not broken; its consequences are being priced. The question the next several weeks will answer is whether the market decides the cost side is a friction that the AI buildout can sustain or a ceiling it cannot clear.
  • The bond rally that drove the UST 10Y from 4.57% to 4.45% in a single session illustrates the dual-market function Treasury bonds serve in the current environment: both safe-haven (buy when equities sell off) and rate-expectation instrument (buy when growth fears increase). Friday's 12-basis-point yield decline reflects both simultaneously — investors are moving to safety from tech equities, and the tech selloff raises questions about whether AI-driven growth is as clean as Micron's numbers suggested, which incrementally reduces the rate hike case that nine FOMC members had built. WTI at $70.34 was already building the disinflationary case; the tech selloff adds a growth uncertainty layer that compounds the bond bull argument. At 4.45%, the 10-year is now pricing less FOMC rate action than it was 24 hours ago — a move that helps the equity market's valuation case for non-tech names even as tech itself is being repriced lower.
