Markets · Deep Dive · Q3 Day 1 · Best Quarter Since 2020 Now Behind Us

The S&P 500 slipped 0.38% to 7,471 on the first day of Q3, giving back a fraction of a quarter in which it gained 14% — its best performance since 2020 — as oil fell 1.1% to $68.77 after Iran refused formal high-level US talks in Qatar, and Fed Chair Warsh's appearance at the ECB Forum in Sintra kept the September rate decision front of mind.

Q3 opened without the excitement that closed Q2: no relief rally to amplify the gains, no dramatic catalyst to spark a new direction. Instead, the opening session of the new quarter reflects what markets are working with — a strong Q2 behind them, a hawkish Fed in front of them, oil back under $70 as Iran manages its diplomatic exposure in Qatar, and a data-heavy day that includes ADP employment, ISM Manufacturing, and a closely-watched Warsh speech. Gold eased 0.21% to $4,030, and the 10-year Treasury yield slipped 3 basis points to 4.38% as early Q3 flows push modestly into fixed income.

Iran's refusal to meet US envoys Witkoff and Kushner at the senior level is a diplomatic signal, not a kinetic escalation — but it is enough to pull oil back as the market reassesses the pace of Hormuz administration progress. The S&P 500 opened Q3 2026 with a 0.38% decline to 7,471 — a modest pullback from a quarter that produced the index's best performance since 2020. Q2 2026 saw the S&P gain 14%, the Nasdaq soar 20%, and the Dow add 12%, driven by the Iran stand-down rally in the final week of June, chipmaker outperformance through the quarter, and the risk-on environment that followed the Islamabad Memorandum. TheStreet reported the session opened flat to slightly lower as investors weighed the record-setting quarter against a busy day of economic data and Fed commentary. The Magnificent Seven shed approximately $2.3 trillion in market value during June on AI capital expenditure return-on-investment concerns, but that June pullback was absorbed by the broader quarter's gains across the tech and AI sector.

Q3's opening session lacks the catalyst clarity that defined the end of Q2. There's no stand-down announcement, no landmark signing, no single external event reshaping the risk picture. What Q3 opens with is a data calendar: ADP employment, ISM Manufacturing, and a Fed chair speech today, followed by non-farm payrolls in a holiday-shortened Friday session ahead of the July 4 weekend. The sectors that led Q2's gains — semiconductors, AI infrastructure, energy names that benefited from Hormuz-driven oil pricing — are pausing. Oil's 1.1% decline on Iran's Qatar diplomacy snub is Q3 Day 1's most significant specific driver, reducing the energy sector's Q2 tailwind and pulling oil back below $70 for the first time since the stand-down. The S&P 500's Q3 trajectory will be shaped by whether the data over the next two weeks shows the labor market softening enough to credibly shift Fed rate expectations — or whether Q2's hot equity tape becomes a reason for the Fed to stay hawkish longer.

West Texas Intermediate crude fell 1.1% to $68.77 per barrel and Brent slipped 1% to $72.20 Wednesday after Iran said it would not meet with US delegates for formal talks in Qatar, deflating part of the stand-down optimism premium that had helped oil stabilize after the Hormuz crisis in late June. The signal is diplomatic rather than kinetic: Iran's technical negotiators are continuing their discussions in Qatar implementing the Islamabad Memorandum's framework, but Tehran is explicitly refusing senior-level engagement with US envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner. The refusal does not break the stand-down — ships continue moving freely through the Strait of Hormuz — but it signals that the pace of formal diplomatic progress is slower than the stand-down rally had implied, and that the Hormuz administration question Oman's proposal is designed to address is not being resolved quickly.

Oil's price sensitivity to Iran-Qatar diplomacy news illustrates a structural dynamic that has emerged since the Islamabad Memorandum: oil is now partly an Iran-diplomacy tracking instrument, in addition to its traditional roles as an energy supply indicator and a global growth proxy. The stand-down rally in late June pushed WTI from an overnight spike level back to the $70 range; today's Iran diplomatic snub pulls it back below $70. The $68–72 range is establishing itself as the Hormuz-managed equilibrium: high enough to reflect residual geopolitical risk, low enough to reflect the Hormuz stand-down's supply assurance, and sensitive to every diplomatic signal in either direction. The energy inflation channel for the Fed — which matters for the September rate decision — is correspondingly quieter when oil is below $70, which is the current picture.

Federal Reserve Chair Kevin Warsh is speaking Wednesday at the European Central Bank Forum on Central Banking in Sintra, Portugal — the same venue where Ben Bernanke and Janet Yellen delivered market-moving statements on future rate trajectories, making the annual forum a watched signal point regardless of official framing. Warsh, who took the chair position in 2026, has maintained a hawkish posture through the June dot plot in which nine FOMC members projected a rate hike for later this year. Investors will be parsing his Sintra remarks for any acknowledgment that the Iran stand-down's disinflationary oil effect, combined with any signs of labor market softening, could shift the September calculus. A Warsh speech that is unambiguously hawkish, combined with a strong ADP print, would be the scenario most likely to push the 10-year yield back above 4.40% and extend Wednesday's equity weakness into the session.

June ADP employment data and the ISM Manufacturing index are the two other scheduled data points Wednesday, providing the first major Q3 economic prints and the first labor market signal since the June NFP. ADP's methodology tracks private sector payrolls and tends to correlate with — though not perfectly predict — Friday's official Bureau of Labor Statistics non-farm payrolls release. An ADP figure that shows private sector hiring below trend would be a soft signal for Friday's NFP and would strengthen the rate-hold case for September. The ISM Manufacturing index's June reading provides the manufacturing sector's first self-reported view of Q3 business conditions. Gold at $4,030 (▼0.21%) is giving back a minimal portion of the Iran safety premium, while the 10-year Treasury at 4.38% (▼3bp) reflects modest fixed-income buying as Q3 begins with a cautious rather than risk-on tone.

Quick hits

  • The Warsh-Sintra appearance is the most watched Fed event of the week for one specific reason: Sintra is where Powell, in 2021, first publicly acknowledged that inflation was "transitory" — and where, a year later, the ECB signaled the end of its negative rate era. The venue carries signal weight beyond the content, and Warsh's remarks will be parsed not just for what he says about September but for what the chosen venue and tone suggest about his approach to forward guidance under his chairmanship.
  • Friday's non-farm payrolls arrive in a holiday-shortened session ahead of July 4. The combination of high-impact data and low liquidity means Friday's initial price reaction will likely be exaggerated in both directions — the market moves faster with fewer participants, then partially corrects when full liquidity returns on July 7. Investors managing risk around NFP should factor in the four-day weekend that follows: you cannot reposition through the weekend if the data surprise is larger than expected.
