US equity markets are closed for the weekend. Thursday's close — Nasdaq +1.91% to 26,517, S&P +1.08% to 7,500, Russell 2000 +2.10% to 2,979 — heads into Monday with a materially improved geopolitical picture: Vance arrived in Switzerland on Sunday for nuclear talks on Day 4, and Iran's Hormuz re-closure claim was denied by US transit data.

Thursday's equity recovery made two bets: that Wednesday's FOMC selloff overestimated the hawkish commitment in Warsh's dot plot, and that the nuclear talks postponement was temporary. Sunday morning validates both. Nine of eighteen FOMC members project a hike — a distribution of opinion, not a committed vote — and no FOMC speaker since Wednesday's press conference has moved toward committed hike language. The nuclear talks delay lasted four days and ended with Vance in Switzerland on Sunday, exactly as Thursday's buyers bet it would. Monday's open is the first equity session that can price both validations, plus the additional input of Iran's Hormuz closure claim being denied with 16 million barrels of daily transit data and Vance's own departure as the rebuttal. The geopolitical backdrop on Monday morning is cleaner than it was at Thursday's close. What remains unchanged is the macro backdrop: Warsh's hawkish dot plot with PCE projected at 3.6%, the 10-year Treasury at 4.53%, dollar at yearly highs, Gold under pressure at $4,228. The July CPI print — mid-July — remains the single data event capable of changing that picture. Between now and mid-July, equity markets are trading off geopolitical signals against the rate-hike backdrop that Warsh established on June 18.

The Anchor

Thursday's recovery was right on both counts. Nuclear talks are underway. The Hormuz scare is resolved. Monday opens with the geopolitical picture Thursday's buyers priced — and then some.

Thursday's equity recovery was the market correcting Wednesday's overreaction to the FOMC signal. The Nasdaq's 1.91% and Russell's 2.10% gains said: Warsh's dot plot is a view, not a commitment, and the nuclear talks delay is temporary. Both readings were correct. The weekend's diplomatic drama — Iran declaring Hormuz closed Saturday, the US denying it with transit data, Vance getting on a plane Saturday and arriving in Switzerland Sunday — unfolded at a pace that confirmed Thursday's thesis faster than the market had reason to expect. Monday's buyers inherit a more complete picture: not only did Thursday's bets prove right, but they proved right quickly enough that there is limited incremental new uncertainty to price on Monday's open.

The remaining pressure variable is the FOMC. Warsh's hawkish dot plot — nine of eighteen members projecting a hike, with PCE forecast at 3.6% — is not going to be revised before the July CPI print. FOMC speakers scheduled for next week will reinforce or modestly soften that signal with specific language choices (watch for "committed to hiking if data warrants" versus "inclined to hike" versus "ready to hike" — each is a different implied probability). Until the July CPI print, equity markets are in a tug-of-war between geopolitical positive (Swiss nuclear talks underway) and macro headwind (4.53% 10-year, hawkish dot plot). The Colombia runoff result — arriving Sunday night or Monday morning — is a secondary variable that affects specific sector exposures but is unlikely to move the headline indices significantly.

US equity markets closed Sunday for the regular weekend, with Thursday's recovery closing prices — Nasdaq 26,517 (+1.91%), S&P 500 7,500 (+1.08%), Dow 51,650 (+0.31%), Russell 2000 2,979 (+2.10%) — heading into Monday with two of the weekend's key uncertainties resolved favorably. VP Vance arrived in Switzerland on Sunday for nuclear talks, validating the thesis that Thursday's postponement was temporary. Iran's Saturday Hormuz closure declaration was denied by US transit data — Vance cited 16 million barrels of oil transited Friday, a record — and Vance departed for Switzerland anyway, operationally dismissing the Iranian pressure tactic. Monday's open is the first session to price both resolutions.

The Russell 2000's Thursday leadership — 2.10% versus Nasdaq's 1.91% — was the most precise read of market psychology available. Small caps lead recoveries when markets don't believe in rate hikes: floating-rate debt costs rise disproportionately for small borrowers, so Russell 2000 outperformance is a direct vote of "the FOMC isn't committed to hiking." If FOMC speakers this week use language that escalates toward commitment — "we expect to raise rates" rather than "nine members project a rate increase" — the Russell 2000 will underperform as the first and most sensitive indicator of a re-pricing. That language distinction, across perhaps five to eight speaker appearances next week, is the single most important equity market input between now and the July CPI print.

The Federal Reserve's June decision — unanimous hold at 3.5%–3.75% under Warsh's first press conference — left the rate path dependent on data and the dot plot carrying a hawkish implication: nine of 18 FOMC members project at least one rate hike by year-end, with headline PCE inflation forecast at 3.6% and core at 3.3% for 2026. The 10-year Treasury responded by rising 7 basis points to 4.53%, where it holds heading into Monday's open. Warsh's language at the press conference was interpreted as leaning hawkish — "inflation continues to hover above the Fed's 2% target" — without committing to a specific hike timeline, which is why Thursday's market correctly read the selloff as excessive.

The July CPI print is the single data event capable of modifying the FOMC's hawkish posture before September's meeting. The print reports June's inflation data — the first month to capture any contribution from the post-deal oil price move, which brought WTI from approximately $84 pre-deal to the current $76.60. If June headline CPI shows even 0.2–0.3 percentage point relief attributable to energy prices, markets will begin pricing a higher probability of Warsh revising the September dot plot toward a hold. WTI at $76.60 rather than the $74–75 trajectory that immediate full Hormuz reopening would have supported means the June oil contribution is real but modest — enough to show in CPI but not enough to dominate it. The Hormuz main-channel clearance timeline, expected late June to early July, determines how much July CPI captures versus the August CPI that reports July data. Both prints matter; the July print is first.

WTI crude held at $76.60 through Thursday's close and into the weekend as Hormuz main-channel mine clearance entered its fourth day. The US-European mine-clearance mission is proceeding on schedule toward the estimated two-to-three-week timeline, with full main-channel reopening expected late June to early July. Iran's Saturday claim that the strait was closed was rebutted by Vance's specific transit data — 16 million barrels on Friday alone, "a record going back to even before the conflict started" — and oil markets did not spike on the Iranian declaration, reflecting markets' own read that physical transit was uninterrupted. Colombia votes today in a presidential runoff that affects Bogotá's crude oil and coal export policies.

The oil price's position at $76.60 — above the $75 trajectory that full immediate Hormuz reopening would have supported — is the most important input to the July CPI scenario and therefore to the September FOMC path. The Hormuz alternative routes are handling current commercial traffic, but the main channel handles very large crude carriers that cannot transit the alternatives due to depth constraints. Full crude supply ramp-up from Iranian oil exports requires the main channel. The current constrained throughput explains why WTI has not declined further toward $74–75: the oil supply increase the deal promised is real but arriving more slowly than deal-day pricing implied. Each day the main channel clearance progresses is a day closer to the full throughput that will press WTI lower and deliver the energy disinflation that the FOMC's September calculus needs to see.

Gold held at approximately $4,228 through Thursday's close, remaining under pressure from the dollar testing yearly highs and the 10-year Treasury at 4.53% — 7 basis points above its pre-FOMC level. Both headwinds are mechanically straightforward: dollar strength makes gold more expensive in non-US currencies (reducing global demand), while a 4.53% risk-free Treasury yield creates a real opportunity cost for a non-yielding asset. Gold at $4,228 needs the data sequence — WTI declining, June CPI showing energy relief, July CPI print confirming disinflation — before the FOMC revision probability rises enough to soften the dollar and reduce real yields sufficiently to unlock a gold recovery.

The geopolitical tailwind from Vance's Swiss arrival is insufficient on its own to move gold significantly. Gold responds to rate expectations and dollar direction more reliably than to geopolitical diplomatic signals — Vance in Switzerland does not change Warsh's dot plot or the 10-year yield, which are the variables that mechanically determine gold's near-term direction. What Vance's Switzerland trip does for gold is indirect: it reduces the probability of a Hormuz crisis escalation that would have pushed oil prices higher and extended the rate-hike timeline by keeping CPI elevated. A stable Hormuz — physical transit at record levels — is the precondition for the oil price decline that eventually gets into June and July CPI and creates the data basis for FOMC revision. Gold needs that whole sequence to play out. The Vance trip keeps the sequence alive; it does not accelerate it.

Quick hits

  • The Russell 2000's outperformance on Thursday's recovery carries a specific implication: small caps leading means markets read the FOMC signal as opinion, not commitment. The test for this week is FOMC speaker language. The Federal Reserve has a packed speaking schedule most weeks — three to five board governors and bank presidents typically appear in public forums in the week after a meeting. Watch the verb tense and modal language each speaker uses when discussing the rate outlook: "we are prepared to raise rates" (committed) versus "nine members project a rate increase" (describing the dot plot) versus "I would consider a rate increase if inflation data warrants" (conditional). Each is a different probability signal. The Russell 2000 will react first and most acutely to escalation from the third form toward the first — that's the real-time rate-expectations barometer to watch alongside the 10-year yield spread during next week's equity sessions.
  • The Colombia runoff's impact on commodity markets is worth flagging for energy sector investors. Colombia produces approximately 800,000 barrels of oil per day and is the world's fourth-largest coal exporter — levels that make its energy policy direction significant for global supply. The two candidates in the runoff hold different positions on oil exploration permitting, coal export expansion, and foreign investment in the energy sector. The winning candidate's posture on hydrocarbon development will affect the US-Colombia energy relationship, with implications for LNG, coal, and crude oil flows that are relevant to commodity-exposed equities in those sectors. The direct equity market impact is limited to specific name exposures, but sector ETFs covering Latin American energy, coal producers with Colombian assets, and US LNG exporters with Colombian commercial relationships will track the result closely Monday morning.
