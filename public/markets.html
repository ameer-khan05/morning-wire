When both beat on the same month's data, it establishes that the disinflation was present at every link in the chain — not a retail discounting anomaly or a producer-cost spike hidden below the consumer surface. June was that month. The problem is that June is also specifically the month when the Hormuz blockade had not yet begun, oil averaged well below $80, and the conflict environment that will dominate July data did not exist. Annual PPI at 5.5% captures the cumulative effect of conflict-period oil on upstream costs; the June monthly beat reflects the pre-conflict world. Warsh's "not mission accomplished" continues to apply: two backward-looking beats do not change the forward oil arithmetic.

Quick hits

  • The back-to-back CPI and PPI beats deserve to be read against the Federal Reserve's analytical framework for what "two-measure disinflation" means for policy. The Fed targets PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures), not CPI or PPI directly, but CPI and PPI are both inputs to the PCE deflator calculation that the BEA publishes monthly. When both CPI and PPI beat expectations in the same month, the PCE read is directionally strong — not arithmetically certain, because PCE weights differ from CPI/PPI, but the combined signal is the best available leading indicator of the PCE direction. If June PCE (released later this month) also confirms disinflation, the Fed has a three-measure confirmation of June's benign inflation environment. Warsh's "not mission accomplished" framing is still analytically correct for the forward path, but a three-measure June confirmation would establish the cleanest pre-conflict disinflation baseline since 2021. The July 29 FOMC decision will be made against that backdrop — good backward-looking June data but forward oil-conflict risk fully visible in commodity markets.
  • Brent at $84.90 declining 0.48% on the same day WTI rose 0.51% is an unusual divergence between the two benchmark crude prices. Brent and WTI typically move in the same direction, with a spread that reflects transportation costs and regional supply differentials. A day where Brent declines and WTI rises simultaneously can reflect specific factors: Brent is more directly exposed to Middle East conflict premium (it is priced on North Sea crude but used as the global reference for conflict-risk oil pricing); WTI is more exposed to US domestic supply dynamics. A fifth-day Iran strike wave that is 90 minutes rather than 7 hours may be reducing the daily Hormuz disruption premium in Brent pricing, while US domestic crude dynamics — including pipeline flows and refinery utilization — push WTI slightly higher. The net effect is that the Brent-WTI spread is narrowing on this session, which is consistent with a market assessment that the immediate daily strike intensity is declining even as the underlying conflict continues. Whether the spread continues to narrow depends on whether Thursday's sixth strike day (if it occurs) is similarly shorter-duration than the early-week campaigns.
