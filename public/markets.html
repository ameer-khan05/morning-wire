The Anchor

Wall Street says the S&P 500 will hit 7,650 by year-end — and Big Tech earnings this week will determine whether that call holds.

Sunday offers markets no new data — US equities are closed, and the Nasdaq (24,836.60) and S&P 500 (7,165.08) both sit at record closes from Friday. A survey of 21 investment banks and research firms puts the median 2026 year-end S&P 500 target at 7,650, implying 6.8% further upside from Friday's record — above the 30-year annual average. The bull case rests on two pillars: continued AI capex translating into enterprise software revenue growth, and a Fed that stays on hold without tightening into a strong economy. The week ahead begins testing pillar one: Bridgewater estimates $650 billion in combined Big Tech AI capex for 2026, up from $410 billion in 2025. Every earnings transcript Wednesday and Thursday will be searched for proof that the spending is producing proportional revenue, stronger margins, and durable monetization. The Motley Fool calls Microsoft's April 29 print "the most important tech event of 2026."

The macro backdrop is complicated. Trump's cancellation of the Pakistan Iran peace talks Saturday removes the most plausible near-term route to Hormuz blockade removal, keeping Brent crude above $104 and the 8–12 per barrel oil risk premium entrenched. The 10-year Treasury yield holds at 4.31%; gold is steady at $4,725.40; the dollar index is 99.45. For each central bank decision in the week ahead: the ECB is expected to cut 25 basis points on April 30 despite Brent above $100, with ECB President Lagarde facing the challenge of framing an oil-driven headline inflation reading as transitory supply shock rather than demand-driven persistence. The FOMC meets May 7 — one week away; the April PCE inflation print publishes May 1, giving the Fed and markets one last data point before the decision. Futures are pricing the Fed on hold at May 7.

Wall Street's median S&P 500 2026 target is 7,650 — implying 6.8% more upside and a year that beats the 30-year average.

A survey of 21 investment banks and research institutions puts the median 2026 S&P 500 year-end target at 7,650, implying 6.8% further upside from Friday's record close of 7,165.08. A full-year gain of that magnitude would exceed the 30-year average annual return of the index — a bold call at a time of active geopolitical risk, elevated oil, and a record-high starting point. The bull case rests on AI capex producing enterprise software revenue and the Fed staying on hold without tightening. The bear case: elevated oil keeps PCE inflation stickier than expected, forcing the Fed to hold longer; or Big Tech earnings this week disappoint on capex-to-revenue conversion, triggering a valuation reset. Both risk scenarios are live entering earnings week.

Alphabet Q1 preview: $92.2B revenue expected (+20.6%) — Google Cloud growth above 50% is the critical metric.

Alphabet reports Q1 2026 results Wednesday after the close. Analyst consensus is $92.2 billion in revenue, a 20.6% year-on-year increase, with EPS expected at $2.64. Google Cloud is expected to grow above 50% year-on-year — the central metric that will determine whether Alphabet's $175–$185 billion 2026 capex guidance is viewed as a value-creating bet or a cash-burning exercise. The Google–Anthropic $40 billion deal closed Friday makes the Cloud segment's AI monetization story more urgent: investors will want evidence that the infrastructure investment is attracting enterprise customers at scale, not just winning model benchmarks. Search advertising revenue is also in focus as AI-generated search summaries begin cannibalizing traditional click-through rates.

Meta · Q1 Preview $55.5B +31% YoY · post-layoffs · $115–135B capex

Meta Q1 preview: $55.5B revenue expected (+31%) — 10% workforce cut frames the print as margin-positive AI pivot.

Meta reports Q1 2026 results Wednesday after the close. Revenue consensus is $55.5 billion (+31% year-on-year) with adjusted EPS expected at $7.51. The print comes two trading days after Meta announced a 10% global workforce reduction — roughly 7,000 employees — framed by management as redirecting headcount investment into AI infrastructure and agents rather than operations or sales. Investors will scrutinize whether the cuts flow through to Q2 operating margin guidance or are offset by the $115–$135 billion capex plan for 2026. Meta shares closed up 2.48% Friday despite the layoff announcement, signaling the market's initial read was margin-positive. Zuckerberg has staked Meta's multi-year strategy on AI agents replacing software engineers — this is the quarter where that strategy begins producing visible numbers.

ECB · April 30 −25 bps? Rate decision · Brent complicates · Lagarde signal

ECB meets April 30 — 25 bps cut expected, but Brent above $104 and collapsed Iran talks complicate the picture.

The European Central Bank holds its April 30 rate decision on the same day as Amazon's Q1 earnings — the most data-dense single day in months. Markets are pricing a 25 basis point cut; the ECB last moved in March. The complication: Brent crude above $100 is keeping eurozone headline inflation elevated, and Trump's cancellation of Pakistan Iran talks Saturday removes the most plausible near-term route to Hormuz blockade removal and an oil price correction. ECB President Lagarde faces a communication challenge — cutting while headline inflation rises risks appearing inconsistent with the price-stability mandate. Analysts expect a cut accompanied by explicit guidance framing the oil component as a temporary supply shock, with a data-dependent signal for subsequent meetings.

FOMC · May 7 · Oil May 7 Fed on hold · PCE May 1 · Brent $104.63

FOMC meets May 7 — futures price the Fed on hold as PCE publishes May 1 with oil above $100 keeping inflation sticky.

The Federal Reserve's next meeting is May 7, one week away. Futures markets are pricing the Fed on hold — no rate change expected. The critical data point arrives May 1: the April PCE inflation reading, the Fed's preferred price measure, which will reflect the full first month of elevated Brent crude above $100. Energy's pass-through to core PCE is modest but real — every 10% increase in Brent above $90 adds an estimated 0.2–0.3 percentage points to US core PCE inflation — a complication for a Fed that has been trying to thread the needle between a strong labor market and falling inflation. Trump's Iran talk cancellation Saturday, keeping geopolitical risk elevated and the Hormuz blockade in place, means the oil risk premium is not dissipating before the May 7 decision.
