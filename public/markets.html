US equity markets opened Monday mixed as Swiss nuclear talks' "good foundation" readout and WTI crude easing to $75.75 lifted industrials, energy, and financials — reflected in the Dow's 0.44% gain to 51,877 — while a 42-state attorney general investigation into OpenAI created regulatory overhang for AI and tech names that pushed the Nasdaq 0.27% lower to 26,445. The S&P 500 blended both forces for a 0.12% gain to 7,509. The Russell 2000's 0.20% advance to 2,985, leading the Nasdaq for the second time in three sessions, confirms that Thursday's key market read — the FOMC is non-committed, not hawkishly locked-in — has survived into the new week.

The analytical significance of Monday's mixed open is what it reveals about prior-session pricing. Thursday's 1.91% Nasdaq recovery was a forward bet that the nuclear talks delay was temporary, and Monday's "good foundation" readout validates that bet. But validation of a correctly priced bet does not produce additional gains — the market already paid for the thesis. What Monday adds is the new negative: the 42-state AG probe into OpenAI is not priced anywhere in Thursday's or Friday's closes. For tech and AI investors, the AG probe is a legitimate new risk factor — not a company-specific one (OpenAI is still private) but a sector-level one, as the investigation signals that multiple state AGs with consumer protection authority have concluded that AI companies' advertising, data, and product design practices warrant formal scrutiny. That signal extrapolates to public AI companies and to the SaaS vendors that have deployed AI features in their products, which is a broad set of Nasdaq constituents.

The Nasdaq-100 and S&P 500 both implemented their June quarterly rebalances effective Monday's market open — the Nasdaq-100 swapping five companies in and five out, the S&P 500 adding two and removing two — as part of the scheduled quarterly index maintenance cycle tied to the third Friday of March, June, September, and December. The rebalances generate mandatory buy flows for additions and sell flows for deletions across the trillions of dollars in assets managed by index-tracking funds and ETFs that must continuously match the indices' composition.

The timing of Monday's rebalances alongside significant geopolitical and regulatory news — Swiss nuclear talks progress, the 42-state AG OpenAI probe, WTI oil easing — makes individual name interpretation particularly unreliable. A stock moving 1–2% in either direction on Monday may be reacting to fundamental news, experiencing rebalance-driven mechanical flows, or both. Analysts and investors reading Monday's tape should focus on sector-level signals (Dow, Nasdaq, Russell performance relative to each other) rather than individual name moves for Monday's fundamental conclusions. Sector signals are clean: cyclicals and value up, tech and growth down, rate-sensitive small caps modestly positive. Those signals are not distorted by the rebalance and accurately represent Monday's underlying market dynamics.

Alan Greenspan Dies at 100 · Fed Chairman 1987–2006 · Great Moderation · Central Banking Era Closes 1987–2006 Alan Greenspan: died age 100 · Fed chairman: August 1987 to January 2006 · 18.5 years — longest-serving Fed chair in history · era: "Great Moderation" · low inflation + sustained growth + two mild recessions (1990–91, 2001) · achievements: 1987 stock market crash response (coordinated liquidity) + 1990s expansion ("new economy" thesis) · criticism post-2006: housing bubble accommodation + "irrational exuberance" speech underenforced · framework legacy: Bernanke + Yellen + Powell + Warsh operate within institutional and communication frameworks Greenspan built · current relevance: Warsh's inflation-management challenge with PCE at 3.6% is exactly the scenario Greenspan's Great Moderation claimed to have permanently resolved

Alan Greenspan, chairman of the Federal Reserve from August 1987 to January 2006 and the architect of the "Great Moderation" era of low inflation and sustained economic growth, died at age 100. Greenspan's 18.5-year tenure — the longest in Fed history — spanned the 1987 stock market crash, which he managed with coordinated liquidity support, the technology boom and bust of the 1990s, the 2001 recession, and a prolonged era of declining inflation and interest rates that defined a generation of financial markets. His successors — Ben Bernanke, Janet Yellen, Jerome Powell, and Kevin Warsh — each inherited the institutional communication frameworks, dual mandate interpretation, and forward guidance practices Greenspan developed.

Greenspan's death arrives as the Fed under Warsh confronts the precise challenge — persistent above-target inflation (PCE at 3.6%) alongside a strong labor market — that Greenspan's Great Moderation claimed to have permanently tamed through superior monetary policy management and better economic policy frameworks. The question Greenspan's legacy poses for Monday's market is not nostalgic but analytical: the inflation management success of the 1990s that Greenspan received credit for was substantially a supply-side windfall from globalization, technology productivity, and demographic tailwinds that his successors do not have access to in the same form. Warsh's inflation management challenge is harder than Greenspan's was, and the 4.51% 10-year yield that markets are trading on Monday is one measure of how much harder. The Great Moderation's end was not Greenspan's failure; it was the exhaustion of its structural preconditions.
