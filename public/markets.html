S&P 500 · Nasdaq · Records Both 7,173.91 · 24,887.10 · first simultaneous records since January

S&P 500 and Nasdaq both hit all-time record closes Monday — the first simultaneous records since January.

Monday's late-session rally drove both the S&P 500 and Nasdaq Composite to all-time record closing highs for the first time in the same session since January. The S&P 500 reached 7,173.91 (+0.12%) and the Nasdaq 24,887.10 (+0.20%), with the Nasdaq's outperformance driven by Qualcomm's 11% surge on the OpenAI smartphone chip report — the single largest positive market-cap addition of the session. The Dow (49,167.79, -0.13%) diverged as oil-sensitive industrials and energy company earnings concerns weighed. The Russell 2000 (2,812, +0.47%) outperformed both large-cap indices, continuing the pattern of small-cap rotation alongside AI megacap momentum that has characterized 2026's unusually broad market structure. The VIX closed at 16.20 — up 3.85% on the day but still historically low — as options markets began pricing the concentrated earnings risk arriving tonight and tomorrow.

Brent · $111 · Iran Day 60 $111.50 ▲ 3.14% · WTI $99.77 · highest since Hormuz closure · Tue AM

Brent breaks $111 Tuesday morning on Iran Day 60 stalemate — highest since the Hormuz closure began, WTI crosses $99.

Brent crude surged 3.14% to $111.50 per barrel in early Tuesday trading — its highest level since the Hormuz closure began in late February — as Day 60 of the US-Iran conflict opened without a deal and UN Secretary-General Guterres warned of "the worst supply chain disruption since COVID-19 and the war in Ukraine." WTI crude rose 3.39% to $99.77, approaching the $100 threshold for the first time in this cycle. The price action reflects a market pricing out near-term resolution probability: Iran's Hormuz proposal makes no nuclear concessions, Trump has said there is "no time frame" on the war, and sixty-plus nations have called for reopening without producing a US response. Goldman Sachs's $90 Brent late-2026 target, predicated on a Hormuz resolution within the model window, is increasingly a longer-dated call. Every additional week of $100+ Brent is estimated to add approximately 0.05–0.1 percentage points to US core PCE inflation — a compounding effect that the April 1 PCE print (released May 1) will capture for the first time.

Kevin Warsh Senate Banking vote tomorrow — the Fed chair transition arrives as Brent breaks $111 and Big Tech earnings land on the same day.

The Senate Banking Committee votes Wednesday April 29 on Kevin Warsh's confirmation as Federal Reserve chair, following Senator Thom Tillis's Sunday announcement dropping his blockade. With Tillis, the vote advances 13-11; the full Senate confirms before Powell's May 15 term end. The simultaneous arrival of Warsh's confirmation vote and Big Tech earnings on April 29 makes it the highest-stakes single trading day of 2026: monetary policy continuity and AI infrastructure profitability both resolved in the same session. For markets, the Warsh transition is significant because the FOMC meets May 7 — just 8 days after confirmation — with Brent at $111, the April PCE printing May 1, and Big Tech guidance freshly published. Warsh vowed not to be Trump's "sock puppet" on rates; futures markets price the May 7 decision at "hold" regardless. The real question is whether Warsh's June and September dot-plot additions signal a more hawkish tightening bias than Powell's final meetings suggested — and whether Big Tech's AI capex cycle survives a rate environment that rises rather than falls through 2026.

ECB · April 30 · −25 bps? Wed Rate decision · Amazon same day · Brent $111 complicates mandate

ECB meets Wednesday with Brent at $111 — 25 bps cut expected despite oil complicating the price-stability mandate.

The European Central Bank holds its April 30 rate decision Wednesday, on the same day as Amazon's Q1 earnings. Markets price a 25 basis point cut. The complication is acute: Brent above $111 is the highest oil price since before the Hormuz closure, and eurozone headline inflation reflects the oil passthrough more directly than the US does. ECB President Lagarde faces the same communication challenge that has defined every central bank decision since February: cut to support growth while oil-driven inflation is rising, or pause and accept the recession risk of higher rates in an energy-shocked economy. Analysts expect a cut accompanied by language framing the oil component as a supply-driven, temporary shock — the same framing the ECB used for COVID-era energy spikes. The ECB's willingness to cut despite $111 Brent, if it materializes, will send an implicit message to the Federal Reserve ahead of the May 7 FOMC meeting: supply-driven energy inflation is categorically different from demand-driven inflation and should not trigger tightening — a view Warsh has not yet publicly endorsed.

April PCE prints May 1 — first full month of $100+ Brent captured; Warsh's first FOMC meeting follows six days later.

The April PCE inflation print — the Federal Reserve's preferred inflation measure — publishes May 1, six days before the FOMC meeting on May 7. April is the first full calendar month in which Brent was consistently above $100 per barrel throughout the period. Energy's direct contribution to PCE headline inflation is meaningful: every 10% increase in oil above $90 adds an estimated 0.2–0.3 percentage points to headline PCE; core PCE sees a smaller but non-trivial passthrough via transportation and manufacturing costs. If April core PCE surprises above the 2.6% consensus estimate — driven by Brent's trajectory from $97 to $111 over the month — Warsh's first FOMC meeting becomes a live rate-path question rather than the hold-and-wait outcome futures currently price. The combination of Big Tech earnings, an ECB decision, and an April PCE print all arriving before Warsh's first FOMC meeting makes the next seven days the most information-dense macro week of 2026 — and the most consequential for whether the year's record equity run survives the energy overlay.

Quick hits

  • The simultaneous record close for the S&P 500 and Nasdaq on Monday is unusual in the current environment because it occurred alongside Brent at $108 and 10Y yields at 4.27% — a combination of tight financial conditions and record equity valuations that historically resolves in one of two ways: earnings confirm AI capex returns (rally continues) or they disappoint (sharp correction). Tonight provides the answer.
  • Brent at $111.50 — up from $108.11 just two trading days ago — means the oil premium is accelerating, not stabilizing. Each $3 move higher in Brent adds approximately $15 billion per month in global energy costs. Day 60 without a deal is not just a geopolitical data point; it is a compounding economic tax on every country that imports oil, which is most of them.
  • The 10-year Treasury yield rising to 4.34% on Tuesday — up 7 basis points in a single session — reflects the oil-driven inflation repricing, not a flight from Treasuries per se. The combination of higher oil + higher yields + lower equity futures is the "stagflation signal" that markets have been most anxious to avoid; if it persists through earnings, it will test whether the AI-driven earnings growth story is strong enough to offset the macro headwind.
  • Gold fell to $4,682 (▼ 0.57%) even as Brent surged and equities fell — a divergence from the normal safe-haven pattern. The explanation: the dollar index (DXY) rose 0.48% to 99.45, and gold and the dollar have a strong inverse correlation. Dollar strength driven by risk-off is the specific condition that suppresses gold even during geopolitical risk spikes — and Tuesday's session is showing exactly that dynamic.
  • Alphabet's expected $107 billion quarterly revenue would represent a 19% year-over-year growth rate for the world's largest advertising company — in an environment where oil-driven inflation is squeezing consumer budgets and advertisers are increasingly concerned about demand softness. If Alphabet hits consensus, it means digital advertising has fully decoupled from consumer spending cycles in a way that traditional ad markets never managed — a structural rather than cyclical growth story.
  • Amazon reporting Thursday with the ECB rate decision on the same day creates a compounding event sequence: Big Tech (GOOGL/META/MSFT tonight), Fed chair transition (Warsh vote tomorrow), Amazon + ECB Thursday, PCE Friday (May 1), FOMC next Wednesday (May 7). No week in the first half of 2026 will have produced more market-moving data in seven days. Volatility compression before this week — VIX at 16.20 — will likely not survive it.
