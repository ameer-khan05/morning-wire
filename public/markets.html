Nasdaq closes at a record as Intel's quarter drives chips to 18-day winning streak.

The Anchor

Intel's 25% surge sends the Nasdaq to a record — and ends the week with the market separating fundamentals from fear.

Friday's session was a case study in markets choosing earnings over geopolitics. Iran's Hormuz escalation Thursday drove a broad selloff; Friday's Intel Q1 print reversed it entirely. Intel beat Q1 consensus estimates on both revenue and earnings per share, raised full-year guidance, and for the first time disclosed meaningful AI accelerator revenue alongside its traditional CPU business. The stock surged more than 25%, crossing $90 for the first time since the year 2000 — a psychological milestone that drew in momentum buyers and retail volume throughout the day. The US government's equity-adjacent position in Intel, taken via CHIPS Act agreements in August 2025 at approximately $9 billion, is now worth roughly $36 billion on paper. Nvidia separately retook its $5 trillion market cap, making it once again the world's most valuable company by that metric. Together they drove the Nasdaq Composite to a record close of 24,836.60 (+1.63%) and the S&P 500 to 7,165.08 (+0.80%).

The Dow was the session's only major index in the red, slipping 0.16% to 49,230.71 — a Dow composition story, not a market weakness story: the index is weighted toward older industrial and financial names that don't benefit as directly from the AI semiconductor surge. The VIX fell 9.63% to 14.82, reversing more than half of Thursday's spike. The 10-year Treasury yield dropped 11 basis points to 4.31% as strong earnings reduced stagflation anxiety and risk appetite returned. The dollar index fell 0.73% to 99.45 in a classic risk-on day. Oil retreated slightly — WTI to $95.13 (▼0.76%) and Brent to $104.63 (▼0.42%) — as the Israel-Lebanon ceasefire extension provided partial geopolitical relief, though the Hormuz premium remains entrenched until Iran releases the seized vessels or a blockade compromise is reached. Gold was essentially flat at $4,725.40.

Intel · Q1 Earnings +25% 2000 high topped · US stake $36B · chip leadership

Intel's Q1 blowout tops its year-2000 high — raised guidance and AI revenue send shares up 25%+.

Intel's Q1 2026 results beat analyst expectations on both the top and bottom lines, with the company raising full-year guidance and for the first time disclosing meaningful AI accelerator revenue alongside CPU sales. Shares surged more than 25%, crossing $90 for the first time since the year 2000. The US government's equity-adjacent CHIPS Act position — approximately $9 billion entered in August 2025 — is now worth roughly $36 billion, a roughly $27 billion paper gain and one of the largest returns on a US industrial-policy stake in modern history. The print was the single most important individual earnings beat of the week, providing the catalyst that reversed Thursday's macro-driven selloff.

Oil · Hormuz $104.63 Brent · slightly lower · premium entrenched

Brent slips to $104.63 but holds above $100 — Hormuz premium entrenched as Iran holds seized vessels.

Brent crude eased 0.42% to $104.63 on Friday and WTI declined 0.76% to $95.13, as equities' risk-on rally provided partial offset to the Hormuz tension. But both benchmarks remain well above their pre-escalation levels — the Hormuz premium, estimated at $8–12 per barrel by energy analysts, is not reversing while Iran holds the seized vessels MSC Francesca and Epaminondas and the US naval blockade remains in place. A full ceasefire with blockade removal is not on the near-term negotiating table; oil will stay elevated. For every 10% increase in Brent above $90, US core PCE inflation is estimated to rise 0.2–0.3 percentage points — a rounding error for the Fed, but a meaningful complication for the ECB.

ECB · April 30 −25 bps? Rate decision · oil complicates · Lagarde signal

ECB decision April 30 — 25 bps cut expected, but Brent above $104 complicates the communication.

The European Central Bank holds its next rate decision on April 30 — the same day Amazon reports Q1 and the same week Big Tech earnings dominate US markets. Markets are pricing a 25 basis point cut; the ECB last moved in March. The complication: Brent crude above $100 is keeping eurozone headline inflation elevated, a supply-side shock largely outside the reach of monetary policy. ECB President Lagarde faces a communication challenge — cutting while headline inflation rises risks appearing inconsistent with the price-stability mandate. Analysts expect a cut accompanied by explicit guidance framing the oil component as temporary, and a data-dependent signal for future meetings.

Bonds · Dollar · Gold 4.31% UST 10Y · DXY down · gold flat

10-year yield falls 11 bps to 4.31%; dollar slides 0.73% as risk appetite returns on Intel's quarter.

The 10-year Treasury yield fell 11 basis points to 4.31% Friday as strong earnings reduced stagflation anxiety — the sharpest single-session yield decline in two weeks. The dollar index slid 0.73% to 99.45 as risk appetite returned and investors rotated out of safe-haven dollar exposure. Gold was essentially flat at $4,725.40 (+0.03%), holding its safe-haven premium without participating in the risk-on equity rally — a pattern suggesting the gold market is waiting for resolution on Iran and Lebanon before making a directional move. The gold/Brent ratio remains compressed, historically a signal of oil strength or gold consolidation ahead.

Quick hits

  • VIX fell 9.63% to 14.82 Friday — reversing more than half of Thursday's 18.84% spike, as Intel's earnings print restored confidence in the tech-earnings narrative.
  • Advancing issues outnumbered decliners nearly 3:1 on the NYSE Friday — broad participation in the rally, not just mega-cap concentration.
  • The Russell 2000's 0.43% gain Friday was more modest than large-cap indices — small caps still carry more direct energy-cost exposure from oil above $100.
  • Dollar index at 99.45 is its lowest level in over a week as risk-on flows reversed Thursday's safe-haven dollar bid — a favorable macro signal for emerging-market debt.
  • "SaaSpocalypse 3.0" — a second wave of SaaS stock pressure — continued Friday for select names even as the broader Nasdaq rallied; the selective damage reinforces investor preference for hardware and infrastructure over pure-play software.
  • The Dow's 0.16% decline reflects its index composition more than broad market weakness: Intel is not a Dow component, and Nvidia's weighting is different from the Nasdaq; the Dow's 30-stock structure systematically underweights the AI beneficiary trade.
