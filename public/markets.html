The Nasdaq fell 2.15% to 25,137.69 Thursday — its largest single-day decline in a month — as Alphabet dropped 7% and Tesla fell 14% in a session compounded by Brent crude crossing $100 for the first time since May after Houthis struck Saudi oil tankers in the Red Sea; the S&P 500 lost 1.21% to 7,408.30, the Dow shed 0.97% to 51,711.65, and the Russell 2000 fell 0.89% to 2,933.64; September Federal Reserve rate hike odds surged to 80% as Brent's $100 crossing intensified the inflation-pass-through signal and the UST 10-year yield rose to 4.72% (▲4 basis points); gold declined 1.39% to $4,028 as the geopolitical premium was replaced by the inflation premium; and Friday's early futures point higher as oil retreats from $100 and the Polymarket contract implies a 66% probability of a higher open, suggesting Thursday's sell-off may be treated as a mean-reversion opportunity if Brent stabilizes below $100.

Thursday confirmed the three-way market equation that has governed equities since the Iran conflict began: oil price determines inflation expectations, inflation expectations determine rate odds, rate odds determine the discount rate on growth-stock multiples. At $100+ Brent with 80% September hike odds and a 4.72% 10-year yield, FCF-negative AI capex stories get the maximum rate-driven multiple compression. Alphabet's Cloud 82% beat — the strongest AI enterprise revenue data of the cycle — drove a 7% decline in the session because the discount rate is doing the math the multiple cannot escape. Friday's futures rebound suggests the market is not abandoning the AI thesis, only re-rating the specific FCF timeline under updated rate assumptions. The variable is oil: if Brent falls back below $95 this week, September hike odds compress and the rate-multiple equation relaxes. If Brent holds above $100, the compression is structural through the September 16 FOMC.

Gold's 1.39% decline while oil crossed $100 confirms the inflation-versus-geopolitical-premium rotation. Through the Iran escalation, gold had carried both the inflation hedge bid (oil up) and the geopolitical shock bid (conflict escalation). At $100 Brent, the inflation hedge dominates and the geopolitical premium becomes redundant — gold buyers who accumulated on geopolitical grounds are taking profits while the inflation-premium holders remain. The result is a lower total gold price despite more oil-driven inflation pressure: the geopolitical component is cashed, the inflation component persists.

The Nasdaq fell 2.15% to 25,137.69 Thursday — its largest single-session decline in a month — as Alphabet's 7% drop and Tesla's 14% fall translated Wednesday night's after-hours earnings into the session alongside Brent crossing $100 and September rate hike odds hitting 80%. The S&P 500 fell 1.21% to 7,408.30, the Dow shed 0.97% to 51,711.65, and the Russell 2000 lost 0.89% to 2,933.64. Combined, Alphabet and Tesla erased more than $200 billion in market capitalization in a single session — the largest single-day AI-sector earnings-driven loss in the current cycle.

The Nasdaq's 2.15% decline is a rate-environment story more than an earnings story. Alphabet's Cloud 82% beat is the strongest AI revenue confirmation this cycle; the sell-off reflects not a rejection of that data but a re-pricing under the discount rate implied by 80% September hike odds and a 4.72% 10-year yield. At those rates, a company producing -$5.9B FCF on a 2028 normalization timeline gets a lower multiple than it would at 2025 rate levels — regardless of how good the growth rate is. Tesla's problem is structural, not rate-driven: 1.4% operating margin on record revenue does not validate the FSD-and-software future multiple even at low rates. Both declined for different reasons in the same session.

Brent crude closed at $100.69 (▲2.29%) Thursday — crossing $100 for the first time since May — as Yemen's Houthis struck two Saudi oil tankers in the Red Sea and threatened to blockade the Bab al-Mandeb Strait, which carries 12–15% of global maritime trade connecting the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden. President Trump stated that the United States would hold Iran responsible for any future Houthi attacks on shipping, threatening "major military punishment." The dual-theater Gulf disruption — the Iran-US Hormuz conflict and the Houthi Red Sea campaign — means full oil-price normalization requires two separate diplomatic resolutions, both of which remain absent.

The Bab al-Mandeb threat introduces a third chokepoint to the Gulf energy supply story. Prior disruption had centered on Hormuz (the eastern Gulf exit) and the existing Houthi attacks on commercial shipping. A Bab al-Mandeb blockade would close the western exit from the Red Sea — the route that Saudi and Gulf oil uses to reach European markets without transiting Africa. The 12–15% of global maritime trade routing through that strait includes far more than oil: container shipping, LNG, and commercial goods all use the same passage. A Bab al-Mandeb blockade would produce a supply shock with broader trade implications than a Hormuz closure of equivalent scale. Friday's oil retreat from $100 suggests the market is treating the threat as a warning rather than an execution — for now.

September Federal Reserve rate hike odds surged to 80% Thursday as Brent crude crossing $100 activated the inflation-regime signal that markets treat as a qualitative threshold: below $100, the oil-CPI channel is elevated but bounded; above $100, the CPI signal from energy is strong enough that the September 16 FOMC meeting is effectively constrained by the July CPI print due in mid-August rather than by discretionary FOMC judgment. The UST 10-year yield rose to 4.72% (▲4 basis points) and gold declined 1.39% to $4,028 as geopolitical-premium holders cashed profits while inflation-premium holders held through the sell-off. The July 29 FOMC meeting remains near-certain to hold; September is the live decision. Friday futures point higher as oil retreats from $100 and the Polymarket September-hike contract may soften with it.

Powell's language at the July 29 FOMC press conference becomes the most important rates market signal before the mid-August CPI print. If Powell signals that $100+ Brent is incorporated into the FOMC's inflation baseline, September becomes a near-certain hike. If he frames it as a supply shock that the FOMC will wait to assess in September data, the 80% odds compress and the market trades toward a 50/50 September decision. That distinction — incorporated vs. monitored — is what the July 29 meeting will clarify. Gold at $4,028 defending $4,000 is the level that determines whether the inflation premium alone can hold the bid that was previously supported by geopolitical demand as well. If oil retreats below $95 on ceasefire progress, both the geopolitical and inflation premiums compress simultaneously, and gold's $4,000 support becomes the relevant test level.
