Brent · $108 · Iran $108.11 +2.64% · Hormuz stall · OPEC+ 206K bpd · Goldman $90

Brent closes at $108.11 — Iran Hormuz stalemate keeps supply premium intact as OPEC+ hike proves symbolic.

Brent crude closed at $108.11 per barrel Monday, up 2.64%, after Iran's Hormuz reopening proposal showed no sign of US acceptance and OPEC+'s 206,000 barrels per day production increase for May — agreed in principle Sunday — proved too small to move the price needle. The OPEC+ hike represents less than 2% of the supply disrupted by the Hormuz closure; the eight member countries confirming the increase are themselves operating below capacity due to the conflict's impact on Gulf shipping lanes. WTI crude rose 2.07% to $97.10. Goldman Sachs this week revised its late-2026 Brent forecast down to $90, estimating that global inventories are drawing at a record pace but that a Hormuz resolution — whenever it comes — will drive a sharp price correction. The bank acknowledged the premium may persist longer than initially modeled. Every additional week of $100+ Brent is estimated to reduce global GDP growth by 0.05–0.1 percentage points — a compounding drag as negotiations stall.

Kevin Warsh · April 29 Vote Senate Banking · Tillis clears · May 15 Powell end · FOMC May 7

Kevin Warsh Fed chair vote Wednesday — Senate Banking Committee clears path to confirmation before Powell's May 15 term end.

The Senate Banking Committee is set to vote Wednesday April 29 on Kevin Warsh's nomination as Federal Reserve chair, after Senator Thom Tillis announced Sunday he would end his blockade following the DOJ's decision to drop its investigation of Jerome Powell. With Tillis's vote, the committee advances 13-11 on party lines; the full Senate is expected to confirm Warsh before Powell's term expires May 15. The monetary policy transition arrives at a critical moment: the FOMC meets May 7, the April PCE inflation print publishes May 1, and Brent is above $108. Warsh vowed in his April 21 confirmation hearing not to be Trump's "sock puppet" on rates — a statement markets will test almost immediately. Futures markets are pricing the Fed on hold at May 7 regardless of chair; the real question is how Warsh's communication style and risk tolerance shape the June and September FOMC meetings as oil prices and AI capex returns drive the inflation trajectory.

ECB · April 30 −25 bps? Rate decision · Brent $108 complicates · Amazon same day

ECB meets April 30 on the same day as Amazon earnings — 25 bps cut expected despite Brent at $108 straining the mandate.

The European Central Bank holds its April 30 rate decision on the same day as Amazon's Q1 earnings — the most data-dense Thursday in months. Markets price a 25 basis point cut. The complication: Brent above $108 is keeping eurozone headline inflation elevated, and Iran's Hormuz proposal — if accepted, reducing oil — is not yet reality. ECB President Lagarde faces a communication challenge: cutting while headline inflation rises risks appearing inconsistent with the price-stability mandate. Analysts expect a cut accompanied by explicit guidance framing the oil component as a temporary supply shock, with a data-dependent signal for subsequent meetings. The ECB's last move was a 25 bps cut in March. If the ECB cuts despite $108 Brent, it implicitly endorses the view that the energy shock is transitory and supply-driven — a view the US Fed has been more reluctant to take publicly with the FOMC meeting just one week later on May 7.

FOMC · May 7 · PCE May 1 May 7 Fed on hold · first Warsh meeting · PCE + oil · Brent $108

FOMC meets May 7 as Warsh takes the chair — PCE on May 1 with $108 Brent will be Warsh's first test in real time.

The Federal Reserve's next meeting is May 7 — almost certainly Kevin Warsh's first as chair, assuming Senate confirmation completes before May 15. Futures markets are pricing the Fed on hold. The April PCE inflation print publishes May 1, six days before the decision — the Fed's preferred inflation measure will reflect the first full month of Brent above $100. Energy's pass-through to core PCE is modest: every 10% increase in Brent above $90 adds an estimated 0.2–0.3 percentage points to US core PCE inflation. That is a complication for a new Fed chair attempting to establish credibility on price stability while oil markets remain distorted by a geopolitical supply shock. The market will scrutinize Warsh's post-meeting statement for any shift in tone from Powell's "data dependent" framework — and watch whether the new chair is more or less hawkish on oil-driven inflation than his predecessor.

Quick hits

  • April 29 is arguably the most event-dense single trading session of 2026: Alphabet, Meta, and Microsoft earnings after the close; the Kevin Warsh confirmation vote at Senate Banking; and the market digesting Monday's Microsoft-OpenAI multicloud restructuring. It is the first day since January where a single macro shock, a monetary policy event, and three $1T+ earnings reports coincide.
  • Goldman Sachs's $90 Brent late-2026 forecast implies a $18+ decline from current levels — driven entirely by the assumption that a Hormuz resolution eventually occurs. Every week that assumption fails produces an additional compounding GDP drag and an incremental inflation premium that the Fed must account for in its dot plot.
  • The VIX at 15.60, up 5.26% Monday, is still historically low — but the move from 14.82 to 15.60 in a single session on what was broadly flat equity action suggests options markets are hedging the concentrated risk of April 29. A VIX spike above 20 on an earnings miss this week would signal a regime change from the post-January low-volatility environment.
  • Qualcomm's 11% gain on an unconfirmed analyst report reflects the market's willingness to assign enormous option value to AI hardware positioning — even before a product exists. The ~$15 billion single-day market cap addition exceeds the annual revenue of QCOM's automotive division, which has taken years to build.
  • The dollar index at 98.98 — its second consecutive decline — benefits EM sovereign borrowers with USD-denominated debt; every 1% dollar decline reduces EM debt service costs by roughly $30 billion annually. The declining DXY is partly a function of geopolitical risk diversification and partly a function of the ECB's perceived willingness to cut ahead of the Fed.
  • Gold at $4,742 — up 0.35% Monday and hovering near its all-time high — is holding its safe-haven premium even as equities make new records, suggesting the gold market is pricing a scenario where the Iran conflict persists well beyond current consensus timelines. Gold and equities don't usually move together at record highs — when they do, it typically signals elevated tail risk that equity volatility hasn't yet priced.
