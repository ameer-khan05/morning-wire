June CPI fell 0.4% month-on-month — twice the expected decline and the largest single-month drop since April 2020 — with the annual rate coming in at 3.5% vs. 3.8% consensus and core CPI running flat for the month at 0.0% (vs. 0.2% expected) and 2.6% annually (vs. 2.9% expected), the energy index declining 5.7% on June's gasoline price cooling, the BLS confirming the official release and Seeking Alpha noting the data "undercuts the hawkish case" while Kiplinger and others warned the reading is backward-looking relative to the Hormuz oil surge that has since pushed Brent to $85.

A simultaneous beat on headline, monthly, and core CPI is analytically rare and has maximum market impact because it closes the main exits from the dovish reading: headline cooling could be dismissed as energy-driven, but the core beat at 0.0% MoM removes that escape. All three measures beat simultaneously establishes genuine underlying disinflation in June's data — the cleanest CPI reading in years. The forward problem is symmetrically acute: June covers a period when oil averaged around $69. The August CPI release (covering July) will show what $83–87 Brent does to the index. The inflation trajectory reversal is not hypothetical; it is already priced into the forward oil curve. Warsh's "not mission accomplished" is analytically correct: June is a historical snapshot of a world that no longer exists.

PPI tomorrow is the market session's most critical forward data point in the current conflict environment. June CPI covers a period when oil averaged around $69 and the ceasefire was still nominally in effect. PPI data released Thursday covers more recent producer-price behavior including the start of the Hormuz conflict's oil-price impact on upstream costs. Energy-intensive industries — petrochemicals, transportation, manufacturing, agriculture — all absorb oil price changes in their producer cost structures before those costs pass through to consumer prices; the PPI print will show how much of the Hormuz oil premium has already entered the pipeline as a cost rather than a market-price uncertainty. If producer prices show significant oil pass-through, the forward CPI path is materially steeper than the June headline suggests. Warsh's testimony today is the context-setting event that frames how markets interpret tomorrow's PPI data — which makes his Senate testimony the pivot between the backward-looking good news from June CPI and the forward-looking inflation risk from an $85 Brent environment.

Quick hits

  • The Goldman Sachs 45% EPS beat is worth examining as a predictive signal for the rest of earnings season. Goldman operates at the intersection of every significant capital allocation decision in the US economy: M&A advisory, equity and fixed income trading, asset management, and principal investing. When Goldman beats consensus by 45%, it means the deal pipeline, trading volume, and advisory fees in the real economy were all running at a significantly higher pace than analyst models had incorporated. That excess activity — the difference between the $14.48 modeled and the $20.98 actual — reflects the AI investment cycle's compounding effect on capital markets velocity. Every enterprise that is building out AI infrastructure, consolidating AI-adjacent acquisitions, or accessing capital markets for AI expansion generates Goldman revenue. The beat quantifies how much faster that cycle is running than outside models predicted, and the implication for the rest of earnings season is that companies downstream of the AI investment cycle — cloud providers, infrastructure firms, enterprise software — are also likely to beat consensus if Goldman's signal generalizes.
  • The PPI tomorrow is the week's most consequential data point for the September FOMC, not the June CPI. June CPI is the Fed's baseline reading of a world that predates the current oil-conflict environment. PPI covers producer prices in a period that overlaps with the early Hormuz conflict escalation. If upstream oil pass-through is showing up in producer prices — in transportation, chemical, and manufacturing costs — the Fed's September meeting faces a more complex picture than the June CPI headline suggests. A hawkish PPI combined with soft CPI creates the analytical split that makes rate decisions genuinely uncertain: backward-looking consumer price moderation vs. forward-looking producer price acceleration. Warsh's challenge in testimony today and tomorrow is to communicate a coherent policy framework under that uncertainty without either over-committing to a September hike on forward oil fears or signaling a hold based on backward-looking CPI cooling. That is a genuinely difficult communication problem, and the PPI data tomorrow determines whether it gets harder or easier.
