Friday's S&P 500 records a fresh close at 7,398.93 as April jobs beat the consensus at +115,000 versus 65,000 — the sixth consecutive weekly equity gain, the longest winning streak since 2024. Monday opens with oil surging more than $4 to WTI $98.50 and Brent $104.20 after Trump rejects Iran's counterproposal as "TOTALLY UNACCEPTABLE."

Friday was the week's crescendo: the +115,000 jobs print, against a consensus that had been expecting 65,000 and a worst-case scenario of a 62,000 stagflation signal, delivered the single data point that equity markets needed to confirm their thesis. The economy is holding under conflict conditions. Healthcare, transportation, and retail added jobs in April despite WTI above $95 for the entire survey month; the unemployment rate held at 4.3%. The S&P 500 gained 0.84% to 7,398.93; the Nasdaq gained 1.71% to 26,247.08, with AI-linked technology names as the session's primary driver. The Dow edged up 12 points to 49,609.16; the Russell 2000 gained 0.76% to 2,861.21. All four major indices posted their sixth consecutive weekly gain — the longest unbroken winning streak since 2024. Monday morning erases a portion of that relief: Iran's Saturday counterproposal — demanding Hormuz sovereignty, war reparations, and all sanctions lifted — was called "TOTALLY UNACCEPTABLE" by Trump on Sunday, sending WTI to $98.50 and Brent to $104.20, with the S&P 500 and Nasdaq opening modestly lower while oil-exposed equities face fresh pressure.

The Anchor

The April +115K jobs beat is the most important economic data point of the conflict period: the economy is outperforming stagflation expectations for a second consecutive month, under full oil-price pressure. The resilience changes the monetary policy calculus in the medium term even if the Iran deal stalls.

The April +115,000 print needs to be understood against the expectation context to grasp its significance. The March +178,000 print had already beaten consensus; April was expected to show the oil-economy transmission arriving at the employment data, with the pessimistic consensus scenario at +62,000 specifically tied to the sectors most exposed to $95-105 WTI: transportation, logistics, manufacturing, and food production. The actual +115,000 — nearly double the median consensus expectation — means the oil-economy employment transmission that was expected to materialize in April was either delayed or offset by countervailing factors. Healthcare added jobs on its structural demand growth trajectory; transportation and warehousing added jobs despite elevated fuel costs; retail gained on consumer spending that held up better than expected under energy price pressure. The employment resilience does not mean the oil cost is not a drag on the economy — it means the drag has been absorbed more broadly than the bear case assumed, and that the segments generating job growth are more resilient to oil prices than the conflict-period employment models predicted. The practical implication for monetary policy: Chair Warsh's stagflation dilemma — raise rates on inflation, cut on employment weakness — is now less acute. The employment side of the dual mandate is not yet sending a clear distress signal; the inflation side (WTI $98.50 Monday after the Iran rejection) is worsening. The asymmetric pressure remains hawkish, but the April jobs data pushes a rate cut decision from "politically impossible" to "data-dependent and potentially Q3."

Friday's Nasdaq +1.71% was the AI infrastructure trade expressing itself in a strong-data-good environment: when the economy is strong and the inflation signal is not immediately tightening, AI-linked growth stocks benefit from both the earnings growth (strong economy) and the valuation expansion (less pressure on the discount rate). NVIDIA, AMD, and Microsoft led the Nasdaq's session gain, with NVIDIA specifically benefiting from the carry-through effect of the IREN infrastructure partnership announcement from the prior week. The strong jobs number also reinforced the AI spending cycle: CFOs at firms with AI budget commitments are less likely to cut those commitments in an economy adding 115,000 jobs in April than in one adding 62,000. The jobs beat therefore functions as a secondary positive catalyst for AI infrastructure spending — not through the direct mechanism of interest rates, but through the indirect mechanism of corporate confidence in maintaining capital expenditure plans during the conflict period.

The employment resilience story in April's data is a sector-level one that is worth examining. Healthcare continued its structural demand-driven hiring — this sector is not oil-sensitive and adds jobs consistently regardless of macro conditions. Transportation and warehousing — which was the sector most expected to show oil-economy weakness — actually added jobs, suggesting that while elevated fuel costs compress margins, demand for transportation services has held sufficiently strong to maintain headcount. Retail trade gains reflect a consumer spending environment that is more resilient than energy cost models predicted: US consumers are absorbing $4-per-gallon gasoline without the demand collapse that prior oil shock episodes produced, partly because household savings rates built during 2024 provide a buffer, and partly because wage growth has partially offset the energy cost increase. The combination of the two consecutive beats (+178K in March, +115K in April) establishes that the April stagflation reading — oil above $95 simultaneously with employment weakness — is not yet the data's verdict. May's report (June 5) will capture the first month of WTI above $98 (if oil stays elevated after the Iran rejection) and will be the definitive test of whether April's resilience is the new baseline or a statistical reprieve.

WTI crude surges to $98.50 Monday morning — up $3.88 per barrel — and Brent rises to $104.20 after Trump calls Iran's counterproposal "TOTALLY UNACCEPTABLE." The S&P 500 and Nasdaq open modestly lower after Friday's records. Iran's demands included sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz, war reparations, and full sanctions removal — terms the administration cannot accept, reinstating the full conflict-period oil risk premium.

The Monday oil surge is the market's mechanical response to Iran deal probability falling from approximately 50% toward materially lower. The oil market pricing arithmetic: WTI at approximately $95 before the Iran rejection reflects a market pricing roughly 50% probability of a deal that would send WTI toward $78-82 (the pre-conflict equilibrium). WTI at $98.50 after the rejection reflects the market pricing the deal probability significantly lower — closer to 20-30% on a near-term horizon — which pushes the probability-weighted price toward the conflict-period high range. The $3.88-per-barrel surge on the Iran rejection news is a directionally correct response; the magnitude reflects both the market's prior 50% deal probability pricing being revised down and the CFTC data showing that speculative net short positions in crude oil had been building ahead of the weekend's diplomatic developments, creating a short-squeeze dynamic when the data confirmed the bearish scenario. The S&P 500's modest Monday decline — approximately 0.1% early — is notable for its restraint: the market is not abandoning the six-week equity bull thesis on a single catalyst reversal, correctly identifying that Friday's jobs beat represents a genuine economic improvement that is not reversed by the Iran rejection.

The Trump-Xi Beijing summit May 14-15 is now carrying the Iran war as its central agenda item — Trump has been pressing Xi to use China's influence over Tehran — alongside a trade agenda that includes a proposed "Board of Trade" mechanism for Chinese purchase commitments and potential tariff adjustments. China has been purchasing Iranian oil at $15-20 per barrel below market; the summit's Iran dimension runs through China's oil purchase leverage.

The trade dimensions of the Trump-Xi summit have been developing for weeks: the proposed Board of Trade mechanism — a bilateral senior official body to oversee implementation of Chinese purchase commitments — is designed to address the failure of the 2020 Phase One trade deal, where China committed to purchasing $200 billion in US goods and services but did not follow through on the full commitment. The 2026 proposed commitments focus on agriculture (soybeans, corn), aerospace (Boeing aircraft orders), and energy (US crude oil and LNG), with the combination designed to benefit key US political constituencies ahead of the November midterm elections. The Iran dimension is newer and more urgent: China has been purchasing approximately 600,000 barrels per day of Iranian oil at a significant discount, providing Tehran with hard currency that has partially offset US sanctions pressure and extended Iran's capacity to sustain the Hormuz closure. If Trump asks Xi to reduce or eliminate those purchases in exchange for tariff relief, and Xi agrees, the resulting reduction in Iranian revenue would meaningfully increase Iran's economic incentive to reach a deal with the US. This is the summit's most consequential unpublicized agenda item — more impactful on the Iran deal timeline than any directly bilateral US-Iran negotiation.

The cross-asset signals reflect a market pricing geopolitical risk (dollar up, oil up) more than recession risk (bonds would rally if recession fear dominated).

Monday's cross-asset moves tell a specific geopolitical story rather than a broad risk-off story. A pure risk-off move would drive capital into bonds (yields down) and gold simultaneously, as investors seek safety in non-dollar assets alongside dollar assets. Instead, Monday shows the dollar strengthening on the geopolitical uncertainty while bonds decline slightly (yields up 5 bps) and gold retreats marginally — a pattern consistent with a geopolitical risk-off that is dollar-positive but not recession-negative. The dollar's safe-haven bid in geopolitical uncertainty reflects the US dollar's role as the global crisis currency: regardless of the geopolitical risk's origin (even US-related risks often strengthen the dollar in the short term as global capital concentrates in the most liquid safe-haven asset). Gold's 0.40% Monday pullback is technically unremarkable — it gives back a fraction of last week's gains and remains near multi-year highs. The CPI print on Wednesday is the bond market's next significant input: a higher-than-expected CPI will confirm that the Iran-driven oil prices are embedding in consumer prices, justifying the yield rise; a lower-than-expected CPI would partially reverse Monday's yield move by showing the oil-to-CPI transmission is slower than the market assumed.

Quick hits

  • The April jobs report's two consecutive beats merit a structural reframing of the conflict-period economic narrative. The prevailing view entering April's report was that the March +178,000 was a statistical anomaly — a last gasp of pre-conflict hiring momentum before the oil-economy transmission fully arrived in the employment data. April's +115,000 refutes the anomaly hypothesis: two consecutive beats establish a pattern of resilience. The revised narrative is that the US labor market is absorbing the oil shock better than prior supply-shock episodes predicted, possibly because the energy intensity of the US economy (energy consumption per dollar of GDP) has declined significantly since the 1970s and 1980s supply shocks, because the shale revolution means higher oil prices benefit US energy producers while hurting consumers (partially offsetting), and because the service-dominated US employment base is less sensitive to energy costs than the manufacturing-dominated base of prior oil shock episodes. If this structural resilience interpretation is correct, the Fed's rate-cut timeline is less urgent than the stagflation scenario implied — but also less blocked. The path to a rate cut runs through inflation declining as the conflict resolves rather than through employment collapsing to force the Fed's hand.
  • The S&P 500's modest Monday decline — approximately 0.1% early — relative to the oil surge of $3.88 per barrel is the most informative signal in Monday's market data. In prior sessions where oil surged significantly on Iran deal deterioration, equities declined more sharply: the February 28 conflict onset produced a 2-3% S&P decline on the initial oil surge. Monday's 0.1% decline on a $3.88 oil surge represents a market that has absorbed and partially priced the ongoing oil risk premium, reduced its sensitivity to individual Iran news events, and established a "floor" below which the economy's fundamental resilience (confirmed by the jobs beat) provides support. The implication is not that equities are complacent about the Iran deal — the six-week winning streak is partly built on deal optimism — but that the market has learned to differentiate between a deal-status decline (temporary, reversible when negotiations resume) and a deal-collapse (permanent, requiring full oil-risk repricing). Monday's rejection is being classified as the former: reversible when negotiations resume, rather than a terminal breakdown.
  • The Board of Trade mechanism in the Trump-Xi summit trade agenda is the institutional innovation that both sides need to make Chinese purchase commitments credible. The failure of the 2020 Phase One deal — where China committed to $200 billion in purchases but delivered approximately $130 billion — was an implementation failure, not a commitment failure: the commitment was in the agreement but the mechanism for monitoring and enforcing implementation was insufficient. The proposed Board of Trade addresses this by creating a standing senior official body — below the head-of-state level but above the working-level technical teams — that can resolve disputes, adjust purchase timelines, and communicate implementation challenges in real time rather than through the slow cycle of diplomatic complaints. The specific product focus (agriculture, aerospace, energy) is calibrated to US political constituents: soybean purchases benefit Midwestern farmers; Boeing orders benefit a high-profile US manufacturer; LNG purchases benefit the Gulf Coast energy sector. All three were politically significant in the 2024 election and remain significant ahead of the 2026 midterms — making them the natural trade currency for a summit that serves both leaders' domestic political needs.
  • Gold's behavior this week is the most analytically interesting asset class to watch in the context of the Iran rejection. Gold performed strongly last week (+3.14% Wednesday) on Iran peace deal hope — which seems counterintuitive, since a peace deal reduces geopolitical risk and should theoretically reduce gold's safe-haven premium. The explanation is that gold was responding to the deal's implied dollar weakness: a peace deal sends oil lower, PCE lower, Fed cuts sooner, dollar weaker — and dollar weakness is a primary positive driver of gold prices. Gold surged on the deal hope because the deal's macro consequences (lower oil → lower inflation → weaker dollar) are gold-bullish. Monday's 0.40% gold decline after the Iran rejection is then also counterintuitive: geopolitical risk is rising, which should be gold-bullish, but the dollar is strengthening on the geopolitical safe-haven bid, which competes with gold for safe-haven capital. The two forces (geopolitical risk positive for gold; dollar strength negative for gold) are partially offsetting, explaining the small Monday gold decline. The net direction: if the Iran deal stalls for weeks and oil stays above $95, gold is in a mixed environment — geopolitically supported but dollar-constrained. The CPI print Wednesday will sharpen the dollar picture and give gold a clearer directional signal.
  • The Dow's microscopic Friday gain (+12 points to 49,609.16) versus the Nasdaq's +1.71% tells the session's sector story precisely. The Dow is weighted toward large industrial, financial, and consumer staples companies that benefit from an Iran peace deal scenario (lower energy costs, stable rates, strong domestic demand) but are not especially benefited by AI spending momentum. The Nasdaq's +1.71% is dominated by AI-linked technology names that benefit from both the jobs beat (strong economy) and the AI spending cycle (independent of Iran). The Dow's near-flat performance on a strong jobs beat is the market correctly identifying that +115,000 jobs is not the same macro catalyst as a signed Iran deal: it is good for the economy in general, but it does not lower oil, does not cut rates, and does not deliver the specific Hormuz reopening benefit that the Dow's energy-exposed and rate-sensitive components need to advance substantially from the 49,609 level. The Dow will next test 50,000 when the Iran deal is signed; the Nasdaq will continue to advance as the AI earnings cycle confirms itself, with or without the Iran deal.
  • The CPI print on Wednesday May 13 is the first major data point after the Iran rejection that could move the market's rate-cut timeline significantly in either direction. The elevated April oil environment (WTI averaged above $95 for the entire April survey month) creates upside risk for the headline CPI, because energy is directly in the CPI basket and higher gasoline, heating oil, and utility costs flow directly into the consumer price index. The core CPI (excluding food and energy) will be the indicator markets focus on: if core inflation remains controlled (below 3%) despite headline elevation, the Fed can credibly describe the inflation as supply-shock driven and transitory, opening space for a rate cut once the supply shock resolves. If core CPI is also elevated (above 3.5%), the argument that inflation is purely oil-driven becomes harder to sustain, and the rate-cut timeline extends. The consensus core CPI expectation for April is approximately 3.1-3.2%; an upside surprise to 3.5%+ would be the market-moving scenario, sending yields higher, dollar stronger, equities lower, and further extending the BTC ceiling.
