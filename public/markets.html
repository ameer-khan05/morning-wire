The S&P 500 and Nasdaq closed at new all-time records on Thursday — S&P at 7,563.63 (+0.58%), Nasdaq at 26,917.47 (+0.91%) — despite April PCE inflation reaching its highest level in nearly three years at 3.8%, as Snowflake's 36-39% post-earnings surge and the Iran deal's proximity overrode the macro headwind. WTI fell to $87.20 Friday on tentative deal reports. Friday futures are edging up: S&P +0.16%, Nasdaq +0.20%, Russell 2000 +0.57%.

Thursday's simultaneous record closes and the highest PCE reading since 2023 produced a market result that defies the simple inflation-destroys-equities narrative: the S&P and Nasdaq made new highs on a day when inflation data argued for caution. The resolution is that two forces ran in opposite directions simultaneously. On the bearish side: April PCE 3.8% confirmed the Iran war's oil premium is embedded in consumer prices, and the CME FedWatch Fed rate-hike probability hit 39% in the morning. On the bullish side: Snowflake's 36-39% surge on a $1.39 billion Q1 beat and $6 billion AWS deal lifted the AI-infrastructure narrative just as it needed lifting; and Thursday afternoon brought reports that US-Iran negotiators had reached a tentative deal, sending WTI lower and rate-hike expectations down with it. The market closed at records because the forward-looking signals — Snowflake's AI demand confirmation, Iran deal probability — outweighed the backward-looking PCE confirmation of what April's oil prices had already done. Friday morning continues the Iran deal narrative: WTI at $87.20, futures edging up, and the decision on Trump's desk.

The Anchor

Records on a day of 3.8% PCE inflation. The market is not ignoring the data — it is pricing the forward path: an Iran deal that sends WTI from $87 toward $82 would reduce the May PCE energy contribution enough to pull Fed rate-hike probability from 39% back toward 10-15%, unlocking the equity multiple expansion that the records are anticipating.

The S&P 500's record 7,563.63 on Thursday requires understanding three simultaneous drivers. The first is Snowflake: a 36-39% single-day surge in a major technology company adds meaningful S&P points directly (Snowflake is in the index) and signals the AI-infrastructure demand thesis that has supported the entire tech-heavy index's elevation above pre-conflict levels. Snowflake's Q1 FY2027 revenue of $1.39 billion — beating the $1.32 billion estimate by 5.3%, growing 33% year-over-year — and the $6 billion AWS partnership announced simultaneously are the confirmations that enterprise AI data-platform demand is accelerating, not plateauing, as Nvidia's Q1 result confirmed hyperscaler AI capex earlier in the week. The second driver is the afternoon Iran deal report: when Reuters and NBC News reported that US-Iran negotiators had reached a tentative deal, WTI moved from the $89-90 range toward $87, and the implied Fed rate-hike probability began to fall from 39% toward the mid-20s range that a $85-87 WTI and declining energy PCE contribution would imply. The equity market immediately priced the lower rate environment that a deal would deliver — the rate-sensitive Russell 2000 gaining 0.62% is the clearest signal of this mechanism. The third driver is the VIX: declining from 19.84 to 18.92 on the day as geopolitical risk was repriced downward by the deal reports, reducing the equity risk premium and supporting multiples across the index.

The 10-year Treasury yield at 4.46% — down 4 basis points from Thursday's PCE-driven uptick to 4.50% — is the yield-curve signal of the net Thursday impact: the morning PCE pushed yields up, the afternoon deal news pulled them down, and the net is 4 basis points lower. The yield decline is modest relative to the equity records, reflecting the market's recognition that the deal is "tentative" and unsigned — the full yield decline that a completed deal would produce (potentially 15-25 basis points toward 4.30-4.35% if WTI reaches $82-84) is still in front of the market if Trump signs. Friday morning's S&P futures at +0.16% suggest the market is holding its record position while awaiting the signature that would unlock the next leg of the rally. The key risk to current market levels is simple: if Trump rejects the deal framework and announces resumed military operations, WTI would likely rebound from $87 toward $94-97, rate-hike probability would jump back to 39%+, and the S&P records would face a reversal of the forward-pricing they currently reflect. The record close is both a real achievement and a pricing of an outcome that has not yet occurred.

The S&P 500 closed at a new all-time high of 7,563.63 (+0.58%) and the Nasdaq at a new record 26,917.47 (+0.91%) on Thursday — despite April PCE inflation printing at 3.8%, its highest level in nearly three years. The Dow added 0.05% to 50,668.97 and the Russell 2000 gained 0.62% to 2,937.82. Snowflake's 36-39% surge and the afternoon emergence of Iran deal reports sent WTI lower and rate-hike expectations declining, producing records on a day that began with the worst PCE reading since 2023.

The specific mechanism by which records emerged from a bad inflation print requires tracking the market's intraday progression. Thursday opened with the PCE data at 7:00 AM ET: 3.8% headline, 3.3% core, highest since 2023. CME FedWatch jumped to 39% rate-hike probability. S&P futures, which had been slightly positive pre-market on Snowflake optimism, initially turned negative as the PCE data registered. By 10 AM, Snowflake's Q1 results were fully absorbed — the company's AI-driven revenue acceleration and the $6 billion AWS deal were sufficiently positive to lift tech broadly. By 2 PM, reports of the US-Iran tentative deal framework reached the market, sending WTI from approximately $89.50 toward $87.50 and the implied PCE trajectory for May measurably lower. The afternoon deal-and-oil-decline effect produced the conditions for the tech and rate-sensitive sectors to close at records even with the morning's inflation print on the screen. The S&P 7,563 close is approximately 6% above the 7,134 level before Nvidia's earnings on May 27, reflecting the three-day compound effect of the AI earnings season (Nvidia, Snowflake), the Iran deal proximity, and the resulting yield decline from the May 20 high of 4.657%.

Snowflake stock surged 36-39% Thursday — from $175.26 to $238.37 — after reporting Q1 FY2027 revenue of $1.39 billion, beating the $1.32 billion estimate by 5.3% and growing 33% year-over-year. The company simultaneously announced a $6 billion, five-year partnership with AWS to enhance enterprise AI adoption, including expanded collaboration with Amazon's Graviton AI chips and cloud-based GPU infrastructure. CEO Sridhar Ramaswamy called it a "milestone quarter" with "the strongest sequential dollar growth in our company's history." Net revenue retention hit 126%.

Snowflake's result is the data-platform complement to Nvidia's infrastructure result from Tuesday: while Nvidia confirmed that AI hardware demand is accelerating at the chip layer, Snowflake confirms that enterprise AI data-management demand is accelerating at the platform layer. The $6 billion AWS deal is structurally significant because it commits Snowflake to AWS GPU infrastructure for the AI workloads — training, inference, and data processing — that its enterprise customers run on the Snowflake platform. This commitment reduces Snowflake's multi-cloud agnosticism in exchange for AWS's scale and pricing, and it provides AWS with a major enterprise data platform customer for its AI GPU infrastructure, including the Graviton chips that Amazon has been investing in as a complement to Nvidia's dominance. For the S&P 500, Snowflake's 36-39% single-day move is the most significant single-stock index contribution since Nvidia's earnings week, and the magnitude reflects how thoroughly the market had discounted Snowflake's AI-monetization potential after a difficult 2025. The RPO of $9.21 billion — contracted future revenue — growing 38% year-over-year is the clearest forward indicator that the AI demand driving Snowflake's current revenue will continue through FY2028: enterprise customers are committing future spending on the platform, not merely consuming current capacity.

WTI crude fell to $87.20 Friday (-3.48%) and Brent to $93.45 (-3.23%) as reports of a tentative US-Iran 60-day ceasefire extension deal on Trump's desk sent the oil market's deal probability from approximately 60-65% toward 70-75%. WTI has fallen $21.39 from its May 20 peak of $108.59 as deal expectations built, with the Friday morning level representing the oil market's pricing of a high-but-not-certain deal probability. A signed deal with Hormuz reopening would likely send WTI toward $82-84 as the Hormuz premium fully unwinds.

The WTI $87.20 level requires parsing the Hormuz premium that remains embedded in the current price. Before February 28, WTI averaged approximately $76-82 based on pre-conflict supply-demand fundamentals. The current $87.20 includes approximately $5-11 of risk premium for Hormuz disruption that a signed deal would eliminate. The $82-84 post-deal estimate reflects: the base level ($77-82) plus a small residual premium ($2-3) for uncertainty about Iran's mine-clearing pace and Hormuz's full operational restoration. The market cannot price a $77-80 WTI until mines are actually cleared and tanker traffic is confirmed — the 30-day mine-clearing timeline in the deal framework means full Hormuz price normalization would take approximately 30-45 days post-signing. For the PCE trajectory, WTI at $82-84 for May would produce a May PCE energy contribution materially below April's level, allowing the June PCE report (released late July) to show the first meaningful moderation since the conflict began. This would reduce the Fed rate-hike probability from 39% toward 10-15% and lower the 10-year yield from 4.46% toward 4.25-4.35% — unlocking the equity multiple expansion that the record closes are already partially anticipating.

US Sanctions 8 Iran Oil Tankers · Dual-Track as Deal Negotiates 8 Tankers State Dept + Treasury May 28 · 8 oil tankers sanctioned · shadow fleet vessels: Marshall Islands Flora, Comoros Hauncayo, Panama Ill Gap, 5 others · Bessent: "not allow Iran to increase oil revenue for military reconstitution" · Oman threatened sanctions if helps Iran collect Hormuz fees · Iranian airlines targeted · dual-track: sanctions pressure maintained during deal close

The State Department and Treasury sanctioned eight Iranian oil tankers Thursday evening — while the deal text was reportedly being reviewed by the president — targeting shadow fleet vessels including the Marshall Islands-flagged Flora, Comoros-flagged Hauncayo, and Panama-flagged Ill Gap that have been transporting Iranian crude to buyers at discounted prices. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent stated: "We will not allow the Iranian government to increase its oil revenue for the purpose of reconstituting its armed forces." Oman was simultaneously threatened with sanctions if it helps Iran collect Hormuz transit fees.

For the oil market, the tanker sanctions have a limited near-term price impact — shadow fleet vessels are already priced into the current supply-demand equation, and removing eight vessels from the approximately 100-150 vessel shadow fleet does not materially change the flow of Iranian oil to China and India. The economic pressure significance is primarily deterrence: by sanctioning vessels while the deal is being finalized, the US signals that its oil sanctions enforcement will remain active even as diplomatic progress advances. The message to Iran's government: sanctions relief requires signed deal compliance, not anticipated compliance. For the broader market, the dual-track dynamic — sanctions while negotiating, drones shot down while deal text is reviewed — is now the established operational pattern of the endgame. Markets are pricing the diplomatic track (WTI at $87, S&P at record) while monitoring the military and sanctions tracks as risk factors that could reverse the pricing if they produce a deal collapse. The tanker sanctions do not change the market's deal-probability assessment because they are consistent with the observed pattern; an escalation of the military track (new large-scale strikes, Iranian retaliation) or a Trump deal rejection would.

Quick hits

  • Snowflake's 36-39% single-day gain deserves examination as a signal about the AI-monetization thesis at the data platform layer. Snowflake's business model differs fundamentally from Nvidia's: rather than selling hardware that enables AI workloads, Snowflake sells the data platform on which AI workloads run — the database, query engine, and data-sharing infrastructure that enterprise AI applications use to access, process, and share the data that AI models need. This means Snowflake's revenue growth is a direct function of how much enterprise AI workload is being run, not merely how much AI infrastructure is being purchased. Nvidia's $81.6 billion Q1 revenue tells you that AI hardware is being bought at an accelerating rate; Snowflake's $1.39 billion Q1 revenue tells you that enterprise AI data workloads are being run at an accelerating rate. The two data points together confirm the AI buildout at both the infrastructure layer (Nvidia) and the application layer (Snowflake), providing the most complete single-week picture of the AI deployment cycle since the Blackwell architecture launched. The $6 billion AWS deal is the commercial structure that ties Snowflake's platform growth to AWS's GPU and AI chip infrastructure — a bet by both companies that enterprise AI data workload growth will accelerate further over the next five years.
  • The VIX declining from 19.84 to 18.92 on Thursday is analytically significant for equity market conditions. The VIX at 19-20 is at the boundary between "moderate uncertainty" and "elevated concern" in historical volatility regime terms — values below 20 typically correspond to equity market expansions and risk-on conditions, while values above 20 correspond to more defensive positioning. The move from 19.84 (Thursday morning, PCE-driven) to 18.92 (Thursday close, deal-driven) reflects the Iran deal reports' impact on implied volatility: investors are purchasing less insurance against near-term S&P declines as the deal probability rises. For options traders, a VIX below 18 would suggest the market has moved to "deal expected and priced" territory and is reducing near-term hedges accordingly. Friday's futures edging up suggest the VIX may fall further if the deal advances toward signature. The specific VIX level to watch: a sustained move below 17 would signal that the market has essentially priced the deal as done and eliminated the geopolitical risk premium from equity volatility, unlocking the multiple-expansion leg of the rally that the records are beginning to anticipate.
  • The interaction between Thursday's records and the Fed's June meeting (June 16-17) creates a specific analytical question: will the Fed's reaction to Thursday's PCE data and the record equity closes produce hawkish language that tests the records? The Fed monitors equity valuations as a financial conditions indicator — record S&P closes while PCE inflation is at 3.8% create tighter financial conditions (higher equity wealth effect) than the record closes in isolation, compounding the PCE data's tightening signal. If the June meeting statement acknowledges both the PCE elevation and the equity records as evidence of financial conditions that are too accommodative for rapid inflation return to 2%, the implied rate-hike probability could increase from 39% toward 50%+, which would produce a significant equity market adjustment. The key Fed language to monitor: any change in the phrase "holding rates" toward "considering additional firming" or any acknowledgment that the neutral rate may have shifted higher would be the signal that the Fed is moving toward the rate-hike scenario the options market is partially pricing. The Iran deal's timing is crucial for this dynamic: if Trump signs by June 3-7, WTI would be falling toward $82-84 before the June 4 pre-meeting quiet period begins, and the Fed would have forward-looking evidence that the PCE trajectory is improving. If the deal is delayed past June 9 (when the Fed's blackout period begins), the June meeting will occur with WTI still above $87 and no confirmed inflation relief trajectory, making a hawkish June statement more likely.
  • The Russell 2000 at 2,937.82 — its Thursday close — is now approximately 4.8% above its May 20 level of 2,803.40 and within striking distance of the 3,000 level that would represent a new recovery high since the conflict began. The Russell 2000's trajectory over the past nine days provides the clearest market narrative of the Iran deal's economic impact: small caps were the index most crushed by the conflict (higher rates, lower growth expectations, regional bank stress) and are most sensitive to its resolution. The 4.8% recovery from the May 20 low in nine days tracks precisely with the 10-year yield decline from 4.657% to 4.46% over the same period — a 20-basis-point yield decline producing a 4.8% small-cap recovery implies approximately 0.24% Russell 2000 gain per basis point of 10-year yield decline. If the Iran deal sends the 10-year yield from 4.46% toward 4.30% — a 16-basis-point further decline from a signed deal — the model implies an additional 3.8% Russell 2000 gain from current levels, putting the index around 3,050-3,060. Whether that mathematical relationship holds depends on whether the yield decline is driven by oil-price relief (deal confirmed) or by a growth concern (deal collapsed and economic slowdown feared) — only the former is Russell-positive.
  • Gold at $4,468.20 — down 0.44% on Friday — is telling a consistent story that it has told throughout the deal-progress period: risk-on geopolitical events (deal approaching) reduce gold's safe-haven premium faster than the inflation-hedge bid can replace it. The mechanisms run in opposite directions: deal optimism reduces the geopolitical risk premium (negative for gold), but deal optimism also reduces Fed rate-hike probability and weakens the dollar (positive for gold). On a day when the deal is "very close" but unsigned, the geopolitical-premium reduction slightly outweighs the dollar-weakening effect, producing the modest -0.44% decline. Gold's level at $4,468 remains historically elevated — WTI at current levels keeps inflation expectations above 2% target, and gold's inflation-hedge component is not eliminated by deal optimism, merely reduced by it. The structural gold story — dollar reserve diversification by central banks, inflation-hedge demand at 3.8% PCE — remains intact regardless of the deal's timing. Gold is likely to stay in the $4,400-4,520 range until either a signed deal (sends it toward $4,300-4,350 as inflation expectations fall materially) or a deal collapse (sends it toward $4,540-4,600 as geopolitical premium rebuilds and oil-inflation fears return).
  • The correlation between Snowflake's +36-39% and the broader S&P's +0.58% record is worth examining for what it reveals about index concentration and AI premium pricing. A single stock generating a 36-39% move in a market-cap weighted index requires either very large market cap or disproportionate contagion effects on related stocks. Snowflake entered Thursday at approximately $175 per share with a market cap of approximately $58 billion — not large enough to move the S&P 500 materially on its own (it represents approximately 0.09% of the S&P). The S&P's 0.58% gain reflects two mechanisms beyond Snowflake's direct weight: sector contagion (Snowflake's result lifted data platforms, cloud infrastructure, and AI-adjacent software stocks broadly), and sentiment amplification (a 36% move in an AI-infrastructure stock on the day of April PCE's 3.8% print signals that AI demand growth is strong enough to override macro headwinds, which reprices the entire tech sector's growth premium). The S&P 7,563 is not Snowflake's record — it is the record of the index pricing AI-demand confirmation at a moment when the Iran deal provided the macro tailwind to sustain rather than test those valuations. The combination is what produced records, not either factor alone.
