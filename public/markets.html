WTI at $70.18 recovered from an overnight low as the Hormuz reopening announcement reduced acute supply-disruption fears; the oil market is roughly flat versus Friday's settlement rather than the dramatic spike that a sustained Hormuz blockade would have produced. The week ahead closes with June non-farm payrolls on Thursday July 3 — holiday-shortened ahead of the July 4 weekend — concentrating the year's most FOMC-critical employment data into a single low-liquidity trading day before a long weekend.

The Anchor

S&P ▲0.96%. Nasdaq ▲0.99%. Gold ▲2.12%. WTI flat. Iran stand-down. Q2 end today. NFP Thursday. The relief rally is real — but it lands into a triple rebalancing event and a holiday-shortened NFP week that adds mechanical selling pressure to the tech sector even as geopolitical risk eases.

The Iran stand-down rally has a clean causal logic. Last week's Nasdaq decline had two components: a valuation rotation out of AI/tech as Citi and others downgraded the sector and Microsoft and Meta entered bear market territory; and a geopolitical overlay from the Hormuz crisis, which added oil-inflation and Fed-rate-cut-delay risk on top of the valuation concerns. Monday's stand-down announcement addresses the geopolitical component directly — ships are moving freely through the strait, oil hasn't spiked, and the acute Hormuz supply disruption risk is temporarily off the table. The valuation component of last week's decline is not addressed by a diplomatic stand-down: the structural question of how many companies in the AI/tech space can simultaneously sustain premium valuations when model pricing is collapsing (DeepSeek V4-Pro permanent at $0.435/M input, undercutting GPT-5.5 by 5.7×) and the regulatory environment for AI has become more complex (Anthropic-DOD litigation, OpenAI government vetting) remains the medium-term story for the Nasdaq.

Gold's 2.12% gain from Friday's $3,962 to Monday's $4,046 is the asset class that most cleanly reflects the overnight Iran dynamics. Gold did not rally on Friday's equity selloff — a valuation story — because valuation selloffs in equities don't typically produce gold safe-haven demand. But a geopolitical military exchange at the world's most important energy chokepoint is exactly the scenario that produces gold safe-haven demand. Gold's partial retention of those gains even as equities rally suggests some investors are maintaining geopolitical hedges despite the stand-down, reflecting the underlying uncertainty about IRGC operational independence and whether the Doha talks Tuesday will hold the stand-down or see a resumption of the retaliatory exchange cycle. The 10-year Treasury at 4.41% is down 4 basis points from Friday — a modest flight-to-quality signal that is absorbing some of the same geopolitical risk premium that gold is retaining.

US equity markets opened sharply higher Monday after the United States and Iran announced a mutual stand-down, with both sides halting the retaliatory exchange cycle that ran through the early hours of Sunday and the US confirming that trade vessels can move freely in the Strait of Hormuz. The S&P 500 climbed 0.96% to 7,395, the Nasdaq rose 0.99% to 25,432, the Dow gained 0.28% to 52,120, and the Russell 2000 advanced 0.79% to 3,050. The rally partially reverses last week's tech-led selldown — in which the S&P fell 1.95% and the Nasdaq fell 4.6%, its worst weekly performance since early 2026 — as the geopolitical overlay of the Iran Hormuz crisis is removed from the market's near-term calculus. Bloomberg reported futures climbed Sunday evening on initial reports that peace talks would resume.

The stand-down addresses the Iran-specific components of last week's sell-off but does not address the valuation rotation that drove the decline. The Nasdaq's 4.6% decline was driven by multiple simultaneous forces: the Hormuz ceasefire crisis; a Citi downgrade of the AI/tech sector on the grounds that "it is difficult to see how everyone in the AI/Tech path wins"; the continued pressure of DeepSeek V4-Pro's permanent 5.7× pricing discount versus GPT-5.5; and the Anthropic-DOD litigation, which adds a regulatory complexity premium to AI model vendors. Monday's stand-down removes the first of those four forces. The other three remain in play, which is why the Nasdaq's 0.99% gain is a partial reversal of a 4.6% decline rather than a full recovery. Energy sector equities are the relative beneficiaries of an oil market that has been re-priced modestly higher on Hormuz risk without spiking dramatically, while tech and consumer discretionary retain their valuation headwinds.

Q2 and H1 2026 end today — creating triple portfolio rebalancing flows simultaneously across monthly, quarterly, and semi-annual cycles on the same day that the Iran stand-down produces a relief rally. Tech-heavy institutional portfolios that underperformed during the Nasdaq's 4.6% weekly decline will mechanically sell technology to return to benchmark weighting, adding structural selling pressure to the sector that is also the primary beneficiary of the geopolitical relief rally. The interplay between mechanical rebalancing outflows and stand-down-driven inflows may compress the Nasdaq's opening gains through the afternoon session. June non-farm payrolls arrive Thursday July 3 — a holiday-shortened session ahead of the July 4 weekend — in a single-day window that concentrates the year's most FOMC-critical employment data into a low-liquidity market before a four-day break.

The September FOMC meeting's rate decision now incorporates three significant new inputs relative to the June meeting: the Iran stand-down (which eases the oil inflation risk that had been building since the Hormuz crisis began on Friday), Thursday's NFP data (the first major employment signal after the June dot plot's 9-member rate-hike projection), and the June CPI print arriving mid-July. Early forecasts for Thursday's NFP suggest June jobs growth eased from May's 172,000 figure; a number significantly below 172,000 combined with the Hormuz stand-down's oil price containment would materially improve the September rate-hold argument. If the Doha talks Tuesday maintain the stand-down through mid-July's CPI print, the September FOMC meeting could be the first in the current cycle where the energy inflation argument for a rate hike is genuinely weaker than at the prior meeting. That scenario — soft NFP, contained oil, Hormuz functional — is the basis for Monday's equity rally, priced in before the data arrives.

Gold at $4,046 — up 2.12% from Friday's $3,962 — is the cleanest market expression of the overnight Iran dynamics. The metal spiked during Sunday night's IRGC counter-strikes against US-linked targets, attracting safe-haven demand from investors who correctly identified the Hormuz military exchange as a geopolitical risk event, not a valuation event — the distinction that explains why gold did not rally on Friday's tech selloff but did spike on Sunday's escalation. The partial retention of the $84 safe-haven premium into Monday's stand-down-driven equity rally reflects residual uncertainty: the stand-down is between governments, the IRGC has demonstrated operational independence from Iran's diplomatic negotiators, and the Doha talks Tuesday are the first test of whether the stand-down holds when the two sides are actually in the same room.

WTI at $70.18 tells a more restrained story: the oil market spiked overnight on the Hormuz crisis but recovered on the stand-down news, leaving crude roughly flat versus Friday's $70.34 settlement. The symmetry of the oil move — spike and recovery within 36 hours — reflects markets' assessment that the Hormuz stand-down is credible enough to prevent the supply disruption that would have required a sustained oil risk premium. If the Doha talks hold, WTI could fall back toward $69–70 as the last of the Hormuz risk premium deflates. If the talks break down and the IRGC resumes attacks, WTI would re-price upward from the $70 base, adding back the inflation risk that Monday's stand-down rally has removed. The 10-year Treasury at 4.41% — down 4 basis points from Friday's 4.45% — is absorbing a modest flight-to-quality bid even as equities rally, suggesting markets are pricing some probability that the stand-down does not hold.

Quick hits

  • The Dow's outperformance relative to the Nasdaq — ▲0.28% versus ▲0.99% today — appears contradictory at first: if the stand-down is the catalyst, why does the more geopolitically sensitive energy-heavy Dow underperform the tech-heavy Nasdaq? The answer is that the Nasdaq's 4.6% decline last week was disproportionately tech-driven: the relief from the stand-down disproportionately benefits what was disproportionately sold. The Dow, which includes Boeing, Caterpillar, and energy companies, held up better last week precisely because its sector composition is less AI-tech-concentrated. A small move in a less-damaged index versus a larger move in a more-damaged one is consistent with the same catalyst: the stand-down reverses the Hormuz-specific selling in tech portfolios, not the defensive-value reallocation that held the Dow steady last week.
  • June non-farm payrolls on Thursday create a structural risk management problem for institutional investors. A weak print — suggesting the labor market is softening enough to credibly shift the Fed toward a September rate hold — would normally produce an equity rally. But a rally on Thursday arrives into a Friday July 4 holiday, meaning institutions cannot reposition at scale before a four-day weekend. If the data surprises strongly on either side, the initial price move will be exaggerated by low liquidity and then may partially reverse when full liquidity returns on July 7. Plan accordingly: Thursday's NFP session is not the final word on how markets interpret the data.
