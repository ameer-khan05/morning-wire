Brent crude hit $98.44 (▲3.11%) and WTI $88.17 (▲3.24%) Thursday as Iran struck US military bases in Jordan and Bahrain — the first expansion of Iranian targeting to US installations outside Iran's borders — and Russia's role helping Iran plan strikes on CIA facilities was publicly confirmed. Oil is within $1.56 of the $100 threshold that historically triggers a qualitative shift in the inflation-CPI pass-through signal, and the dual-theater Gulf disruption (Hormuz conflict + Houthi Saudi port blockade) means full normalization requires two independent diplomatic resolutions.

The $100 Brent threshold matters not just as a price level but as a political economy trigger. Below $100, gasoline prices in the US stay roughly below $5 per gallon nationally — an elevated but politically manageable level. Above $100, national average gasoline approaches levels that drove consumer sentiment to historic lows in summer 2022 and prompted the largest US Strategic Petroleum Reserve release in history. The political pressure on the White House to act — through accelerated ceasefire diplomacy or SPR release — increases sharply above $100. The Iran escalation to Jordan and Bahrain targeting raises the question of whether the US responds symmetrically, which would escalate the conflict further, or accepts the hits and presses for the ceasefire, which could signal willingness to accept Iran's terms. That choice is the variable that sets the $100 trajectory in the next 48 hours.

Gold declined to $4,085 (▼0.80%) as some geopolitical premium was taken off after Wednesday's strong close despite Iran's escalation to Jordan and Bahrain targets. The Treasury yield path from 4.63% (Wednesday) to 4.68% (Thursday) is the rates market processing the $100 Brent threshold as a qualitative signal, not just a quantitative price move. Below $100, the inflation channel from oil is real but bounded: the Fed can wait for labor market and core PCE data before adjusting the path. At $100+, the CPI signal from energy becomes unambiguous enough that the September meeting pricing shifts toward lean-hike from lean-hold. The July 29 FOMC meeting — eight days away — will provide Fed Chair Powell's first public framing of the oil-inflation dynamic in the context of Iran. His language about September will be the rates market's most important signal before the July CPI print in mid-August. Gold's decline into Iran escalation is unusual — typically geopolitical escalation drives gold up. The ▼0.80% suggests that the inflation-hedge demand and geopolitical-premium demand that had been running together in gold are partially separating: some geopolitical premium unwound as the $100 Brent inflation signal became the dominant frame.
