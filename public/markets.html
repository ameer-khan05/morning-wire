Markets · Deep Dive

The S&P 500 held near Monday's Iran-deal gains at 7,552 (+0.13%) Tuesday as markets paused ahead of the Federal Reserve meeting and digested a sharp miss in May housing starts — down 15.4% to 1.177 million annualized, the largest single-month miss in years. WTI crude extended its post-deal decline to $78.66 as the confirmed MOU's sanctions relief on Iranian oil expanded the supply picture. The Dow gained 0.78% to 52,220 while Nasdaq barely moved.

Tuesday's session had two stories running simultaneously. The first is consolidation: markets held Monday's Iran-deal gains without extending them, which is normal behavior ahead of a Federal Reserve meeting — investors rarely take large directional bets while the Fed is in session, preferring to wait for rate decision confirmation before repricing positions. The S&P at 7,552 is 28 points from its May 31 all-time record of 7,580; getting from here to there requires either the Fed meeting to go as expected (hold) or the June 19 Geneva signing to provide the next risk-on catalyst. The second story is housing: the 15.4% collapse in May housing starts to 1.177 million annualized is the largest single-month miss in recent memory and confirms that the prolonged high-rate environment is still actively suppressing new residential construction even as the rate-cut repricing begins. The housing miss is a lagging signal — it reflects borrowing conditions from 60-90 days ago, before the Iran deal reduced oil and began the rate repricing — but it establishes a lower baseline from which the eventual rate-cut stimulus will need to work. Lower rates improving housing affordability into a suppressed starts environment should produce a sharper and more sustained construction rebound than rate-cut stimulation into a higher-baseline market would.

The Anchor

S&P 7,552. WTI $78.66. Housing starts crashed 15.4%. The Fed is meeting. Markets are in a holding pattern — not because the Iran deal is in doubt, but because the Fed meeting is in session and no one takes a big bet into a rate decision.

The S&P at 7,552 is 28 points from its May 31 all-time record of 7,580 — a gap that would take roughly a quarter-session of Monday's pace to close. It has not closed Tuesday because markets are waiting for two things: the Federal Reserve's rate decision and the June 19 Geneva signing. The Fed is widely expected to hold rates — the oil-driven CPI passthrough that would justify a cut has not yet appeared in data — but the language of the Fed's statement and Chair Powell's press conference will be parsed for signals about the July-September cut path. A statement that acknowledges the Iran deal's inflation implications and signals increased confidence in the cut path would be the second catalyst the S&P needs to reach 7,580. The Geneva signing on Thursday is the third.

The housing start miss is the economic story of Tuesday's session. May housing starts at 1.177 million annualized — versus the 1.43 million economists expected — confirm what the rate environment has been doing to residential construction for months: high mortgage rates have suppressed new home building to levels that will eventually produce a supply shortage even as demand softens. The 15.4% single-month decline is the largest in recent data and suggests the construction financing environment deteriorated sharply in May, before the Iran deal's rate-cut repricing began. The silver lining is structural: a deeply suppressed starts baseline means the housing recovery from rate cuts will be sharper and more durable than it would be from a high baseline, because a genuine supply shortage will meet pent-up demand when affordability improves.

Once the Fed confirms a hold with hawkish-dovish language that acknowledges the Iran deal's oil implications, and once the Geneva signing converts the Truth Social announcement into a signed treaty, the macro environment will have resolved both of the outstanding variables that markets have been pricing as risk. The S&P at 7,580 is the baseline scenario for the week; the question is which catalyst — Fed language or Geneva signing — gets there first.

US housing starts fell 15.4% in May to an annualized pace of 1.177 million — far below economists' 1.43 million expectation and the largest single-month miss in years — confirming that the prolonged high-rate environment is still actively suppressing new residential construction even as the Iran deal begins the rate-cut repricing. The May data reflects borrowing conditions from 60-90 days earlier, before WTI crashed to $78.66 and UST 10Y began declining from 4.47%. The housing sector has not yet benefited from any of the rate improvement the Iran deal has triggered; the construction suppression measured in May's data will eventually become a supply shortage as demand recovers when rates fall.

The housing start miss is the strongest economic argument for the Fed to signal a clear July-September cut path at its current meeting. The residential construction sector — which leads economic cycles and provides significant employment — is operating at levels that will produce a supply shortage in 12-18 months if construction does not recover. A clear Fed cut signal lowers mortgage rates (which follow the 10Y with a spread) and restores construction financing to levels that allow builders to start new projects. The Iran deal's oil-to-CPI-to-rate-cut chain provides the data justification the Fed needs to act — but the housing start data establishes the urgency that the Fed's July meeting is not too early.

WTI crude fell to $78.66 Tuesday — continuing lower from Monday's $79.46 post-deal level — as the confirmed Iran MOU's sanctions relief on Iranian oil exports expanded the supply picture beyond the Hormuz reopening alone. Brent fell 2.40% to $81.17. The two-pronged supply increase now in play — Hormuz shipping resuming after mine clearance and Iranian oil exports resuming under a sanctions waiver — gives the oil price path from $78.66 more downside momentum toward $75-76 over the coming weeks than the Hormuz-only scenario implied. Each dollar of WTI decline from pre-deal levels translates to approximately 0.04% of CPI relief from the oil channel; from $84 to $78, that's roughly 0.24% of direct CPI benefit before indirect effects.

The European Hormuz mission — Germany, France, Britain, and Italy pledging a "purely defensive" mine-clearance and escort operation — adds logistics support that accelerates the physical reopening timeline. Mine clearance is the rate-limiting step between deal announcement and oil flow resumption; European naval assets joining the US-supervised clearance operation should compress the mine-removal timeline from months to weeks. The faster Hormuz physically opens to tanker traffic, the faster the supply surge appears in WTI price action, and the faster the CPI passthrough materializes in headline inflation data that the Fed can cite for its rate-cut decision.

The Federal Reserve meeting is the primary market focus Tuesday as investors hold positions rather than extend Monday's Iran-deal gains into an uncertain rate decision. The rate decision itself is not in dispute — consensus is firmly for a hold at the current level — but the language of the statement and Powell's press conference will signal the timing of the first cut. A dovish statement that acknowledges the Iran deal's oil price implications, references declining inflation expectations from WTI's decline, and cites the housing start miss as evidence that the economy needs rate relief would signal a July cut and produce the catalyst that closes the 28-point gap from 7,552 to the S&P's all-time record of 7,580.

The S&P has a two-catalyst path to a record this week: the Fed meeting (today/tomorrow) and the Geneva signing (Thursday). If the Fed's statement acknowledges the oil-to-CPI-to-rate-cut chain and signals July as the likely cut window, the S&P will likely attempt the record before the signing. If the Fed is more cautious — citing remaining core PCE stickiness despite the oil decline — the June 19 Geneva signing becomes the primary catalyst for the record attempt. Either path lands the S&P at or above 7,580 before week-end. The scenario where both the Fed and Geneva disappoint simultaneously — Fed sticks to September and deal signing is delayed — is the tail risk that keeps some investors in a holding pattern rather than extending positions on Tuesday.

Quick hits

  • The Dow's 0.78% outperformance versus the S&P's 0.13% and Nasdaq's 0.02% on Tuesday is structurally different from Monday's pattern, where Nasdaq led the Iran-deal rally. Tuesday's Dow outperformance reflects the shift from initial risk-on (Monday, when growth and tech lead) to deal-durability pricing (Tuesday, when industrials and cyclicals that benefit from actual Hormuz shipping resumption, European military engagement, and an improving macro backdrop lead). The Dow's industrial composition — shipping, defense, energy infrastructure, heavy equipment — benefits from a confirmed Hormuz mission and the operational military engagement of European NATO allies, which reduces the risk that mine clearance stalls or that the deal collapses before physical oil flow resumes.
  • Gold at $4,064 (-0.27%) is declining at a rate that is more gradual than historical geopolitical resolution events typically produce. Gold has fallen only 0.27% on Tuesday versus its $4,075 Monday close — suggesting that a meaningful share of gold holders are not treating the Iran deal confirmation as complete enough to exit hedges. The European Hormuz mission adds certainty to the physical reopening, but the 60-day nuclear negotiation window — and Israel's refusal to withdraw from Lebanon — creates enough residual geopolitical uncertainty that the structural gold bid from risk-aware investors and central banks persists. Gold at $4,064 is pricing a deal that is real but not fully settled. That is an accurate assessment.
  • The UST 10Y's modest 1-basis-point decline to 4.33% on Tuesday reflects the Fed meeting constraint: bond markets do not move aggressively ahead of a rate decision they have not yet received. The 4.33% level implies that markets are pricing approximately 80-85% probability of a hold today and a 60-70% probability of a July cut, with August and September as fallback scenarios. Once the Fed decision is announced and Powell's press conference signals the cut path, the 10Y will reprice more decisively — toward 4.10-4.20% if July is confirmed, toward 4.25-4.30% if September is signaled. The housing start miss adds pressure for the faster path; the oil-decline-to-CPI sequence provides the data justification for Powell to make the case.
