US equity futures pointed modestly higher Wednesday — S&P 500 ▲0.20%, Nasdaq ▲0.49%, Dow ▲0.09%, Russell 2000 ▲0.16% — as investors positioned ahead of Micron's fiscal Q3 earnings after the closing bell and the Iran ceasefire declaration provided mild geopolitical risk relief. Micron bounced 4.1% in Wednesday premarket after Tuesday's 13% decline, as investors returned to AI memory positions ahead of results that analysts project will show 932% year-over-year EPS growth. The recovery is deliberate rather than emphatic: S&P ▲0.20% recovers only a fraction of Tuesday's ▼1.44% loss, consistent with a market waiting for data rather than one that has decided the selloff was overdone.

The character of Wednesday's early session is informative. A confident AI demand thesis recovery would have produced a sharper Nasdaq bounce — the kind of immediate reversal seen after earnings-driven overshoots. Wednesday's measured advance instead reads as professional repositioning: traders who shorted or reduced on Tuesday's rout are re-entering positions they believe will be validated by Micron's results, but are not pre-committing to a magnitude that the earnings themselves haven't yet justified. The distinction matters for reading tonight's post-earnings reaction: a strong Micron report into a lightly-positioned tape typically produces a larger upside move than the same report into a fully-recovered setup.

Micron Technology's fiscal Q3 2026 results arrive after tonight's close with a remarkably concentrated expectation: 932% year-over-year EPS growth, $35.5 billion in revenue, and a narrative of AI memory demand so strong the company has stated its high-bandwidth memory capacity is sold out through the end of 2026. Micron's stock is down 13% from Tuesday's levels but still up 268% year-to-date before the selloff — a valuation that has embedded a specific AI demand assumption. Tonight's results will test whether that assumption holds at the number that has been priced in. The single most important disclosure will not be Q3 results themselves — those are already embedded in consensus — but Q4 guidance and any HBM order visibility commentary beyond December 2026.

The market's question for tonight is not "did Micron grow?" but "is the growth durable?" At 932% EPS growth for Q3, the base comparisons are now difficult: the easy money year-over-year gains from the AI memory ramp have been captured, and the next leg of growth requires HBM demand to extend into 2027 at the same rate or higher. Micron has previously indicated its HBM3E capacity for 2027 is already substantially committed to customers. Whether tonight's call confirms that commitment — and whether those customers (Nvidia, AMD, hyperscalers) are showing signs of pulling forward or pushing back orders — is the specific language investors will parse for directional signal. The chip selloff will end or deepen on those words.

WTI crude extended its post-sanctions-waiver decline Wednesday, falling a further 2.25% to $72.06 — the lowest level of 2026 — as Iranian crude continued entering global supply chains following Tuesday's 60-day sanctions waiver that covered not just oil production but the full trade support chain of banking, shipping, and maritime insurance. Brent dropped to $75.86 (▼1.35%). The two-session cumulative decline from pre-waiver levels above $76.90 to $72.06 represents a 6.3% oil price drop in 48 hours, driven by market participants pricing both the additional Iranian volume and the simplified legal pathway for buyers who had previously used evasion channels that limited volume and added cost.

The magnitude of the oil price decline has direct macroeconomic implications for the FOMC's September meeting calculus. From the pre-Iran-deal WTI baseline of approximately $84 to Wednesday's $72.06 represents a 14.2% oil price decline that will be partially but substantially captured in June's CPI energy component (gasoline prices and utility costs). June CPI data (published mid-July) will be the FOMC's first concrete reading of the oil-to-inflation transmission from the Iran deal. With nine FOMC members projecting a September rate hike in June's dot plot — a projection made with oil near $84 — the $72.06 current level significantly weakens the inflation case that justified those projections. Watch for FOMC communication in late July and August for the first signals that the oil data is shifting individual member views.

Quick hits

  • The Micron premarket bounce of 4.1% after a 13% Tuesday decline sets an interesting technical context for tonight's earnings. If Micron's results beat consensus and guidance is strong, the post-earnings gap-up will add to the premarket recovery and create a 2-day reversal that erases most of Tuesday's loss. If results are in-line but guidance is cautious, the stock may give back the premarket gain and revisit Tuesday's lows. The asymmetric risk tonight is slightly downward: a strong beat that matches consensus expectations may already be partially priced into the premarket recovery, while a guidance miss would hit a tape that has already partially re-entered AI memory positions.
  • WTI's two-session, 6.3% decline — from pre-waiver $76.90+ to $72.06 — is fast by oil market standards and reflects the sanctions waiver's unusual legal clarity. Previous Iranian oil re-entry events (partial waivers, country-specific exemptions) produced smaller, slower oil price reactions because buyers faced legal uncertainty about which specific transactions were covered. The Treasury's 60-day waiver covering the full supply chain — oil, banking, shipping, insurance — eliminated the legal ambiguity that previously limited buyer participation. The result is a faster, larger price discovery process than the market has seen from Iranian supply events historically. If WTI stabilizes near $72–73 through June, the monthly average will be well below the June baseline that fed last year's elevated CPI.
