The Anchor

Hormuz escalation sends Brent above $100 — equities snap their streak as IBM and ServiceNow collapse on earnings.

Thursday was a bad day for bulls. Iran's Revolutionary Guard seized two vessels in the Strait of Hormuz and fired on a third — a direct escalation that sent WTI crude to $94.46 (+1.67%) and pushed Brent above $103, crossing $100 for the first time since this conflict began. Investors who had been building on two weeks of risk appetite needed no further prompt to take profit. The Nasdaq Composite fell 0.89% to 24,438.50. The S&P 500 dropped 0.41% to 7,108.40. The Dow shed 179 points to 49,310.32. The VIX — which had been falling for two consecutive sessions — surged 18.84% to 16.40. The 10-year Treasury yield edged up 6 basis points to 4.42% as elevated oil prices fed inflation expectations, muting the safe-haven bid that had kept yields lower earlier in the week. The dollar index climbed 0.73% to 100.18.

The earnings picture on Thursday did not help. IBM beat its Q1 consensus on both the top and bottom lines — but management maintained full-year guidance rather than raising it, disappointing an investor base that had priced in an upgrade. The stock fell more than 8%. ServiceNow was worse: shares cratered nearly 18% after the company reported that subscription revenue growth was hindered by the Middle East conflict, a direct and unusual attribution of macro-conflict headwinds to enterprise software sales. With Alphabet, Meta, and Microsoft all reporting April 29 and Amazon following April 30, the earnings week ahead will carry outsized weight — combined, these four companies have guided more than $350 billion in AI capital expenditure for 2026. Every transcript will be searched for the same signal: is the spending producing revenue? Friday morning pre-market points mildly higher as Trump's Israel–Lebanon ceasefire extension provides some relief, but the Hormuz premium in oil is not going away while the blockade holds.

Nasdaq falls 0.89%, S&P drops 0.41% as Iran's Hormuz escalation ends the multi-day rally.

The Nasdaq Composite declined 0.89% to 24,438.50, the S&P 500 fell 0.41% to 7,108.40, and the Dow shed 0.36% to 49,310.32 Thursday — the first meaningful pullback of the week. Defensive sectors held up: utilities gained 2.80% and industrials added 1.75%, a classic risk-off rotation. Technology and software led the declines, with the VIX surging 18.84% to 16.40. Oil · Hormuz $103.38 Brent · first $100+ close of conflict

Brent crosses $100 for the first time — Iran's Hormuz vessel seizures escalate the supply-risk premium.

Brent crude closed at $103.38 on Thursday, its first triple-digit settlement since the US-Iran conflict began — with WTI at $94.46. The catalyst: Iran's Revolutionary Guard seized the MSC Francesca and Epaminondas and fired on a third container ship in the Strait of Hormuz, a direct escalation that markets interpreted as a meaningful increase in supply disruption risk. Oil analysts estimate that WTI could retrace to $83–85 if a full ceasefire with blockade removal is agreed — a scenario that is not currently on the table. The Hormuz premium is structural until the ships move.

ECB · April 30 −25 bps? Rate decision · oil inflation signal

ECB decision April 30 — 25 bps cut expected, but Brent above $100 complicates Lagarde's communication.

The European Central Bank holds its next rate decision on April 30, the same day as Amazon's Q1 earnings. Markets are pricing a 25 basis point cut — the ECB last moved in March. The complication: Brent crude above $100 is keeping headline inflation elevated, a supply-side shock that is largely outside monetary policy's reach. ECB President Lagarde faces a communication challenge: cutting rates while oil drives headline inflation higher risks appearing inconsistent. Analysts expect a cut accompanied by guidance that explicitly acknowledges the temporary nature of the oil-driven inflation component.

Bonds · Gold · DXY 4.42% UST 10Y · dollar up · gold holds

10-year yield rises 6 bps to 4.42%; dollar climbs 0.73% as oil-driven inflation fears lift rates.

The 10-year Treasury yield rose 6 basis points to 4.42% Thursday as Brent crossing $100 stoked inflation expectations and reduced appetite for rate-sensitive assets. The dollar index rose 0.73% to 100.18 — the biggest single-day DXY gain of the week — as higher-for-longer rate expectations and energy market anxiety drove flows back to the greenback. Gold held at $4,756, up 0.77% Friday morning, maintaining its safe-haven bid even as the broader risk-off was less severe than during last week's conflict peak. The gold/oil ratio is compressing, a pattern that historically signals continued oil strength or gold weakness ahead.
