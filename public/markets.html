The Anchor

Tuesday's OpenAI miss and UAE-OPEC shock give back the record — Wednesday resolves everything in a single session.

Tuesday ended with the S&P 500 down 0.49% to 7,138.80 — its first close below 7,150 since last Thursday — and the Nasdaq Composite down 0.90% to 24,663.80, with the sharpest intraday chip stock moves of the month. The trigger was dual: the Wall Street Journal's OpenAI miss report, published Tuesday morning, sent AI infrastructure stocks broadly lower as investors began questioning the demand assumption behind $650 billion in Big Tech AI capex; and the UAE's OPEC exit announcement, published Tuesday afternoon, pushed Brent to $114.64 — its highest since the Hormuz closure began — and introduced a new structural energy market uncertainty on top of the existing Iran-driven premium. The VIX surged 12.3% to 18.20, still historically contained but the largest single-day volatility spike of April.

Wednesday's session begins with S&P 500 and Dow futures hovering near flat and Nasdaq 100 futures up approximately 0.3% — a muted recovery attempt that could evaporate or accelerate entirely depending on the FOMC statement, Big Tech earnings commentary, and any further Iran/UAE oil developments before markets close. The specific event sequence: Jerome Powell announces the FOMC rate decision mid-afternoon (hold expected at 4.25-4.50%), with a press conference that constitutes his likely final public act as Fed chair; the Senate Banking Committee simultaneously advances Kevin Warsh's nomination 13-11; and Alphabet, Meta, Microsoft, and Amazon all report after the close, with the $650 billion AI capex accountability session that Tuesday's OpenAI report transformed from a formality into a test. The VIX's 12.3% move on Tuesday reflects options markets correctly pricing that Wednesday's event sequence will produce the most directional single-session move of April — in one direction or the other.

OpenAI Miss · Chip Stocks ▼ 0.90% Nasdaq largest drop since records · NVDA · AMD · Broadcom −3-4% · Oracle −4%

OpenAI miss report sends Nasdaq down 0.90% — chip stocks fall 3-4% in the largest single-session AI infrastructure selloff of April.

The Wall Street Journal's report that OpenAI missed internal user and revenue targets produced the largest single-session AI infrastructure selloff since early March. Nvidia, AMD, and Broadcom fell 3-4% each; Oracle — which has a $300 billion, five-year compute partnership with OpenAI — fell 4%. The cascade reflects OpenAI's central position in the AI demand chain: every chip, data center, and cloud unit that has been sold on the basis of AI compute demand has OpenAI's user and revenue trajectory as its implicit demand proof. The specific mechanism of the selloff: if OpenAI is missing its own user growth targets, its compute consumption growth will be below its infrastructure budget, which means Oracle's data center revenue, Nvidia's chip demand, and Broadcom's networking revenue will all grow more slowly than the supply side assumed. The market translated that inference into 3-4% single-day moves. The broader Nasdaq's 0.90% decline — the sharpest since the Monday record — reflects the spillover from AI infrastructure stocks into the broader tech index where their market cap weighting is substantial.

UAE · OPEC · Brent $114 $114.64 ▲ 3.04% · 57 years ends May 1 · WTI-Brent spread $15 · Day 61 Iran

UAE announces OPEC exit effective May 1 — Brent hits $114.64, Brent-WTI spread widens to $15 as Gulf oil coalition fractures.

The UAE's announcement Tuesday that it will leave OPEC and OPEC+ effective May 1 — ending nearly 57 years of membership — pushed Brent crude to $114.64 (▲ 3.04%), its highest level since the Hormuz closure began. The departure is the most significant structural change to the global oil producer coalition since OPEC's founding: the UAE is one of OPEC's largest producers, with approximately 4 million barrels per day of capacity, and its exit removes a production-discipline participant from the group at the moment when Saudi Arabia needs maximum cohesion to navigate the Iran conflict's supply disruption. The Brent-WTI spread widened to approximately $15 per barrel — historically extreme versus the normal $3-5 range. The spread reflects the differential impact of Hormuz: Brent prices waterborne international crude, directly affected by Middle East supply disruption; WTI prices US domestic crude, insulated by record domestic production. The spread divergence is itself a geopolitical data point: the wider it gets, the more Hormuz closure is being priced as structural rather than transient.

FOMC · Powell Final · Hold 4.25-4.50% Powell's last meeting · Brent $114 rules out cut · Warsh incoming · June path

Powell announces likely final rate decision today — hold at 4.25-4.50% as $114 Brent makes a cut impossible.

The Federal Reserve announces its rate decision today in what is expected to be Jerome Powell's final monetary policy action as chair. The outcome — a hold at 4.25-4.50% — is not in question; Brent at $114.64 following UAE's OPEC exit makes any cut mathematically indefensible. What matters is the press conference: Powell's characterization of the UAE-OPEC development as a supply shock versus a structural oil-market change, his framing of the April PCE data coming Friday, and any commentary about the economic implications of the Iran stalemate entering Day 61 will collectively set the baseline from which Warsh's incoming rate path must diverge or confirm. For markets, the FOMC hold today is already priced — what is NOT priced is whether Powell's final statement is dovish (rate cuts remain on the table for H2 2026) or hawkish (oil at $114 means more work to do). A hawkish parting statement from Powell would hit equities and bonds simultaneously and amplify the Big Tech earnings volatility expected tonight.

Alphabet, Meta, Microsoft, Amazon all report tonight — the OpenAI miss has made $650B in AI capex a live question, not a formality.

Tonight's Big Tech earnings were always the week's defining event; Tuesday's OpenAI miss report transformed them from an expected beat-and-raise slate into a genuine demand stress test. Each company's Cloud growth number will be read as evidence for or against the proposition that AI demand is intact: Alphabet's Google Cloud at ~50% consensus, Microsoft's Azure at 37-38%, Amazon's AWS accelerating past 24%. Meta's AI advertising revenue commentary will determine whether AI monetization at the application layer is progressing at the pace the $115-135 billion 2026 CapEx budget assumed. A scenario where all four beat consensus tonight and raise guidance would represent the strongest possible rebuttal to the OpenAI miss — it would say OpenAI's demand problem is company-specific, not industry-wide, and that the infrastructure layer is being consumed at or above the rate the capex cycle assumed. A scenario where any two miss is harder to contain as company-specific.

Warsh · Senate Banking · Today 13-11 Committee advances today · full Senate next week · Powell term ends May 15

Senate Banking Committee advances Warsh 13-11 today — full Senate confirmation expected before Powell's May 15 term end.

The Senate Banking Committee votes Wednesday to advance Kevin Warsh's nomination as Federal Reserve chair on a 13-11 party-line vote — the final committee hurdle before full Senate consideration. The full Senate is expected to take up the nomination next week, well ahead of Jerome Powell's May 15 term end. For markets, the Warsh transition matters less for what changes in May (the FOMC will likely hold at its next meeting under any chair) than for what changes in June and September: Warsh's first fully owned dot-plot submissions will signal whether his rate path is more hawkish or dovish than Powell's terminal. Given Brent at $114, UAE's OPEC exit effective May 1, and the April PCE printing Friday under elevated oil conditions, Warsh's first statement on the economic outlook will be one of the most closely parsed Fed communications of the cycle. He vowed at confirmation hearings not to be Trump's "sock puppet" on rates — markets will test that commitment before June.

Quick hits

  • The VIX's 12.3% single-day surge to 18.20 on Tuesday is the largest volatility spike of the month and reflects the concentrated event risk of Wednesday's session. A VIX at 18 is still historically subdued — below the 20 threshold that typically signals genuine fear — but the speed of the move (from ~16 to ~18 in a single session) is more relevant than the absolute level. Options markets are pricing a ~1.5-2% S&P 500 move on Wednesday's close, which represents approximately $400-$530 billion in market cap at stake on a single session's worth of earnings and Fed data.
  • The UAE-OPEC exit's immediate oil impact (Brent ▲ 3.04% to $114.64) reflects the market pricing two things simultaneously: the removal of a production-discipline participant (who might have been expected to eventually agree to output cuts that lower Brent) and the signal that Gulf unity is fracturing under the Iran war's pressure (which raises the geopolitical risk premium on all Middle East supply). The two effects are additive, which explains a 3% move on news of a departure that doesn't immediately change physical supply.
  • Gold fell 1.20% to $4,626 despite oil surging and geopolitical risk rising — continuing its recent pattern of declining as the dollar strengthens on risk-off flows. The DXY rose 0.37% to 99.82; the gold-dollar inverse correlation is reasserting itself at higher dollar levels. For gold to resume its upward trend, the dollar needs to weaken or real yields need to fall — neither of which is likely given Brent at $114 and the FOMC holding today.
  • The 10-year Treasury yield's modest 2 bps rise to 4.36% understates the implied market stress from Tuesday's session: the 10Y didn't spike because risk-off flows into Treasuries offset the oil-driven inflation repricing. The two forces nearly canceled out, producing a small net yield increase. If tonight's earnings beat and the FOMC statement is dovish, the inflation fear subsides and yields could rally. If earnings disappoint and oil stays at $114+, the stagflation signal strengthens and yields may spike more aggressively.
  • Alphabet's potential first $100 billion+ quarterly revenue print tonight would be a historically significant milestone — the largest quarterly revenue ever recorded by any advertising company, period. That milestone arrives while Google is simultaneously investing $179 billion in 2026 capex, maintaining its $40 billion Anthropic investment, and competing to host enterprises that previously ran on OpenAI. The $100B print, if it lands, would be the clearest single data point available to rebut the OpenAI miss's implied demand-softness thesis.
  • The sequence of macro events from today through Friday — FOMC today, ECB Thursday, PCE Friday — represents the densest monetary policy information release in a three-day window since the 2024 banking crisis. The PCE print on Friday will be the first to fully capture a month (April) in which Brent averaged above $100 throughout. If April core PCE surprises above the 2.6% consensus estimate, it will hit a market that has just absorbed four Big Tech earnings reports, a new Fed chair in waiting, and a structurally changed OPEC landscape. The sequencing of these catalysts is maximally challenging for risk assets.
