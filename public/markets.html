US equities closed higher Friday — S&P 500 ▲0.42% to 7,575.39, Nasdaq ▲0.29% to 26,281.61, Dow ▲0.28% to 52,637 — as SK Hynix's 13% Nasdaq debut close validated the AI hardware trade and confirmed the week's verdict on AI memory demand, while the Russell 2000 declined 0.49% to 2,977.81, diverging from the large-cap rally in a small-cap move analysts attributed to defensive repositioning ahead of an earnings season beginning next week.

Friday's final session resolved the week's central open question — does the AI hardware trade survive Samsung's paradox? — with the clearest possible answer: SK Hynix's 13% IPO gain held through the full first trading day, the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index closed higher, and AI names led the Nasdaq to its third positive session in the last four. Brent crude's further $0.69 decline to $76.11 (▼0.90%) and gold's partial recovery to $4,129 (▲0.44%) are the commodity and safe-haven markets processing the Iran de-escalation signal across a second session, with the oil war premium continuing its partial unwind while gold partially recovered safe-haven bids.

The S&P 500's 0.42% gain to 7,575.39 and the Nasdaq's 0.29% gain to 26,281.61 are modest on a Friday, but they complete a weekly pattern in which the AI and chip trade has outperformed the broader market in every direction: when Iran escalated, AI names held better; when Iran de-escalated, AI names led the recovery; when SK Hynix IPO'd, AI hardware stocks surged 3.06% in the semiconductor index. The Russell 2000's -0.49% underperformance on a broadly up day is the signal that deserves the most analytical weight entering earnings season. The Q2 earnings season beginning next week will test whether the AI-large-cap premium can sustain in the face of the broader market's divergence — or whether the gap closes as earnings reports provide a more fundamental basis for evaluation than narrative premiums.

The Russell 2000's 0.49% decline to 2,977.81 on a Friday when the S&P 500 gained 0.42% and the Nasdaq gained 0.29% is the market's most informative divergence of the week — not a dramatic move, but a directional signal that the equity rally's health is concentrated in large-cap AI-adjacent names rather than distributed across the market. Small-cap companies disproportionately finance via floating-rate instruments where the 10-year yield's 4.56% level is more immediately punitive; they face thinner margins where oil's still-elevated $76.11 Brent price creates ongoing input-cost headwind; and they carry earnings quality questions that the AI hardware or AI software premium narrative does not address. The Russell 2000's defensive repositioning entering a week of Q2 earnings reports is the market pricing those structural disadvantages before they appear in print.

The large-cap versus small-cap gap that widened on Friday will be tested by earnings season more directly than by any Fed communication or geopolitical signal. When Q2 earnings reports begin arriving next week, the gap's sustainability depends on two questions: first, whether large-cap AI-adjacent companies report the revenue and guidance that justify their current AI-premium multiples; and second, whether small-cap companies report earnings quality that is better than the defensive positioning implies. If large-cap AI earnings disappoint at current valuations, the multiple compression will be severe. If small-cap earnings beat low expectations, the gap closes from below. Friday's divergence sets the baseline from which earnings season re-evaluates both sides.

Brent crude fell a further $0.69 to $76.11 (▼0.90%) on Friday as the Iran de-escalation continued its partial unwind of the war premium that had pushed oil from pre-conflict levels near $69 to Thursday's $76.80 — WTI declining similarly to $71.20 (▼1.38%) — while gold made a partial recovery to $4,129 (▲0.44%), reversing some of Thursday's $52 safe-haven compression as the US government's hardened Iran posture (demanding formal public confirmation of Hormuz openness and guarantees against ship attacks) reintroduced a residual geopolitical bid into the precious metals market; the 10-year Treasury yield rose 2 basis points to 4.56%, a mild risk-on yield normalization consistent with Friday's equity rally rather than the inflation-concern-driven 7-basis-point rise that Thursday's Iran escalation had produced.

The commodity and safe-haven markets are now in a partial de-escalation pricing mode that will hold until either the US gets Iran's formal Hormuz guarantee (further oil decline, gold decline) or the Iran process stalls (oil recovers toward $79, gold recovers toward $4,160). Brent at $76.11 is below the $79 threshold where sustained crude materially affects CPI expectations on the Fed's inflation-evaluation lag — which partially restores the September rate-cut probability that the $79 Brent level had complicated Thursday. But $76.11 is still $7.11 above pre-conflict levels, meaning the full inflation-channel relief is not yet in hand. The week's oil market behavior — from $69 to $79.10 through the conflict escalation, then from $79.10 to $76.11 through the de-escalation — is the fastest oil war-premium accumulation and partial unwind the market has seen in a single week since the February airstrikes.
