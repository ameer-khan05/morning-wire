The S&P 500 closed at a new record of 7,580.06 (+0.22%) and the Nasdaq at 26,972.62 (+0.20%) on Friday — the S&P's ninth consecutive week of gains — while the Dow crossed 51,000 for the first time at 51,032.46 (+0.72%), capping May with the Nasdaq's 8% monthly advance. Friday's record closes capped a week in which the AI infrastructure earnings trifecta — Nvidia (chip layer, Tuesday), Snowflake (data platform, Thursday), Dell (enterprise hardware, Thursday evening/Friday) — confirmed that the AI buildout is real at every layer of the supply chain, growing at rates ranging from 33% (Snowflake) to 757% (Dell AI servers) to 85% (Nvidia data center), and that the enterprise procurement cycle is accelerating rather than plateauing. The S&P's ninth consecutive weekly gain was driven by this confirmation, partially offset by Trump's Friday Situation Room meeting that produced new Iran demands rather than a deal signature — keeping the Russell 2000 in negative territory for the day as the rate-hike probability failed to decline further. The May 2026 market scorecard is among the strongest monthly performances in recent years: Nasdaq +8% for the month, S&P nine consecutive winning weeks, the Dow crossing 51,000 for the first time, and the AI earnings season producing three consecutive weeks of supply-chain-level demand confirmations that have fundamentally repriced the enterprise AI infrastructure investment thesis from "hoped-for" to "confirmed and accelerating." Markets open Monday with Iran's weekend response to Trump's new conditions as the primary macro variable, and with Tuesday's first major June economic data releases beginning to frame the next month's rate expectations.

The Anchor

Records on a deal-stall Friday. The market is pricing the AI infrastructure trifecta confirmation as a primary driver and the Iran deal as a secondary one — and Friday's divergence between large caps (records) and small caps (Russell -0.59%) shows exactly where the deal's delay matters: the rate-sensitive small-cap sectors, which need the yield decline a signed deal would produce, did not get it on Friday.

The Friday session's internal market structure reveals the two forces operating simultaneously. The first force — AI infrastructure earnings confirmation — lifted the large-cap tech-weighted indices. Dell's 32% surge on AI server revenue growing 757% year-over-year is not a marginal technology sector event; it is the third data point in a week of supply-chain-level confirmations that together establish that AI hardware procurement is growing at extraordinary rates across all three layers of the infrastructure stack (chips, data platforms, enterprise servers). The S&P 500's technology and communication services sectors — which together represent approximately 40% of the index by market cap — responded to this confirmation by closing at new all-time highs. The Nasdaq, which is more concentrated in technology, followed. The Dow — which includes Dell directly and benefits from technology sector contagion — crossed 51,000 for the first time in its history. The second force — Iran deal delay — held back the rate-sensitive sectors that the S&P's overall gain would otherwise have lifted uniformly. Trump's Situation Room meeting produced new demands (never-nuclear, no-toll Hormuz, HEU destruction) rather than a signature, keeping rate-hike probability elevated and preventing the 10-year yield from declining below 4.45%. The Russell 2000's 0.59% decline — while large caps gained 0.20-0.72% — captures this divergence precisely: small caps' disproportionate exposure to regional banks, REITs, and consumer discretionary companies that benefit from rate declines meant that the absence of the Iran deal's rate relief was bearish for the Russell even on a day the S&P reached a record.

The May 2026 market month deserves examination as a period that produced remarkable results under difficult conditions. May began with the Iran conflict in its third month, WTI near $100, PCE inflation elevated, and the 10-year yield at 4.50%+. It ends with WTI at $87.20 (down from the May 20 peak of $108.59 as deal expectations built), the 10-year at 4.45%, the S&P at a new record, the Nasdaq up 8% for the month, and the Dow above 51,000 for the first time. The driver of that performance is the intersection of two trends: Iran deal optimism reducing the geopolitical risk premium on equities (particularly oil-sensitive and rate-sensitive sectors), and the AI earnings season — Nvidia, Snowflake, Dell — confirming that the AI buildout's fundamental demand is strong enough to sustain elevated valuations even as inflation remains above target. The combination produced a month where the S&P gained approximately 5-6% despite PCE printing at 3.8% (April) and the Iran conflict reaching its most militarily active phase in May 20-28. For June, the primary question is whether the Iran deal resolves (unlocking the full rate-relief rally that the records are already partially anticipating) or stalls further (risking a reversal from record levels as the rate-hike probability resets higher without the oil-price relief of a deal).

The Russell 2000's 0.59% decline to 2,919.34 on Friday — the only major US index to fall while large caps reached records — is the clearest market signal of the Iran deal delay's specific economic cost. Small caps' disproportionate exposure to the sectors most sensitive to interest rates (regional banks, REITs, consumer discretionary, smaller industrials) means the Russell functions as the market's rate-sensitivity barometer: when the deal advances and rate-hike probability declines, the Russell outperforms; when the deal stalls and rate-hike probability remains elevated, the Russell underperforms. Friday's Situation Room without a signature kept rate-hike probability at ~32-36% and the 10-year yield at 4.45% — insufficient for the Russell to sustain the gains from its May 20-28 recovery.

The Russell 2000's performance since May 20 illustrates the rate-sensitivity dynamic precisely. On May 20, when the Iran talks collapsed and WTI hit $108.59 (its peak), the Russell closed near its 2026 low at approximately 2,803. As the Iran deal narrative built from May 23 through May 28, the Russell recovered to 2,937 — a 4.8% gain in eight days, driven by the same 10-year yield decline from 4.657% to 4.46% that produced the rate-relief rally. Friday's reversal from 2,937 to 2,919 is the partial unwind of that rate-relief pricing in response to Trump's stiffened demands, which kept the 10-year yield from declining further. The full rate-relief rally — which analysts model as Russell 2000 at 2,970-3,000 — requires the 10-year yield below 4.30-4.35%, itself requiring WTI at $82-84 and Fed rate-hike probability below 15%. That combination requires a signed Iran deal. Until then, the Russell's level will track the deal's probability: higher on confirmed progress, lower on stalls.

Quick hits

  • The AI infrastructure earnings trifecta — Nvidia, Snowflake, Dell — deserves analysis as a supply-chain confirmation model rather than a collection of individual earnings beats. Each company measures AI demand at a different layer of the infrastructure stack, and all three are growing simultaneously at extraordinary rates. Nvidia at the chip layer grew data center revenue 85% year-over-year to $81.6 billion in Q1, with Q2 guided at $91 billion — confirming that AI silicon procurement is constrained by Nvidia's manufacturing capacity (TSMC CoWoS advanced packaging) rather than demand. Snowflake at the data platform layer grew revenue 33% year-over-year to $1.39 billion with $9.21 billion in RPO growing 38% — confirming that enterprise AI data workloads are growing faster than current revenue reflects, with the RPO indicating future revenue acceleration. Dell at the enterprise hardware layer grew AI server revenue 757% year-over-year to $16.1 billion with $24.4 billion in AI orders — confirming that enterprise hardware procurement is the fastest-growing layer of the AI supply chain, driven by the Nvidia Blackwell GPUs that Dell ships in its AI servers. The three layers are not independent — they are interdependent and mutually reinforcing: more Nvidia chips shipped → more Dell AI servers ordered to house those chips → more Snowflake data platform contracts to process the data those servers generate. The simultaneous acceleration at all three layers is the market's strongest evidence yet that the AI infrastructure buildout is a genuine multi-year supercycle rather than a single-company story.
  • The Dow's first close above 51,000 requires context about what the Dow Jones Industrial Average actually measures. The Dow is a price-weighted index of 30 large-cap US companies — unusual among major indices in using price weighting (higher-priced stocks have more influence) rather than market-cap weighting (larger companies have more influence). The Dow crossing 51,000 reflects the combined effect of technology sector strength (Goldman Sachs, Microsoft, Apple, Visa are the largest Dow weights by price), industrials recovery (Boeing, Caterpillar, Honeywell), and financial sector performance (JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs). Dell is not a Dow component, so Dell's 32% surge does not directly add Dow points — but Dell's result lifts the AI narrative that reprices Microsoft, Apple, and the technology-adjacent companies that constitute the Dow's most price-weighted components. The Dow's crossing of 51,000 is less analytically significant than the S&P's market-cap-weighted record (which more accurately reflects the performance of the US equity market), but it is historically meaningful as a milestone and functions as a signal to retail investors and media coverage that the market's performance is broad-based across blue-chip companies, not just in the technology sector.
  • Gold's $4,498 Friday close — "steadied near $4,500" — is revealing about the current gold market structure. Gold has been in the $4,300-4,520 range since May 20, the peak Iran conflict risk period, and has not broken definitively in either direction despite significant daily variation in the Iran deal probability. The stability of gold near $4,500 in this range reflects a specific market structure: the geopolitical safe-haven premium and the inflation-hedge demand are approximately canceling each other's directional effects. When deal optimism increases (geopolitical premium decreasing, bearish for gold), it simultaneously decreases rate-hike expectations (gold-positive as real rates decline), producing no net directional move. When deal pessimism increases (geopolitical premium increasing, gold-positive), it simultaneously increases rate-hike expectations (gold-negative as real rates rise). This balance means gold's next directional move requires an asymmetric shock: a deal that fully eliminates the geopolitical premium while reducing inflation expectations enough to offset the geopolitical effect (bearish, toward $4,300-4,350), or a deal collapse that produces enough geopolitical premium to overwhelm the rate-hike effect (bullish, toward $4,550-4,620). At current prices, the market is saying neither scenario is yet confirmed.
  • The 10-year Treasury yield's behavior on Friday — declining 1 basis point to 4.45% — is less analytically significant than its stability: despite a day of S&P records, Dow milestone, and tech sector outperformance, the 10-year yield declined only 1 basis point. Normally, equity records produce Treasury selling (investors rotating from bonds to equities, pushing yields higher) — not yield declines. The fact that the 10-year declined even marginally on a day of equity records suggests that the bond market is independently pricing Iran deal probability (which is modestly improving from Thursday's stalled levels) even as equities are pricing AI earnings strength. The bond market's 1 basis point yield decline is the deal-probability signal; the equity market's record close is the AI earnings signal. Both signals are present simultaneously, and they are not contradictory: equities can be at records on AI earnings strength while bonds are simultaneously pricing modest deal progress through a 1 basis point yield decline. The June FOMC meeting at June 16-17 creates the next significant bond market catalyst: the Fed's statement on inflation trajectory and rate expectations will determine whether the 10-year breaks below 4.30% (deal signed, oil at $82-84, PCE improvement confirmed) or reverts toward 4.50%+ (deal stalled, oil unchanged, PCE remaining elevated).
  • May 2026's S&P performance of approximately 5-6% for the month — achieved despite April PCE at 3.8%, the Iran conflict reaching its most militarily active phase May 20-28, and the deal remaining unsigned at month-end — is the data point that defines how the market has fundamentally repriced the Iran conflict's macroeconomic implications since February 28. In February and March, the market was treating the Iran conflict as a pure risk-off event: oil higher → inflation higher → Fed tighter → equities lower. By May, the market has developed a more nuanced model: oil higher but deal expected → inflation temporarily elevated but declining when deal closes → Fed temporarily elevated probability but declining when deal closes → equities at records because the AI earnings confirmation provides a primary driver that runs independently of the geopolitical macro noise. The S&P's ability to make new records on a day when the Iran deal stalled and Trump added harder conditions is the clearest evidence of this repricing: the market now treats the Iran deal as a secondary (though meaningful) driver rather than the primary one, with the AI infrastructure earnings season providing the primary upward driver that the market was looking for. June will test whether this repricing holds if the Iran deal delays further and the June PCE data does not improve as expected.
