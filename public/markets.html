The Anchor

S&P 500 closes above 7,200 first time ever — Apple's $111.18B Q2 closes the best April since 2020 as PCE at 3.5% complicates every assumption about May.

Thursday's session produced a genuinely historic equity close: the S&P 500 at 7,209.01, above 7,200 for the first time in the index's history. The Dow surged 790.33 points — its strongest single session since Alphabet's Q4 2025 beat — to close at 49,652.14. The Nasdaq gained 219 points to 24,892.31, notching its own record close. April's cumulative performance now ranks as the best month for US equities since November 2020. The primary catalyst was Apple's after-close Q2 FY2026 report: $111.18 billion in revenue, beating the $109.66 billion consensus by 1.4%; EPS of $2.01 against a $1.94 estimate; and a Services all-time record of $30.98 billion that confirmed the AI-driven installed-base monetization thesis. AAPL jumped 3% in premarket Friday. Thursday's morning GDP print — Q1 2026 at +2.0% annualized, recovering from Q4 2025's 0.5% — provided additional macro support: the growth scare that characterized Q1's narrative is resolved, at least by the preliminary estimate.

The complication is significant and arrives in the same data release: March PCE inflation of 3.5% year over year — the hottest consumer price read since 2022 and 0.9 percentage points above the 2.6% consensus. Core PCE in March was 3.2%. The full Q1 2026 PCE price index surged to 4.5% — the highest quarterly inflation read since Q3 2022 — a figure that confirms the Brent-above-$100 quarter produced exactly the consumer price acceleration the Fed feared. The 10-year Treasury yield rose 10 basis points to 4.52% on the PCE print, its highest since late March. For May, the market inherits a paradox: the best equity month in six years built on AI earnings strength, and the hottest inflation read in three years built on oil-conflict prices. May's first trading session opens into that paradox, with Trump signaling new Iran strikes and the War Powers 60-day deadline landing on the same morning that US equity futures are little changed to mildly positive on the Apple premarket pop.

For the broader equity market, Apple's result closes the Magnificent Seven season with five decisive beats and confirms that the AI investment thesis — infrastructure (Alphabet, Amazon), productivity (Microsoft), and consumer (Apple) — is producing real revenue growth at scale. The S&P 500's 7,200 close is the market's aggregate verdict on that confirmation: AI earnings are real, they are growing, and they justify paying up for the companies generating them.

Q1 GDP rebounds to +2.0% — but Q1 PCE surges to 4.5% and March PCE hits 3.5%, the hottest since 2022. Warsh assumes the chairmanship into the most complicated inflation data in years.

Thursday's simultaneous GDP and PCE release produced a data combination that defies simple characterization: strong growth (+2.0% annualized, recovering from 0.5% in Q4 2025) alongside the hottest inflation since Q3 2022 (Q1 PCE at 4.5%, March PCE at 3.5% year over year). The GDP beat the Atlanta Fed's GDPNow projection of 1.2% significantly — government spending and investment contributed positively, with consumer spending decelerating but remaining positive. The PCE acceleration, however, is the more consequential data point for monetary policy: Q1 PCE at 4.5% means the Fed ended the quarter 2.5 percentage points above its inflation target, with the oil-conflict-driven energy price trajectory still unresolved. March's 3.5% PCE — up from 2.8% in February — means the inflation is accelerating, not decelerating, and core PCE at 3.2% demonstrates that the acceleration is not entirely an energy pass-through. Kevin Warsh assumes the Fed chairmanship into a data environment where the prior chair's 8-4 split FOMC board and an accelerating PCE print await his first meeting. The 10-year Treasury's move to 4.52% — 10 basis points on a single session — is the bond market's assessment of what this data means for rate expectations: fewer cuts, later, higher for longer.

Oil climbs on May 1 as Iran War Powers deadline hits and Trump signals strikes — Brent recovering from $114 settle toward $117, WTI above $104.

Oil prices climbed Friday morning as the Iran conflict's War Powers Resolution 60-day deadline arrived simultaneously with Trump's signal that Washington "might need" to restart active military operations. Brent crude, which had settled at $114.01 after Thursday's $126.41 four-year-high spike, was recovering toward $116.90 in early Friday trading — up 2.53% from the settle. WTI was trading above $104, up 2.77% from its Thursday settle. The oil market's Friday-morning pricing reflects two overlapping signals: the partial reversal of Thursday's Brent spike as the market absorbs the "just give up" rhetoric as an escalation signal rather than an imminent strike order; and the War Powers deadline's arrival adding a new legal ambiguity that could either constrain military action (if Congress asserts itself) or produce a new strike authorization (if the White House successfully argues the ceasefire halts the clock and then resumes operations). For equity markets, oil at $116.90 Brent is the same macro headwind at the same level — but the War Powers uncertainty means the oil price could move significantly in either direction by Friday's close depending on whether Trump's administration resolves the deadline ambiguity through legal action, diplomatic movement, or a strike announcement.

Gold · UST 10Y · Dollar $4,571 Gold ▼ 1.12% · UST 10Y 4.52% ▲ 10 bps · DXY ▲ 0.17% · PCE forces dollar strength · real-yield headwind

Gold falls to $4,571 as PCE surprise forces dollar strength and the 10-year rises to 4.52% — the real-rate headwind overrides the safe-haven bid from Iran Day 63.

Gold fell 1.12% to $4,571 Friday morning — its largest single-day decline since mid-April — in a move driven by the PCE surprise's direct impact on the dollar and real rates. The March PCE read of 3.5% year over year pushed the dollar index (DXY) up 0.17% to 99.82, a reversal of the prior session's dollar weakness; dollar strength reduces the purchasing power of non-dollar holders of gold and is the single most reliable short-term headwind for the metal. Simultaneously, the 10-year Treasury's 10-basis-point rise to 4.52% increased the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding gold against a risk-free rate that now exceeds 4.5%. The safe-haven argument for gold — which would normally be supported by Iran Day 63's strike signals and the War Powers deadline ambiguity — is being overwhelmed by the real-rate arithmetic on Friday morning. The longer-term safe-haven thesis for gold remains intact: $4,571 is still approximately 40% above gold's level at the start of the Iran conflict, and the conflict's structural uncertainty (no supreme leader, competing Iranian institutional voices, War Powers ambiguity) continues to support a risk premium in the metal. Friday's decline is a PCE-specific repricing, not a structural gold reversal.

Kevin Warsh assumes the Fed chairmanship into a 4.5% Q1 PCE, an 8-4 divided board, and Powell still on the board as a governor. The transition is the most complicated in modern Fed history.

Kevin Warsh's assumption of the Federal Reserve chairmanship occurs in a context without modern parallel: an 8-4 divided FOMC board from the final Powell meeting, PCE inflation at 4.5% for Q1 2026 (the highest since Q3 2022), a prior chair who announced he will remain on the board as a governor specifically because of Trump's "legal attacks," and a War Powers deadline on Warsh's first day as the institutional context. Warsh stated during his confirmation hearings that he would not be Trump's "sock puppet" on rates — a declaration that will be immediately tested by whether his first FOMC statement signals any change from the hold-with-easing-bias posture that Powell's final meeting maintained under an 8-4 dissent. The board composition provides no easy consensus: three hawkish dissents in Powell's final meeting (opposing easing bias) and one dovish dissent (Miran, preferring a cut) means Warsh's first meeting will require him to either maintain the existing hold with its internally contested easing bias, or signal a new direction that could trigger additional dissents. Markets will be watching Warsh's first FOMC communication with the same intensity they brought to watching Powell's final meeting. The 10-year yield's rise to 4.52% on the PCE print suggests markets are already pricing a Warsh-era Fed that is more hawkish than the Powell-era forward guidance implied — a pricing that could reverse quickly if Warsh signals continuity in his first public statements.

Quick hits

  • The S&P 500's April performance — closing above 7,200 for the first time — occurred in a month when Brent crude averaged above $100 per barrel for every trading day. The historical pattern for equity markets during sustained Brent above $100 is consistently negative in quarters 2-4 of the shock (the first quarter benefits from the growth momentum preceding the shock). April's equity rally is therefore an outlier within a historically reliable pattern, driven by AI earnings that are sufficiently large to offset the oil-driven margin pressure at the current Brent level. The question for May: is $116 Brent the level at which AI earnings growth outweighs energy cost headwinds, or does $126 Brent (which touched Thursday) represent the threshold above which even AI earnings cannot sustain the offset?
  • Apple's Services segment at $30.98 billion quarterly — an annualized run rate of approximately $124 billion — has now crossed a specific structural threshold: it is generating more revenue annually than the entire 2025 revenue of any single software company in the S&P 500 other than Microsoft. Apple is simultaneously the world's largest consumer hardware company and what is effectively the world's second-largest software company by revenue — a duality that no other company in history has achieved at this scale. The AI-driven Services acceleration (up 14-16% year over year consistently) means this position will become more dominant, not less, through 2027 and 2028.
  • The Q1 2026 PCE of 4.5% — while alarming — requires context about its composition. Energy prices contributed approximately 1.5-2.0 percentage points of the 4.5% total, given Brent averaging above $100 for the quarter. Strip the energy contribution and Q1 core PCE was approximately 3.2% — still 1.2 percentage points above the Fed's target, but less alarming than the headline suggests. The more concerning signal is March PCE's 3.5% — by which time markets had expected some energy pass-through to have partially dissipated, but the energy contribution remained elevated and non-energy inflation (shelter, services) continued above 3.0%. The March data suggests the secondary inflation effects from the energy shock are beginning to build, not just the direct energy pass-through — which is what creates the more persistent inflation concern for the Fed.
  • Gold at $4,571 — down 1.12% on PCE-driven dollar strength — is still approximately $550 above the pre-Iran conflict price from late February 2026. The PCE-driven decline is a one-session real-rate repricing; the structural $550 risk premium reflects the conflict's unresolved supply-shock potential. If the War Powers deadline produces a diplomatic resolution or Congressional authorization of the blockade (removing legal ambiguity), a portion of that $550 risk premium would be candidates for compression. If Trump resumes active strikes, Brent spikes, and the premium would likely expand rather than contract. Gold's Friday morning level reflects the market's probability-weighted assessment of those two scenarios: currently assigning approximately 60-65% probability to diplomatic continuation of the blockade and 35-40% to renewed military action.
  • The Dow's 790-point Thursday session — its strongest since Alphabet's Q4 2025 beat — is notable for which components drove it: Apple (large weight, +3% premarket contributing to Thursday's setup), Alphabet (substantial weight on Cloud +63%), and Caterpillar (which reported Thursday with results sensitive to global infrastructure spending, a proxy for the Iran-conflict-era infrastructure surge in US ally countries). Caterpillar's contribution to the Dow's Thursday performance reflects a less-discussed aspect of the Iran conflict's economic impact: the defensive infrastructure spending surge by Middle East US allies and European NATO members has increased demand for heavy industrial equipment. Caterpillar's infrastructure-exposure revenue has been one of the conflict era's unexpected beneficiaries.
  • Kevin Warsh assuming the chairmanship with a 10-year at 4.52% is a specific test of his stated independence. During his confirmation, Warsh said he would not prioritize rate cuts to satisfy political pressure — but 4.52% is also the level at which consumer mortgage rates approach 7%, auto loan rates approach 8%, and small business borrowing costs approach 9-10%. These real-economy costs are the pressure points that generate the most intense political pressure on Fed chairs historically. Warsh's first three months will reveal whether his stated independence holds against that specific pressure. The "no sock puppet" declaration is easy to make; maintaining it when PCE is running at 4.5% and the president who nominated you is simultaneously threatening Iran with military strikes is the test that has broken the stated independence of multiple prior Fed chairs.
