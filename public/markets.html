Markets · Deep Dive

US equity markets are closed for the weekend. Thursday's close — Nasdaq +1.91% to 26,517, S&P +1.08% to 7,500, Russell 2000 +2.10% to 2,979 — heads into Saturday as the last priced state of US equities, with Monday's open inheriting nuclear talks stalled at Day 3, Israel's Knesset advancing toward snap elections, and WTI stable at $76.60.

The weekend's diplomatic environment will set the terms of Monday's open more than any data release. Thursday's equity recovery — the market's verdict that Wednesday's post-FOMC selloff overreacted to a non-majority hawkish dot plot — was priced before either the nuclear talks postponement or the Knesset dissolution vote. Monday's buyers will need to incorporate both. The nuclear talks stall is the more immediately market-moving of the two: markets priced the MOU's signing as a durable geopolitical positive because they assumed the second stage — nuclear talks within the 60-day window — would proceed. Three days into the window, the talks have not started. Each day without a Switzerland date compresses what is already a tight negotiating timeline and adds probability mass to the scenario where the talks fail to produce an interim framework within 60 days. The Knesset dissolution is a second-order effect: it makes the Lebanon impasse structurally harder to resolve, extending the timeline uncertainty rather than adding a discrete new risk event. Oil's position at $76.60 — above the $75 trajectory that the full Hormuz reopening would have supported — is relevant for the Fed's September calculus: every dollar of WTI above $75 represents slightly less oil disinflation in Q3 CPI data, slightly less reason for Warsh to revise the hawkish dot plot, and slightly more rate-hike risk priced into September's FOMC. The July CPI print — reporting June data, released mid-July — is the next major market data event and will be the first print to capture even a partial signal from the post-deal oil price move.

The Anchor

Nasdaq 26,517. Russell 2000 2,979. Thursday's recovery was the market calling Wednesday's oversell. Then the weekend arrived with two new unpriced variables. Monday will price the nuclear stall and the Israeli elections that Thursday's buyers never saw.

Thursday's equity recovery is best understood as a technical correction, not a fundamental reversal. The Nasdaq's 1.91% gain and the Russell 2000's 2.10% lead reflected markets concluding that Wednesday's 1.34% Nasdaq drop overstated the implications of a non-majority hawkish dot plot from a first-meeting Fed chair. That correction was justified. What Thursday's recovery did not do is price the Vance postponement — which arrived after Thursday's close — or the Knesset dissolution vote, which advanced Friday while US markets were closed for Juneteenth. Monday's open is the first equity market session that has the opportunity to incorporate both.

The weekend's diplomatic activity determines how much new pressure these variables add to Monday's open. If Saturday or Sunday produces a Lebanon arrangement that allows Vance to rebook Switzerland — a ceasefire monitoring mechanism, a modified UNIFIL deployment, any face-saving formula that gives Iran sufficient political cover to sit down — Monday's equity market can trade on the thesis that the nuclear talks delay is temporary, the 60-day window is intact, and the geopolitical premium from the MOU's signing remains in place. If the weekend produces no movement, Monday's equity market faces pricing a nuclear talks stall of unknown duration alongside an Israeli political environment that makes resolution harder. Neither scenario is catastrophic for equities — the MOU is still signed, Hormuz is still reopening — but the two scenarios produce meaningfully different opening print directions.

US equity markets are closed Saturday for the regular weekend following Friday's Juneteenth federal holiday. Thursday's closing prices — Nasdaq 26,517 (+1.91%), S&P 500 7,500 (+1.08%), Dow 51,650 (+0.31%), Russell 2000 2,979 (+2.10%) — are the last priced state of US equity markets heading into the weekend. Monday's open will be the first equity session to incorporate two developments that arrived after Thursday's close: VP Vance's postponement of the Switzerland nuclear talks trip (announced Thursday evening), and Israel's Knesset unanimous advancement of the dissolution bill toward snap elections (Friday, while US markets were closed for Juneteenth).

The gap between what Thursday priced and what Monday will need to price depends on the weekend's diplomatic activity. Analysts who track Middle East diplomacy are watching for any signal from the White House, Israeli government, or Iranian Foreign Ministry about weekend contacts — specifically whether the US is attempting to construct a Lebanon monitoring arrangement that would allow Iran to enter nuclear talks without a full IDF withdrawal from south of the Litani River. A Lebanon formula that Iran can accept as meeting its ceasefire condition would allow Vance to rebook Switzerland for early next week, and Monday's equity market would likely open on that positive geopolitical resolution signal. No formula over the weekend means Monday faces pricing two accumulated variables simultaneously.

Israel's Knesset advancing its dissolution bill toward September–October elections is a Monday equity market variable that Thursday's recovery never priced. The elections are being driven by a domestic Israeli coalition fracture over Haredi military draft exemptions — the ultra-Orthodox parties that sustained Netanyahu's governing majority withdrew support when Netanyahu signaled he could not pass the draft bill in the current session. The elections create a structural complication for Lebanon negotiations that global equity markets will need to incorporate into their pricing of the MOU's durability.

Markets had priced the MOU's signing as a geopolitical positive on the assumption that implementation would proceed — that the nuclear talks would begin within the 60-day window and the Lebanon ceasefire provisions would be observed. The Knesset dissolution introduces a political incentive structure that works against Lebanon concessions: no Israeli government heading into elections will offer the IDF operational modifications that Iran demands without paying a domestic political price that is particularly high in the current environment. This does not make Lebanon resolution impossible — US diplomatic pressure on Israel can be effective regardless of domestic Israeli politics — but it makes it slower and more uncertain. Global equity markets that priced the MOU as a clean geopolitical positive now need to price a more complex implementation path.

WTI crude stabilized at $76.60 through Thursday's close and into the weekend as the Hormuz main-channel mine clearance enters its third day. The main channel's approximately 80 mines require two to three weeks of clearance work by the US-European mission; commercial shipping is using alternative routes through Iranian and Omani waters but at lower throughput rates than the main channel would support. The result is that the oil supply increase from Hormuz reopening is real and proceeding — but slower than the immediate post-deal $75 trajectory that markets priced on the day of the Versailles signing. WTI at $76.60 represents the market's reassessment of how quickly the full supply release arrives.

The oil-to-FOMC chain is the most important macroeconomic connection to understand for Monday's markets. The Fed's September rate decision will be data-dependent. The data that will most influence it is CPI — specifically whether June and July headline CPI shows the oil price decline flowing through to consumer prices. WTI averaging $76–77 through late June rather than $75 reduces the magnitude of oil's disinflationary contribution to June CPI. Smaller oil disinflation means less data reason for Warsh to revise the hawkish September dot plot. Markets understanding this chain will watch the Hormuz clearance timeline and WTI's trajectory as closely as any FOMC speaker remarks, because the mine-clearance pace is determining the speed of the oil disinflation that is the primary potential offset to the current rate-hike projection.

Gold held at approximately $4,228 through Thursday's close, still under pressure from the dollar testing yearly highs and the 10-year Treasury yield at 4.53% — 7 basis points above the pre-FOMC level. Both dollar strength and rising real yields mechanically pressure gold: a stronger dollar makes gold more expensive in non-dollar currencies (reducing global demand), while higher real rates increase the opportunity cost of holding a non-yielding asset at a time when Treasuries offer 4.53% with no credit risk. Gold's recovery path depends on the oil-disinflation-to-FOMC-revision sequence playing out through Q3 data.

The July CPI print — scheduled for mid-July and reporting June inflation data — is the next major market data event for gold, bonds, and equity valuations simultaneously. It will be the first CPI print to capture any portion of the post-deal oil price move, which shifted WTI from approximately $84 before the deal to the current $76.60. The question the July print will begin to answer: is the oil move large enough and fast enough to appear meaningfully in headline CPI? If June CPI shows even 0.1–0.2% headline relief attributable to oil, markets will begin pricing the probability of a September dot plot revision — which would soften the dollar, reduce real yield pressure, and allow gold a path back toward its prior highs. The July CPI date is the market event to watch between now and the September FOMC.

Quick hits

  • The Russell 2000's outperformance on Thursday's recovery — 2.10% versus Nasdaq's 1.91% — carries a specific implication for how markets read the Warsh dot plot. Small caps carry more floating-rate debt than large caps and are more sensitive to rate changes. If the market genuinely believed a rate hike was coming in September, small caps would underperform on the recovery, not lead it — higher rates raise floating-rate debt service costs disproportionately for small borrowers. The Russell 2000 leading Thursday's recovery tells you that markets read the hawkish dot plot as a distribution that discloses opinion, not a commitment that prices policy. If FOMC members in speeches next week use language like "ready to hike if data warrants" versus "expecting to hike," the difference will move small caps in opposite directions — watch the Russell 2000 as the sensitivity indicator for how markets are reading Fed speaker tone.
  • A second weekend variable worth tracking: the G7 Évian joint statement on Lebanon from June 17 called for Lebanon ceasefire and Hezbollah disarmament. G7 statements don't carry enforcement weight, but the Évian language provides diplomatic scaffolding that the US can use to pressure both Israel and Iran: the international community's stated position is that Lebanon should be ceasefire-compliant, which aligns with Iran's condition. The Biden administration used G7 alignment as a tool for pressuring Israel in the 2024 Gaza context; the Trump administration has been less inclined toward multilateral pressure on Israel. Whether the White House is willing to invoke the G7 Évian language as diplomatic leverage over Israeli Lebanon operations in the context of the nuclear talks window is one of the weekend's open questions that markets cannot price until Monday.
