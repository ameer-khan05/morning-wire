US equity markets are closed for the weekend. Friday's records — S&P 500 at 7,209.01, Dow at 49,652.14, Nasdaq at 24,892.31 — close April as the best month since 2020. Monday opens into fresh geopolitical uncertainty.

Friday's record equity close capped a month that will be remembered as one of the most contradictory in recent market history: the best monthly performance for US stocks since November 2020, achieved during an active armed conflict, a hot-inflation regime, a historic Federal Reserve leadership transition, and a Brent-above-$100 oil price that had not been sustained since 2022. The April rally is fundamentally an AI-earnings story: Alphabet, Amazon, and Apple — the three largest cloud and consumer-tech platforms — all beat their primary AI metrics decisively, and the market's aggregate assessment of those beats pushed equity multiples to new historical highs. The VIX at 16.40 on Friday's close — its lowest since the Iran conflict began — is the fear-gauge reading that best captures April's dominant sentiment: AI earnings have been strong enough to suppress conflict-era volatility below the pre-conflict baseline. The question for May: whether Trump's Iran deal rejection on Friday, combined with Lebanon's ceasefire deterioration, re-establishes the geopolitical risk premium that April's AI earnings managed to compress. Monday's opening trade will be the first answer.

Trump rejects Iran's peace proposal — "can't agree to terms" — and Brent stays near $116.90 heading into Monday. Lebanon ceasefire killing 30-plus a day with 12 days to expiry adds a fresh Brent-spike pathway.

Trump's public rejection of Iran's latest peace proposal on Friday — "They want to make a deal, but I'm not satisfied with it; they're asking for things I can't agree to" — is the single most market-relevant weekend development for energy prices. Brent crude closed Friday at approximately $116.90, having recovered from Thursday's $114 settle after the prior session's $126.41 four-year-high spike. The rejection sustains the military-option risk premium that Iran's proposal was designed to remove. For Monday's oil market open, the pricing logic is direct: a diplomatic breakthrough would have removed the blockade uncertainty premium and moved Brent toward $100-105; the rejection keeps that premium in place at $116-117. Lebanon's simultaneous deterioration — 30-plus killed by IDF strikes in a single day, the IDF chief declaring "there is no ceasefire" — adds a second oil-supply pathway: if Lebanon re-escalates into full combat before May 14, the combined Hormuz blockade and Lebanon conflict could generate the oil market's most significant supply-shock scenario since the conflict began. The $126 spike from April 29 — already a four-year high — could be revisited in a Lebanon re-escalation scenario.

Kevin Warsh's first full week as Fed chair begins Monday — no scheduled communications, a 4.5% Q1 PCE, and a divided 8-4 board inherited from Powell's final meeting. The market is watching every word.

Kevin Warsh's assumption of the Federal Reserve chairmanship enters its first full working week on Monday with an unusual combination of high market attention and low scheduled communication. Warsh has no public events confirmed for the week of May 4; his first scheduled communication opportunity is likely the May FOMC meeting or a university speech that has not been publicly announced. The market's interest is acute: Warsh assumes the chairmanship into Q1 PCE of 4.5% — the highest quarterly inflation read since Q3 2022 — a 10-year Treasury yield of 4.52%, and a prior FOMC board split of 8-4 at Powell's final meeting. Three hawks dissented against any easing bias; one dove preferred an immediate cut. Warsh's first FOMC meeting will require him to either maintain the existing hold-with-easing-bias posture or signal a new direction that could trigger additional dissents from either end of the split board. During his confirmation hearings, Warsh said he would not be "Trump's sock puppet on rates" — a statement the market is now watching for its operational expression. At 4.52% on the 10-year, markets are currently pricing a Warsh-era Fed that is marginally more hawkish than Powell's final stance implied; that pricing is subject to rapid revision based on Warsh's first public statements.

April nonfarm payrolls release May 8 — the next major macro catalyst after Thursday's PCE shock. March's +178K and 4.3% unemployment will be the baseline; April's read will be the first labor market data under the inflation-acceleration regime.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics releases the April employment situation summary on Friday, May 8 — the next scheduled major macro data release after Thursday's March PCE print of 3.5% year over year. March's payrolls figure was +178,000 with an unemployment rate of 4.3% — solidly positive but with some deceleration signals relative to the 200-plus range from 2024-early 2025. The April figure will be the first employment report to capture any labor market response to the Iran conflict's economic pressure: higher energy costs, reduced consumer discretionary spending, and potential hiring softness in sectors most exposed to the oil-price shock (transportation, logistics, consumer retail). The employment data will be read in the context of Q1's GDP and PCE: if April payrolls come in above +180,000, the "soft landing" narrative survives; if they come in below +150,000 with rising unemployment, the stagflation-adjacent narrative — strong inflation but softening growth — gains credibility and creates a genuine policy dilemma for Warsh's first months as Fed chair. The jobs report is the bridge between Q1's confusing data combination (good growth, hot inflation) and Q2's emerging trajectory.

Gold at $4,571 — down 1.12% on Friday's PCE-driven dollar strength — is experiencing a one-session real-rate repricing, not a structural reversal. The Iran risk premium that drove gold to $4,571 remains intact after the deal rejection.

Gold's 1.12% Friday decline to $4,571 — driven by the March PCE print of 3.5% pushing the dollar index (DXY) to 99.82 and the 10-year Treasury yield to 4.52% — is a technical repricing of real-rate opportunity cost rather than a structural change in gold's conflict-era positioning. The real-rate arithmetic is direct: a higher 10-year yield increases the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding gold against a risk-free rate above 4.5%, and dollar strength reduces the purchasing power of non-dollar gold holders. On Friday, those two factors outweighed the safe-haven argument that the Iran Day 63 strike signals and the War Powers deadline would normally support. But over the weekend, the balance shifts: Trump's Iran deal rejection sustains the conflict's oil-disruption risk premium, and Lebanon's ceasefire deterioration adds a new military-escalation path. The Iran risk premium that has driven gold approximately 40% above its pre-conflict price since late February remains structurally intact after Friday's PCE-driven decline. $4,571 is not gold's peak under the current conflict regime; it is a real-rate pause within a conflict-driven trend.

Quick hits

  • Friday's VIX close at 16.40 — its lowest since the Iran conflict began — is the most counterintuitive single data point in April's market narrative. A VIX of 16.40 implies that options market participants expect the S&P 500 to move approximately 1.0% per day over the next 30 days — a historically modest level of expected volatility for a market operating against the backdrop of an active armed conflict, a Brent near $117, a 10-year at 4.52%, and a Fed leadership transition. The VIX's compression reflects the dominant market logic of April: AI earnings have been strong enough to suppress both the conflict risk premium and the macro-uncertainty premium simultaneously. Whether that VIX compression persists through May — which brings Lebanon's ceasefire expiry, Trump's deal rejection, and Warsh's first FOMC meeting — will be a direct test of the AI-earnings thesis as a volatility-dampening force.
  • Gold at $4,571 closing the week represents an approximate $550 premium above the pre-Iran conflict price from late February 2026. That $550 premium is the market's current pricing of the Iran conflict's ongoing supply-disruption risk — and it is a risk premium that Trump's deal rejection just extended rather than reduced. For the gold market's forward pricing of the next two weeks: a Lebanon ceasefire expiry that produces an IDF escalation (Brent spike scenario) would likely push gold above $4,700; a Pakistan-mediated breakthrough on Iran (Brent collapse scenario) would likely compress gold toward $4,200-4,300 as the conflict risk premium unwinds. The $4,571 level reflects approximately 60% probability of continued blockade with no near-term resolution — a probability that Trump's "blast them away" statement may have just increased.
  • The April jobs report due May 8 will be the first employment data processed by Warsh's FOMC. If the report is strong (above +200,000 payrolls), it removes any growth-emergency argument for a rate cut and confirms that Warsh's "hold firm on inflation" posture is appropriate. If it is weak (below +150,000), it creates the uncomfortable scenario of hot inflation and softening employment — precisely the stagflation-adjacent environment that created the most damaging political pressure on Fed chairs in the 1970s. Warsh's stated independence ("not Trump's sock puppet") will be most severely tested if April's jobs report is soft enough to generate political pressure for a cut in the context of 3.5% PCE. The May 8 data point is Warsh's first real test, not his first FOMC meeting.
  • The Dow Jones Industrial Average at 49,652.14 is now less than 350 points from 50,000 — a psychological threshold that will generate significant media attention when it is crossed, regardless of its mathematical significance. Apple's 3% Friday jump was the primary contributor to the Dow's Thursday 790-point session that first closed above 49,000; if AAPL holds its Friday gains into Monday, the Dow's proximity to 50,000 will be a market story in its own right. The 50,000 narrative — like the S&P 500's 7,200 crossing on Thursday — provides a psychological anchor that tends to generate above-average retail investor attention and options market activity at the threshold level.
  • The real-rate arithmetic heading into Warsh's first week: UST 10Y at 4.52%, March PCE at 3.5% year over year produces a real yield of approximately 1.02% (10Y nominal minus PCE). That real yield has been positive and rising since November 2025 — it is the direct expression of the "higher for longer" regime that suppresses both crypto (no yield) and growth-equity multiples (discounted at higher rates) simultaneously. Warsh's challenge is that the only mechanisms that reduce the real rate — a Fed cut (reduces nominal) or a PCE decline (reduces inflation) — both require conditions that do not currently exist: either a growth emergency to justify a cut or a Brent decline below $90 to decompress PCE. The Iran deal rejection just extended the timeline for the second mechanism. The real rate is likely to stay above 1% for at least another quarter.
  • The Lebanon ceasefire's May 14 expiry arriving 12 days away is the most concrete near-term market trigger that does not have a policy option behind it. The War Powers deadline (May 1) had a legal argument; the FOMC has a meeting; the jobs report has a date. The Lebanon ceasefire expiry is a contractual military deadline, and the current trajectory — 30-plus killed in a single day, IDF chief saying "no ceasefire" — suggests it will expire without renewal in the context of ongoing violence. Unlike the Iran negotiations (which have a Pakistan mediation track and ongoing US-Iran channel), the Lebanon ceasefire has no equivalent diplomatic infrastructure. Its expiry, combined with the Iran deal rejection, could produce May's first genuine oil-market and equity-volatility event: a scenario in which both the Hormuz blockade and active Lebanon combat are simultaneously active for the first time since early April.
