Markets · Deep Dive · Independence Day Closure · Dow ATH Last Close · Reopen Monday July 6

US equity markets are closed for Independence Day — both the observed Friday July 3 and the actual Saturday July 4 — with Thursday's close as the last mark: Dow Jones at a record 52,900, S&P 500 flat at 7,483, Nasdaq down 0.80% at 25,833, and the 10-year Treasury at 4.25%, before trading resumes Monday July 6 facing the Hyperliquid token unlock and Khamenei funeral geopolitical risk on oil.

The Independence Day holiday is the punctuation mark between two distinct market chapters. The chapter that closed on Thursday is the one defined by the June NFP miss: 57,000 jobs versus the 115,000 consensus, the Dow to an all-time high on rate-hold bets, the Nasdaq down on demand concerns, gold and bonds up, oil down. The chapter that opens on Monday is the one in which those same data points are re-priced by the full institutional participant set that was partly inactive during the holiday-thinned Thursday and Friday sessions. The key open questions for Monday: does the Dow's all-time high survive the full repricing, does the Nasdaq's tech selloff continue or reverse, and does the Khamenei funeral's geopolitical background — specifically the Katz threat against Iran's new supreme leader and the Lebanon ceasefire's contested security zone — reintroduce Hormuz risk premium into crude oil prices that fell on Thursday's diplomatic pause. Monday July 6 will answer all three simultaneously.

The Thursday close is the last institutional mark until Monday — and the July 6 open inherits both the NFP rate-hold narrative and the geopolitical risk that the funeral period is keeping in suspension.

The Thursday close is remarkable for its internal contradictions, which reflect the genuine ambiguity of what the June NFP miss means for different asset classes. The Dow reached an all-time high because the NFP miss strengthened the case for a September rate hold — lower-for-longer supports the earnings multiples of the Dow's value-heavy, rate-sensitive components. The Nasdaq fell 0.80% because the same NFP miss signaled softer demand — tech companies need revenue growth, not just cheaper capital, and 57,000 jobs in June does not indicate an economy generating the kind of consumer and enterprise spending that tech's Q3 guidance assumes. Bonds rallied (10Y yield ▼7bp to 4.25%) for the same reason as the Dow: the rate-hold narrative is bullish for Treasuries. Gold gained 0.29% as a macro-uncertainty hedge. Oil fell on both demand weakness and the Iran diplomatic pause. The same data produced different directional readings in every asset class simultaneously — which is exactly what happens when a single data point (NFP miss) contains two distinct macro signals (good for rates, bad for demand).

The July 6 reopen inherits this contradiction. The four-day closure does not resolve whether the NFP miss is net bullish (rate hold) or net bearish (demand weakness) — it simply delays the institutional repricing until Monday. When that repricing occurs, it occurs alongside the Hyperliquid unlock, the return of full cryptocurrency liquidity, the Khamenei funeral's fifth day of mourning ceremonies, and the full event calendar of the first post-holiday week. The Dow's all-time high is the mark that equity markets will defend or abandon; the Nasdaq's 0.80% decline is the correction that tech may reverse or extend; and the 4.25% 10-year yield is the rate that the bond market will hold or push lower as the September FOMC narrative firms. None of those questions were resolved over the holiday weekend. Monday answers them all.

The New York Stock Exchange and Nasdaq are closed Friday July 3 and Saturday July 4 for Independence Day — July 4 falling on a Saturday this year, with Friday July 3 as the observed federal holiday — leaving Thursday July 2 as the last institutional equity close before the extended weekend. The Thursday close was the NFP reaction session: 57,000 jobs added in June versus the 115,000 consensus drove the Dow Jones Industrial Average to an all-time high of 52,900 (▲1.14%) on rate-hold bets, the S&P 500 to a flat close at 7,483, and the Nasdaq Composite down 0.80% to 25,833 as technology stocks — facing the dual headwind of softer demand from the weak labor market data and re-evaluated AI infrastructure spending — sold off in a session where the market delivered a split verdict on the same data point.

The split verdict between the Dow (bullish on NFP) and the Nasdaq (bearish on NFP) is the central analytical tension that the July 6 reopen must resolve. The Dow's all-time high reflects the rate-hold narrative winning in value-heavy, dividend-paying sectors where lower borrowing costs directly improve near-term earnings. The Nasdaq's decline reflects the demand-weakness narrative winning in growth-heavy, revenue-dependent sectors where softer consumer and enterprise spending from a 57,000-job economy threatens the top-line growth that justifies elevated technology multiples. Both readings are logical; neither is wrong. The holiday closure has not resolved which reading the full institutional market endorses — that resolution happens on July 6, when every participant prices the same Thursday data simultaneously.

Gold gained 0.29% to $4,115 and the 10-year Treasury yield fell 7 basis points to 4.25% as the June NFP miss firmed September rate-hold expectations — clean directional moves in the classic lower-rates direction that confirm the bond and gold markets are reading the 57,000 jobs print as the data point that takes a September hike off the table. The 10-year's decline to 4.25% reflects duration buyers extending Treasury positions on the premise that the Fed, which entered June's FOMC with nine dot-plot members projecting a hike, now has the labor market softness data it needs to justify a September hold without characterizing it as a dovish pivot — the unemployment rate is stable, participation is declining, and three consecutive months of below-consensus payroll additions establish a trend rather than a one-month miss.

WTI crude declined 1.79% to $67.10, its third consecutive session of losses, driven by the same dual catalyst that has weighed on oil through the week: weak demand expectations from the June NFP miss and the reduced near-term Hormuz risk that accompanied Iran's suspension of Doha nuclear talks for the funeral period. The $67 level is consistent with a Hormuz stand-down that remains in effect — above the pre-war fundamental equilibrium because the ceasefire's durability remains uncertain, but well below the crisis-period spike when Hormuz closure was a real near-term risk. The critical watch for the July 6 reopen is whether Israel Defense Minister Katz's stated threat against Mojtaba Khamenei — declared while Iran's state funeral is ongoing — translates into action during the five-day mourning period. Any Israeli strike during the funeral would reverse WTI's Thursday decline immediately and re-inject the geopolitical risk premium that three sessions of diplomatic progress have been gradually unwinding.

Quick hits

  • The Dow's all-time high on a day of the weakest jobs data since 2024 is explicable in market mechanics terms but confusing in economic narrative terms — and that confusion will not persist past the July 6 full-liquidity repricing. The value sectors in the Dow (industrials, financials, healthcare, consumer staples) genuinely benefit from a rate hold: lower borrowing costs on corporate balance sheets, better credit availability for consumers in rate-sensitive categories, reduced pressure on financials' net interest margins. Those benefits are real and they are captured in Thursday's price. But the same weak labor market that produced the rate-hold bet also signals lower corporate revenue growth in the second half — particularly for the consumer-facing companies that depend on employed, spending Americans. Thursday's Dow is the rate-hold benefit priced, without the demand-weakness cost yet reflected. The July 6 full-participation session is where both get priced simultaneously, and it is not obvious that the net is still an all-time high.
  • The July 31 Federal Open Market Committee meeting is the near-term anchor for the rate narrative that Thursday's NFP established. Between now and July 31, the relevant data points are: the July CPI print (which will arrive mid-month), any additional Fed speak clarifying the Warsh Sintra signal from last week, and the geopolitical developments from the Khamenei funeral period. If July CPI continues the cooling trend that the NFP data's demand-weakness signal implies, the September rate-hold probability that Thursday's markets priced will firm further by July 31. If July CPI reverses and surprises higher, the entire rate-hold narrative that drove the Dow's all-time high will be re-evaluated — and re-evaluated in a market context where equity valuations were set with the rate-hold priced in. The July 31 FOMC will not cut rates; the question is whether it signals September is a hold or keeps the hiking optionality from the June dot plot alive.
