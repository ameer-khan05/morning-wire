Markets · Deep Dive · Semi Rout · Oil Spike · Iran Ceasefire Broken · 10Y Yield 4.45%

The Nasdaq fell 1.16% to 25,818 and the S&P 500 declined 0.45% to 7,503 as Samsung's paradoxical 19-fold profit jump and reports of DeepSeek developing its own AI chip drove the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index down more than 5% — then US forces struck dozens of Iranian targets overnight after Iran attacked three commercial tankers in the Strait of Hormuz, sending WTI above $72 and Brent to $73.83 while the 10-year Treasury yield spiked 17 basis points to 4.45% on oil-inflation fears.

Tuesday delivered two independent market shocks operating simultaneously: a semiconductor demand-duration repricing triggered by Samsung's peak-earnings paradox and DeepSeek's chip development ambitions, and a geopolitical oil shock from the US-Iran Strait of Hormuz military exchange. Each shock alone would have produced a meaningful equity sell-off. Together they erased Monday's record Dow close and reset the bull case from "rate-hold endorsement" back to "geopolitical uncertainty." Wednesday opens with futures flat — not bouncing — because neither shock has resolved: CENTCOM struck Iran but has not confirmed Iranian stand-down, and the semiconductor sector's duration-risk repricing is a sentiment shift that takes sessions to fully process, not hours. The question entering Wednesday's session is whether these are one-day shocks that the Tuesday close has fully priced, or whether Iranian retaliation or continued semiconductor selling produces a second leg down.

Tuesday's market session had its narrative established before US forces struck Iran: Samsung reported the semiconductor sector's most dramatic quarterly results in years — a 19-fold operating profit jump — and lost $80 billion in market capitalization in the same session. The market's verdict was precise: AI memory demand is real at record scale, but the peak is visible, and investors had already priced a cycle that extends past the peak. DeepSeek's AI chip development reports arrived alongside the Samsung results, adding a structural mechanism to the peak-pricing intuition: if China's leading frontier AI lab reduces its Nvidia dependence, the demand curve the market has been valuing extends less far than the current semiconductor multiple implies. The combination drove the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index down more than 5%, pulling Nasdaq to 25,818 and the S&P 500 to 7,503.

Then US forces struck Iran. CENTCOM announced strikes on air defenses, radar installations, anti-ship missile sites, and IRGC small boats after three commercial tankers were attacked in the Strait of Hormuz — including the LNG tanker Al Rekayat, which suffered a fire in its engine room and risked explosion. Oil, which had been priced for geopolitical risk through the Mojtaba funeral period, repriced for active military exchange: WTI jumped to $72.12 (▲4.53% from $69), Brent reached $73.83 (▲2.49%), and the 10-year Treasury yield spiked 17 basis points to 4.45% as oil inflation fears priced into rates. Gold declined slightly to $4,145 (▼0.43%) — an unusual response for a safe-haven asset during a geopolitical shock that typically suggests investors expect the exchange to be short-duration rather than a sustained conflict escalation.

Samsung Electronics' second-quarter results — a 19-fold year-over-year operating profit jump to an estimated ₩58 trillion, beating every analyst estimate by a significant margin on record AI memory demand — produced an $80 billion market capitalization loss in a single session as investors simultaneously digested Reuters reporting that China's DeepSeek is developing its own AI chip. The combination drove the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index down more than 5% on Tuesday, with Intel, Micron, Marvell, Western Digital, and SanDisk posting steep individual losses; in Europe, ASML fell more than 5% and Soitec fell more than 10%, pulling the Nasdaq Composite to 25,818.69 (▼1.16%) and the S&P 500 to 7,503.85 (▼0.45%).

Samsung's paradoxical results are the clearest possible statement of the semiconductor sector's current condition: AI demand at its peak produced a record quarter, and the market sold those record results because the peak is where the selling starts. The pattern is not new — it appeared in the prior quarter when Samsung shed more than $100 billion in market value despite strong earnings. When consecutive record quarters are consistently sell-on-news events, the market is not responding to the quarters; it is pricing the duration of the cycle past them. DeepSeek's chip development provides the most credible mechanism yet for why that duration might be shorter than the current Nvidia multiple implies.

US military forces conducted "a series of powerful strikes" against dozens of Iranian targets — including air defense batteries, radar installations, anti-ship missile sites, and IRGC small boats — after Iran attacked three commercial vessels in the Strait of Hormuz, including the Qatari LNG tanker Al Rekayat (which suffered a fire in its engine room threatening explosion) and the Saudi-flagged supertanker Wedyan; the US Treasury simultaneously revoked the sanctions waiver that had allowed Iran to export oil globally, effectively reinstating full energy sanctions. WTI crude jumped from $69.00 to $72.12 (▲4.53%), Brent from $72.04 to $73.83 (▲2.49%), and the 10-year Treasury yield spiked 17 basis points to 4.45% as oil-inflation fears priced into rates. Wednesday futures are flat as markets weigh Iranian stand-down versus retaliation.

The oil spike is the mechanism that makes the Hormuz escalation a macro event rather than a geopolitical headline: WTI at $72 raises Q3 headline inflation expectations, and if sustained, it delays the September Fed rate cut path that equity and crypto markets are using as their medium-term bull case. Gold's mild decline to $4,145 — unusual for a safe-haven during active military strikes — suggests the market is pricing a short-duration exchange rather than a sustained escalation: both sides have struck, and the question is whether Iran retaliates or stands down. If Iran stands down and the Strait reopens, oil gives back most of the $3+ spike within days. If Iran retaliates, WTI above $80 is a realistic scenario before Wednesday closes.

The 10-year Treasury yield spiked 17 basis points to 4.45% on Tuesday as oil's jump to $72+ introduced an inflation premium into rates — a significant single-session yield move that tightened financial conditions simultaneously with the equity sell-off — while gold edged down to $4,145 (▼0.43%) in a counterintuitive response that suggests markets are pricing a short-duration US-Iran military exchange rather than a sustained escalation. Weak US jobs data arriving in the same session created a competing signal: labor market softness raises the probability of a September Fed rate cut, which would lower rates and ease financial conditions, representing the structural bull case for equities and crypto that the oil spike is now complicating. Wednesday: S&P futures -0.1%, Dow futures -0.3% — flat to marginally lower — as markets weigh whether Iran's response to overnight US strikes continues the escalation or marks the peak.

The yield spike to 4.45% is the market's one-session assessment of the oil-inflation risk: a 17 basis-point move in the benchmark rate on a single day's geopolitical event is not routine. It reflects the market pricing the tail risk — that a sustained Strait of Hormuz closure drives oil above $80, reigniting inflation that forces the Fed to hold rates through September and into year-end. Gold's mild decline cuts against that tail risk pricing: safe-haven assets typically rally during sustained conflict uncertainty, not decline. The combination — yields spiking (inflation fear) and gold declining (conflict expected to be short) — is internally consistent with a market that fears oil inflation but not a prolonged war. The FOMC on July 31 is where these competing reads resolve.

Quick hits

  • Gold declining during a military exchange is the market's most precise signal that the oil spike is being priced as a supply shock, not a safe-haven event. A genuine sustained escalation would drive gold higher (flight to safety) while also driving oil higher (supply disruption). When only oil spikes and gold edges down, the market consensus is: real oil disruption, expected short-duration. If Iran retaliates and gold turns back up, that consensus is being revised.
  • The 10-year yield's 17 basis-point jump in one session is the market pricing the tail risk more than the base case. The base case — Iran backs down, Strait reopens, oil gives back half the spike — does not produce a 17 bp move. The tail risk — sustained Hormuz closure, oil at $80+, Fed holds through year-end — does. If the tail resolves (Iran stands down by Thursday), the 10Y should retrace toward 4.30%. If it doesn't retrace, the market is telling you something about what it expects from Iran.
