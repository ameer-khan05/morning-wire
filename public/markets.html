Nasdaq 100 futures surged 2.1% and S&P 500 futures added 0.70% Thursday as Micron Technology's fiscal Q3 blowout — $41.46 billion in revenue, EPS $25.11, Q4 guided $50 billion — triggered the broadest semiconductor rally in months, reclassifying Tuesday's global chip rout as a valuation correction after an extended rally rather than a demand-driven structural break. The semiconductor sector is on track for its strongest single-day gain since the AI infrastructure rally began in 2025.

The character of Thursday's premarket rally differs meaningfully from Wednesday's measured 0.20-0.49% recovery: that session was professional repositioning into Micron results with uncertainty about what those results would show. Thursday is the response to a definitive answer — and the answer beat on every line. A Nasdaq 100 advance of 2.1% in premarket on the back of a 15.7% revenue beat and a 14.7% Q4 guidance beat is a calibrated response to the information content of Micron's earnings: the market is marking up the entire AI infrastructure investment thesis proportionally to the degree to which it was validated. Micron at +18% is the epicenter; the rest of the semiconductor sector follows based on how directly each company benefits from Micron's demand environment.

May PCE — the Federal Reserve's preferred measure of inflation — releases at 8:30 AM ET Thursday alongside the final Q1 2026 GDP estimate and May durable goods orders, a data-dense opening hour that will either complement or complicate the Micron-driven risk-on tone. The consensus for May PCE is a headline rise of 0.5% month-over-month (up from April's 0.4%) and core PCE roughly flat at 3.3% year-over-year. The FOMC, holding the funds rate at 3.50%–3.75%, assembled its June dot plot — in which nine members projected a September rate hike — with oil trading near $84 and inflation running above expectations through the spring.

May PCE matters for markets today because the Micron blowout has already answered the growth side of the Fed's mandate: AI infrastructure demand is real, accelerating, and generating record revenue. The outstanding question is whether inflation is cooperating. With WTI at $70.42 — 16.2% below the pre-deal baseline of $84 — the energy component of June CPI is building a disinflationary tailwind that will arrive in mid-July data. But May PCE is the immediate, real-time reading. An in-line print keeps the rate-hold case intact and allows Thursday's risk-on rally to run through the session; a hot print would reintroduce the FOMC hike risk that suppressed markets in early June, even if the oil disinflation building toward July ultimately overwhelms it. The final Q1 GDP estimate will also be watched: upward revision strengthens the growth narrative further, downward revision introduces questions about the economy's durability beneath the AI sector.

WTI crude extended its post-sanctions-waiver decline Thursday to $70.42 — below $70 for the first time since late February, and down 8.4% over three sessions from pre-waiver levels above $76.90 — as Iranian tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz continued building volume even as Iran declared the new routing protocol "unacceptable and dangerous." Brent dropped to $74.16 (▼2.24%). The mechanism: the 60-day sanctions waiver covers not just Iranian oil production but the full trade support chain — banking, shipping, and maritime insurance — meaning buyers who previously transacted through opaque evasion channels can now use normal legal payment rails, increasing both volume and price transparency simultaneously.

The magnitude and pace of the WTI decline has direct macroeconomic implications that extend well beyond the oil market. From the pre-Iran-deal WTI baseline of approximately $84 to Thursday's $70.42 represents a 16.2% oil price decline that will be progressively captured in the CPI energy components for June (published mid-July) and July (published mid-August) — the two prints the FOMC will read before its September meeting. Nine FOMC members projected a September rate hike in June's dot plot, a projection anchored to an inflation environment with oil near $84. With WTI at $70.42 and the 60-day waiver covering supply certainty through August 21 — guaranteeing that low-price Iranian supply enters both the June and July CPI calculations — the rate hike case those nine members were building is being materially weakened by energy price data in real time.

Quick hits

  • The UST 10Y's 7-basis-point rise to 4.57% Thursday — despite WTI falling to $70.42, which is disinflationary — is not a contradiction. Bond yields rise for two reasons: inflation expectations increase (hawkish) or growth expectations increase (non-hawkish). Thursday's yield move is entirely growth-driven: Micron's blowout is evidence that AI infrastructure capex is generating extraordinary revenue growth, which means GDP is being supported by real capital investment rather than consumption-driven demand. Growth-driven yield increases are supportive of equities (because the earnings that justify higher stock prices are also rising) rather than threatening — the equity rally alongside the yield increase is the market correctly interpreting the signal. An inflation-driven yield spike would produce the opposite: equity selloff and yield spike simultaneously, the "bad inflation" regime. Thursday's correlated equity rally and modest yield increase is the "good growth" regime.
  • Qualcomm's 10% gain deserves attention beyond the Micron-sector-lift contribution. The Modular acquisition is Qualcomm's most significant strategic move of 2026: it gives Qualcomm a software layer that abstracts AI inference across heterogeneous hardware, meaning Qualcomm can sell inference optimization services regardless of which accelerator a customer runs. This is the "Switzerland of AI chips" position — working across Nvidia, AMD, Intel, and Qualcomm's own silicon simultaneously. If the AI inference market develops as expected (larger in total compute spend than training by 2027-2028), and if hardware neutrality becomes a procurement requirement for enterprise buyers who don't want single-vendor lock-in, Modular's software becomes a strategic asset that scales independently of Qualcomm's handset revenue. The $4 billion acquisition price — expensive but not extraordinary for a category-defining software platform — reflects that option value.
