The IRGC struck and disabled two commercial tankers in the Strait of Hormuz overnight Saturday — after US equity markets closed on Friday. The first direct tanker kills of the conflict occurred after Brent's $88.10 close, meaning Monday's oil open prices not just the desalination plant strike and "full-scale offensive" warning (already in Friday's price) but two disabled vessels and a structurally changed Hormuz risk environment.

War-risk insurance is the mechanism that converts two tanker strikes into a market structure change. Diverting around Hormuz was a risk-management decision supported by available war-risk coverage — expensive, but available. Direct IRGC weapon engagement against commercial vessels changes the underwriting calculation: hull war-risk policies from Lloyd's and other markets may now exclude Hormuz transit at any premium, not merely price it at conflict-risk rates. If commercial marine insurance structurally excludes Hormuz, the near-blockade becomes a legal impossibility rather than a commercial deterrence — and Brent's Monday open reflects that structural shift, not just the risk premium.
