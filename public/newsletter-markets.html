Markets Whipsaw as Trump's Iran Address Fails to Calm Investors

President Trump took to the airwaves on April 2 with his first national address on the Iran conflict, declaring the war "nearing completion" and attempting to assure markets that a resolution is in sight. The message, however, fell flat with investors still reeling from weeks of geopolitical turbulence.

The S&P 500 surrendered 0.52% on the session as traders parsed through the rhetoric, while the more volatile Nasdaq 100 slumped 1.18%. Yet the Dow managed to buck the trend, rising 0.48% on strength in travel and technology shares—a telling divergence that reveals the market's mixed sentiment heading into Good Friday, which will close equity markets and put a temporary halt to trading volatility.

Perhaps most notably, oil prices surged following Trump's address, undoing some of the gains from earlier in the week when peace hopes had briefly pushed crude lower. The action suggests that, despite the president's dovish rhetoric, markets remain uncertain about the timeline for stability in the Middle East and the eventual normalization of shipping through the Strait of Hormuz.

Sources: Yahoo Finance, CNBC

Oil Prices Remain Volatile Around $104 Barrel as EIA Forecasts Summer Decline

Brent crude oil settled at $104.86 per barrel on Thursday, down 5.27% from its recent highs but still well above the $100 threshold that dominated headlines just days earlier. The commodity's behavior has been nothing short of erratic, careening between optimism over potential peace and anxiety over prolonged supply disruptions.

The Energy Information Administration released updated guidance suggesting prices will remain above $95 per barrel for the next two months before declining below $80 in the third quarter of 2026—a scenario that assumes the Iran conflict reaches a meaningful resolution. From the start of the year, crude had already surged 50% as shipping through the Strait of Hormuz deteriorated, creating one of the most significant supply shocks to the global energy market in years.

Sources: Fortune, U.S. Energy Information Administration

Software Sector Rallies as AI Monetization Skeptics Finally Capitulate

The software sector staged a remarkable renaissance Thursday as enterprise spending on AI tools accelerated beyond even the most bullish analyst projections. ServiceNow surged 5.5%, while Salesforce climbed 3% and Adobe gained 2.7%, putting these previously whipsawed stocks back on firm footing after months of uncertainty.

The catalyst? A significant shift in the legal landscape. Early 2026 brought crucial changes to AI copyright policy that removed lingering legal obstacles to widespread deployment. Corporate legal departments, having finally given the green light, opened the floodgates for massive enterprise AI initiatives. This represents a critical turning point: the speculation phase is ending, and the monetization phase is beginning in earnest.

Source: Financial Content

Nike Stumbles on Weak Outlook, Dragging Consumer Discretionary Sector

Athletic apparel heavyweight Nike delivered disappointing forward guidance on Thursday, triggering a 15% decline in its stock price and sending shockwaves through the consumer discretionary sector. The move underscores growing concerns about consumer spending resilience as higher oil prices threaten to reignite inflation across the economy.

Nike's weakness is particularly significant given its status as a bellwether for consumer health. If even premium athletic brands are struggling to maintain momentum, it raises questions about the broader health of discretionary spending in a rising-rate environment.

Source: Yahoo Finance

Gold Hits $4,760 as Safe-Haven Demand Reaches Peak Amid Iran Tensions

The precious metals complex delivered a decisive vote of no confidence in equities Thursday, as gold surged 1.74% to settle at $4,760 per ounce. This move reflects the classic flight-to-quality response that occurs when geopolitical uncertainty spikes and investor risk appetite contracts sharply.

With the Iran conflict showing no signs of abating despite Trump's optimistic rhetoric, traditional safe-haven assets like gold continue to attract capital. The action is particularly noteworthy because it suggests institutional investors are hedging their equity portfolios against further downside volatility.

Source: Trading Economics

Dow Surged 1,100 Points on March 30 After Glimmers of Iran Peace Hope

Earlier in the week, the market had staged a brief rally fueled by tentative hopes for an end to the Iran conflict. On March 30, the S&P 500 posted its best day since May 2025, with the Dow gaining over 1,100 points as investors bet that a resolution was imminent. That optimism proved premature, as Trump's Thursday address failed to provide the clarity markets desperately sought.

The whipsaw from Monday's euphoria to Thursday's disappointment underscores how reactive markets remain to any development in the Middle East. Until there is concrete evidence of a diplomatic breakthrough or military resolution, expect this volatility to persist.

Source: CNBC

Market Volatility Remains Tethered to Strait of Hormuz Developments

The entire equity market complex has effectively become a proxy for geopolitical developments in the Persian Gulf. When hopes for resolution rise, risk assets rally. When military action or shipping disruptions threaten, capital flees to safety. Oil had surged past $110 earlier this year when the Strait was fully closed, and even modest improvements in the flow have triggered sharp reversals.

Iran confirmed this week that Israel killed an IRGC naval commander responsible for blocking the strait, suggesting that the blockade may not be sustainable indefinitely. However, any replacement could escalate tensions further, keeping markets in a state of perpetual anxiety about energy supplies.

Source: Multiple sources

Travel and Mining Shares Advance on Peace-Trade Optimism

Despite the broad market weakness, certain sectors found refuge Thursday. Travel stocks benefited from investors' hopes that reduced geopolitical risk would translate into improved consumer confidence and leisure spending. Technology names also found support, with semiconductor manufacturers like SanDisk Corp leading gains as demand expectations stabilized.

Mining equities participated in the rally as well, reflecting expectations that global supply chains could normalize faster if the Iran situation resolves. These sector rotations are classic risk-on moves that have characterized each brief period of optimism this week.

Source: Yahoo Finance

EIA Economic Outlook: Oil May Plunge Below $80 in Q3 If Demand Erodes Further

Beyond its commodity price forecasts, the Energy Information Administration's latest outlook paints a sobering picture for the broader economy. Higher oil prices are beginning to erode demand across the product spectrum, from transportation fuels to petrochemical feedstocks. If this demand destruction accelerates, the EIA warns, crude could collapse below $80 in the third quarter—a scenario that would provide welcome relief to consumers but also signal serious economic weakness.

The risk-reward calculus is therefore becoming more complex. Lower oil prices would be bullish for growth-sensitive equities, but they would likely arrive only after a significant slowdown in economic activity has taken hold. Markets must now grapple with the possibility that the only path to lower energy prices is through recession.

Source: U.S. Energy Information Administration
