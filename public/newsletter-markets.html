Lead Story

The rally nobody drew: stocks at records, oil at $98, and inflation above 3%

If you had said a year ago that the S&P 500 would be setting records in the middle of a US-Iran war, with oil above $90 and CPI printing 3.3%, most strategists would have bet against you. And yet here we are. The S&P 500 has logged eight record closes so far in 2026. The Nasdaq is on track to extend its winning streak to 12 days — the longest since July 2009. Both benchmarks are up more than 3% over the past two weeks.

The driver is not fundamentals; it's the slope of risk. Any hint that the Iran conflict could end — and this week delivered several, capped by today's Israel–Lebanon truce — removes a left tail that traders were paying up to hedge. When that tail compresses, equity multiples expand mechanically. Add resilient earnings from the hyperscalers and an AI-spending cycle that keeps delivering, and the melt-up has room to run.

The quieter story, the one that will matter in six months, is inflation. March CPI jumped to 3.3% from 2.4% in February — the fastest monthly pickup in two years — entirely explained by the oil spike. The Fed's March minutes note that "further progress" on inflation has stalled. If the conflict really does wind down in April, the inflation pulse fades with it. If it doesn't, the Fed's patience gets tested at exactly the wrong moment.

Inflation & the Fed

March CPI: 3.3% y/y, 0.9% m/m

The fastest headline acceleration in two years, driven almost entirely by energy. Core inflation rose more modestly, suggesting the pulse is narrow rather than broad.

Source: BLS

FOMC minutes: "further progress has been absent"

Several participants at the March 17–18 meeting noted that recent months have shown no additional disinflation. The committee left rates unchanged and signaled that cuts previously expected in Q2 are now on hold.

Economists at the Peterson Institute see a swift decline if the Strait returns to normal traffic in May. A prolonged conflict, they warn, raises the odds of pass-through into core goods.
