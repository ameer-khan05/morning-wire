★ Lead Story

Q1 Ends with a Whimper: S&P 500 Posts Five Straight Weekly Declines as Oil Crosses $100

The final trading day of Q1 2026 arrives with Wall Street caught between two powerful forces: the relentless upward pressure of oil prices driven by the Iran war, and glimmers of hope that the conflict could soon end. The S&P 500 has posted five consecutive weekly declines — its longest losing streak since early 2022 — while WTI crude settled at $102.88 per barrel on Monday, its highest close since July.

But Tuesday's premarket tells a different story. S&P 500 futures jumped 0.76% to 6,436.75 and Nasdaq futures gained 0.69% after the Wall Street Journal reported that President Trump has signaled willingness to end the Iran campaign. If confirmed, a ceasefire could rapidly deflate oil prices and trigger a significant equity rally — but traders have been burned by false ceasefire signals before.

The energy shock has fundamentally reshaped the economic outlook for 2026. The Fed has revised inflation forecasts from 2.4% to 2.7%, and Chair Powell has made clear that rate cuts are off the table until energy prices stabilize. The 15% global tariff overhang adds another layer of uncertainty, leaving portfolio managers with few places to hide.

Federal Reserve & Monetary Policy

Powell: "We Feel Like Our Policy's in a Good Place to Wait and See"

Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell delivered measured remarks, acknowledging that Middle East events "will certainly affect gas prices" but maintaining that current rates are appropriate for the environment. The message is clear: don't expect any easing until the geopolitical fog lifts. Markets now price in no rate cut before December 2026 at the earliest, a dramatic shift from January when two cuts by mid-year were widely expected.

Energy & Commodities

Oil's Stunning Quarter: WTI Up 48%, Brent Up 51% on Iran War Supply Fears

The Iran war has produced the most significant oil supply disruption since the 1970s energy crisis. Brent crude recorded a staggering 51% monthly gain in March, with disruptions around the Strait of Hormuz — through which approximately 20% of global oil flows — pushing prices to multi-year highs. WTI briefly crossed $103 before settling at $102.88. The price swung between gains and losses Monday as markets weighed Trump's reported willingness to negotiate, but the physical supply picture remains extremely tight.

Fortune · Angle 360

Gold Drops Nearly 10% in Worst Weekly Rout Since 2011 — Then Bounces

In one of the month's more surprising moves, gold fell almost 15% in March despite the geopolitical turmoil that would typically drive safe-haven buying. The sell-off was driven by margin calls and liquidity needs as investors scrambled to cover losses elsewhere. Gold bounced to $4,585.10 on Monday (+0.61%), but the drawdown underscores how extreme stress can break even the most reliable safe-haven correlations.

Nike Q3 2026 Earnings: The Consumer Spending Bellwether Reports After Close

Nike reports Q3 results after market close today, with analysts expecting EPS of $0.29. As a bellwether for global consumer discretionary spending, Nike's guidance will be crucial for market sentiment heading into April. The key question: how much are rising energy costs eating into consumer budgets, and is Nike's ongoing turnaround strategy gaining traction despite macro headwinds? Global Economic Impact

India's GDP Growth Could Erode by 1 Percentage Point if Iran War Persists

An EY analysis projects that India's real GDP growth for the next fiscal year could decline by approximately one percentage point while retail inflation could rise by 1.5 percentage points if the Middle East conflict continues. India imports roughly 85% of its crude oil, making it one of the most exposed major economies. Similar calculations are being made by finance ministries across Asia and Europe, where energy dependency remains high.

EY

The Tariff Overhang: 15% Global Duties Continue to Weigh on Trade

Beyond the Iran war, the 15% global tariff regime imposed by the Trump administration continues to dampen trade flows and corporate earnings visibility. Companies across sectors have struggled to pass through costs, and the tariffs compound the energy shock by adding another inflationary layer. With no indication of policy reversal, the tariff headwind is now priced in as a semi-permanent feature of the economic landscape.
