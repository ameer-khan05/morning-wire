Bitcoin's War-Torn Quarter: Down 19% YTD as Geopolitics Crushes Risk Assets

Bitcoin ends Q1 2026 in the red, closing today at approximately $66,410 — down 19% year-to-date and sitting 43% below its all-time high set in October 2025. The quarter's defining narrative has been the Iran war, which pushed oil above $100 starting February 28, sent inflation expectations surging, and forced the Fed to revise its 2026 forecast from 2.4% to 2.7%. Rate cut expectations have been pushed to December at the earliest.

The correlation between crypto and traditional risk assets has tightened under the geopolitical pressure. Bitcoin's brief rally to $72,000 last week faded within days as oil spiked again, and the $70,000 threshold has become a key psychological battleground. Roughly $296 million in spot ETF outflows in recent sessions illustrate the risk-off sentiment, though March as a whole saw $2.5 billion in net inflows — effectively reversing four months of outflows totaling $8.39 billion.

The question heading into Q2 is whether a ceasefire in Iran — which futures markets increasingly expect — could unlock a relief rally, or whether structural headwinds from tariffs and higher-for-longer rates will continue to suppress prices. The 15% global tariff overhang has weighed on risk assets all year, and there's been nothing to absorb selling when it comes.

Regulation & Policy

SEC & CFTC Jointly Classify 16 Crypto Assets as Digital Commodities — A Decade of Ambiguity Ends

In what may be the most significant regulatory event in crypto history, the SEC and CFTC published a joint 68-page interpretive document on March 17 classifying Bitcoin, Ethereum, Solana, XRP, and 12 other digital assets as digital commodities. The ruling shifts spot market jurisdiction to the CFTC, unblocks the ETF pipeline for all 16 tokens, and confirms that staking is not a securities transaction. After a decade of regulatory uncertainty, the industry finally has a clear framework.

Kraken Financial Becomes First Digital Asset Bank with Fed Master Account

The Kansas City Fed approved a limited-purpose master account for Kraken Financial, granting the first digital asset bank direct access to Fedwire — the Federal Reserve's real-time gross settlement system. This is a milestone for crypto-banking integration, signaling that the Fed is willing to bring crypto-native institutions into the traditional banking infrastructure.

GENIUS Act Implementation Underway — California's Digital Asset License Takes Effect July 1

Treasury is working on regulations for the GENIUS Act (signed by President Trump in July 2025), with full implementation targeted for 2026 and rules finalized by July 18. Meanwhile, California's Digital Financial Assets Law goes live July 1, requiring anyone conducting digital financial asset business with California residents to obtain a state license — adding another compliance layer for U.S. exchanges.

Altcoin Landscape

Ethereum Breaks Below $2,000 — Solana Down 72% from Peak

The altcoin market is under severe pressure. Ethereum has fallen below $2,000 for the first time since mid-2024, while Solana has been hit hardest — down 72% from its 2025 peak with on-chain activity declining alongside price. Solana network transactions fell 3.2% and active addresses dropped 11% over the past month. XRP is testing $1.28 support. The silver lining: all of these assets now have regulatory clarity as digital commodities, which should support long-term institutional adoption.

Institutional & Adoption

Jack Dorsey's Square Auto-Enables Bitcoin Payments for Millions of U.S. Businesses

Square has rolled out automatic Bitcoin payment acceptance for millions of U.S. small businesses using its point-of-sale system. The feature converts BTC to dollars by default, removing friction for merchants who don't want crypto exposure while expanding Bitcoin's real-world utility as a payment network. It's the largest single merchant-side crypto deployment to date.

Security & Threats

Quantum Computing Threat Closer Than Expected: 10,000 Qubits Could Empty Crypto Wallets

New research suggests quantum computers may need just 10,000 qubits — far fewer than previously estimated — to break Bitcoin and Ethereum wallet encryption. While current quantum computers are still below this threshold, the timeline has compressed dramatically. The finding adds urgency to post-quantum cryptography migration efforts already underway at major blockchain projects, and raises fundamental questions about the long-term security model of existing chains.
