Lead Story

Artemis II: a record-breaking flight, and a hinge moment for human spaceflight

The crew of Artemis II — NASA's Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover and Christina Koch, and CSA's Jeremy Hansen — splashed down on April 10 after ten days in space, closing out a mission that rewrote the record books and reopened the era of human lunar exploration. On April 6, the four astronauts crossed the line set by Apollo 13 in 1970, reaching 248,655 miles from Earth — farther than any human crew in history.

The flyby carried new human-rated systems, tested life support under deep-space radiation, and produced a single image, since titled "Earthset," that may become the decade's defining photograph. In it, a crescent Earth rises above the lunar horizon — an inversion of Apollo 8's "Earthrise," taken almost exactly 58 years earlier.

Artemis II was a test flight. The next step — Artemis III — will attempt a crewed landing near the lunar south pole. What this mission proved is that the vehicle works, the crew works, and the political will, for now at least, works too.
