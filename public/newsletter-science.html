Lead Story

"Mirror" Molecule Can Starve Cancer Cells Without Harming Healthy Ones

Scientists have discovered that D-cysteine — a rare mirror-image version of the amino acid cysteine — can dramatically slow the growth of certain cancers while leaving healthy cells largely untouched. The molecule is preferentially taken up by cancer cells and shuts down a crucial mitochondrial enzyme they rely on for energy production and DNA maintenance. This represents a fundamentally new approach to targeted cancer therapy — starving tumors at the metabolic level rather than attacking them with toxic chemotherapy.

Protein Loss in Small Cell Lung Cancer Triggers Pro-Tumor Inflammation

Scientists discovered that losing a key protein in small cell lung cancer triggers an inflammatory response that paradoxically helps tumors grow and spread. Understanding this mechanism opens a new avenue for immunotherapy approaches that target the tumor's inflammatory environment rather than the cancer cells directly.

New Blood Test Could Catch Pancreatic Cancer Far Earlier

Researchers identified two previously unknown proteins that could form the basis of a new blood test for early pancreatic cancer detection. Pancreatic cancer has one of the lowest survival rates precisely because it's typically caught late — a reliable early-detection blood test could dramatically change outcomes for this devastating disease.

Blood Protein Shapes May Detect Alzheimer's Years Before Symptoms

Researchers found that subtle structural changes in three blood proteins closely track Alzheimer's progression. Unlike existing biomarkers that measure protein quantities, this approach detects shape changes — a more sensitive signal that could identify the disease years before cognitive symptoms appear.

Alzheimer's ScienceDaily

Scientists Find Hidden "Death Switch" Driving Alzheimer's — and Turn It Off in Mice

A separate team uncovered a previously unknown cellular mechanism — a "death switch" — that triggers neuronal death in Alzheimer's disease. In a promising proof of concept, they successfully deactivated this switch in mouse models, slowing disease progression. The finding offers a new therapeutic target distinct from the amyloid plaques that current drugs focus on.

Neuroscience ScienceDaily

Depression May Be a Metabolic Disorder, Not Just a Chemical Imbalance

Brain and blood cells in young adults with major depressive disorder produce more energy molecules at rest but struggle to increase energy when under stress. This reframes depression as potentially a metabolic or energy-regulation problem — a paradigm shift from the "chemical imbalance" model that has dominated for decades. It suggests entirely new treatment approaches targeting cellular energy pathways.

Vitamin B3 Shuts Down Key Genetic Driver of Fatty Liver Disease

Researchers identified microRNA-93 as a key genetic driver of non-alcoholic fatty liver disease and discovered that vitamin B3 (niacin) can effectively suppress it. Given that fatty liver disease affects roughly 25% of the global population, a cheap and widely available intervention like B3 could have enormous public health implications.

Metformin Works Through the Brain — Not Just the Body

The diabetes drug metformin was found to lower blood sugar through a previously hidden brain pathway — switching off a key protein and activating specific neurons. This dual mechanism explains why metformin has such broad health effects beyond glucose control, including potential anti-aging properties, and opens the door to more targeted treatments.

Diabetes ScienceDaily

NAD+ Emerges as Key Molecule in Aging Research

A global team of scientists is zeroing in on NAD+ — a molecule crucial for keeping cells energized and repairing DNA damage. As we age, NAD+ levels decline sharply, and researchers believe boosting it could reshape how we age. The research has attracted significant longevity-focused funding and is moving toward clinical trials.
