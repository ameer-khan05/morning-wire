Astronomers traced a high-energy cosmic neutrino to a galaxy nicknamed Shadow Blaster located 11 billion light-years away — discovering the particle originated from extreme star formation rather than a black hole, published June 19 in Nature Astronomy, with researchers estimating that 20% of the universe's highest-energy neutrinos may originate from similar galaxies.

The Shadow Blaster paper is the kind of finding that revises what scientists look for, not just what they find. For decades the field's working assumption was that the most energetic cosmic neutrinos come from the violent environments around supermassive black holes — active galactic nuclei, blazars, quasars, where matter falling into the black hole produces jets energetic enough to accelerate neutrinos to the extreme velocities that IceCube detects in Antarctica. The Shadow Blaster discovery traces a specific high-energy neutrino to a galaxy that has no bright active black hole. JCMT0402−0424 is a compact, dust-shrouded starburst — a galaxy forming stars at hundreds of times the Milky Way's rate, brilliantly bright in infrared but invisible at optical wavelengths, hidden from virtually every survey that searches for neutrino-producing black holes. If 20% of the universe's high-energy neutrinos come from galaxies like this, then all the searches conducted in blazar catalogs and quasar surveys over the past decade have been looking in the right direction but missing an entire category of source. IceCube-Gen2, the next-generation neutrino observatory being planned now, will need to add infrared-bright starburst galaxies to its target catalog — a structural revision to the search strategy that this paper makes unavoidable. Two other science stories this week also revise prior assumptions at a more practical scale: new research finds that five daily fruit and vegetable servings may not deliver the full cardiovascular benefit the recommendation implies without including flavanol-rich foods specifically, and a June 20 study finds that cross-species animal communication is more flexible and sophisticated than the prior model of fixed, species-specific signals assumed.

The Anchor

A cosmic neutrino came from a star-forming galaxy 11 billion light-years away — not a black hole. Five servings of fruit and vegetables may not be enough for your heart. Animals talk across species more flexibly than we knew. This week's science is about what the prior model missed.

The Shadow Blaster detection required years of multi-wavelength follow-up after IceCube recorded neutrino IC-210922A as one of the highest-energy particles ever to pass through Earth. Tracing it to a specific source — rather than a direction in the sky — required JCMT radio telescope observations to identify infrared-bright candidates, followed by ALMA high-resolution imaging to confirm the compact starburst structure of JCMT0402−0424. The result is the strongest association yet established between a single galaxy and a specific high-energy neutrino event. The galaxy's characteristics explain why it was invisible to prior searches: it is completely obscured at optical wavelengths by dense clouds of gas and dust that fuel the star formation, making it absent from optical surveys of active galactic nuclei and blazars that define the standard search catalog. Only infrared and radio observations reveal it — and those searches were not being conducted with neutrino source detection in mind. The 20% estimate for the contribution of Shadow Blaster-type galaxies to the total neutrino flux is a lower bound based on this single detection; the true fraction could be higher as the search expands to include the broader population of compact infrared starbursts.

The flavanol finding matters practically for the hundreds of millions of people who have adopted the five-servings-a-day recommendation as a sufficient cardiovascular intervention. New research published in June establishes that the benefit is not uniformly distributed across all fruits and vegetables: foods with high flavanol content — blueberries, strawberries, apples, cocoa, green tea — deliver disproportionate cardiovascular benefit relative to low-flavanol options like iceberg lettuce, carrots, or most cooked vegetables. Eating five servings daily of predominantly low-flavanol items does not appear to deliver the same heart health outcome as eating five servings that include flavanol-rich foods. The practical implication is a more specific dietary recommendation: not just "eat five servings" but "eat five servings including flavanol-rich foods," a distinction with real population health consequences.

Astronomers traced high-energy neutrino IC-210922A — among the most energetic particles ever detected — back 11 billion light-years to a dusty starburst galaxy nicknamed Shadow Blaster (JCMT0402−0424), finding that the neutrino originated from extreme star formation rather than from a supermassive black hole, in a paper published June 19 in Nature Astronomy. The IceCube Neutrino Observatory in Antarctica detected the particle; JCMT and ALMA radio telescope follow-up confirmed its origin in a compact, dust-shrouded galaxy invisible at optical wavelengths but brilliantly bright in infrared. Researchers estimate that galaxies like Shadow Blaster could account for approximately 20% of all high-energy neutrinos in the universe — a fraction that was entirely absent from the field's prior source models, which focused on active galactic nuclei and blazars.

The detection mechanism in Shadow Blaster is distinct from the black-hole jet process that produces neutrinos around active galactic nuclei. In the Shadow Blaster model, cosmic ray protons — accelerated within the dense interstellar medium of the starburst — collide with interstellar gas clouds and produce pions that decay into neutrinos. The same fundamental physics underlies both processes, but the driver is the density and turbulence of star-forming gas rather than the energy of a black hole's accretion disk. The practical implication for IceCube-Gen2 is a broader target catalog: infrared-bright, optically obscured compact starburst galaxies at high redshift must now be added as a source class alongside the blazars and quasars that previous searches prioritized. The universe may have many more neutrino factories than the prior model accounted for.

Flavanols: Five Servings Not Enough · Heart Health Requires Flavanol-Rich Foods · Berries, Cocoa, Green Tea Flavanols Finding: five daily fruit + vegetable servings insufficient for full heart health without flavanol-rich foods · flavanols: plant compound subclass · high-flavanol foods: blueberries, strawberries, apples, cocoa/dark chocolate, green tea, red wine · low-flavanol foods: cooked vegetables, root vegetables, most leafy greens · prior recommendation: 5 servings of any fruit/veg · updated finding: composition of those 5 servings determines cardiovascular benefit · mechanism: flavanols → endothelial function improvement + blood pressure reduction + inflammation reduction · implication: public health guidelines need to specify flavanol content not just serving count · research: June 19, 2026

New research published June 19 found that eating five daily servings of fruits and vegetables may not be enough for optimal heart health if those servings lack flavanol-rich foods, qualifying one of public health nutrition's most consistent recommendations. Flavanols — a specific subclass of plant compounds found at high concentrations in berries, apples, cocoa, and green tea — appear to drive a disproportionate share of the cardiovascular benefit that the five-servings recommendation is designed to deliver. Five servings of predominantly low-flavanol vegetables do not produce the same measurable improvements in endothelial function, blood pressure, and inflammatory markers as five servings that include flavanol-dense options. The finding is consistent with a growing body of research that has moved nutrition science away from simple count-based dietary guidelines toward more specific compositional recommendations.

The practical implication is a targeted refinement of advice that hundreds of millions of people already follow. People eating five servings of carrots, lettuce, and cooked vegetables may be receiving less cardiovascular benefit than they expect. The flavanol-rich foods that deliver the most measurable heart health benefit — blueberries, strawberries, raw apples, dark chocolate with high cocoa content, and green tea — are accessible and affordable for most populations but are not always the default choices when people try to hit a five-servings-a-day target. Public health guidelines from the American Heart Association, NHS, and equivalent bodies will need to incorporate the flavanol specificity into their recommendations — not to replace the five-servings target but to qualify the composition of those servings in a way that makes the recommendation more actionable for heart disease prevention.

Interspecies Communication · Cross-Species Conversations More Flexible Than Assumed · June 20 Study Animals Finding: interspecies animal communication more flexible than prior fixed-signal model · prior model: species communicate via fixed, species-specific signals (alarm calls, food signals, distress vocalizations) → other species either learn them or don't · new finding: cross-species "conversations" are adaptive → animals adjust communication style depending on species they are interacting with · examples: alarm calls modified for audience species · food-finding cooperation · parasite-removal signaling between species · published: June 20, 2026 · significance: interspecies communication is a sophisticated, learned behavior not just a fixed code → implies greater cognitive flexibility than prior model assumed · implications for animal cognition research and conservation biology

Research published June 20 found that interspecies animal communication — the "conversations" between animals of different species when coordinating feeding, finding food, cleaning parasites, or gaining protection — is more flexible and adaptive than the prior model of fixed species-specific signals assumed. Animals from different species often rely on surprisingly sophisticated communication to work together across a range of cooperative behaviors. The new study found that these cross-species exchanges are not just animals passively receiving fixed signals from another species but are active, adaptive communications that animals adjust depending on which species they are interacting with, how familiar those species are, and what the cooperative goal is.

The finding has implications for both animal cognition research and conservation biology. If interspecies communication is a learned, flexible behavior rather than a fixed code, it suggests a degree of cognitive flexibility across a broader range of species than the prior model credited. For conservation, the finding matters because ecosystem disruption affects interspecies communication networks: when a keystone species is removed or a population is isolated, the interspecies communication relationships that support cooperative feeding, predator detection, and habitat maintenance are disrupted in ways that simple population counts do not capture. Understanding the flexibility and fragility of those communication networks adds a dimension to conservation biology that ecological models based on species counts and food webs have not fully incorporated.

IceCube-Gen2 Implications · Search Strategy Revision · Starburst Galaxy Target Class Added IceCube IceCube Observatory: South Pole, Antarctica · current instrument: 1 cubic km of ice instrumented · detections: ~300 high-energy neutrino events identified · source attribution: historically difficult → most events directionless · IC-210922A: one of strongest source associations yet established · IceCube-Gen2: planned upgrade to ~8 cubic km · planned search strategy: active galactic nuclei + blazars (prior to Shadow Blaster) · revised strategy needed: add infrared-bright starburst galaxies to target catalog · implication: if 20% of neutrinos from starbursts → Gen2 must search different galaxy classes → multi-wavelength catalog expansion required → collaboration with infrared observatories (JWST, Spitzer successors) · timeline: Gen2 planning phase ongoing

The Shadow Blaster detection has direct implications for IceCube-Gen2, the planned next-generation neutrino observatory that will instrument approximately 8 cubic kilometers of Antarctic ice — eight times IceCube's current volume — to detect far larger numbers of high-energy neutrino events. The Gen2 project has been developing its search strategies based on the assumption that active galactic nuclei and blazars are the primary source class; that assumption guided decisions about which catalogs to cross-reference, which sky regions to prioritize, and which multi-wavelength follow-up observatories to partner with. The Shadow Blaster result requires an addition to that strategy: infrared-bright, optically obscured compact starburst galaxies at high redshift must be added as a target class.

The practical challenge for Gen2 is that starburst galaxies are most easily identified at infrared and radio wavelengths, not at the optical wavelengths of the sky surveys that neutrino search strategies have historically been built on. Adding infrared starburst galaxies to the Gen2 target catalog requires partnerships with infrared observatories — JWST is the most capable current instrument — and possibly the construction of dedicated infrared sky surveys designed to identify the compact, high-redshift starburst galaxies that Shadow Blaster represents. The 20% contribution estimate from Shadow Blaster-type sources, if confirmed by additional detections, would justify this infrastructure investment. Finding the second and third Shadow Blasters in IceCube data — now that the search team knows what infrared signatures to look for — is the next observational step.

Quick hits

  • The neutrino source question is fundamentally a question about where the universe's most extreme particle accelerators are. Everything we know about cosmic ray and neutrino production points toward environments where matter is being violently accelerated — whether by the gravitational energy of a black hole's accretion disk or by the shock waves propagating through a dense starburst's gas clouds. What the Shadow Blaster finding adds to the model is the starburst pathway: in the densest star-forming environments, where molecular clouds are colliding at high velocities, cosmic ray protons are scattered and accelerated by the same turbulent magnetic fields that shape the star formation itself. The resulting proton-proton collisions that produce neutrinos are statistically rare but enormously frequent in aggregate given the sheer mass of gas involved in a compact starburst. Extrapolating to the population of all compact starburst galaxies across cosmic history — which number in the billions — the 20% contribution estimate becomes plausible and may be conservative.
  • The interspecies communication finding fits into a broader revision in animal cognition research that has been underway for roughly two decades. The prior model — sometimes called the "fixed action pattern" framework — held that animal communication signals are largely innate and species-specific, transmitted genetically rather than learned. The growing body of research on animal communication flexibility — dolphins that modify their vocalizations for different audiences, birds that use conspecific alarm calls for other species, cleaner fish that negotiate with client species through gesture sequences — challenges the fixed-action-pattern model at its core. The June 20 study adds evidence that even the cross-species dimension of this flexibility is more structured and adaptive than the prior model predicted. The implication for how we understand intelligence is significant: if communication flexibility is widely distributed across the animal kingdom, the cognitive capacities underlying that flexibility may be similarly widespread — a conclusion with implications for how conservation policy values animal welfare across non-human species.
