A JAMA Health Forum study of 463,396 adolescents found that teen cannabis use roughly doubles the risk of psychotic and bipolar disorders — with diagnoses following use by nearly two years — while a separate study identified a vitamin B12–based compound that can cross the blood-brain barrier and home in on glioblastoma tumors in animal models, and a two-year randomized trial found fish oil supplements delivered omega-3s to the brain but produced no meaningful cognitive or Alzheimer's benefits.

The cannabis finding is the largest longitudinal study yet of adolescent marijuana use and mental health outcomes — 463,396 teens followed from ages 13–17 through age 26 by researchers from Kaiser Permanente, UCSF, the University of Southern California, and the Public Health Institute — and it arrives as recreational cannabis legalization has expanded access across most US states, where the product is now marketed with scant regulatory restriction on adolescent exposure to advertising and retail. The glioblastoma-targeting B12 compound addresses one of the most persistent obstacles in treating the deadliest primary brain cancer: delivering therapeutic agents through the blood-brain barrier to tumor tissue specifically rather than dispersing them throughout the brain. The fish oil null result is the most definitive answer yet to one of consumer health's most commercially significant questions, with tens of millions of people taking omega-3 supplements primarily for perceived brain health benefits.

Teen Cannabis Use Doubles Psychosis and Bipolar Risk · 463K JAMA Study · 2-Year Lag (Use Precedes Diagnosis) · Kaiser Permanente · UCSF 2× JAMA Health Forum 2026: 463,396 adolescents ages 13–17 → followed to age 26 · past-year cannabis use: 2× psychotic disorder risk · 2× bipolar disorder risk · depression + anxiety elevated · cannabis use precedes diagnosis by ~2 years · Kaiser Permanente + UCSF + USC + Public Health Institute · THC disrupts dopaminergic signaling in developing adolescent brain

A JAMA Health Forum study followed 463,396 adolescents ages 13 to 17 through age 26 and found that past-year cannabis use during adolescence was associated with roughly doubled risk of developing incident psychotic disorders and doubled risk of developing bipolar disorders, with cannabis use preceding psychiatric diagnoses by nearly two years in the study sample. Elevated risk was also found for depressive and anxiety disorders, though at lower effect sizes than for the psychotic and bipolar diagnoses. The study was conducted by researchers from Kaiser Permanente, the Public Health Institute's Getting It Right from the Start initiative, UCSF, and the University of Southern California — representing one of the largest and most rigorously designed longitudinal examinations of adolescent cannabis use and psychiatric outcomes ever conducted. The two-year temporal gap between cannabis use and subsequent diagnosis strengthens the concern that the relationship is causal rather than purely correlational: if pre-existing psychiatric risk were simply driving adolescents toward cannabis use, you would expect diagnoses to precede or coincide with use rather than follow it by two years.

The THC-specific neurobiological mechanism is consistent with the psychiatric outcomes observed. Delta-9-tetrahydrocannabinol — the primary psychoactive compound in cannabis, and the compound that has increased dramatically in concentration in commercially available products since legalization — disrupts dopaminergic signaling in the developing adolescent brain. Both psychotic disorders (schizophrenia spectrum) and bipolar disorder have established dopaminergic components: antipsychotics used in both conditions work primarily through dopamine receptor blockade. Adolescent brains are in active dopaminergic system development through the mid-20s, which may explain why the psychiatric risk is concentrated in adolescent users rather than adult users at the same exposure levels. The study arrives as cannabis marketing — including influencer-driven social media content and dispensary retail — reaches adolescents through channels that state-level minimum age restrictions at the point of sale do not address.

B12-Based Compound Crosses Blood-Brain Barrier · Targets Glioblastoma Tumors · Delivers Nitric Oxide · Animal Study · ScienceDaily Jun 22 B12 BBB ScienceDaily Jun 22 2026: B12-based compound crosses blood-brain barrier via transcobalamin receptor · accumulates preferentially in glioblastoma tumor tissue · delivers sustained nitric oxide · anti-tumor + anti-angiogenic · animal study · glioblastoma: deadliest primary brain cancer · median survival 14–16 months · potential drug delivery platform for other BBB-impermeant therapeutics

Researchers have identified a vitamin B12–based compound that can cross the blood-brain barrier — one of the primary obstacles to treating glioblastoma, the most common and deadliest primary brain tumor — and accumulate preferentially in glioblastoma tumor tissue rather than dispersing through surrounding healthy brain tissue, delivering sustained nitric oxide to cancer cells in animal studies. The mechanism exploits a biological transport system that the blood-brain barrier uses for vitamin B12: B12 crosses the barrier via active transcobalamin receptor–mediated transport, and the B12-based compound uses this same pathway to piggyback across the BBB that would otherwise block large molecules from reaching brain tissue. The preferential accumulation in tumor tissue — rather than in healthy brain — is the finding's most clinically significant aspect: it suggests the compound can achieve a therapeutic concentration in the cancer while limiting exposure in surrounding tissue, a property that improves therapeutic index and reduces the collateral neurological damage that has constrained glioblastoma treatment options.

Glioblastoma's treatment resistance stems in part from the blood-brain barrier's efficiency at excluding most chemotherapeutic agents. Current standard-of-care for glioblastoma — surgical resection, radiation, temozolomide chemotherapy, and tumor treating fields — has a median overall survival of approximately 15 months from diagnosis, with the five-year survival rate below 10%. The major bottleneck for new therapeutic approaches is not a lack of candidate compounds that kill glioblastoma cells in vitro — there are many — but a lack of compounds that can reach tumor tissue at therapeutic concentrations in a living brain without causing unacceptable systemic or neurological toxicity. A B12-based delivery vehicle that uses the BBB's own transport machinery to carry therapeutic payload specifically to tumor tissue addresses this bottleneck at the mechanism level. The compound delivers nitric oxide, which has established anti-tumor properties including the ability to induce cancer cell apoptosis and to sensitize tumor cells to other therapeutic modalities. Animal studies are the necessary preclinical step before human trials.

Fish Oil 2-Year Trial: Omega-3s Reach Brain — But No Memory, Cognition, or Alzheimer's Benefits · Major Null Result for Common Supplement No Benefit 2-year RCT: fish oil (EPA + DHA) · omega-3s confirmed delivered to brain · but no improvement: memory · cognition · Alzheimer's-related biomarkers vs placebo · major null result for widely used supplement · does not rule out cardiovascular benefits · does undercut the neurological rationale that drives most consumer use

A two-year randomized controlled trial of fish oil supplementation found that omega-3 fatty acids — EPA and DHA, the biologically active components of fish oil — were successfully delivered to the brain in participants who took the supplements, confirming that the dose was adequate and that the supplements worked as pharmacological delivery vehicles for omega-3s to brain tissue. Despite that successful delivery, the trial found no meaningful improvements in memory, cognition, or Alzheimer's disease–related brain changes — including relevant biomarkers — compared to participants who received placebo over the same two-year period. The finding is the most rigorous null result yet for one of the world's most widely used dietary supplements, sold globally with implicit and sometimes explicit claims about brain health, cognitive protection, and Alzheimer's risk reduction. The trial's two-year duration is sufficient to detect effects of the magnitude that would be clinically relevant; a two-year null result with confirmed brain delivery is a definitive negative on the brain health rationale for fish oil supplementation.

The null result does not rule out all benefits of fish oil supplementation. Omega-3 fatty acids have substantial evidence for cardiovascular benefits, anti-inflammatory effects, and specific applications in conditions including certain forms of hypertriglyceridemia — none of which are affected by this trial's findings on cognitive outcomes. What the trial does substantially undercut is the neurological rationale that is the primary consumer motivation for fish oil supplementation in most age groups, particularly older adults who take fish oil specifically for cognitive protection and Alzheimer's prevention. The delivery confirmation is particularly important: prior negative fish oil brain health trials could be explained by insufficient bioavailability (the omega-3s weren't actually reaching the brain in therapeutic amounts); this trial eliminates that explanation. Omega-3s reached the brain; they did not improve cognition or Alzheimer's-related measures. The fish oil brain health rationale now lacks both a 2-year RCT efficacy finding and its primary confound defense.

Fructose vs Glucose: Brain Treats Them Very Differently · Same Nutrition Label · Reward Pathways + Satiety Signals Diverge · New Study Fructose New study: fructose and glucose appear identical on nutrition labels (both = 4 kcal/g "sugar") · brain processes them differently · fructose: different hypothalamic response · weaker satiety signal suppression · different dopaminergic reward response · fructose metabolized primarily by liver (de novo lipogenesis) vs glucose metabolized across tissues · labeling gap: "added sugar" does not distinguish fructose from glucose

A new study found that fructose and glucose — two sugars that appear identically on nutrition labels as "sugar" at 4 calories per gram — are processed by the brain through fundamentally different pathways, with divergent effects on satiety signaling, reward circuitry, and hormonal responses. Fructose stimulates hypothalamic brain regions differently than glucose, may not suppress appetite-stimulating hormones as effectively, and may produce different dopaminergic reward responses — potentially creating a drive for further consumption that glucose does not generate to the same degree. The difference matters clinically because fructose and glucose are not interchangeable in the modern diet: high-fructose corn syrup, used in soft drinks, processed foods, and sweetened beverages, delivers fructose at concentrations and without the fiber matrix that natural fruit sources provide, where the glucose-to-fructose ratio differs substantially from the equal-parts mix in sucrose.

The practical implication is a labeling gap: nutrition labels that report total "sugar" without distinguishing fructose from glucose obscure a metabolically and neurologically meaningful difference. Fructose is metabolized primarily by the liver, where excess fructose is converted to fat through de novo lipogenesis — a pathway that glucose does not preferentially activate at equivalent doses. This is why fructose-heavy diets have been associated with non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, metabolic syndrome, and insulin resistance at levels that glucose-equivalent calorie intake does not produce to the same degree. The brain findings add a neurological dimension to what was previously understood primarily as a hepatic and metabolic concern: the brain's different response to fructose and glucose suggests that the neurological drive to consume more after a fructose-sweetened beverage may differ from the drive after a glucose-sweetened one, with implications for appetite regulation and dietary behavior that are not captured by calorie equivalence on a nutrition label.
