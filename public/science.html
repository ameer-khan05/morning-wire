The Anchor

NIH is $1 billion behind on grants — and the odds of winning one at NCI just fell from 1-in-10 to 1-in-25.

The National Institutes of Health, the nation's largest funder of biomedical research, is approximately $1 billion behind its typical timeline for awarding new grants this year, according to a report published in Nature. The $1 billion gap represents not just a funding number but a cascade of compounding failures: White House Office of Management and Budget spending holds delayed access to newly appropriated funds; a 22% staffing cut last year left fewer personnel to process and administer grants; and a new policy requiring that multi-year grants be funded in a single upfront lump sum — rather than year by year — is sharply lowering the probability that any given application will succeed. At the National Cancer Institute, the odds of winning a grant have fallen from approximately 1-in-10 to 1-in-25. A national survey of NIH-funded researchers found that 81% of junior tenure-track scientists are very or somewhat concerned that funding disruptions could threaten their chances of earning tenure — and dozens of labs are on track to shut permanently.

The funding crisis lands alongside a significant diagnostic advance in Alzheimer's research. A study from NYU Langone analyzing data from nearly 400,000 patients across two healthcare systems found that elevated neutrophil levels — the immune system's first-responder white blood cells — relative to other immune cells on a routine complete blood count can predict an increased risk of Alzheimer's disease and related dementias years before clinical symptoms appear. The finding represents the first large-scale investigation to validate the predictive power of neutrophil measurement for neurodegeneration. The neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio (NLR) is already obtained from complete blood cell counts used routinely in clinical practice, meaning the early-detection window it opens requires no new tests — only a reinterpretation of data clinicians are already collecting.

Alzheimer's · Blood Test Years early Neutrophil NLR · 400K patients · NYU Langone

Routine blood test can flag Alzheimer's risk years before symptoms — neutrophil levels are the marker.

A NYU Langone study of nearly 400,000 patients across two healthcare systems found that higher neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio (NLR) — easily derived from a standard complete blood cell count — is significantly associated with future Alzheimer's and dementia risk. The finding is the first large-scale validation that neutrophil measurement has predictive power for neurodegeneration. The association was stronger in Hispanic patients and women. Researchers caution that neutrophils may be a marker of, rather than a cause of, Alzheimer's progression — but the clinical implication is clear: early-risk identification using data already routinely collected.

Source: NYU Langone · ScienceDaily
Fatty Liver · Drug Combo Dramatic Two existing drugs · liver fat · heart risk

Two existing drugs combined dramatically reduce liver fat — and cut heart-related risks in fatty liver disease.

Scientists have found that combining two drugs already approved for other conditions can dramatically reduce liver fat in patients with metabolic-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD), one of the most common liver conditions globally. The combination showed significant reduction in both liver fat content and markers of heart-related risk, while requiring lower doses of each drug than when used individually — reducing side-effect concerns. The approach could offer a near-term treatment pathway without waiting for novel drug approvals, as both drugs are already in widespread clinical use and generically available.

Melanoma · HOXD13 New target Tumor immune evasion · blood supply · T-cells

HOXD13 protein identified as key driver of melanoma immune evasion — a new therapeutic target.

A new study identifies the HOXD13 protein as a key factor enabling melanoma tumors to grow and evade the immune system: HOXD13 boosts the tumor's blood supply while simultaneously blocking the activity of cancer-fighting T-cells. The two-pronged mechanism — promoting growth while suppressing immunity — makes HOXD13 an especially high-value therapeutic target, as inhibiting it would address both the vascular and immunological defenses tumors deploy. Researchers are developing small-molecule inhibitors of HOXD13 activity, with preclinical testing ongoing.

Source: SciTechDaily · ScienceDaily
Olive Oil · Brain 2 years Cognitive protection · gut-brain axis · study

Two-year study: daily extra virgin olive oil significantly improves cognitive performance through the gut.

A two-year randomized study found that people who consumed extra virgin olive oil daily had significantly better cognitive performance and more diverse gut microbiome composition than those using refined olive oil — suggesting the mechanism of cognitive protection runs through the gut-brain axis rather than direct neurological effects. Polyphenols unique to extra virgin olive oil appear to selectively promote beneficial gut bacteria that produce compounds with neuroprotective properties. Researchers say the finding strengthens the case for dietary-level interventions as a complement to pharmacological approaches in Alzheimer's and cognitive decline prevention.

Source: ScienceDaily · Medical News Today
World Hormone Day April 24 Global endocrine health · awareness

World Hormone Day April 24 — global endocrine health awareness amid GLP-1 sub-dose research advances.

Today, April 24, marks World Hormone Day, an annual global awareness campaign organized by the endocrine community to highlight the role of hormones in health and disease. This year's edition arrives amid accelerating GLP-1 research: scientists are investigating whether the metabolic and weight-loss effects of Ozempic-class drugs can be replicated at doses smaller than those in current approved protocols — a line of inquiry that could dramatically expand therapeutic access. Mass General Brigham projects GLP-1 prescriptions will overtake statins by 2028 based on current adoption trajectories. The FDA also fast-tracked a new tau-PET Alzheimer's diagnostic agent for accelerated review earlier this month.
