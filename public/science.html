A Cyclospora parasite outbreak has reached 1,645 CDC-confirmed cases across 31 states — 5,100+ more under investigation — traced to shredded iceberg lettuce at Taco Bell via a specific supplier (Washington Post). CDC Health Alert Network notice: test specifically for Cyclospora cayetanensis in unresolved intestinal illness. Case count 6.6x the 2025 equivalent period.

Cyclospora cayetanensis causes watery diarrhea, nausea, and weight loss persisting weeks without antibiotic treatment. The CDC advisory's key clinical point: Cyclospora is absent from standard stool pathogen panels, and effective treatment is TMP-SMX — not metronidazole (ineffective). The 31-state spread reflects centralized supply chain amplification: a single contaminated shredding facility distributing to Taco Bell's national network constitutes a national exposure event. The 5,100+ cases under investigation suggest the final confirmed total will substantially exceed 1,645.

An experimental treatment may have found a way to overcome the blood-brain barrier that defeats most glioblastoma therapies — ScienceDaily: may "outsmart glioblastoma's toughest defense." Potential expansion of pharmacological options for a cancer with ~15-month median survival under current standard of care.

GBM faces two converging challenges: aggressive tumor biology and the blood-brain barrier's prevention of drug delivery. The BBB — tight-junction proteins lining brain capillary endothelial cells — forces a choice between BBB-permeable molecules (limited potency) and potent molecules (BBB-impermeable). The new approach targets the delivery problem rather than just tumor biology. If reliable drug delivery to GBM at therapeutic concentrations is achievable, it unlocks a large library of agents that showed preclinical efficacy but could not be deployed because of the barrier.

Researchers identified claudin-4 — a tight-junction protein in the colon epithelium — as the receptor a bacterial toxin binds before damaging colon tissue, SciTechDaily: resolves "a long-standing mystery behind how a bacterial toxin associated with colorectal cancer damages the colon." Creates a defined molecular target for drug development and diagnostics.

Identifying the binding receptor converts the mechanism from "associated with" to "mechanistically understood." The bacterial toxin–colorectal cancer link had been established epidemiologically, but the molecular pathway was unknown — making rational drug design impossible. Claudin-4 as the binding receptor enables design of blocking agents (small molecules or biologics that prevent toxin binding without disrupting claudin-4's normal tight-junction function) and potential diagnostics based on claudin-4 expression. Colorectal cancer is the second leading cause of cancer death in the US; a mechanism-specific causal pathway is a significant drug development target.
