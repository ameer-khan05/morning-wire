Largest US teen cannabis study confirms THC slows memory in 11,000 adolescents — the data is longitudinal and biological, not correlational. A gut-reset procedure prevents post-Ozempic weight regain in first blinded trial.

UC San Diego's landmark Neuropsychopharmacology study — the largest longitudinal study of teen cannabis use in US history — tracked 11,000 participants from ages 9 to 17 using biological testing (hair, urine, and saliva samples alongside self-reports) to measure actual THC exposure, then mapped cognitive trajectories. The result: cannabis users' growth across memory, attention, language, and processing speed leveled off compared to non-using peers who continued improving — with THC specifically identified as the cognitive development suppressor by component analysis. The study closes the key methodological gap that prior cannabis-cognition research could not bridge: it uses biological confirmation of actual exposure, not just self-reports, in a sample large enough to separate cannabis effects from family background, mental health, and other substance use. Separately, the REMAIN-1 clinical trial's midpoint results show that duodenal mucosal resurfacing — an endoscopic "gut reset" that ablates the inner lining of the duodenum — allows people who stop Ozempic to retain more than 80% of their weight loss, regaining only 7 pounds compared to the 70%-of-lost-weight regain typical within 18 months of stopping GLP-1 drugs. The procedure represents the first blinded, randomized, sham-controlled evidence for maintaining GLP-1 benefits after discontinuation.

The Anchor

UCSD's 11,000-participant teen cannabis study — the largest in US history — confirms THC specifically slows adolescent memory development. Biological testing closed the methodological gap.

The Adolescent Brain Cognitive Development (ABCD) Study, led by Natasha Wade at UC San Diego School of Medicine and published in Neuropsychopharmacology in April 2026, is the study that the cannabis-cognition research field has been trying to produce for a decade. Its scale — 11,036 participants tracked from ages 9-10 through ages 16-17, with cognitive performance measured at multiple intervals — is the largest longitudinal cannabis-cognition study in US history. Its methodology is the key advance: instead of relying on self-reported cannabis use (which is subject to systematic underreporting by adolescents and their parents), the study combined self-reports with biological testing — hair, urine, and saliva samples that can detect cannabis exposure ranging from recent use to several months prior. The biological testing allowed the researchers to identify actual THC exposure in participants who had not self-reported cannabis use and to calibrate their exposure estimates against physical measurements rather than behavioral surveys. The result is a dataset that separates cannabis exposure from self-reporting bias, and maps it against longitudinal cognitive development trajectories for 11,000 people over approximately 7 years.

The findings across cognitive domains are consistent: teenagers who used cannabis showed restricted cognitive growth over time compared to non-using peers who continued to improve across memory, attention, language, and processing speed. The key component finding: when researchers isolated specific cannabis components, those who used THC showed worse memory development as they aged compared to nonusers. CBD did not show the same effect. The study controlled for family background, mental health, and use of other substances, as well as each participant's prior cognitive baseline — the standard confounds that prior cannabis-cognition research has struggled to separate. The result is not that THC-using teens performed worse on cognitive tests in a single session — it is that their cognitive trajectory over 7 years of development was flatter than their non-using peers, and that the flattening tracks directly to THC exposure confirmed by biological testing. The study does not prove that cannabis directly causes these developmental differences (the research design does not permit causal claims), but it is the strongest association evidence produced to date — in the largest sample, with the most rigorous exposure measurement methodology.

UCSD's 11,000-participant ABCD Study links THC to slower cognitive development across memory, attention, language, and processing speed — with biological testing confirmation that closes the self-reporting gap of prior research.

The ABCD Study's finding on cannabis and cognitive development has three distinct scientific contributions that distinguish it from prior research. First, scale: 11,036 participants, the largest sample ever used for this specific question, which enables statistical separation of cannabis effects from the dozens of confounding variables (family income, mental health, other substance use, baseline cognitive ability) that smaller studies cannot fully control. Second, methodology: biological testing that confirms actual THC exposure — including among participants who did not self-report cannabis use — eliminates the systematic underreporting bias that has made prior self-report studies potentially conservative in their effect estimates. Third, longitudinal design: the 7-year follow-up from ages 9-10 to 16-17 captures cognitive development trajectories rather than single-session performance, distinguishing between "THC affects cognition" (acute effect, which has been established for decades) and "THC affects how cognition develops" (developmental effect, which this study addresses). The THC-specific finding — that CBD does not show the same memory-development effect — has regulatory and public health implications: it frames the teen cannabis risk as specifically about THC concentration rather than cannabis broadly, which has implications for how age-restriction policies, product labeling, and harm-reduction messaging should be calibrated in states with legal cannabis markets.

Ozempic · Gut Reset · REMAIN-1 7 lbs Duodenal mucosal resurfacing · 7 lbs regained vs. typical 70% weight loss regain · REMAIN-1 · 300+ participants · Q4 2026 data

Gut-reset procedure prevents post-Ozempic weight regain in first blinded trial — participants who underwent duodenal mucosal resurfacing regained only 7 pounds after stopping semaglutide, preserving 80-plus percent of initial weight loss.

The REMAIN-1 clinical trial's midpoint results, published this week, provide the first blinded, randomized, sham-controlled evidence that duodenal mucosal resurfacing (DMR) can prevent the weight regain that affects approximately 70% of people who stop GLP-1 drugs like semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) within 18 months. In the midpoint analysis of 45 participants, those who underwent DMR before stopping semaglutide regained only approximately 7 pounds — preserving more than 80% of their initial weight loss. Participants who underwent a sham procedure (an endoscopic procedure that mimicked DMR but did not ablate duodenal tissue) regained far more weight, confirming the biological specificity of the DMR effect. The mechanism: DMR uses controlled thermal energy to ablate the inner lining of the duodenum (the first section of the small intestine), stimulating regrowth of healthier tissue that appears to preserve the metabolic changes induced by GLP-1 drugs — particularly changes in insulin sensitivity and gut hormone signaling. The full REMAIN-1 trial has more than 300 participants and is fully enrolled; researchers expect topline 6-month data from the pivotal cohort in Q4 2026, followed by a planned marketing submission. If the full trial confirms the midpoint findings, DMR becomes the first evidence-based intervention for preventing GLP-1 weight regain — addressing a clinical problem that affects tens of millions of patients worldwide who stop these medications due to cost, side effects, or medical guidance.

NIH · Trials Interrupting · Crisis Active Mid-course trial interruptions · not just delayed new grants · 1-in-25 NCI rate · 100+ advisory committees terminated · Nature

Continuing: NIH crisis enters a new phase — active mid-course trial interruptions are accumulating, not just delayed new grants. Nature analysis documents 100-plus terminated science advisory committees.

The NIH funding crisis has moved from delayed new grants to a qualitatively more severe phase: active mid-course clinical trial interruptions. Multiple scientific journals are now documenting withdrawals and pauses in studies that were underway — enrolled patients losing study access, preliminary data abandoned, research that cannot be reproduced or completed. The distinction from grant delays is clinically significant: when a trial withdraws after enrollment, patients who relied on study access for treatment lose that access, preliminary data that took years to gather is lost, and the scientific community cannot complete or reproduce results that may have been months from meaningful conclusions. A Nature analysis published this week documents that the Trump administration has terminated more than 100 advisory committees to federal science agencies — NIH, FDA, EPA, and others — reducing the independent expert review that regulatory and funding decisions depend on. The NCI grant success rate at 1-in-25 — the lowest in modern history — will produce mortality consequences in the 2030s as treatments that would have been discovered in 2026-2028 are not discovered. The advisory committee terminations add a separate dimension: the US scientific research enterprise is being degraded in volume (fewer grants) and rigor (fewer independent reviews) simultaneously.

Continuing: Stanford CGIC chronic pain circuit enters Day 6 — pharmaceutical researchers accelerating re-evaluation of abandoned compounds, NIH Pain Consortium expediting a request for applications targeting the circuit.

Stanford's Nature paper identifying the caudal granular insular cortex (CGIC) as the brain's chronic pain command center enters its sixth day of global clinical response with the pharmaceutical and device research communities both showing unusual speed of engagement. The most significant development since Day 5's pharmaceutical company activity: the NIH Pain Consortium has reportedly begun expediting a request for applications (RFA) specifically targeting CGIC-based interventions — an unusual step given the broader NIH funding crisis, and one that reflects the clinical urgency the pain research community attaches to translating a finding that could affect 1.5 billion global chronic pain patients. Device researchers are evaluating whether FDA-cleared transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) and focused ultrasound technologies can be precisely targeted at the CGIC's anatomical location — a targeting question that the paper's precise anatomical mapping now makes answerable for the first time. The 6-day pharmaceutical response timeline remains extraordinarily fast for basic neuroscience: companies are reviewing abandoned compound libraries for drugs that produced insular-cortex-specific analgesic activity without a known mechanism, and now have the CGIC finding as a retroactive mechanistic framework. The speed of industry response is itself the strongest endorsement of the finding's robustness and translational potential.

SPHEREx · Mars · Astrobiology Week Continuing SPHEREx interstellar glaciers · Mars TMAH organic molecules · combined astrobiology signal · strongest week in space science

Continuing: SPHEREx's interstellar glacier mapping and Curiosity's 7-molecule TMAH finding — both published within days of each other — together represent the strongest combined astrobiological evidence package in a single week of space science.

The astrobiology significance of SPHEREx's "interstellar glaciers" finding and Curiosity's TMAH-enabled organic chemistry discovery continues to be processed by the planetary and astrobiology research communities. The two results are scientifically complementary: SPHEREx confirms that water ice is systematically and abundantly delivered to every new solar system forming in a molecular cloud (establishing that the chemical prerequisites for life are universally available at the planet-formation stage), while Curiosity confirms that complex organic chemistry — including nitrogen-bearing compounds structurally analogous to DNA precursors — can survive on a planetary surface for 3.5 billion years in a chemically preserved state. The combined implication is the strongest single-week astrobiological statement in the history of space science: the raw materials for life's chemistry are abundant and delivered to new planets systematically; those materials can survive for geological timescales on planetary surfaces. Neither result says life exists elsewhere. Both results say the conditions for life are more common and more durable than the previous scientific consensus held. The Mars sample-return mission — which will bring Gale Crater and Jezero Crater materials to Earth for the isotopic and morphological analysis that could definitively address the biology question — remains the necessary next step to convert the Curiosity TMAH finding from "strongest biosignature case yet" to "resolved."

APOL1 · Kidney · Nine Proteins 9 Markers Nine-protein plasma signature · APOL1 high-risk genotype · predicts kidney events and mortality · outperforms existing tools

A nine-protein plasma signature predicts kidney disease events and mortality in high-risk APOL1 genotype patients — outperforming all existing clinical and genetic risk tools in a new study with immediate clinical implications.

A new study published this week identifies a nine-protein plasma signature that predicts kidney disease events and mortality specifically in individuals with a high-risk APOL1 genotype and preserved estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) — the highest-risk group for rapid kidney function decline. APOL1 is a gene with variant forms that confer dramatic increases in kidney disease risk: high-risk APOL1 variants, which are found predominantly in people of West African ancestry, increase the lifetime risk of kidney failure by 7-10 fold. The nine-protein signature — identified through proteomics analysis of large biobank datasets — outperforms all existing clinical tools and the APOL1 genotype alone in predicting which individuals with high-risk APOL1 variants will experience kidney events and mortality within a defined follow-up window. For clinical practice, the finding addresses a specific unmet need: knowing that a patient has a high-risk APOL1 variant tells clinicians the patient is at elevated lifetime risk, but does not tell them when or how rapidly that risk will materialize. The nine-protein signature adds a temporal precision layer — identifying the subset of high-risk APOL1 patients for whom urgent intervention is warranted now versus those who require monitoring over a longer horizon. The finding has specific implications for clinical trials targeting APOL1-mediated kidney disease: better risk stratification enables more efficient trial design and endpoint selection.

Quick hits

  • The UCSD cannabis study's THC-specific finding — that CBD does not show the same cognitive development suppression — is the most actionable regulatory signal in the research. If the teen cannabis risk is specifically about THC concentration rather than cannabis broadly, it argues for age-specific labeling and marketing requirements that reflect THC content rather than generic "cannabis" warnings, targeted restrictions on high-THC products specifically in states with legal cannabis markets, and public health messaging that distinguishes THC from CBD. The distinction also has implications for medical cannabis programs: patients under 18 using cannabis-based medications for epilepsy (typically CBD-dominant, like Epidiolex) face a categorically different risk profile than those using high-THC products recreationally. Regulators in the 24 states with legal adult-use cannabis have a specific reason to revisit their age-verification and product-labeling frameworks in light of the UCSD data.
  • The duodenal mucosal resurfacing finding addresses the GLP-1 drug market's largest structural limitation: the weight-regain problem that has constrained these drugs' long-term adoption. The approximately 70% weight-regain rate within 18 months of stopping semaglutide or tirzepatide means that the drugs functionally require lifelong use to maintain their benefits — a cost and side-effect burden that many patients cannot or will not sustain. A procedure that preserves 80%-plus of weight loss after discontinuation at a one-time cost (rather than ongoing monthly drug costs) addresses the payer, patient, and clinical decision tree simultaneously. If the full REMAIN-1 trial confirms the midpoint findings, Novo Nordisk's pipeline faces a competitive dynamic where a one-time DMR procedure could replace a permanent Ozempic subscription for patients willing to undergo an endoscopic intervention — a dynamic that Novo, interestingly, may be preparing for given its OpenAI partnership's focus on identifying the next generation of obesity treatment approaches.
  • The NIH advisory committee terminations — 100-plus documented by Nature — represent a structural degradation of US regulatory science that will have consequences beyond NIH's own research portfolio. FDA's advisory committees provide the independent expert review that drug approval decisions depend on; when those committees are eliminated or compromised, the scientific quality of FDA approval decisions is degraded. EPA's advisory committees review the scientific basis for environmental regulations; their elimination reduces the independence of the regulatory science underlying environmental standards. The advisory committee infrastructure is not just a bureaucratic convenience — it is the mechanism by which the scientific community's independent judgment enters the regulatory process. Eliminating it does not eliminate the need for scientific judgment in regulatory decisions; it transfers that judgment to agency staff and political appointees operating without independent expert review. The long-term consequence is a regulatory system where scientific consensus can be overridden without the friction that advisory committees provide.
  • The nine-protein APOL1 kidney signature's outperformance of existing tools is significant for a specific reason: APOL1 high-risk variants are carried by approximately 13% of Black Americans and confer dramatically elevated kidney failure risk. The APOL1-related kidney disease burden is one of the most significant health disparities in American medicine — a genetic risk factor that disproportionately affects one demographic group and for which no specific approved treatments existed until very recently. Improved risk stratification through the nine-protein signature enables the more precise clinical and research interventions that the APOL1-disease community needs: identifying the patients who need preventive intervention urgently rather than applying broad, resource-intensive monitoring to all carriers of high-risk variants. For the clinical trials targeting APOL1-mediated disease, the signature's predictive power may be the enabling tool that makes efficient trial design possible.
  • The SPHEREx and Curiosity findings' combined astrobiological implication — that water and organics are abundant at planet formation and durable on planetary surfaces — converges with the UCSD cannabis finding in a specific methodological observation: the two most significant scientific findings of the current week both relied on measurement advances, not conceptual advances. SPHEREx found interstellar glaciers because its 102-band infrared photometry was sensitive enough to detect molecular ice signatures across 600 light-years; prior instruments could not. Curiosity found seven new organic molecules because the TMAH dissolution technique could access thermally fragile compounds that pyrolysis destroyed; prior techniques could not. The UCSD cannabis study found THC's developmental effects because biological testing could confirm actual exposure that self-reports missed. All three findings were possible before their actual discovery — the science was not waiting for conceptual breakthroughs but for measurement tools sensitive enough to access what was already there. The next generation of instruments will find more of the same: things that exist, that we haven't been able to measure.
  • The Stanford CGIC finding's Day 6 pharmaceutical response — companies re-evaluating abandoned compound libraries — is a specific form of scientific productivity that the research community rarely discusses: the retroactive mechanistic validation of prior failed drug candidates. A compound that showed some analgesic activity in a clinical trial but was abandoned because the mechanism was unknown may now have a scientifically valid retesting rationale if its activity tracks to CGIC engagement. The sunk cost of prior failed trials is not lost if the finding can redirect those compounds to a mechanistically informed second evaluation. The number of abandoned analgesic compounds in pharmaceutical libraries that may now have a CGIC-based retesting justification is not public, but given the billions spent on pain drug development in the prior decade — much of which failed because of mechanism-agnostic trial design — the retroactive value of the CGIC finding to the pharmaceutical industry may be substantial. The 1.5 billion chronic pain patients worldwide are the downstream beneficiaries of how quickly that value can be translated.
