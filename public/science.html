The Anchor

MUSC finds EPA omega-3 in fish oil may raise CTE risk after brain injury — complicating advice for millions.

A study from the Medical University of South Carolina, published today in Cell Reports, found that EPA — eicosapentaenoic acid, one of the two main omega-3 fatty acids in fish oil supplements — may interfere with the brain's ability to repair itself after repetitive mild traumatic brain injuries rather than helping recovery. In experimental models with repeated mild head trauma, higher levels of EPA in the brain were associated with weakened blood vessel stability, disrupted healing signals, and perivascular tau protein buildup — a pathology closely linked to chronic traumatic encephalopathy. Docosahexaenoic acid (DHA), the other primary omega-3 in fish oil and a well-established component of neuronal membranes, did not show these negative effects. The neuroscientist leading the study, Onder Albayram at MUSC, noted that not all omega-3s behave the same way — a nuance lost in decades of generalized fish oil supplementation guidance. The finding has particular relevance for athletes in contact sports, military personnel, and anyone who has experienced repetitive head impacts, a population in the tens of millions.

The MUSC study is part of a broader week of science that is reshaping assumptions across multiple domains. KAIST researchers published in Advanced Functional Materials the precise mechanism by which graphene oxide selectively kills bacteria: the material binds to POPG (1-palmitoyl-2-oleoyl-sn-glycero-3-phospho-(1'-rac-glycerol)) phospholipids uniquely present in bacterial membranes, disrupting cell integrity while leaving POPG-free human cells entirely unharmed. The discovery explains graphene oxide's selective antibacterial action against drug-resistant superbugs at a molecular level for the first time — and points toward applications in wound-healing materials, antibacterial coatings, and potentially systemic infection treatment. Separately, a Nature study led by McGill University and UC Davis challenges the long-held model of a single ancestral African population as the origin of modern humans, finding genomic evidence for at least two weakly differentiated populations that mixed over hundreds of thousands of years before the migrations out of Africa.

Fish oil's EPA omega-3 may increase CTE risk after brain injury — MUSC study upends supplement guidance.

Researchers at MUSC found that EPA — eicosapentaenoic acid, one of the two main omega-3 fatty acids in fish oil — may hinder brain repair after repetitive mild traumatic brain injuries rather than aiding recovery. In models with repeated head trauma, higher EPA levels were associated with reduced vascular stability, impaired endothelial repair, and perivascular tau pathology linked to chronic traumatic encephalopathy. DHA, fish oil's other main omega-3, did not show these effects — confirming that the two omega-3s have meaningfully different biological roles in the brain under injury conditions. The MUSC team, led by neuroscientist Onder Albayram, concluded that EPA supplementation may be context-dependent: beneficial in some settings, potentially harmful in those with repetitive mild head trauma. The findings add urgency to targeted research distinguishing DHA-only from combined EPA/DHA supplement recommendations for at-risk populations.

Graphene Oxide · KAIST Superbugs POPG targeting · human cells spared · wound healing

KAIST reveals how graphene oxide kills antibiotic-resistant superbugs while leaving human cells unharmed.

Researchers led by Professor Sang Ouk Kim at KAIST published the mechanism behind graphene oxide's selective antibacterial action: the material binds to POPG phospholipids that are present in bacterial membranes but absent in human cell membranes — selectively disrupting bacterial cell integrity while leaving human cells entirely intact. The finding explains a phenomenon observed for years in the lab but never mechanistically understood. Graphene oxide works against drug-resistant superbugs including MRSA, promotes faster wound healing, and retains its antibacterial properties even after repeated washing — critical for practical applications in textiles and wound dressings. The material has already appeared in commercial antibacterial toothbrushes (10 million+ units sold) and is expected in functional sportswear at major upcoming events. The published mechanism opens a rational design path for next-generation graphene-based antibiotics targeting specific bacterial lipid profiles.

Human Origins · Nature Multi-pop McGill · UC Davis · Africa · 120K–135K split

New model of human origins: multiple ancestral African populations, not one — Nature study challenges the textbook.

A study led by Brenna Henn at UC Davis and Simon Gravel at McGill University, published in Nature, challenges the long-held "single origin" model of modern human ancestry. Integrating genomic data from southern, eastern, and western Africa — including newly sequenced genomes from the Nama population in southern Africa — the team found evidence for at least two weakly genetically differentiated ancestral populations that mixed over hundreds of thousands of years before migrations out of Africa. The earliest detectable population split occurred 120,000 to 135,000 years ago. The model explains patterns of genetic diversity in contemporary humans that the single-population model struggles to account for, and suggests 1–4% of genetic differentiation among living humans traces back to variation between these ancestral populations. The finding reshapes how researchers model human genetic history and has implications for pharmaceutical research that relies on ancestral assumptions.

SuperAgers · Brain 80 = 50 Memory · tau · brain connectivity · ongoing research

Continuing: SuperAgers over 80 with 50-year-old memory show lower tau and distinctive brain connectivity.

Ongoing SuperAger research continues to refine the biological portrait of adults over 80 who maintain memory comparable to people three decades younger. The SuperAger population shows consistently lower tau protein accumulation — the neurodegeneration marker linked to Alzheimer's — and distinctive brain connectivity patterns that resist typical age-related atrophy. New findings this week reinforce the central scientific question: what protects SuperAger brains from the tau-driven neurodegeneration that typically accelerates in the eighth decade? Candidate factors include genetic variants in tau-clearing pathways, immune system tone, and potentially lifestyle variables including diet — the olive oil gut-brain connection research published separately this week is being examined by researchers in this context. The clinical implication is that significant cognitive decline in the 80s is not biologically inevitable.

Olive Oil · Gut · Brain 2 years Cognitive performance · gut bacteria · microbiome

Two-year study: extra virgin olive oil improves cognitive performance by working through the gut microbiome.

A two-year dietary intervention study found that participants who consumed extra virgin olive oil daily showed better cognitive performance outcomes and maintained more diverse gut bacteria compared to controls, suggesting olive oil's brain benefits operate through the gut-brain axis rather than direct neurological mechanisms. Participants with greater olive oil consumption had higher microbiome diversity — particularly in bacteria linked to reduced systemic inflammation — and performed better on memory and processing speed tests at the study's conclusion. The gut-brain pathway for olive oil's cognitive effects is consistent with broader evidence that dietary polyphenols modulate the microbiome, which in turn regulates neuroinflammation via the vagus nerve. For SuperAger researchers, the finding raises the question of whether a gut microbiome optimized by Mediterranean diet adherence is a contributing factor to the tau-resistance observed in that population.

Source: ScienceDaily · SciTechDaily
NIH · Funding Crisis $1B Grants behind · NCI 1-in-25 · labs on track to close

Continuing: NIH is $1 billion behind on grants — NCI odds fall to 1-in-25, labs on track to permanently close.

The National Institutes of Health remains approximately $1 billion behind its typical grant-award timeline, per Nature reporting, as White House spending holds, a 22% staffing reduction, and a new lump-sum multi-year funding policy compound to delay thousands of research projects. At the National Cancer Institute, grant success odds have fallen from 1-in-10 to 1-in-25. A national survey found 81% of junior tenure-track scientists worry that funding disruptions could cost them tenure. Today's MUSC fish oil study — a multi-year Cell Reports project — represents exactly the type of foundational basic research most penalized by the new lump-sum funding policy, which systematically disadvantages projects with long timelines and uncertain near-term outputs. Labs that cannot sustain operations through the funding gap are closing permanently; a disruption to multi-year project continuity that peer-reviewed science does not easily recover from.

Quick hits

  • EPA and DHA are both omega-3 fatty acids in fish oil, but their roles in the brain diverge: DHA is structurally incorporated into neuronal membranes and is well-established as neuroprotective; EPA is more involved in inflammation signaling pathways — and under injury conditions, that inflammatory role may be harmful rather than helpful.
  • Graphene oxide's POPG-targeting mechanism is significant because POPG is essentially absent in human cell membranes but present at 5–15% in bacterial membranes — making it a near-ideal selectivity target for materials designed to kill bacteria without harming host tissue.
  • The McGill/UC Davis human origins model's "weakly structured stem" — two populations mixing for 100,000+ years — is consistent with archaeological evidence of multiple African stone tool traditions that were long puzzling under a single-origin model, suggesting culture and genetics co-evolved in a more complex geography than previously assumed.
  • The olive oil gut-brain study's mechanism — polyphenol modulation of gut bacteria → reduced neuroinflammation → cognitive protection — is consistent with the PREDIMED trial's cardiovascular findings and extends the Mediterranean diet's evidence base to cognitive outcomes via a biologically plausible pathway.
  • KAIST's graphene oxide finding has commercial implications beyond medicine: the same POPG-targeting mechanism that kills bacteria could be used to design selective antimicrobial coatings for hospital surfaces, water purification membranes, and food packaging — applications where chemical antibiotics face regulatory and safety barriers.
  • NIH's 1-in-25 NCI grant success rate is the lowest in the institute's modern history; at that odds level, funding a research program becomes statistically comparable to a lottery — a structural change that will take years to reverse even if funding is fully restored, because junior scientists who exit the field now will not return.
