AI spots melanoma up to five years before it strikes — in 6 million Swedes.

Researchers from the University of Gothenburg and Chalmers University of Technology analyzed routine health registry data covering Sweden's entire adult population. Their AI model identified melanoma risk with 73% accuracy — compared to 64% from age and sex alone. Individuals flagged in the highest-risk tier faced up to a 33% chance of developing melanoma within five years. Separately, a two-year randomized trial links extra virgin olive oil to improved cognition and gut diversity.

At a glance · this week 73% AI melanoma prediction · 6M Swedes
The Anchor

AI reads routine health records and predicts skin cancer five years early.

The study is remarkable not for the AI, but for what the AI was fed. Researchers at the University of Gothenburg and Chalmers University of Technology did not use dermatology images or genetic data — they used the kind of information already sitting in every Swede's medical record: age, sex, diagnoses on file, medications, and socioeconomic status. They applied machine learning to 6,036,186 individuals from Sweden's national health registry, of whom 38,582 (0.64%) developed melanoma over the five-year study window. The AI correctly distinguished melanoma developers from non-developers in 73% of cases — versus 64% when only age and gender were considered as predictors.

The clinical significance is in the tail. The study identified a small subset of high-risk individuals — those flagged by multiple converging risk signals — who carried up to a 33% probability of developing melanoma within five years. For that group, targeted screening would be both cost-effective and medically actionable: melanoma caught early has a 98% five-year survival rate; melanoma found at stage IV has 35%. Lead researcher Sam Polesie at the University of Gothenburg emphasized that the method requires further validation before clinical implementation. But the infrastructure is already there — the registry data, the compute, and now the demonstrated predictive power.

AI · Melanoma 73% Chalmers · Gothenburg · registry data

AI identifies melanoma risk in 6 million Swedes — up to five years before diagnosis.

Using only routine registry information — diagnoses, medications, demographics, socioeconomic data — the model outperformed traditional risk assessment by 9 percentage points. No dermatology images required. The highest-risk tier showed a 33% five-year melanoma incidence rate. Lead researcher Sam Polesie says selective screening of small, high-risk groups flagged by the model "could lead to both more accurate monitoring and more efficient use of healthcare resources."

Nutrition · Cognition EVOO Olive oil · brain · gut diversity

Extra virgin olive oil outperforms refined oil on cognition and gut diversity over two years.

A two-year randomized study found that participants assigned to extra virgin olive oil scored higher on cognitive performance tests and showed significantly more diverse gut microbiome populations compared to those using refined olive oil. The finding strengthens the mechanistic link between EVOO's polyphenol content and brain health — and points to the gut-brain axis as a key transmission pathway for dietary interventions in neurological aging.

Source: ScienceDaily Health
Lung Cancer · Paradox Non-smokers Healthy diet · unexpected risk link

Healthy diet linked to elevated lung cancer risk in younger non-smokers — researchers puzzled.

A new study found that younger non-smokers following highly nutrient-rich diets — high in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains — showed elevated lung cancer rates compared to less-health-focused peers. Researchers hypothesize the confounding factor may be air pollution: health-conscious individuals in dense urban areas may face higher particulate matter exposure, or the dietary pattern may correlate with other unmeasured risk factors. The finding does not suggest diet causes lung cancer — it suggests the relationship is more complex than previously assumed.

Source: ScienceDaily
Aging · Blood Mitochondria Blood stem cells · cell death protein

Pro-apoptosis protein secretly drives blood stem cell aging by damaging mitochondria.

A new study identifies a protein classically associated with programmed cell death as the unexpected driver of blood stem cell aging — not by killing cells outright, but by gradually damaging their mitochondria, depleting energy, and impairing renewal capacity. Blocking the protein in animal models restored stem cell function comparable to younger specimens, pointing to a potential therapeutic target for age-related blood disorders including certain forms of anemia and myelodysplastic syndromes.

Source: ScienceDaily Health
Microbiome · Social Shared Social bonds · gut bacteria · bird study

Animals with stronger social bonds share more gut bacteria — a finding that extends to humans.

A study of island bird populations found that individuals with more robust social connections harbored more similar gut microbiomes to their closest associates than to birds with weaker social ties. The effect persisted after controlling for diet and environment. Researchers say social interaction itself is a meaningful driver of microbiome composition — a finding with implications for human immune function, mental health, and the biology of loneliness.

Neuro · Interface Printed Neurons · Nature Nanotechnology · continuing

Continuing: Northwestern's 3D-printed neurons communicate with living brain cells.

Published April 15 in Nature Nanotechnology, the Northwestern team's flexible neurons — printed from molybdenum disulfide and graphene inks — generate electrical spikes that match the timing and duration of real neurons. Tested on mouse brain tissue, the artificial neurons successfully triggered real neural circuit responses. The technology is now receiving expanded attention from neuroscience and neuroprosthetics researchers following last week's publication.
