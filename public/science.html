Scientists mapped underground fungal networks spanning 110 quadrillion kilometers that move approximately 4 billion tons of carbon dioxide into soils each year — making mycorrhizal fungi the largest natural carbon sequestration mechanism on Earth — as separate research showed that the mammalian ability to regenerate complex body parts may be switched off rather than evolutionarily lost, and probiotics demonstrated mental health benefits for older adults with depression.

The fungal network finding is a carbon accounting revelation of the first order. Four billion tons of CO2 per year into soils is more than any engineered carbon capture system currently operating or in planned deployment — and the mycorrhizal network doing that work is already woven into the root systems of approximately 75% of all terrestrial plant species globally. It is the most scalable carbon sink available to humanity, operating through natural biological processes that evolved over hundreds of millions of years. The finding does not discover something new about fungi — mycorrhizal networks have been known for decades — but the mapping of their global extent and the quantification of their annual CO2 transfer into soils at this scale is a new scientific result with immediate implications for carbon accounting, conservation policy, and the prioritization of threats to the fungal network (agricultural practices, tilling, fungicide use, monoculture farming) that may be inadvertently dismantling the planet's largest carbon sink. The mammalian regeneration finding is the week's most consequential finding for medicine's long-term trajectory. If the molecular switches that enable salamanders, planarians, and other species to regenerate lost limbs and organs are present in mammals but turned off — not absent, just dormant — then the scientific project of identifying those switches and understanding how to activate them becomes a new and plausible research frontier in regenerative medicine. Human beings regenerating lost fingers, restoring damaged spinal cord tissue, or rebuilding cardiac muscle after heart attacks has been the territory of science fiction. The dormant-switch model changes the question from "is it possible in mammals?" to "what would turn it back on?"

The Anchor

Underground fungal networks move 4 billion tons of CO2 into soils every year. That is more than any machine we have built. And we have been disrupting these networks with agricultural practices for centuries without knowing what we were erasing. The largest carbon capture system on Earth is biological, ancient, and already installed. Protecting it turns out to be climate policy.

The mapping of mycorrhizal fungal networks at global scale finds that they collectively span 110 quadrillion kilometers — a distance so large it is essentially incomprehensible without a reference: light travels approximately 9.46 trillion kilometers in a year, so 110 quadrillion kilometers of fungal network is roughly 11,640 light-years of biological filament, threaded through the soil beneath nearly every forest, grassland, and agricultural field on Earth. These networks are the interface between plant roots and soil chemistry: the fungi trade mineral nutrients and water access for carbon compounds from the plants, and in the process, they transfer approximately 4 billion tons of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere through plant photosynthesis into stable carbon forms in the soil. This is sequestration — carbon leaving the atmosphere and staying out, stored in the ground.

The policy implication is not subtle. Every agricultural practice that destroys mycorrhizal networks — deep tilling, broad-spectrum fungicide use, monoculture planting that excludes the plant diversity that sustains fungal diversity — is destroying carbon-capture capacity. The amount of CO2 sequestration potentially lost to global agricultural disruption of mycorrhizal networks over the past century of industrial agriculture is unknown, but the finding establishes the scale of what is at stake: 4 billion tons per year is the baseline for what an intact network can sequester. Regenerative agriculture practices that minimize soil disturbance and maintain plant diversity are, in effect, carbon capture infrastructure policy.

Fungal Networks: 110 Quadrillion km · 4 Billion Tons CO2/yr Into Soils · Largest Natural Carbon Sink 4B tons Discovery: global mapping of mycorrhizal fungal underground networks · extent: 110 quadrillion km (11,640 light-years equivalent) · CO2 transfer: ~4 billion tons into soils per year · mechanism: fungi exchange mineral nutrients + water for plant carbon compounds → carbon stored in stable soil forms → sequestration · scale: more than any engineered carbon capture system operating or planned · mycorrhizal fungi: symbiotic with ~75% of terrestrial plant species globally · threats: deep tilling + fungicide use + monoculture farming → destroy network integrity → reduce sequestration capacity · policy implication: regenerative agriculture = carbon capture infrastructure · biodegradable protein beads (dairy/tofu waste): additional CO2 capture discovery

Scientists published a global mapping of underground mycorrhizal fungal networks, finding they span approximately 110 quadrillion kilometers and transfer about 4 billion tons of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere into soil each year — making the mycorrhizal network the largest natural carbon sequestration system on Earth. The network operates through the symbiotic relationship between fungi and the roots of approximately 75% of all terrestrial plant species: the fungi provide mineral nutrients and water access to plant roots and receive carbon compounds from plant photosynthesis in exchange, and in the process, that carbon is transferred from the atmosphere through the plant into soil-stable forms. The 4 billion tons per year figure exceeds the annual carbon capture capacity of any engineered carbon removal system currently operating or in near-term development.

The policy implications flow directly from the finding's scale. Agricultural practices that disrupt mycorrhizal networks — deep tilling that severs fungal filaments, broad-spectrum fungicide application, monoculture farming that reduces plant diversity and thus fungal diversity — are reducing the network's carbon sequestration capacity at an unknown but potentially significant scale. Quantifying the sequestration losses from the past century of industrial agricultural soil disruption is a research question the finding opens but does not answer. The conservation policy implication, however, is clear: protecting soil mycorrhizal networks through regenerative agriculture practices (minimal tilling, fungicide reduction, polyculture planting) should be recognized as carbon capture infrastructure policy, not merely soil health practice. Separately, scientists developed biodegradable protein beads made from dairy and tofu waste that capture CO2 from the atmosphere more efficiently than most current engineered carbon capture technologies — a complementary finding on the same day that establishes multiple new approaches to the carbon sequestration challenge.

Mammalian Regeneration May Be Switched Off · Not Lost · New Regenerative Medicine Frontier Switched Off Finding: scientists demonstrated that the ability to rebuild complex body parts in mammals may not be evolutionarily lost but simply switched off at the molecular level · prior assumption: regeneration capacity (salamanders, planarians, zebrafish) was lost in mammalian evolution · new model: regeneration genes/switches exist in mammals in dormant state · implication: pharmacological or genetic approaches to reactivate switches become plausible research target · previous thinking: human regeneration of lost limbs/complex organs = impossible · new thinking: dormant capacity exists; question is what activates it · research frontier: identify molecular switches + understand activation conditions · adjacent: axolotl regeneration studies + planarian stem cell biology

Scientists reported a finding that challenges the central assumption of mammalian regenerative medicine: the ability to rebuild complex body parts — limbs, organs, spinal cord segments — may not be evolutionarily absent in mammals but simply switched off at the molecular level. The study, which demonstrated that regenerative capacity is present in a mammalian system in a dormant state, reframes the question from "is complex regeneration possible in mammals?" to "what molecular switches would need to be activated to enable it?" The finding draws on comparison with non-mammalian species that retain regenerative capacity — salamanders that regrow limbs, zebrafish that regenerate cardiac tissue, planarians that can rebuild from a small fragment — to suggest that the biological machinery for regeneration is evolutionarily conserved but suppressed in mammals.

The practical medical implications of the dormant-switch model are long-range but foundational. If the molecular switches for complex tissue regeneration are present in mammalian genomes in a suppressed state, the therapeutic objective shifts: instead of building regenerative capacity from scratch (engineering stem cells, bioprinting tissues, implanting scaffolds), researchers would focus on identifying the specific molecular switches, understanding the conditions under which non-mammalian species turn them on during regeneration, and finding pharmacological or genetic approaches to reactivate them in mammalian tissue. Human beings regenerating severed fingers, recovering spinal cord function after injury, or restoring cardiac muscle after heart attacks remains far from clinical reality — but the dormant-switch finding is the kind of conceptual shift that marks the beginning of a new research era, not a marginal incremental finding. It changes what is plausible and therefore what is worth trying to do.

Probiotics: Mental Health Benefits for Older Adults With Depression · Clinical Trial Probiotics Study: small clinical trial · population: older adults with depression · intervention: probiotics · finding: meaningful mental health benefits · mechanism: gut-brain axis → microbiome → serotonin + other neurotransmitter pathways · significance: low-cost, low-risk intervention with measurable effect in high-burden population · older adults with depression: undertreated population (antidepressant side effects limiting uptake in elderly) · gut-brain research: growing evidence base for microbiome → mental health pathway · context: brain health improves at any age (prior finding) → modifiable risk factors for cognitive and mental health · next step: larger RCT needed to confirm effects

A small clinical trial found that probiotic supplementation offered meaningful mental health benefits for older adults with depression — adding a low-cost, low-risk intervention to a population that is significantly undertreated for depression relative to the disease burden. The trial found that participants receiving probiotics showed improvements in depression measures compared to controls, consistent with the growing body of research on the gut-brain axis: the pathway through which gut microbiome composition influences neurotransmitter production, inflammatory signaling, and ultimately mood and cognitive function. The finding is preliminary — a small trial generates a hypothesis, not a conclusion — but it joins a body of evidence that is increasingly specific about the microbiome's role in mental health outcomes.

The clinical significance of the finding, if confirmed in larger trials, is substantial for the older adult population specifically. Antidepressant medications — the standard of care for depression across age groups — are frequently associated with side effects that are particularly problematic in elderly patients: falls risk from orthostatic hypotension, cognitive effects, drug-drug interactions with the multiple medications many older adults take, and QT prolongation concerns with certain antidepressants in patients with cardiac comorbidities. A probiotic intervention that produced measurable antidepressant effects without those side effect profiles would be a significant addition to the treatment landscape for older adults with depression. The trial's preliminary finding warrants the randomized controlled trials needed to confirm the effect size and identify which probiotic formulations, in which patient populations, produce reliable benefits — research that the existing gut-brain infrastructure at major institutions is well-positioned to run.

PFAS "Forever Chemicals" Destroyed by UV Hydrogen Radicals · No Added Chemicals Needed UV Break Finding: hydrogen radicals generated by intense UV light can break down PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) "forever chemicals" · key: no added chemicals required · prior challenge: PFAS extremely stable → resistant to conventional chemical degradation and water treatment · PFAS: found in nonstick coatings · firefighting foam · food packaging · industrial chemicals → bioaccumulate in environment + organisms → linked to cancer + thyroid disease + immune disruption · UV hydrogen radical mechanism: strong UV generates H-radicals → break C-F bonds (among strongest in chemistry) → full degradation of PFAS · scalability: UV systems exist in water treatment + industrial settings → adaptation possible · complementary: biodegradable beads discovery (same day)

Researchers demonstrated that hydrogen radicals generated by intense UV light can break down PFAS — per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances, the class of synthetic chemicals known as "forever chemicals" for their extreme environmental persistence — without requiring any added chemical reagents. PFAS compounds, used widely in nonstick cookware, firefighting foam, food packaging, and industrial applications, accumulate in the environment and in organisms and have been linked to cancer, thyroid disease, and immune system disruption. The challenge of PFAS remediation has been the extraordinary strength of the carbon-fluorine bonds in PFAS molecules — among the strongest bonds in chemistry — which resist conventional chemical degradation and survive standard water treatment processes including chlorination. UV-generated hydrogen radicals break those bonds directly.

The UV hydrogen radical approach is significant because it operates without added chemical inputs — the only inputs are the UV light source and water containing the PFAS. Existing water treatment infrastructure already uses UV disinfection systems, meaning the hardware for UV PFAS treatment exists in many treatment plants and could potentially be adapted or scaled. The challenge will be the intensity of UV required to generate H-radicals at concentrations sufficient for full PFAS degradation at the flow volumes that water treatment facilities handle — a question of engineering scaling, not of fundamental chemistry. If the UV approach scales to municipal water treatment volumes, it would represent the first practical large-scale remediation pathway for PFAS in drinking water supplies. Current PFAS removal techniques (activated carbon filtration, reverse osmosis) capture PFAS without destroying it, creating a secondary waste stream that requires disposal. UV hydrogen radical degradation destroys the PFAS entirely, eliminating the secondary waste problem.

Quick hits

  • The fungal network finding has an immediate implication for carbon offset markets that may not be fully appreciated in the initial coverage. If intact mycorrhizal fungal networks sequester 4 billion tons of CO2 annually, then agricultural land-use decisions that preserve or restore those networks have measurable, quantifiable climate value. Carbon offset methodologies for soil carbon have been controversial — the measurement challenge for below-ground carbon storage has made verification difficult. The fungal network finding provides a new measurement target: the extent and integrity of mycorrhizal networks, which is a biological quantity that can potentially be measured through soil sampling and metagenomic analysis, and which can be linked to CO2 sequestration rates. Verifiable fungal network integrity could become a new basis for soil carbon credits that are more defensible than current above-ground biomass estimates. The policy and market infrastructure for fungal network carbon credits does not yet exist, but the scientific foundation for building it has been laid.
  • The PFAS finding arrives in a regulatory environment where PFAS contamination has become a major liability question for municipalities, industrial sites, and manufacturers. The EPA's 2024 maximum contaminant level rules for PFAS in drinking water created compliance timelines that many water utilities are struggling to meet with current technologies. Activated carbon and reverse osmosis remove PFAS but at high cost and with secondary waste streams. A UV hydrogen radical approach that destroys PFAS without generating secondary waste would represent a step-change improvement in remediation economics, not just a marginal efficiency gain. For utilities facing PFAS compliance timelines, the finding is a signal that a more cost-effective technology pathway may be available within the current investment cycle — depending on the scale-up timeline, which the research has not yet established. Regulatory agencies and utilities should be tracking the scale-up research closely.
  • The mammalian regeneration finding connects to a broader research trajectory in the biology of aging and cellular reprogramming that has been accelerating. The discovery that cells retain more developmental plasticity than previously thought — a theme running through recent work on partial cellular reprogramming, epigenetic clocks, and senescence reversal — is consistent with the regeneration finding: if cells retain access to developmental programs that were thought to be permanently silenced in adult tissue, those programs represent a scientific and therapeutic resource that researchers are only beginning to characterize. The regeneration dormant-switch finding is one data point in a larger pattern of discoveries suggesting that the "one-way street" model of mammalian development and aging is more reversible at the molecular level than classical biology assumed. The clinical translation timelines are long, but the conceptual foundation for a new era of regenerative and longevity biology is being built in real time.
