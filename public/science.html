Three amino acids that could remake gene therapy.

A Chan Zuckerberg Biohub team found that adding methionine, arginine, and serine to lipid nanoparticles pushes mRNA delivery 20-fold and CRISPR efficiency to nearly 90%. The Anchor

The three amino acids that supercharge everything gene therapy is trying to do.

The fix turns out to be embarrassingly simple. Researchers at the Chan Zuckerberg Biohub screened combinations of amino acids added alongside lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) — the delivery system that carries mRNA and CRISPR tools into cells. Through systematic screening, they identified an optimized cocktail: methionine, arginine, and serine. The three amino acids don't change the drug. They help cells take it in.

The results were dramatic. mRNA delivery increased up to 20-fold. CRISPR gene-editing efficiency jumped from a baseline of roughly 25% to nearly 90% in a single dose. In a mouse model of acute liver failure, survival with the amino acid supplement was 100% — versus 33% without. The ingredients themselves are cheap, produced at massive scale, and considered safe. They can be added to existing LNP formulations without modifying the underlying drug. Published today in the journal Science Translational Medicine, this is the kind of platform-level finding that reshapes an entire therapeutic modality within months.

Neuro · Interface Printed Neurons · Nature Nanotechnology

Northwestern engineers print neurons that communicate with living brain cells.

Published April 15 in Nature Nanotechnology, the Northwestern team printed flexible neurons from molybdenum disulfide and graphene inks that generate electrical spikes matching the timing and duration of real neurons. Tested on mouse brain tissue, the artificial neurons successfully triggered real neural circuit responses — a foundation for next-generation neuroprosthetics and brain-machine interfaces.

Inflammation, not just cholesterol, may be killing hearts — and colchicine can help.

Scientific American's May 2026 cover story, "Your Heart in Flames," centers on research showing chronic inflammation as a primary driver of cardiovascular disease. The finding points to cheap, widely available inflammation-reducing drugs like colchicine as a potential new frontline treatment — an unexpected alternative to statins that's been underutilized for decades.

Climate · Coastal Sinking River deltas · sea rise

World's largest river deltas are sinking faster than seas are rising.

A sweeping global study finds that many of the world's largest river deltas — home to hundreds of millions of people — are subsiding faster than sea-level rise can account for, driven by groundwater extraction, sediment compaction, and upstream damming. The Mekong, Nile, and Ganges-Brahmaputra deltas are among the most at-risk.

Source: SciTechDaily
Geology 200 yrs Dolomite · lab-grown

Dolomite grown in a lab for the first time after 200 years of failed attempts.

The mineral dolomite, which forms massive geological formations on every continent, has puzzled geochemists for two centuries: it forms abundantly in ancient rocks but resists laboratory synthesis under standard conditions. Researchers cracked the puzzle by replicating specific microbial conditions present in ancient shallow-sea environments. The finding reshapes models of Earth's ancient oceans.

Source: SciTechDaily
Microplastics Moringa Seeds · filtration

Moringa seeds pull microplastics from water by clumping particles for filtration.

Researchers found that a plant-based extract derived from moringa seeds causes microplastic particles to aggregate, making them dramatically easier to filter from water. The method works across plastic types and concentrations and uses a material that is cheap, renewable, and non-toxic — a potential intervention for both municipal water systems and household filtration.

Source: SciTechDaily
Cancer · Metabolism B7 Biotin · glutamine escape

Vitamin B7 gives cancer cells a metabolic escape route — and a new drug target.

Cancer cells are known for glutamine addiction, but a new study finds that vitamin B7 (biotin) acts as a metabolic "license," enabling an escape through a key enzyme when glutamine is blocked. The finding identifies a specific vulnerability: tumors co-dependent on both glutamine and biotin could be targeted with combination therapies that cut off both routes simultaneously.
