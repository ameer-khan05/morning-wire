Science & Health · Deep Dive

ETH Zurich scientists developed a light-controlled molecular switch — a photoPROTAC — that wakes dormant cancer cells from their treatment-resistant sleep state by degrading the glucocorticoid receptors that keep them dormant, making them vulnerable to drugs again, with lung cancer effectiveness demonstrated and breast and prostate cancer applications in the system's modular design.

Cancer treatment resistance driven by dormancy is one of the most clinically significant unsolved problems in oncology. Chemotherapy and targeted therapies are designed to attack actively dividing cells — cancer cells that are cycling through replication, which makes them accessible to drugs that disrupt DNA synthesis, protein production, or cell division machinery. Dormant cancer cells, by definition, are not doing any of those things: they are sitting in a low-metabolism, low-division state that renders them invisible to most treatments and capable of reactivating weeks, months, or years later as treatment-resistant recurrences. ETH Zurich's photoPROTAC system addresses this problem not by attacking the dormant cells directly, but by removing the molecular signal that keeps them dormant — specifically, by degrading the glucocorticoid receptors through which stress hormones maintain the dormant state. Once the receptor is gone, the cells lose their dormancy signal, reactivate into normal cycling behavior, and become vulnerable to drugs designed for actively dividing cells. The light-control mechanism adds spatial precision: the switch can be activated near the tumor, limiting receptor degradation to the target tissue and reducing systemic glucocorticoid effects.

The Anchor

ETH Zurich's light switch wakes dormant cancer cells and makes them killable. Weizmann's "Mitch" protein is the obesity brake that doesn't cost muscle. ETH Zurich's Compound 10 slows Alzheimer's nerve cell death. Three findings in three disease areas where existing treatments face a specific gap — dormancy, metabolic trade-offs, neurodegeneration — each providing a precise molecular target where the gap was previously a mechanism unknown.

The cancer dormancy problem in clinical oncology is the follow-up relapse that arrives after a complete response. A patient achieves remission — no detectable cancer — because the treatment killed all the dividing cells. The cells that survived by going dormant during treatment reactivate after treatment ends, and the recurrence is now resistant to the drugs that previously worked because the cells were selected for survival under treatment pressure. The ETH Zurich photoPROTAC system intervenes at the dormancy signal itself: if the glucocorticoid receptor that maintains the dormant state is degraded by the light-activated molecular switch, the cell cannot receive the dormancy signal, cannot enter the treatment-resistant state, and remains in the cycling division that makes it vulnerable to conventional treatment. The light-control mechanism is the precision that makes this clinically conceivable rather than systemically toxic: degrading glucocorticoid receptors systemically would disrupt stress hormone signaling throughout the body, with severe physiological consequences; doing it locally at the tumor with a wavelength-specific light activation limits the effect to the target tissue.

The Weizmann Mitch protein finding takes direct aim at the clinical gap in GLP-1 obesity pharmacology. Semaglutide and tirzepatide produce weight loss through appetite suppression — they are effective at reducing total body weight but also reduce lean muscle mass, which is metabolically significant and physically limiting, particularly for older patients for whom muscle mass loss is already a baseline concern. Mitch (MTCH2) operates through a different mechanism: it is a mitochondrial protein whose presence appears to reduce the rate at which cells burn fats and carbohydrates. Disabling it forces cells into higher energy expenditure and prevents new fat cell formation without the appetite-suppression pathway that the GLP-1 drugs use. The muscle preservation in the Mitch-lacking mice is the finding that most distinguishes this mechanism from existing pharmacology — if a Mitch inhibitor drug produces fat loss with muscle preservation in humans, it would address the most clinically significant limitation of the current obesity drug class.

ETH Zurich PhotoPROTAC: Light Switch Wakes Dormant Cancer Cells · Degrades Glucocorticoid Receptors · Cells Reactivate and Become Drug-Vulnerable · Lung Cancer Effective · Breast + Prostate Applications · PNAS May 2026 Dormancy ETH Zurich (Department of Chemistry): photoPROTAC system · target: glucocorticoid receptors in dormant cancer cells · mechanism: cancer cells under stress → glucocorticoid receptor activation → low-division dormant state → treatment-resistant · photoPROTAC: PROTAC (proteolysis-targeting chimera) + photoswitchable element integrated · activation: wavelength-specific light applied near tumor · effect: glucocorticoid receptor tagged for disposal → degraded → cell loses dormancy signal → reactivates → becomes drug-vulnerable · lung cancer: effective in laboratory studies · modular design: adaptable for estrogen receptor (breast cancer) + androgen receptor (advanced prostate cancer) · published: PNAS May 21, 2026 · pre-clinical; clinical development required

ETH Zurich chemists developed a photoPROTAC — a light-controlled proteolysis-targeting chimera — that dissolves the molecular mechanism keeping cancer cells in their treatment-resistant dormant state: cancer cells enter a low-division sleep triggered by stress hormones acting on glucocorticoid receptors, making them invisible to chemotherapy and targeted drugs that require actively dividing cells. The photoPROTAC integrates a photoswitchable element into the PROTAC design; when wavelength-specific light is applied near the tumor, the switch activates, the PROTAC tags the glucocorticoid receptor for degradation, the receptor is destroyed, the dormancy signal is lost, and the cells reactivate into normal division where standard treatments can reach them. The system is effective against lung cancer in laboratory studies, published in PNAS in May 2026, and the modular architecture can be adapted to target the estrogen receptor in hormone-dependent breast cancer and the androgen receptor in advanced prostate cancer.

The light-control mechanism is the design feature that makes this clinically translatable rather than physiologically toxic. PROTACs that degrade glucocorticoid receptors systemically would disrupt the stress hormone signaling that the body uses for inflammation control, immune modulation, and metabolic regulation throughout every tissue — the side effect profile would be severe. The photoPROTAC's wavelength-specific local activation limits the receptor degradation to the tumor site where light can be directed, preserving systemic glucocorticoid signaling everywhere else. The clinical pathway requires the development of light-delivery systems that can reach tumors at the relevant tissue depths (lung tumors are accessible endoscopically; breast tumors via fiber optic guidance; prostate tumors via transrectal delivery) — a tractable engineering challenge rather than a fundamental barrier. The research is pre-clinical and requires clinical trial development, but the mechanism is precisely targeted at the specific failure mode of recurrent treatment-resistant cancer in a way that existing approaches are not.

Weizmann Institute: "Mitch" Protein (MTCH2) Blocks Fat Burning · Disabling It: Fat Loss + Muscle Preservation + Obesity Resistance · Distinct from GLP-1 Mechanism · Small-Molecule Inhibitor in Development Mitch Weizmann Institute of Science: Prof. Atan Gross lab · protein: MTCH2 (nicknamed "Mitch") · location: mitochondria · function: appears to reduce rate cells burn fats + carbohydrates · disabling in human cells: ↑ fat burning + ↑ energy expenditure + ↓ fat cell formation (adipogenesis) · mice lacking Mitch: leaner + more athletic + obesity-resistant + muscle mass preserved · vs GLP-1 (semaglutide, tirzepatide): GLP-1 suppresses appetite → weight loss + muscle loss · Mitch: mitochondrial mechanism → fat loss without appetite suppression + muscle preserved · published: EMBO Journal (June 2026) · Weizmann Bina (translational research unit): developing small-molecule Mitch inhibitor · pre-clinical stage

Researchers at the Weizmann Institute of Science, led by Professor Atan Gross, identified MTCH2 — nicknamed "Mitch" — as a mitochondrial protein whose presence reduces the rate at which cells burn fats and carbohydrates: disabling Mitch in human cells boosted fat burning, increased energy expenditure, and made it significantly harder for cells to form new fat tissue. Mice lacking the protein were leaner and more athletic, resistant to obesity even under conditions that would normally produce weight gain in control animals, and — critically for clinical relevance — preserved their muscle mass in a way that current obesity pharmacology does not achieve. The research was published in the EMBO Journal in June 2026, and the Weizmann Institute's translational unit Bina is developing a small-molecule Mitch inhibitor intended for clinical development as an obesity treatment.

The muscle preservation finding is what separates the Mitch mechanism from the current leading edge of obesity pharmacology. GLP-1 receptor agonists — semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound) — produce clinically meaningful weight loss through appetite suppression that reduces caloric intake and total body mass, including both fat and lean muscle tissue. For patients whose primary concern is total body weight, the muscle loss is an acceptable trade-off; for patients who depend on muscle mass for mobility, fall prevention, and metabolic function — which includes most obese patients over 60 — losing muscle while losing fat is a net negative in the precise areas where weight loss was supposed to help. Mitch inhibition, which operates through increased energy expenditure rather than appetite suppression, produced fat loss with muscle preservation in the Weizmann mouse study. If that finding translates to a human small-molecule inhibitor, it would address the most clinically significant limitation of the GLP-1 class at precisely the patient population where that limitation most matters.

ETH Zurich: Compound 10 Slows Alzheimer's Nerve Cell Death in Mice · Animals Survive Longer · Dementia Nerve Cell Loss Significantly Reduced · Earlier Stage Than Cancer + Obesity Findings Compound 10 ETH Zurich: "Compound 10" drug · tested in Alzheimer's mouse model · finding: nerve cell death (typical in dementia) significantly slower in treated animals · treated mice survive longer than untreated · mechanism: under investigation (compound targets pathway involved in neurodegeneration) · context: current Alzheimer's drugs (lecanemab, donanemab): amyloid-beta removal in early-stage patients · Compound 10: may operate through different neuroprotective mechanism · stage: pre-clinical mouse model only · clinical trials required · published ETH Zurich news release June 2026 · earlier development stage than photoPROTAC or Mitch findings

ETH Zurich researchers tested a compound called Compound 10 in Alzheimer's disease mouse models and found that the typical death of nerve cells seen in dementia was significantly slower in treated animals, with mice receiving Compound 10 surviving longer than untreated controls — a promising pre-clinical result for a disease in which the only approved disease-modifying therapies (lecanemab and donanemab, FDA-approved in 2023–2024) target amyloid-beta plaques in early-stage patients and have shown modest clinical benefit at substantial cost and side-effect burden. The finding was reported in ETH Zurich research news in June 2026; the compound's mechanism of action in reducing neurodegeneration is under investigation, and the finding is at an earlier development stage than the ETH Zurich photoPROTAC cancer dormancy work and the Weizmann Mitch obesity findings that share this edition's science coverage.

The Alzheimer's landscape in 2026 is one where disease-modifying treatments exist for the first time but are limited in both patient eligibility and clinical effect magnitude. Lecanemab and donanemab slow cognitive decline rather than halt it, require early-stage diagnosis for efficacy, and carry infusion-related adverse events and brain imaging abnormalities (ARIA) that limit their use to specialist settings. Compound 10, if its neuroprotective mechanism in mice proves translatable to humans and operable through a different biological pathway than amyloid removal, could represent an additional tool for patients who are not eligible for amyloid-targeting therapies or for whom amyloid reduction is insufficient to prevent neurodegeneration. The pre-clinical stage is a significant caveat — the history of Alzheimer's drug development is full of mouse models that did not translate — but the ETH Zurich finding contributes to the growing evidence that neuroprotective mechanisms beyond amyloid are worth investigating as complementary or alternative therapeutic targets.

Quick hits

  • The ETH Zurich photoPROTAC is the most mechanistically precise cancer dormancy intervention published to date, and its precision is also its limitation. It works by degrading a specific receptor (glucocorticoid) that is responsible for a specific dormancy pathway (stress hormone–driven). Cancer cells can enter dormancy through multiple mechanisms — hypoxia-driven dormancy, niche-driven dormancy, immune pressure–driven dormancy — and the photoPROTAC does not address the others. A clinical deployment would need to identify patients whose cancer dormancy is specifically glucocorticoid receptor–dependent (which requires molecular characterization of the dormant cells) and deliver the light activation to the tumor site (which requires an appropriate endoscopic or guided-fiber delivery system). Both are tractable but non-trivial, and both require clinical trial infrastructure that is not yet built for this specific approach. The finding is a proof of mechanism for light-controlled tumor targeting — the broader platform value may exceed the specific glucocorticoid receptor application.
  • The Mitch protein and the GLP-1 pharmacology landscape are likely to become complementary rather than competitive if the Mitch inhibitor drug development succeeds. GLP-1 drugs have established the therapeutic validity of significant obesity pharmacotherapy and created the prescribing infrastructure and patient awareness that any new obesity drug would benefit from. A Mitch inhibitor that addresses the muscle-loss limitation of GLP-1 drugs would likely be developed first as a combination therapy — GLP-1 for appetite suppression and weight loss, Mitch inhibitor for maintaining the muscle mass that GLP-1 reduces — before being evaluated as a standalone treatment. The Bina translational development program is at early small-molecule identification stage; the clinical timeline is measured in years, not months. But the finding places a specific molecular mechanism with a specific clinical gap in the pipeline at a moment when the obesity pharmacology market is large enough to fund its development.
