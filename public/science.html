A Nature study published June 9 identified SLAMF6 as a previously unknown immune checkpoint molecule that cancer uses to exhaust T cells — operating through a self-activating pathway on T cells without requiring tumor contact, explaining why many patients stop responding to immunotherapy. Blocking antibodies restored T-cell function in mice. Scientists also developed biodegradable CO2-capture beads from food industry waste, and the largest-ever opioid review found acute pain drugs deliver small, short-lived benefits.

SLAMF6 is mechanistically distinct from known immune checkpoints (PD-1, CTLA-4): it activates directly on the T cell's own surface, without needing the tumor to initiate suppression. This means cancer's immune-evasion pathway through SLAMF6 is independent of the tumor microenvironment — it can suppress T cells before they reach the tumor, explaining why checkpoint inhibitor drugs targeting PD-1 or CTLA-4 (which block suppression at the tumor interface) leave the SLAMF6 pathway intact in non-responding patients. An antibody that blocks SLAMF6 gives those patients a second immunotherapy switch that does not require the same tumor-T cell contact mechanism. The finding, published in Nature and developed at Université de Montréal, opens a new drug target class with different mechanism, different patient population (specifically the non-responders to existing checkpoints), and different combination therapy potential than anything currently approved.

The Anchor

SLAMF6 is not another immune checkpoint. It is the explanation for why existing checkpoints leave so many cancer patients behind — and a new target to reach them.

Known immune checkpoints — PD-1, PD-L1, CTLA-4 — work at the interface between T cells and tumor cells. Cancer cells express ligands that bind to these receptor molecules on T cells, signaling them to stand down. Drugs like pembrolizumab (Keytruda) and nivolumab (Opdivo) block those receptor-ligand interactions, releasing the T cell to attack. These therapies have transformed outcomes for melanoma, lung cancer, and a range of other cancers — but only in a fraction of patients. For a large portion of cancer patients, checkpoint inhibitor therapy does not produce a durable response, and oncologists have had limited mechanistic understanding of why. SLAMF6 is a candidate answer for at least a subset of those non-responders: it suppresses T cells through a different mechanism, one that does not require the tumor to initiate the suppression. If cancer has activated SLAMF6-mediated T-cell exhaustion before the T cells even reach the tumor, PD-1 blockade cannot help — because the suppression is not at the tumor interface. An anti-SLAMF6 antibody removes that earlier brake, potentially restoring T-cell function in patients who were written off as checkpoint-inhibitor non-responders.

The translational path from mouse models to human clinical trials is long — typically 5-10 years for a new mechanism — but the scientific significance is immediate: SLAMF6 is a validated, targetable immune checkpoint with an antibody-blockade proof-of-concept in a peer-reviewed Nature study. The commercial opportunity is substantial: the global cancer immunotherapy market is approximately $80 billion annually and growing, with the non-responder population representing the largest unmet need in oncology. Companies already in checkpoint inhibitor development — Merck (Keytruda), Bristol-Myers Squibb (Opdivo), AstraZeneca (Durvalumab) — are likely evaluating anti-SLAMF6 antibody combination strategies as of this week.

Dr. André Veillette and colleagues at the Université de Montréal and the Montreal Clinical Research Institute published findings in Nature (June 9) identifying SLAMF6 as a hidden immune checkpoint that allows cancer to evade destruction: unlike PD-1 and CTLA-4, which suppress T cells via tumor-cell contact, SLAMF6 activates directly on the T cell surface without requiring the tumor to initiate the suppression. This "self-activating" mechanism exhausts T cells before they reach the tumor, explaining why a meaningful fraction of patients whose tumors express normal PD-L1 levels still do not respond to checkpoint inhibitor therapy. Antibodies that block SLAMF6 restored T-cell cancer-killing activity in mouse models.

The clinical implication is significant: oncologists currently have limited mechanistic tools for predicting or addressing checkpoint inhibitor non-response. SLAMF6 offers a testable hypothesis (does the patient's T-cell population show elevated SLAMF6 expression?) and a targetable intervention (anti-SLAMF6 antibody, potentially in combination with existing PD-1 inhibitors). The drug development path typically takes 7-12 years from mechanism discovery to approval, but the proof-of-concept in mice at the first publication stage is the essential precondition. Biotech and pharma companies with checkpoint inhibitor programs are the most likely first movers toward SLAMF6-targeting clinical trials.

CO2-Capture Protein Beads · Dairy + Tofu Waste · Biodegradable · More Efficient CO₂ Scientists: biodegradable protein beads from dairy + tofu food industry waste · captures CO2 from atmosphere · more efficient than many current carbon capture technologies · protein-based material: biodegrades after use · circular economy application: food waste → carbon removal · carbon capture technology landscape: current methods expensive + energy-intensive · protein bead approach: lower-cost feedstock from existing waste stream

Scientists have developed biodegradable protein beads made from dairy and tofu manufacturing waste — discarded food industry byproducts — that capture carbon dioxide from the atmosphere more efficiently than many current carbon capture technologies. The beads are protein-based, biodegradable after use, and derived from existing waste streams, potentially reducing both the cost and the lifecycle carbon impact of direct air capture compared to synthetic sorbent materials that require dedicated manufacturing processes.

Carbon capture technology has faced persistent economic headwinds: most current direct air capture methods cost $400-$1,000 per ton of CO2 removed, making large-scale deployment economically challenging without substantial subsidies. Protein-based sorbents from food industry waste address the cost problem at the feedstock level — dairy and tofu production generates protein byproducts at scale that currently have limited industrial applications. If the efficiency advantage demonstrated in laboratory conditions scales to industrial deployment, this approach could materially lower the cost curve for direct air capture. The biodegradability of the protein beads after saturation also avoids the sorbent disposal problem that synthetic materials create.

A study of more than 2,100 adults found that eating more ultra-processed foods was linked to poorer attention and slower mental processing speed — effects observed even among participants with otherwise healthy overall diets, suggesting the association is driven by ultra-processed food's specific composition rather than total caloric intake or macronutrient balance. Concurrently, the largest review ever conducted on opioids for acute pain found that these widely prescribed drugs often deliver only small, short-lived benefits, with the magnitude of pain relief falling short of clinical significance thresholds in a majority of reviewed trials.

The ultra-processed food and cognitive function finding adds to a growing body of evidence linking food additive-heavy diets to neurological effects through gut microbiome disruption and neuroinflammation pathways. The finding that cognitive impact appears even in otherwise healthy diets suggests that ultra-processed food's specific chemical composition — not just excess calories or poor macronutrient balance — drives the observed effects, pointing toward food additives, emulsifiers, and artificial flavoring as the likely mechanisms under investigation. The opioid review is a public health finding with immediate clinical implications: acute pain opioid prescriptions have been issued at rates that far exceed the evidence base for their effectiveness, suggesting that non-opioid pain management protocols deserve significantly higher adoption in acute care settings.

Quick hits

  • SLAMF6's mechanism — self-activation on T cells without tumor contact — means it operates upstream of the tumor microenvironment. This has a practical implication for cancer treatment sequencing: if a patient's T cells are SLAMF6-exhausted before they reach the tumor, no amount of PD-1 or CTLA-4 blockade at the tumor interface can help. Anti-SLAMF6 therapy, if it advances to clinical approval, would likely be administered before or alongside existing checkpoint inhibitors rather than after, changing the treatment algorithm for immunotherapy-eligible patients. The non-responder population it targets — patients whose tumors seem amenable to immunotherapy but who do not respond — is one of the largest unmet needs in oncology.
  • The biodegradable protein bead CO2 capture story is one of a category of carbon removal approaches that repurpose existing biological waste streams rather than building dedicated industrial sorbent manufacturing. Other examples in this category include using agricultural crop residue, food waste digestate, and forestry byproducts as feedstocks for carbon-binding materials. The dairy and tofu protein bead approach is notable because both dairy and tofu manufacturing are large-scale industrial processes that generate protein-rich waste at volumes that could supply meaningful carbon capture capacity if the technology scales from laboratory to industrial demonstration.
  • Scientists discovered a colossal hidden structure beneath Antarctica's ice sheet — details of its nature and composition are still emerging from the initial geological survey — but its existence challenges existing models of Antarctic bedrock geology and ice sheet stability. The structure's depth and extent suggest it formed under conditions predating the current Antarctic ice sheet, which makes its presence relevant to models of how the continent's ice behaves under warming conditions. Antarctica's ice sheet holds enough water to raise global sea levels by approximately 57 meters if fully melted; understanding its bedrock structure is a precondition for accurate ice-loss projections.
