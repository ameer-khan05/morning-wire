UC Irvine reverses age-related vision loss by restoring the ELOVL2 aging gene.

Researchers at UC Irvine published evidence in Science Translational Medicine that targeted supplementation with specific polyunsaturated fatty acids can reverse age-related vision decline by restoring enzymatic function of the ELOVL2 gene in the retina — the first targeted reversal of this aging mechanism in animal models, with direct implications for age-related macular degeneration. Genetic variants in ELOVL2 also predict faster AMD progression, enabling risk stratification. Separately: new SuperAger research reveals adults over 80 can maintain memory equivalent to 50-year-olds; a newly discovered virus inside a common gut bacterium may explain the colorectal cancer link; and colchicine emerges as a potential heart disease treatment via chronic inflammation.

The Anchor

Fatty acid supplementation reverses aging in the retina — UC Irvine finds the ELOVL2 gene is the key.

Scientists at UC Irvine have published a study in Science Translational Medicine demonstrating that age-related vision loss can be reversed by supplementing the retina with specific polyunsaturated fatty acids — not just DHA, but a broader class of long-chain lipids that are produced by the ELOVL2 enzyme and decline as this gene's expression falls with age. ELOVL2 (Elongation of Very Long Chain Fatty Acids Protein 2) is one of the most widely used biological markers of aging; as the gene's activity declines, the retina accumulates a lipid deficit that impairs photoreceptor function and contributes to age-related macular degeneration. The UC Irvine team showed in mouse models that targeted fatty acid supplementation not only restored visual function but also reversed cellular aging signs — making it the first intervention to address the ELOVL2 mechanism directly. Separately, the team identified genetic variants in ELOVL2 associated with faster AMD progression, opening a path to precision risk stratification using simple genetic tests.

The study expands the team's prior work showing that lipid supplementation affects aging beyond the eye: a collaboration with UC San Diego found that a lack of ELOVL2 also accelerates immune-cell aging, and lipid supplementation may counteract age-related changes in the immune system and potentially play a role in blood cancer risk. Meanwhile, research on "SuperAgers" — a rare group of adults over 80 whose memory is comparable to people in their 50s — published separately this week finds that this population shows distinctive brain connectivity patterns and lower rates of tau accumulation, rewriting assumptions about what is biologically possible in the ninth decade of life. The SuperAger findings raise a tractable scientific question: what specific factors protect some brains from the tau-driven neurodegeneration that typically begins decades earlier?

Vision · ELOVL2 Reversed UC Irvine · Science Transl Med · AMD · lipids

UC Irvine reverses age-related vision loss in mice — ELOVL2 gene's lipid deficit is the target.

The ELOVL2 enzyme produces polyunsaturated fatty acids critical for retinal function. As ELOVL2 expression declines with age — a reliable epigenetic aging biomarker — the retina accumulates a lipid deficit that drives photoreceptor dysfunction and AMD progression. UC Irvine demonstrated that supplementing with the specific fatty acids ELOVL2 would normally produce reverses this deficit, restores visual acuity, and reduces cellular aging markers in retinal tissue. The effect held even in animals with ELOVL2 genetic variants associated with faster AMD progression. The researchers also identified genetic variants in ELOVL2 that predict AMD risk, allowing for both early detection and targeted supplementation as a personalized intervention strategy.

SuperAgers · Brain 80 = 50 Memory · tau · brain connectivity · aging

SuperAgers rewrite aging: adults over 80 with 50-year-old memory show distinct brain connectivity patterns.

A rare group of adults over 80 — dubbed "SuperAgers" — maintain memory and cognitive function comparable to people three decades younger, and new research is uncovering what makes them different. The SuperAger population shows lower rates of tau protein accumulation, the neurodegeneration marker associated with Alzheimer's, and distinctive brain connectivity patterns that appear to resist the atrophy typical in this age group. Researchers say the findings challenge the assumption that significant cognitive decline in the 80s is inevitable — and raise a tractable question: can the biological factors protecting SuperAgers be replicated, supplemented, or targeted pharmacologically in the broader aging population?

Colorectal · Virus Phage link Gut bacterium · cancer · Fusobacterium

Virus hiding inside a common gut bacterium may explain the colorectal cancer link — a bacteriophage finding.

Scientists have discovered a virus hiding inside a common gut bacterium — found in both healthy people and colorectal cancer patients — that may explain why the bacterium is consistently associated with cancer risk. Research suggests that the interaction between the bacterium and the bacteriophage it carries is key to understanding whether the host promotes or suppresses carcinogenesis. The finding opens a new axis of cancer biology: rather than asking whether the bacterium itself causes cancer, researchers can now ask whether the phage's integration into bacterial DNA triggers the molecular changes that promote tumor development. The discovery may also explain why the same bacterium appears in healthy and cancerous microbiomes — the phage's presence, not the bacterium alone, may determine risk.

Source: ScienceDaily · SciTechDaily
Heart Disease · Colchicine ScAm May Chronic inflammation · affordable · cardiovascular

Scientific American May cover: cheap colchicine drug could transform heart disease treatment via inflammation.

Scientific American's May 2026 cover story centers on the role of chronic inflammation in cardiovascular disease — and how colchicine, an inexpensive anti-inflammatory drug already approved for gout and used for decades, could offer a new treatment pathway. Recent large trials demonstrate that colchicine significantly reduces cardiovascular events in patients with established heart disease, with the mechanism being suppression of the NLRP3 inflammasome — a molecular pathway now understood to be central to atherosclerosis progression. Colchicine costs pennies per dose. If inflammation-reducing drugs become a standard cardiac prevention strategy, the public health implications — particularly in lower-income populations with limited access to expensive biologics — are substantial.

NIH · Funding Crisis $1B Grants behind · NCI 1-in-25 · labs closing

Continuing: NIH is $1 billion behind on grants — NCI grant odds fall to 1-in-25, labs on track to close.

The National Institutes of Health remains approximately $1 billion behind its typical grant-award timeline, per a Nature report, as White House spending holds, a 22% staffing cut, and a new lump-sum multi-year funding policy compound to delay thousands of research projects. At the National Cancer Institute, the odds of winning a research grant have fallen from 1-in-10 to 1-in-25. A national survey found 81% of junior tenure-track scientists are concerned funding disruptions could threaten their tenure prospects. The administration has partially restored some terminated grants following court orders, but 65% of affected researchers say their funding has not been fully restored. Labs are on track to close permanently. The UC Irvine ELOVL2 research — a multi-year project — is the type of work now most at risk from the new lump-sum funding policy.

Source: US News · Science / AAAS
Alzheimer's · Blood Test Years early Neutrophil NLR · 400K patients · NYU Langone

Continuing: routine blood neutrophil count predicts Alzheimer's risk years before symptoms — 400,000-patient study.

The NYU Langone study published this week — analyzing data from nearly 400,000 patients across two healthcare systems — found that elevated neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio (NLR) on a routine complete blood cell count is significantly associated with future Alzheimer's and dementia risk. The finding is the first large-scale validation that the NLR has predictive power for neurodegeneration. The association was stronger in Hispanic patients and women. The UC Irvine ELOVL2 research this week adds a complementary early-intervention perspective: while NYU Langone provides the detection marker, ELOVL2 research suggests a mechanism through which lipid-level interventions could alter the trajectory of aging-related neurodegeneration before symptoms appear.
