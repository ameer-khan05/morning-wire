A Finnish 10-year randomized controlled trial finds that partial meniscectomy — the most commonly performed orthopedic procedure in the world — accelerates knee osteoarthritis progression: 81% of surgical patients developed worsening OA versus 70% of sham-surgery controls, with 8 surgical reoperations versus 3 in the sham group.

The Finnish trial, published Monday in the New England Journal of Medicine, is the longest-follow-up randomized comparison of partial meniscectomy to sham surgery ever conducted. Approximately four million partial meniscectomies are performed annually worldwide, predominantly for degenerative meniscal tears in patients over forty. The 10-year data answers the question the prior five-year follow-up left open: does the accelerated OA signal seen at five years resolve, stabilize, or compound over a decade? The answer is compound. At ten years, surgical patients have an 11-percentage-point higher OA progression rate and nearly three times the reoperation burden of patients who received sham surgery. The finding challenges the central clinical rationale for meniscectomy in degenerative tears — that removing the damaged tissue prevents the mechanical irritation that drives OA — and replaces it with the opposite thesis: that removing the meniscus, which acts as a load-distributing shock absorber in the knee joint, accelerates the cartilage-on-cartilage loading that drives OA more than the intact degenerative tissue does. Also today: SP6/SP8 limb regeneration genes (PNAS); youthful gut microbiome prevents liver cancer (DDW 2026); self-selected music boosts endurance +20%; NIH crisis deepening.

The Anchor

The 11-percentage-point OA progression gap between surgical and sham patients at ten years is not a side effect of surgery — it is the primary outcome. The meniscus is a load distributor; removing it accelerates cartilage loss faster than a degenerative intact meniscus does. Four million procedures a year now face the question this trial has been building toward for a decade.

The partial meniscectomy debate has been running in the orthopedic literature since the first sham-controlled trial was published in 2013 (the Finnish FIDELITY trial), which found no symptom benefit of meniscectomy over sham surgery at one year. Subsequent follow-up at two and five years found that symptoms remained equivalent between groups while OA signal was elevated in the surgical group. Each finding was met with the same rebuttal: the follow-up period is too short; OA takes a decade to manifest; the five-year data may not reflect the long-term outcome. The ten-year data eliminates that rebuttal. At ten years, the OA progression difference between surgical and sham patients has not resolved — it has grown. The 81%-versus-70% OA progression rate difference represents an absolute 11-percentage-point elevation in OA risk attributable to surgery over sham in the same patient population with the same degenerative meniscal tears. The reoperation gap — 8 surgical versus 3 sham — tells the clinical implementation story: surgical patients are returning to the operating room at nearly three times the rate of sham patients, in many cases for OA-driven total knee replacement that the original meniscectomy was intended to prevent. The mechanism is biomechanical and well understood. The meniscus distributes load across the knee joint surface; without it, load concentrates on the articular cartilage in ways that accelerate the cartilage degradation that is the definition of osteoarthritis. A degenerative meniscal tear — the indication for the majority of meniscectomies performed in patients over forty — is not an injury to a previously healthy meniscus but a degeneration of already-compromised tissue. Removing that tissue eliminates the symptom-generating mechanical irritation but also eliminates the remaining load-distribution function the compromised meniscus still provides. The net effect, at ten years, is accelerated OA.

The clinical practice implication is the hardest part of this finding. Approximately four million partial meniscectomies are performed annually worldwide; in the United States alone, roughly 800,000 are performed each year, generating an estimated $4 billion in annual surgical revenue. The patients receiving these procedures predominantly have degenerative meniscal tears — middle-aged patients with knee pain whose MRIs show meniscal degeneration interpreted as a surgically addressable cause of symptoms. The ten-year Finnish data suggests that this interpretation is wrong in both directions: the surgery does not improve symptoms compared to sham, and it accelerates the OA outcome the patient is most trying to avoid. The practical alternatives — physical therapy, NSAIDs, activity modification, intra-articular injections — are less definitive than surgery in the short term but do not carry the ten-year OA acceleration penalty. The immediate practice question is what orthopedic surgeons tell patients with degenerative meniscal tears presenting today. The honest informed-consent conversation, in light of ten years of randomized data, is that surgery will provide symptom relief equivalent to a sham procedure while increasing the probability of OA progression by 11 percentage points over the following decade and nearly tripling the likelihood of reoperation. That is a different conversation than the one most patients with knee pain and a meniscal tear on MRI are currently receiving.

Meniscectomy · NEJM · 10-Year RCT 81% OA Partial meniscectomy · Finnish 10-year RCT · NEJM · OA progression 81% surgical vs 70% sham · 8 vs 3 reoperations · degenerative tear indication · meniscus load-distribution function · practice-changing

The Finnish FIDELITY-10 trial in NEJM: 10-year follow-up of randomized partial meniscectomy versus sham surgery in degenerative meniscal tears. At 10 years, 81% of surgical patients had OA progression versus 70% of sham patients. Eight surgical patients required reoperation versus three sham patients. The symptom difference between groups: still not significant.

The FIDELITY-10 trial is the culmination of a Finnish academic orthopedic research program that has been systematically building the randomized evidence base on meniscectomy for over a decade. The original FIDELITY trial (Sihvonen et al., NEJM 2013) enrolled 146 patients with symptomatic degenerative meniscal tears who were randomly assigned to arthroscopic partial meniscectomy or sham surgery (skin incisions, no arthroscopic procedure). The one-year follow-up showed no significant difference in knee pain, function, or patient-reported outcomes between groups. The five-year follow-up (published 2018) found continued symptom equivalence plus an emerging OA signal favoring sham surgery. FIDELITY-10 extends the same cohort to ten years, with 89% follow-up retention. The ten-year findings are unambiguous on OA but nuanced on symptoms: the 81%-versus-70% OA progression gap is statistically significant and clinically meaningful; the symptom scores between groups remain not significantly different, meaning surgical patients are experiencing more OA without reporting substantially more pain or functional limitation — a finding consistent with how OA often progresses silently in its early stages before becoming symptomatic in later years. The 8-versus-3 reoperation gap is the harbinger: most of the surgical reoperations were for OA-driven total knee replacement, which is the symptomatic manifestation of the OA acceleration the imaging data has been showing. The practical timeline: OA progression detected by imaging at year 10 typically becomes symptomatic and functionally limiting at year 15–20. The patients in this trial who had meniscectomy in the early 2010s are in their 50s now; they will be in their 60s and 70s when the OA the surgery accelerated reaches the TKR threshold — at significantly higher rates than the sham group.

SP6/SP8 · Limb Regen · PNAS SP8 SP6/SP8 transcription factors · PNAS · axolotl / zebrafish / mice · CRISPR SP8 knockout blocks limb regeneration · FGF8 gene therapy restores mouse digit regrowth · evolutionary conservation · human therapeutic implications

PNAS publishes the identification of SP6 and SP8 transcription factors as master regulators of limb regeneration in axolotl, zebrafish, and mice. CRISPR knockout of SP8 blocks limb regrowth in axolotl. FGF8 gene therapy that restores SP8-pathway signaling partially recovers digit regrowth in adult mice — the first demonstration of induced digit regeneration in a mammalian model.

The SP6/SP8 transcription factor discovery is significant because it provides a mechanistic explanation for why some species regenerate limbs and others do not — and because the explanation is conserved across species in a way that creates a direct therapeutic hypothesis for mammals. SP6 and SP8 are zinc-finger transcription factors expressed during embryonic limb development in all vertebrates; both are required for normal digit formation in the embryo. What the PNAS study identifies is that SP8 remains strongly expressed during limb regeneration in axolotl and zebrafish — species with robust limb regeneration capacity — but is silenced after embryonic development in mice, the species without limb regeneration capacity. The functional test: CRISPR knockout of SP8 in axolotl completely blocks limb regeneration, confirming SP8 as not merely correlated with but required for regeneration. The therapeutic direction: if SP8 silencing explains why mammals cannot regenerate limbs, can re-activating SP8 expression restore regeneration capacity? The PNAS study tests this in adult mice using FGF8 gene therapy — FGF8 is a signaling molecule that activates SP8 expression during embryonic development. Mice treated with intra-wound FGF8 gene therapy after digit amputation showed partial digit tip regrowth in a subset of treated animals — not full digit regeneration, but measurable tissue regrowth that did not occur in untreated controls. The result is incremental: partial digit tip regrowth is far from clinical limb regeneration. But the theoretical framework — SP8 silencing as the evolutionary switch that turns off regeneration in mammals, FGF8 as the pathway to re-activate it — provides a specific molecular target that can be developed systematically toward more complete regeneration. The human therapeutic implication is a decade or more from clinical application, but the mechanism is now identified and the first mammalian proof of concept exists.

Gut Microbiome · Liver Cancer · DDW 2026 0 vs 2/8 Youthful gut microbiome transplant · prevents liver cancer in mice · DDW 2026 · UTMB · Dr. Qingjie Li · 0/group cancer vs 2/8 controls · MDM2 oncogene suppressed · FMT therapeutic direction

Presented at Digestive Disease Week 2026 (DDW): UTMB researcher Dr. Qingjie Li reports that transplanting gut microbiome from young mice into old mice completely prevented liver cancer development in the treated group (0 tumors) versus 2 of 8 controls. MDM2, a key oncogene that promotes tumor cell survival, was suppressed in the transplant group. The finding points toward fecal microbiota transplant (FMT) as a liver cancer prevention strategy.

The UTMB DDW 2026 finding adds to a rapidly growing body of evidence connecting gut microbiome composition to systemic cancer risk — specifically to the age-related increase in liver cancer that has been one of the least-understood patterns in oncology epidemiology. Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) incidence increases sharply with age in the absence of the conventional liver cancer risk factors (hepatitis B/C, cirrhosis, alcohol use); the UTMB study proposes that age-related microbiome dysbiosis — the shift from younger, more diverse microbial communities to older, less diverse ones characterized by different dominant species — is a contributing driver of that age-associated HCC risk. The experimental design: old mice with age-related microbiome profiles received fecal microbiota transplant from young donors before carcinogen exposure. Zero mice in the transplant group developed liver cancer; 2 of 8 control mice (standard old-mouse microbiome) developed tumors. The mechanistic finding — MDM2 suppression in the transplant group — is the most translatable part: MDM2 is a well-characterized oncogene that promotes tumor cell survival by inhibiting p53-mediated apoptosis. Microbial metabolites produced by younger microbiome communities appear to suppress MDM2 expression in hepatocytes, restoring p53-pathway cancer surveillance in cells that older microbiome dysbiosis has left with inadequate tumor-suppressor activity. The clinical direction is FMT: if the anti-cancer effect is transferable through microbiome transplant, the therapeutic strategy is FMT from young donors to aging patients with elevated HCC risk — a non-pharmacological intervention that modifies the gut microbial environment rather than targeting the cancer directly. FMT is an established procedure for Clostridioides difficile infection; its extension to cancer prevention would require demonstration of safety and efficacy in human trials, which this mouse study does not provide. But the mechanistic clarity — specific oncogene suppression linked to a specific microbial community effect — makes the human trial design tractable.

Music · Cycling Endurance · +20% +20% Self-selected music · cycling endurance +20% · RPE reduction · motivation pathway · new research · dissociation hypothesis · arousal regulation · tempo matching · practical athletic application

New research finds that cyclists who selected their own music playlists rode 20% longer at a fixed moderate intensity than those who exercised in silence or with researcher-selected music. The effect is driven by two mechanisms: dissociation from perceived exertion, and music-induced arousal and motivation that counteract the psychological cues driving early cessation.

The music-endurance effect has been studied since the 1990s, with consistent findings that music improves exercise performance through two established mechanisms: dissociation (music competes with fatigue and discomfort signals for attentional resources, reducing perceived exertion) and arousal/motivation (music activates reward circuitry and increases physiological and psychological readiness to sustain effort). The new finding's advance over prior research is in the self-selection dimension: the 20% endurance boost is substantially larger than the 10–15% effects documented in prior studies using researcher-selected music, suggesting that the emotional and motivational resonance of personally meaningful music is a significant component of the effect — not just tempo-matching or arousal level. The self-selected music condition also showed greater reductions in Rating of Perceived Exertion (RPE) than the researcher-selected condition, indicating that the personally meaningful dimension of music selection — the emotional associations, the sense of agency in the choice, the familiarity and positive reinforcement history — amplifies the dissociation mechanism beyond what neutral tempo-matched music achieves. The practical implication for exercise prescription is straightforward and implementation-ready: encouraging patients or athletes to curate their own exercise playlists, rather than listening to generic workout music or silence, can produce endurance improvements that exceed many pharmacological ergogenic aids without any side effects. The finding is most relevant for moderate-intensity sustained exercise (the intensity that provides the greatest cardiovascular health benefits) where psychological factors — not physiological capacity — are the primary constraint on duration. Self-selected music is one of the most accessible behavioral interventions available for improving exercise adherence in populations that struggle with sustained effort.

Continuing: NIH mid-course trial interruptions are the crisis's most direct patient harm — enrolled participants losing study access, in some cases losing their primary source of clinical care. The 1-in-25 NCI grant rate means three-quarters of fundable cancer research is currently unfunded. The meniscectomy story illustrates what the US loses when it cannot fund decade-scale RCTs.

The NIH funding and governance crisis has entered its most clinically consequential phase: not delayed new grants but mid-course interruptions to active trials that enrolled patients are experiencing today. Participants who enrolled in studies — in some cases as their primary source of access to an investigational treatment — are being removed from studies before protocol completion. This disrupts the scientific dataset (partial data from interrupted trials is substantially less useful than completed data) and in some cases removes clinical care from patients who relied on study participation for treatment access. The National Cancer Institute's current grant funding rate — approximately 1 in 25 reviewed applications funded, compared to the historical average of roughly 1 in 6 — means 75% of applications deemed scientifically meritorious by peer review are unfunded for budget reasons. More than 100 federal science advisory committees remain terminated. Today's lead science story — the Finnish partial meniscectomy 10-year RCT — illustrates precisely what the US research infrastructure must maintain to generate this kind of evidence. The FIDELITY program required: a decade of patient follow-up, continuous registry access to national imaging and reoperation data, a sustained academic research group, and public research funding from the Finnish Research Council. The equivalent US trial would require NIH funding for a multi-site RCT with ten years of active follow-up, continuous linkage to Medicare claims data for reoperation tracking, and the sustained investigator infrastructure that NIH R01 grants support. With 800,000 meniscectomies performed annually in the US — generating $4 billion in revenue — the research question of whether those procedures benefit patients is exactly the kind of comparative effectiveness question that NIH's Patient-Centered Outcomes Research Institute (PCORI) was designed to fund. The current funding environment is degrading precisely that capacity at the moment when the Finnish data makes the US comparative effectiveness trial most urgent.

Quick hits

  • The partial meniscectomy finding has a specific implication for how the MRI-finding-to-surgical-recommendation pipeline works in orthopedic practice. The problem the FIDELITY-10 data describes is not that surgeons are performing meniscectomies on healthy menisci — it is that the presence of a degenerative meniscal tear on MRI is being used as a justification for surgery in patients whose symptoms (knee pain, reduced function) would have equivalent outcomes from physical therapy and activity modification without the ten-year OA acceleration risk. The MRI-to-surgery pipeline is driven in part by patient psychology: patients with knee pain who receive an MRI expect the MRI to show something actionable, and a degenerative tear is the visible finding that satisfies the expectation of an actionable diagnosis. The surgeon who recommends physical therapy to a patient who came for confirmation that their MRI finding is surgically correctable faces a patient satisfaction and practice management challenge that the surgeon who recommends surgery does not face. FIDELITY-10 does not change the biomechanics or the imaging findings; it changes the honest interpretation of what surgery does and does not accomplish for those findings. Orthopedic societies' clinical practice guidelines will need to be revised in light of ten years of level 1 evidence; the revision process typically takes 18–24 months from publication to updated guideline. The 800,000 US patients who will have a meniscectomy in the next 18 months will have it under the old guidelines.
  • The SP6/SP8 transcription factor discovery's most important theoretical contribution is the evolutionary framing: it places limb regeneration loss not as a fundamental biological impossibility in mammals but as a specific developmental choice — SP8 silencing after embryogenesis — that can, in principle, be reversed. The prior dominant hypothesis was that mammals lost limb regeneration capacity as an evolutionary trade-off for immune surveillance (the immune system that prevents tumor formation is incompatible with the permissive tissue-growth environment required for regeneration). The SP8 data provides a different explanation: mammals did not lose regeneration capacity wholesale, they turned off the transcription factor that activates the regenerative developmental program after it was no longer needed for embryonic growth. The immune-surveillance explanation remains relevant — human wounds do not regenerate even with SP8 activation, and immune suppression may be required alongside transcription factor re-activation — but the SP8 finding provides a specific molecular entry point that the immune-surveillance hypothesis alone did not. The FGF8 gene therapy result in mice is incremental (partial digit tip regrowth) but structurally important: it demonstrates that the SP8 pathway is still activatable in adult mammalian tissue, not permanently silenced in a way that cannot be reversed. The distance from partial digit tip regrowth in mice to clinical limb regeneration in humans is enormous — but the distance is now measured in specific molecular steps rather than the vague "we don't know how regeneration works" that characterized the field five years ago.
  • The gut microbiome liver cancer prevention finding connects to a broader research question about the relationship between microbiome aging and systemic cancer risk. The hepatocellular carcinoma result is one of three cancer types where microbiome dysbiosis has been mechanistically linked to tumor development in animal models in the past 18 months: colorectal cancer (where specific Fusobacterium nucleatum strains promote tumor progression), pancreatic cancer (where microbiome composition affects immune infiltration of the tumor microenvironment), and now liver cancer (where MDM2 suppression via young-donor microbial metabolites reduces hepatocyte malignant transformation). The common thread is the microbiome's role in systemic immune and metabolic regulation — its influence is not confined to gastrointestinal cancer but extends through circulating metabolite and immune signaling to organs far from the gut. The MDM2 suppression mechanism in the UTMB study is particularly significant because MDM2 is not gut-specific: it is expressed in hepatocytes throughout the body, and its p53-inhibiting function is relevant to cancer surveillance across many tissue types. If young-donor microbial metabolites suppress MDM2 in liver, they may suppress it in other tissues as well — making the youthful microbiome a systemic cancer-surveillance enhancer rather than an organ-specific effect. The human FMT trial design that follows from this finding would test MDM2 expression and cancer biomarkers in aging patients before and after FMT from young donors — a study that could be conducted at multiple sites with existing FMT infrastructure, pending institutional review and FDA investigational new drug approval for the cancer prevention indication.
  • The music endurance finding's 20% improvement in time-to-exhaustion is placed in context by comparison to known ergogenic interventions. Caffeine at 3–6 mg/kg body weight improves endurance performance by approximately 3–5% in trained athletes in controlled trials. Beta-alanine supplementation improves performance in events lasting 1–4 minutes by approximately 2–3%. Altitude training benefits persist for 3–5 weeks post-camp and produce 1–3% performance improvements in trained athletes. Self-selected music's 20% improvement in recreational cyclists is larger than any of these interventions, requires no supplementation, costs nothing, and has no side effects. The caveat is methodological: the 20% figure applies to moderate-intensity sustained cycling to volitional exhaustion in recreational athletes — the conditions under which psychological fatigue rather than physiological capacity determines endpoint. Elite athletes competing at near-maximal intensity have limited room to benefit from dissociation because physiological limitation (not psychological tolerance) terminates their effort. For the target population most relevant to public health — recreational exercisers doing moderate-intensity aerobic exercise where psychological factors determine duration — self-selected music is the most evidence-supported, accessible, and cost-free intervention available for increasing exercise dose. Exercise prescription guidelines do not currently include music recommendations; they should.
  • The NIH crisis's connection to the partial meniscectomy finding is the clearest illustration of what public research funding produces that no other funding source replicates. The FIDELITY trial series was funded by the Finnish Research Council — public funding for a ten-year study of a procedure performed four million times annually worldwide, generating billions in annual revenue. No private funder conducted this research: the medical device and surgical equipment industry that profits from meniscectomy has no financial incentive to fund a trial demonstrating the procedure is harmful; no pharmaceutical company funds surgical procedure trials; no hospital system funds research demonstrating that a high-revenue procedure causes long-term harm. Only public research funding with academic freedom from commercial conflicts can sustain the decade-scale, conflict-free, patient-centered research that generates FIDELITY-10-class evidence. The US equivalent — a ten-year RCT of meniscectomy versus physical therapy with complete Medicare claims follow-up for OA progression and reoperation — would require PCORI or NIH funding. PCORI's budget has been targeted for reduction in the current federal funding environment. The research that would most directly protect 800,000 American patients annually from a procedure this data suggests causes net harm is the research that the current funding trajectory makes least likely to be funded.
  • The four science findings today — meniscectomy OA, SP8 regeneration, gut microbiome cancer prevention, music endurance — share a structural feature with the NIH crisis commentary: they are all examples of research whose time horizons are incompatible with private funding incentives. A ten-year randomized trial of a surgical procedure generates no intellectual property. A transcription factor that unlocks limb regeneration in mice is a decade or more from any clinical product. A microbiome transplant protocol is not patentable in the traditional sense. The behavioral effect of self-selected music cannot be monetized by a pharmaceutical company. All four are the kind of research that public funding systems — NIH, NSF, PCORI, Research Councils — exist to support precisely because private markets will not. The progressive degradation of that public funding infrastructure does not reduce the questions that need answering; it reduces the capacity to answer them. The answers to the meniscectomy question, found in Finland, will change practice globally — including in the United States — but the US will not have generated them. That is the cost of the NIH crisis measured not in grant numbers but in knowledge not produced.
