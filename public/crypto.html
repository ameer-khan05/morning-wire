BTC at $62,029 is below the analyst July floor established before Wednesday's Iran escalation, which introduces a macro context the prior forecasts did not price. The Iran variable has two divergent implications for crypto: in the short term, active military conflict and oil above $72 produce risk-off conditions that correlate with BTC selling; in the medium term, oil inflation that delays the September Fed rate cut extends the risk-off backdrop. The counterweight is the weak US jobs data: labor market softness increases the probability that the Fed cuts in September regardless of oil, because employment weighs more in the Fed's dual mandate than commodity price shocks. BTC at $62,029 is the market pricing both signals simultaneously — geopolitical risk pressing down, rate-cut expectations pushing up. Bitcoin's decline to $62,029 is macro-driven, not crypto-specific. When US forces strike Iranian military targets in the Strait of Hormuz and the semiconductor sector falls 5%+ simultaneously, institutional investors reduce risk across all asset classes indiscriminately — equities, crypto, and other risk assets together. BTC's $2,004 single-session decline from $64,033 measures the market's geopolitical risk premium. The fact that BTC declined 1.16% while the Nasdaq fell 1.16% and the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index fell 5%+ is actually a relative-strength signal: crypto held pace with equities but absorbed far less damage than the sector most directly exposed to the AI chip demand repricing.

The weak US jobs data arriving in the same 24-hour window as the Iran escalation created a competing macro signal that is structurally bullish for BTC but operates on a longer timeline than the geopolitical shock. Labor market softness increases the probability of a September Fed rate cut — which reduces real yields, softens the dollar, and creates the macro conditions for BTC to recover toward the $68,099 analyst monthly average. But "September" is 53 days away; "US-Iran military exchange" is happening now. BTC at $62,029 instead of below $60,000 suggests the rate-cut support is real and creating a floor. The July 31 FOMC is the event that resolves the tension between the two signals.

Bitcoin fell from $64,033 to $62,029 (▼1.16%) on Wednesday as US military strikes on Iran following the Strait of Hormuz tanker attacks — combined with a 5%+ semiconductor sector sell-off from DeepSeek AI chip reports and Samsung's earnings — produced a broad risk-off environment, pushing BTC below the analyst-consensus July floor of $62,939 for the first time this month. Ethereum declined marginally to $1,734 (▼0.17%), holding above the critical $1,716 daily S1 pivot support that the Cryptonomist's analysis identifies as the threshold below which bearish pressure accelerates, with the 200-hour exponential moving average at $1,723 providing intraday support. Both assets declining together — without the sector rotation that produced ETH underperformance on earlier days — signals that selling is macro-driven rather than crypto-specific.

BTC's $2,004 single-day decline from Tuesday's close measures the geopolitical risk premium the market assigned to the US-Iran military exchange. The relative comparison is notable: crypto held pace with the Nasdaq's 1.16% decline but absorbed far less damage than the semiconductor sector's 5%+ crash, suggesting the rate-cut expectation from the weak jobs data created a floor for crypto that equities exposed to chip demand repricing did not have. Ethereum's hold above $1,716 is the first technical test of the support level that analysts identified as the battleground for Wednesday's session.

Weak US jobs data reported alongside Tuesday's Iran escalation produced competing signals for Bitcoin: labor market softening raised Fed rate-cut expectations — Sunday Guardian Live reported BTC crossing $61,800 specifically on the jobs print as investors priced higher probability of a September cut — while the oil shock from the Strait of Hormuz attacks simultaneously pushed 10-year Treasury yields up 17 basis points to 4.45%, creating USD strength that acts as a near-term BTC headwind. The July 31 FOMC is now the resolution event: if the statement confirms September rate-cut expectations despite higher oil, the employment-driven BTC support thesis dominates; if it signals oil inflation delays the September cut, the risk-off backdrop extends. Rain's $812 million unlock arrives July 11 — three days away — into this uncertain macro environment rather than Monday's cleaner post-Hyperliquid context.

The dual-signal environment explains why BTC's decline to $62,029 is shallower than a pure risk-off session would produce. BTC is pricing both signals simultaneously: falling because geopolitical risk-off is real, but not crashing because the rate-cut support is also real. The FOMC on July 31 is not just the next scheduled policy event — it is the mechanism that resolves the competition between the two signals operating on different timelines. Employment data (September cut supportive) wins or oil inflation data (September cut delayed) wins; the FOMC statement is where that verdict lands.
