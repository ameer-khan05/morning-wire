The Hormuz blockade enforcement pushes the 10-year Treasury yield higher (now 4.62%, up 10 basis points from Monday's 4.52%) as oil-driven inflation fears feed into the rate outlook, creating a hawkish macro backdrop that historically works against speculative risk assets including BTC and ETH. ETH's slightly larger decline than BTC on a percentage basis (-2.71% vs -1.49%) may reflect the stablecoin yield uncertainty in the CLARITY Act negotiations: if yield-bearing stablecoins are excluded from the bill, the ETH-denominated DeFi yield ecosystem loses its most significant potential growth catalyst.

Against the risk-off headwind, the CLARITY Act is advancing on the most favorable legislative timeline it has seen. PYMNTS reported Tuesday that the bill "nears the Senate floor ahead of the recess deadline," confirming the floor action targeted for the week of July 20 remains on track. Galaxy Digital has bet $10 million on 2026 passage, and the bill has been placed on the Senate Calendar — the procedural step that confirms leadership's intention to bring it to a floor vote. Yahoo Finance's Senate-sourced analysis identified four remaining steps: a merged bill expected this week (reconciling the Banking and Agriculture Committee texts), a Senate floor vote during the week of July 20, House-Senate conference reconciliation, and presidential signature. The August 7 hard deadline is unchanged. Seven Senate Democrats remain the critical vote count; their appetite for a crypto bill in a week of Hormuz blockade enforcement and oil-driven inflation will be tested against competing legislative priorities.

Yahoo Finance's sourced Senate analysis described Tuesday's CLARITY Act negotiations as "last-ditch talks that could decide crypto's fate for the rest of the decade" — with the merged bill expected this week needing to resolve three disputes: the ethics disclosure provisions around Trump's estimated $1.4 billion in 2025 cryptocurrency income, the law enforcement access rules under Section 604 that the National District Attorneys Association has contested, and the stablecoin yield question that determines whether digital asset platforms can pay interest-equivalent yields on stablecoin balances — the resolution of which sets the structure of the $150+ billion stablecoin market for the next decade, with Coinbase's $1.35 billion in annual USDC rewards and the American Bankers Association's structural deposit-franchise objection representing irreconcilable positions unless a legislative compromise is struck this week.

The stablecoin yield dispute is worth examining at the scale the banking industry is actually concerned about. US retail savings accounts hold roughly $10 trillion in deposits. If yield-bearing stablecoins — accessible through any smartphone, with no minimum balance and no geographic restriction — can offer competitive or superior yields to bank savings products, the structural substitution risk is not a policy abstraction but a business model threat to retail banking's funding model. That is why the American Bankers Association's opposition is categorical: it is not about Coinbase's $1.35 billion; it is about the structural protection of the deposit franchise. The CLARITY Act cannot pass with seven Senate Democrats by including stablecoin yield without managing that opposition — which means the merged bill's stablecoin yield language is the legislative text that will be scrutinized most closely when it drops this week, and whose final form will define whether this week's Senate window represents crypto's decade-defining regulatory moment or another round of productive failure.
