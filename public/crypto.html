Bitcoin fell 4% to $72,714 Thursday morning — the largest single-day decline since the conflict began — as US military strikes on Iranian targets on May 25-27 reignited oil and inflation fears, and the April PCE print of 3.8% confirmed those fears with hard data. BlackRock's Bitcoin ETF shed $528 million in a single session, the second-largest daily outflow on record. Ethereum broke below $2,000 to $1,989. The Federal Reserve's rate-hike probability by year-end hit 39%.

The mechanism behind Thursday's BTC decline is analytically distinct from the macro ceiling that has constrained crypto since October 2025. The previous constraint was "Fed cannot cut" — a ceiling created by oil-driven inflation keeping the Fed frozen at 3.5-3.75%. Thursday's decline introduces a new and more adverse constraint: "Fed may raise" — a 39% probability of at least one rate hike by year-end, up from near zero last week. These are qualitatively different conditions. A Fed that cannot cut requires BTC to sustain against a stable risk-free rate; a Fed that may raise requires BTC to sustain against a rising risk-free rate that will compress every fixed-income and risk-asset market simultaneously. The April PCE data (3.8% headline, 3.3% core) that triggered the rate-hike probability repricing is not a one-month anomaly — it reflects a full month of WTI prices at $100+ (April average approximately $104-107) flowing through the energy component of the consumer price index. The May PCE, to be reported in late June, will reflect April-May oil prices that have ranged from $87 to $108, suggesting headline inflation will remain above 3.5% for at least two more months regardless of whether the Iran deal is completed this week.

The Anchor

BlackRock's $528 million Bitcoin ETF outflow is not just a Bitcoin story. It is a signal that institutional investors are repricing their risk-asset exposure in response to a Fed policy pivot probability that moved from near zero to 39% in less than a week. When the largest institutional Bitcoin vehicle sees its second-biggest daily outflow, the macro ceiling isn't merely intact — it's descended to a new level.

The BlackRock Bitcoin ETF outflow requires understanding the mechanism through which institutional investors respond to rapid changes in Fed policy probability. BlackRock's IBIT is held primarily by institutional investors — endowments, pension funds, hedge funds, and family offices — that manage portfolios using modern portfolio theory frameworks in which each asset's allocation is calibrated against its risk-adjusted return relative to alternatives, particularly Treasury yields. When the Fed's rate-hike probability moves from near zero to 39% in a single week, the expected Treasury yield path shifts significantly: a rate hike to 3.75-4.0% (the implied level if the Fed raises once) would mean 10-year Treasury yields in the 4.6-4.8% range, representing higher real returns on risk-free assets. At that yield level, the risk-adjusted allocation to BTC — which generates no yield and has a realized volatility of approximately 45-50% annualized — declines mechanically under any reasonable portfolio optimization framework. The $528 million outflow represents institutional investors reducing their IBIT position to reflect the new Fed probability, not a capitulation or fundamental reassessment of BTC's long-term value. It is portfolio management in real time, following a hard-data shift in the monetary policy outlook. The scale of the outflow — second-largest on record for the fund — reflects that the PCE and strike news constituted a sufficiently large policy shift to trigger immediate position adjustment rather than wait for the next quarterly rebalancing.

The Iran strikes' contribution to BTC's decline operates through a different but complementary channel. The May 25-27 US military strikes on Iranian targets — launched three to five days after Trump said a deal was "largely negotiated" — created uncertainty about the timing and reliability of the oil price relief that the deal would provide. WTI, which had fallen from $108.59 to below $87 between May 20-26 on deal expectations, rebounded to $90.35 and Brent to $96.57 on the strike news. For BTC, the oil rebound matters because it directly affects the May and June CPI expectations that determine whether the Fed can avoid raising rates. At WTI $90-97 for May, the May CPI energy component contribution will be materially lower than the April contribution (WTI was $104-108 in April), providing some relief on the June PCE report. But the strikes' complication of the deal timeline means the oil price relief from a Hormuz reopening may not fully materialize before the June Fed meeting (June 16-17), leaving the rate-hike risk embedded in the policy calendar regardless of deal progress this week. The GENIUS Act regulatory framework — OCC national trust bank charters for Circle, Paxos, and three other nonbank financial firms, with implementing regulations advancing through the multi-agency rulemaking process — represents the structural regulatory tailwind that continues independently of the macro headwind. Two tracks, different speeds. The macro track moved adversely this week.

The decline extended BTC's pullback from its early May highs above $82,000 and wiped out approximately $900 million in leveraged long positions across crypto markets. The Federal Reserve's rate-hike probability by year-end hit 39% per CME FedWatch following the PCE release.

The BTC-Iran relationship operates through the oil-inflation-rate chain that has defined crypto's macro ceiling since February 28. When US strikes on Iranian targets push oil higher — Brent rose 2.41% to $96.57 on Thursday — they signal that the deal timeline is delayed and the oil price relief from a Hormuz reopening is pushed further out. Delayed oil relief means the May PCE will reflect higher-than-expected energy prices longer, which maintains the Fed's pressure toward rate preservation or, as Thursday's data implies, potential rate hikes. BTC at $72,714 versus its May 5 level of approximately $75,800 — the pre-strike, pre-PCE level — represents the market's pricing of the rate-hike scenario: not a catastrophic decline, but a measurable repricing of the risk-asset premium that BTC requires relative to Treasuries when the Fed may be tightening rather than simply frozen. The early-May high above $82,000 reflected the brief window when Iran deal optimism sent WTI toward $87 and compressed rate-hike expectations. Thursday's reversal restored the macro ceiling and added a new adverse element: rate-hike risk.

BlackRock's Bitcoin ETF (IBIT) shed $528 million in a single trading session Thursday — the second-largest daily outflow since the fund's January 2024 launch — as institutional investors reduced risk-asset exposure in response to the April PCE data confirming inflation above the Fed's target and the Iran-strike news complicating the oil price relief timeline. The outflow's scale, relative to the fund's approximately $22-25 billion in assets, represents a portfolio adjustment of approximately 2-2.5% — consistent with a model-driven rebalancing rather than a discretionary exit.

The IBIT outflow timing and scale are analytically informative about how institutional BTC holders respond to macro policy shifts. The fund saw net inflows throughout April and into early May as the Iran deal optimism built — investors were adding BTC exposure as WTI fell from $108 and rate-cut expectations returned to the market. The reversal on Thursday, following the PCE print and the Iran strike news, represents the speed with which institutional investors can recalibrate when the two inputs that had driven inflows (oil declining, Fed policy improving) reversed simultaneously in a 48-hour window. For the longer-term BTC outlook, the IBIT outflow is informative but not determinative: the institutional adoption thesis for BTC through regulated ETF vehicles is not contradicted by a single large outflow in response to a macro event. The structural tailwind of regulated institutional access (ETFs for BTC and ETH, OCC charters for stablecoin issuers, GENIUS Act framework) remains intact. The question is whether Thursday's 39% rate-hike probability settles back toward lower levels as the Iran deal progresses, or whether the PCE data represents a durable shift in the Fed's probability calculus that sustains the institutional risk-reduction impulse for multiple weeks.

Altcoins declined more sharply: SOL fell 6.2% to $139.20, DOGE fell 6.4% to $0.161. The ETH/BTC ratio is near its annual low, confirming the flight-to-quality-within-crypto pattern that has characterized every macro adverse event since the conflict began.

ETH's break below $2,000 is the specific price event that will attract the most crypto-market attention Thursday, because $2,000 is the psychological support level that had held through multiple macro adverse events since mid-April. The break does not indicate ETH-specific fundamental deterioration — Ethereum's network activity, staking participation, and developer metrics are not the driver of this move. The driver is the same institutional risk-reduction that produced BTC's 4% decline and the IBIT outflow: when institutions reduce crypto exposure, they sell in the liquidity order that produces the best execution, and ETH is the second-most liquid crypto asset after BTC. The BTC dominance rise to 62.1% — up 1.7 percentage points from recent levels — is the compression-within-compression pattern: as risk assets broadly sell off, BTC captures a higher share of declining total crypto market cap because it is the most defensible crypto position in an adverse macro environment. SOL's 6.2% and DOGE's 6.4% declines are proportionally larger than BTC's 4% and ETH's 3%, consistent with the standard risk-reduction hierarchy. The ETH/BTC ratio near its annual low is a reflection of the macro environment's duration: nine months of Iran-driven macro headwinds have steadily compressed the ETH/BTC ratio as each adverse macro event produces capital concentration in BTC over ETH.

The CME FedWatch tool showed a 39% probability of at least one Federal Reserve rate hike by year-end on Thursday morning — up from near zero last week — following the April PCE data (3.8% headline, 3.3% core) released before the market open. The current Fed funds rate is 3.5-3.75%. A rate hike would take it to 3.75-4.0%, raising the 10-year yield toward 4.6-4.8% and compressing risk-asset multiples in a way that is qualitatively different from the "Fed frozen" scenario that has defined the market environment for months.

The 39% rate-hike probability represents a sudden and large shift in the Fed's probability calculus that the BTC market has not previously encountered during this conflict. From February 28 through May 22, the dominant macro constraint on BTC was "Fed cannot cut" — a constraint that kept BTC's ceiling at the 200-day SMA level but did not actively compress its floor. A 39% rate-hike probability introduces a different mechanism: not a ceiling constraint but a floor-compression force, because a rate hike would raise the risk-free rate, increase the discount rate on all risk assets, and produce a cascade of equity multiple compression, bond yield increases, and crypto selling that does not stop at the current level. The practical BTC implication: at 39% rate-hike probability, the expected value of BTC's performance over the next six months includes a 39% probability-weighted scenario in which the macro environment is materially worse than the base case. The market immediately priced some of this through Thursday's 4% decline and $528 million institutional outflow. Whether the rate-hike probability persists at 39% or resolves lower depends on whether the Iran deal reduces oil prices fast enough for the June and July PCE readings to show meaningful moderation — and whether three rounds of US strikes have fundamentally delayed the deal timeline that appeared close just five days ago.

The GENIUS Act's regulatory implementation continues to advance along its multi-agency rulemaking track — OCC national trust bank charters for Circle, Paxos, and three other nonbank financial firms, with the four-agency framework (FDIC, OCC, FinCEN, OFAC) progressing toward final rules. President Trump's executive order directing the Federal Reserve to review crypto firms' access to Fed payment rails adds a payment infrastructure dimension to the charter framework. Non-dollar stablecoins remain below 0.5% of stablecoin market share. The regulatory tailwind is running separately from Thursday's adverse macro event.

The GENIUS Act framework's continued progress through the rulemaking process is the structural story within the crypto market that does not change on a single adverse trading day. The OCC national trust bank charters for Circle and Paxos, granted December 2025, transformed these companies from technology firms into regulated financial institutions with bank-equivalent prudential oversight. The multi-agency implementing regulations — FDIC covering reserve and capital requirements, OCC covering charter standards, FinCEN covering AML/BSA compliance, OFAC covering sanctions — are the operational infrastructure that converts the statutory framework into enforceable standards that institutional users of stablecoin infrastructure can rely on. Trump's executive order on Fed payment rail access is the practical dimension that the GENIUS Act alone cannot address: a GENIUS Act-compliant stablecoin issuer with an OCC national trust bank charter still requires Fed master account access to settle stablecoin transactions directly in Federal Reserve system reserves. The Fed's review of this framework — which the executive order initiated — is the mechanism by which the GENIUS Act's regulatory clarity translates into full payment system infrastructure access for regulated stablecoin issuers. The timing of Thursday's macro adverse event and the GENIUS Act progress do not interact — regulatory timelines run on legislative and administrative schedules that are independent of market prices. The structural tailwind and the macro headwind are both present and running at different speeds.

Quick hits

  • The distinction between "Fed cannot cut" and "Fed may raise" is the most analytically significant development in the BTC macro framework since the conflict began. From February 28 through May 22, the dominant constraint was that the Fed, frozen at 3.5-3.75%, could not cut rates because oil-driven inflation prevented sufficient CPI moderation. This constraint establishes a ceiling: BTC cannot rally beyond the level implied by a frozen risk-free rate and positive real yields. Thursday's shift to 39% rate-hike probability introduces a floor-compression mechanism: if the Fed raises to 3.75-4.0%, the 10-year yield would likely reprice toward 4.6-4.8%, compressing every risk-asset multiple simultaneously. The practical BTC price implication of a 25 bps rate hike — based on the BTC sensitivity to yield changes observed since October 2025 — is approximately a 12-18% decline from current levels, to approximately $60,000-63,000. This is the scenario that the 39% probability is pricing a 39% weight on. The 61% probability-weighted scenario (no hike) would allow BTC to recover if the Iran deal reduces oil and PCE data moderates. The balance of these weighted outcomes produces Thursday's current price, and will continue to produce price as the probability shifts in either direction.
  • BTC dominance at 62.1% requires historical context to understand its significance. The 62.1% level is approximately where BTC dominance peaked before the major altcoin cycles of 2019, 2021, and 2024. In those cycles, the peak dominance level preceded the BTC breakout that triggered altcoin season: BTC broke above its ceiling, dominance fell as capital rotated from BTC to ETH and altcoins, and altcoin season followed. The current 62.1% dominance level on a falling BTC price — not a rising one — is therefore a different pattern from the pre-altcoin-season accumulation. The current pattern is compression-driven dominance: BTC absorbs a declining share of a declining total crypto market cap. The distinction matters because the resolution of compression-driven dominance is different from the resolution of accumulation-driven dominance: compression-driven dominance resolves through further market-wide decline with BTC losing proportionally less, rather than through a BTC breakout that triggers altcoin rotation. The altcoin rotation thesis — which requires BTC to break above its ceiling to activate — is not near-term at 39% rate-hike probability. The ceiling has not broken; it has lowered.
  • The $528 million BlackRock IBIT outflow is the second-largest single-day outflow in the fund's approximately 16-month history, but the largest single-day outflow on record was approximately $550-600 million (the precise amount depends on which date is referenced, but multiple events in the $480-600 million range occurred in 2024-2025 during macro adverse events). The fact that Thursday's outflow approaches the all-time record despite BTC being at $72,714 — not at all-time highs or during a structural bear market — reflects the specific character of Thursday's event: not a price-driven outflow (where investors sell after losses to lock in remaining value) but a policy-driven outflow (where investors reduce allocation in response to a change in the macro policy probability). Policy-driven outflows typically reverse more quickly than price-driven outflows: if the Iran deal closes and oil drops, the PCE trajectory improves, and the rate-hike probability falls back toward 10-15%, the same institutional investors who reduced IBIT positions Thursday will likely rebuild them on the same portfolio optimization logic that required reduction. The speed of the reversal depends on the speed of the macro improvement, which is entirely contingent on the Iran deal timeline.
  • The Coinbase stock price — now at $274.50, down approximately 5.94% Thursday — provides a useful lens on the market's assessment of the crypto ecosystem's health beyond BTC price alone. COIN's decline is larger in percentage terms than BTC's 4% decline, reflecting two compounding headwinds: the exchange business (trading volume and fee revenue) declines when BTC declines, and the equity multiple compression from higher Treasury yields applies to COIN's growth-stock valuation as well as to BTC itself. At $274.50, Coinbase is approximately 6% below its May 20 level of $291.80 — a cumulative decline that tracks the macro environment's evolution since the May 20 edition. The GENIUS Act's OCC charter progress for Circle and Paxos remains a long-term structural positive for Coinbase's custodian and exchange business, as regulatory clarity for stablecoin issuers expands the institutional market for crypto infrastructure services. But near-term, COIN trades as a leveraged proxy for BTC in the macro-dominated environment that Thursday's PCE data reinforced.
  • The ETH Dencun upgrade's impact on Layer 2 costs and activity is worth examining as context for understanding whether ETH's break below $2,000 reflects any fundamental network concern. The Dencun upgrade (March 2024) introduced proto-danksharding that reduced L2 transaction costs by approximately 90%, enabling cheap on-chain activity across Arbitrum, Optimism, Base, and other major Ethereum Layer 2 networks. Since Dencun, L2 transaction volumes have grown substantially while ETH mainnet fee revenue has declined — because cheap L2 activity no longer requires large mainnet gas payments. The ETH price implication is complex: higher L2 activity supports ETH's monetary premium as a settlement asset (L2 security ultimately roots in ETH mainnet), but lower mainnet fees directly reduce the "burn" mechanism that made ETH deflationary and supply-constrained. ETH's break below $2,000 is not a Dencun-related story — it is a macro-driven event identical in mechanism to every other risk-asset decline Thursday. But the longer-term ETH valuation question — whether the L2 scaling model that Dencun enabled creates sufficient demand for ETH as a monetary asset to sustain the price above the levels that lower mainnet fees imply — is the fundamental debate that the macro environment has temporarily obscured. That debate will re-emerge as the dominant ETH narrative once the macro ceiling breaks, and Thursday's price level is not a permanent statement about that debate's resolution.
  • The non-dollar stablecoin market's continued failure to crack 0.5% of stablecoin market share — unchanged from the May 20 edition — is a persistent data point that is increasingly relevant as the GENIUS Act framework matures. The theoretical demand for EUR, JPY, and SGD stablecoins is real and growing as international businesses seek non-USD settlement options and as European and Asian regulatory frameworks (MiCA in Europe, MAS in Singapore) create licensed issuers in those jurisdictions. The practical demand has not materialized because: USD stablecoins are the de facto standard for crypto-native transactions (DeFi, exchange settlement, cross-exchange arbitrage) regardless of the user's currency; the on/off-ramps for non-dollar stablecoins have worse liquidity and wider spreads than USD alternatives; and the GENIUS Act's OCC charter framework has given USD stablecoin issuers a regulatory head start that non-dollar issuers in non-USD jurisdictions will take 18-24 months to match under their respective regulatory frameworks. MiCA's euro stablecoin licensing provisions are expected to produce compliant EUR stablecoin issuers by late 2026 or 2027 — the timeline at which the non-dollar stablecoin market might finally begin to grow above 0.5%. The Thursday macro event does not affect this timeline; it is entirely legislative and rulemaking-dependent.
