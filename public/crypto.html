Bitcoin opened Friday at $73,381 — recovering from Thursday's $72,714 low — as reports that a US-Iran 60-day ceasefire deal text is on Trump's desk improved the macro outlook, sent WTI to $87.20, and began reducing the Fed rate-hike probability from Thursday's 39%. Ethereum recovered above $2,000 to $2,007. Polymarket peace bets hit $213 million. Analysts say the next crypto catalyst is regulatory, not geopolitical.

Friday's crypto recovery — modest but directionally clear — operates through the mirror of Thursday's decline. Thursday's BTC 4% drop and $528 million BlackRock ETF outflow were driven by the April PCE 3.8% print and US strikes on Iranian targets, which together moved the Fed rate-hike probability from near zero to 39%. Friday's recovery is driven by the tentative Iran deal's advancement: the deal text on Trump's desk reduces the oil price forward trajectory, which reduces the expected May PCE contribution, which reduces the Fed rate-hike probability from 39% toward the mid-20s range where WTI at $87 would imply. The recovery is modest ($73,381 vs. Thursday's $72,714 low) because the deal is tentative and unsigned — VP Vance's "not there yet" language is priced into BTC's limited rebound. A signed deal and confirmed Hormuz reopening would be the catalyst for a more substantial BTC recovery: analysts suggest BTC's return to the $78-82 range would require a confirmed deal, WTI at $82-84, and Fed rate-hike probability declining below 15%. The stage is set for that scenario; it has not yet arrived. The regulatory story — GENIUS Act implementation advancing through multi-agency rulemaking — runs independently of the macro track and represents the structural tailwind that continues regardless of the deal's timing.

The Anchor

Bitcoin at $73,381 on Friday morning is recovering, not rallying. The recovery is the Iran deal's advance; the ceiling is the deal's unsigned status. The macro mechanism that produced Thursday's 4% decline is beginning to reverse — but the reversal requires a Trump signature, not a VP's "very close."

The mechanism of Friday's BTC recovery is the inverse of Thursday's decline, but running at lower amplitude because the causal event is probability-improving rather than resolved. Thursday's decline was driven by two simultaneous hard-data events: April PCE 3.8% (backward-looking, definitive) and US military strikes on Iranian targets (backward-looking, definitive). Both events increased the Fed rate-hike probability from near zero to 39% through specific, confirmed data. Friday's recovery is driven by a forward-looking, unconfirmed event: a tentative deal framework on Trump's desk that VP Vance characterizes as "very close" rather than done. The market mechanism is symmetric: deal probability increasing → WTI declining → expected PCE trajectory improving → Fed rate-hike probability declining → BTC opportunity cost versus Treasuries declining → BTC recovers. But the symmetry is imperfect because the magnitude of the probability shift is smaller: from 39% rate-hike probability on Thursday to perhaps 28-32% on Friday morning (based on WTI at $87 versus the $82-84 level that would indicate near-certain deal completion). A smaller probability shift produces a smaller BTC recovery — $73,381 versus the $72,714 low, not the $75,800 pre-PCE level. The full recovery to $75,800 would require WTI at $84-87 sustained for several sessions with confirmed deal progress; the full breakout above the recent ceiling near $82,000 would require WTI at $82 or below with a confirmed deal and Hormuz reopening timeline.

The Polymarket $213 million in Iran peace-deal bets is the prediction market's analog to BTC's recovery: investors are deploying capital on the Iran deal outcome in the prediction market in the same way that crypto investors are beginning to reduce their risk-off positioning in response to deal progress. The $59 million growth in Polymarket volume from $154 million (May 23) to $213 million (May 29) tracks BTC's trajectory over the same period: both are repricing upward as the deal advances, both are constrained by the deal's unsigned status. The analytical implication of the Polymarket-BTC correlation: if Trump signs and Hormuz reopens, the $213 million in Polymarket bets settles to winning positions that may partially recycle into BTC as prediction market participants take profits and reallocate to the next risk-asset with deal-scenario upside. This creates a potential post-signing BTC demand catalyst that is independent of the macro rate-hike probability mechanism — and could produce the kind of short-term price spike that precedes more sustained trend moves in the crypto market.

Compared to where it was at Thursday's worst point ($72,714), it is higher. The Iran deal advance is the factor that prevented continued downside and produced the modest morning recovery; the deal's unsigned status is the factor that prevented the recovery from being larger. For crypto traders, the key level to watch is $75,800 — the pre-PCE, pre-strike price that represents the floor of the "pre-macro-shock" range. Recapturing $75,800 requires not just "very close" but a confirmed deal framework that structurally reduces the rate-hike probability below 20%. The $73,381-75,800 gap is the "unsigned deal premium" — the remaining distance between current prices and the prices that would prevail if the deal were signed today.

ETH's recovery above $2,000 is analytically significant as a "floor recovery" event: the $2,000 level is the psychological support that has held across multiple Iran-conflict adverse events, and its recovery on Friday morning from Thursday's breach signals that the broader risk-off move has not fundamentally rerated ETH's support level to below $2,000. The mechanism is straightforward: ETH broke below $2,000 Thursday because the PCE and Iran strike news triggered institutional risk reduction that sells ETH alongside BTC in the liquidity order (BTC first, ETH second); ETH recovered above $2,000 Friday because the Iran deal advance reversed the institutional risk-reduction impulse and produced modest risk-on positioning. The BTC.D decline from 62.1% to 61.8% — a 0.3 percentage point drop — is the signal that the compression-driven dominance of Thursday is beginning to partially reverse: as the macro environment improves, capital returns to altcoins proportionally faster than to BTC because the flight-to-quality-within-crypto trade unwinds. The SOL 1.65%, XRP 1.16%, DOGE 1.24% gains are all larger than BTC's 0.92%, consistent with this pattern. COIN's 2.08% gain reflects the exchange business's leverage to BTC prices: as BTC recovers, trading volume increases, fee revenue improves, and the exchange equity value rises proportionally more than the underlying asset.

The analyst observation that crypto's next catalyst is regulatory is a forward-looking framing of the post-Iran-deal scenario. In the geopolitical macro-ceiling environment (WTI above $90, PCE above 3.5%, Fed rate-hike probability above 30%), BTC's price is primarily determined by the macro rate equation: higher expected rates = lower BTC. In a post-deal environment (WTI at $82-84, PCE declining toward 2.5-3.0% by year-end, Fed rate-hike probability below 15%), the macro ceiling breaks — BTC can trade on fundamental demand rather than being capped by risk-free rate arbitrage. Once the ceiling breaks, the fundamental demand drivers — institutional adoption through ETFs, regulatory clarity through GENIUS Act implementation, retail demand through 900 million ChatGPT users being introduced to crypto payment rails through adjacent fintech integrations — become the primary price determinants. The GENIUS Act's OCC charter framework for Circle and Paxos is particularly significant for this transition: a regulated stablecoin issuer with an OCC national trust bank charter and Fed payment rail access is a payment infrastructure component, not merely a speculative asset. Institutional adoption of stablecoin payment infrastructure (for cross-border commercial payments, treasury management, and settlement) would represent a structural demand driver for the broader crypto ecosystem that is both larger in scale and more durable in character than the geopolitical-risk-premium that currently constrains it.

Quick hits

  • The BTC recovery to $73,381 — modest relative to the potential gains from a confirmed deal — reflects a specific feature of the current market structure: institutional investors who reduced BTC exposure on Thursday (producing the $528 million BlackRock ETF outflow) do not reverse their position on "very close." They reverse on "signed." The institutional portfolio optimization framework that drove Thursday's rebalancing requires confirmed data to reverse: the 39% rate-hike probability that triggered the outflow was based on confirmed April PCE data; the rate-hike probability will need to fall below 20% on confirmed May PCE data or confirmed deal progress before the rebalancing reverses. This means the Friday morning recovery is primarily retail and algorithmic positioning — traders who see the deal advancing and are positioning ahead of the institutional reversal — not the institutional IBIT-level rebalancing that would produce a $500 million+ inflow and a 4-5% recovery. The institutional reversal will follow the signature. Current BTC prices are the pre-signature positioning, not the post-signature response.
  • ETH's recovery above $2,000 is being watched as a technical indicator by traders who assign significance to the $2,000 level as a support/resistance pivot. The $2,000 level for ETH has served as both psychological support (the minimum valuation at which institutional ETH holders consider the asset fairly valued on network-usage metrics) and as a mechanical resistance level in the options market (large open interest in $2,000 strike calls and puts creates gravitational effects around the level). ETH spending Thursday below $2,000 and Friday above $2,000 is technically a failed breakdown — the level was tested intraday and held in the context of the subsequent price action. For ETH investors, the failed breakdown is modestly bullish: it suggests the institutional holders who define the $2,000 support were buyers on the dip, not sellers, and the level is a genuine floor rather than a cosmetic round number. The ETH/BTC ratio stabilizing at its annual low is the caveat: even a failed ETH breakdown does not resolve the ratio compression that has been accumulating since the conflict began. ETH will need BTC to break above $80,000 before ETH can recapture ratio-level gains — and that requires the deal, not just the deal being "very close."
  • The non-dollar stablecoin market's continued sub-0.5% share — unchanged across the past three editions — is a data point that the GENIUS Act framework's maturation is unlikely to change in the near term. The structural barriers to non-dollar stablecoin adoption are: DeFi protocol pricing in USD; exchange quote currencies defaulting to USD; cross-exchange arbitrage running through USD stablecoins; and on/off-ramp liquidity being deepest for USD pairs. These are not regulatory barriers — they are market structure frictions that would persist even if EUR, JPY, or SGD stablecoins had identical regulatory clarity to USDC or USDT. The GENIUS Act's OCC charter framework advantages USD stablecoin issuers not through deliberate discrimination but through the fact that it was designed for the US dollar system. Non-dollar stablecoin adoption will require: MiCA's EUR stablecoin licensing provisions to mature (expected 2027); exchange integration of EUR/JPY/SGD stablecoin trading pairs with comparable liquidity to USD pairs; and a specific commercial use case (cross-border B2B payments between EU and Asian counterparts) that DeFi's current USD-centric protocol stack cannot serve efficiently. None of these are near-term catalysts. The non-dollar stablecoin market will likely remain below 1% through 2026 regardless of the Iran deal's timing.
  • Coinbase at $280.20 (+2.08%) Friday morning reflects the exchange business's specific recovery mechanics in response to the deal advance. COIN's revenue is primarily trading fee-dependent: when BTC prices rise, retail trading volume increases (both new buyers entering the market and existing holders taking profits), generating fee revenue that directly improves Coinbase's profitability. The 2.08% COIN gain on BTC's 0.92% recovery represents a leverage multiple of approximately 2.3× — consistent with the historical relationship between BTC price moves and COIN equity performance, where a 1% BTC move produces approximately 2-2.5% COIN movement. For COIN investors, the critical question is whether Friday morning's recovery is the beginning of a sustained BTC price recovery (deal-signed scenario) or a one-day bounce before continuation of the Thursday-driven risk-off move. COIN's institutional investor base — which overlaps with the BlackRock IBIT holders who drove the $528 million Thursday outflow — will likely provide the answer by Monday: if IBIT sees inflows next week, COIN's institutional investors are rebuilding exposure and the recovery is real; if IBIT continues to see outflows, the bounce is being sold into. Watch IBIT daily flows next week as the leading indicator of whether the Friday recovery has institutional conviction behind it.
  • The GENIUS Act's progress through multi-agency rulemaking is worth tracking as the regulatory development that is running entirely independently of the Iran deal timeline. The OCC national trust bank charters for Circle and Paxos — granted December 2025 — created the foundational regulatory entities; the four-agency implementing framework (FDIC capital requirements, OCC charter standards, FinCEN AML/BSA compliance, OFAC sanctions compliance) is the operational ruleset that makes the charters meaningful. Trump's executive order directing the Federal Reserve to review crypto firms' access to Fed payment rails is the payment infrastructure dimension: a stablecoin issuer with an OCC charter can issue regulated stablecoins, but without Fed master account access, its stablecoin transactions do not settle directly in Federal Reserve system reserves — meaning it relies on correspondent banking relationships for final settlement rather than the Fed's own infrastructure. The Fed's review timeline — which the executive order initiated but did not specify — is expected to produce preliminary guidance by late 2026. When that guidance arrives, stablecoin infrastructure access to the Fed payment system will create the commercial foundation for stablecoin adoption in institutional payment settlement that is currently blocked by the correspondent-banking bottleneck. This is the regulatory catalyst that analysts say comes after the Iran deal resolves the macro ceiling — and it represents a structural expansion of crypto's institutional utility that is independent of any single macro event.
  • The BTC market capitalization at $73,381 implies approximately $1.45 trillion — placing BTC as approximately the 7th-8th largest financial asset globally by market cap, ahead of major multinational corporations and behind only the largest sovereign bonds and mega-cap equities. This scale matters for understanding the magnitude of the geopolitical risk premium that the Iran conflict has imposed on BTC. At $82,000 (BTC's pre-PCE, pre-strike level in early May), BTC's market cap was approximately $1.63 trillion. The decline from $82,000 to $72,714 (Thursday's low) represents approximately $180-190 billion in market cap reduction — attributable to the macro chain of WTI-PCE-Fed-rate that the Iran conflict drives. $180-190 billion is approximately 0.23% of global equity market capitalization, suggesting that BTC's exposure to the Iran conflict macro chain — while significant in relative terms to BTC itself — is a rounding error in global asset allocation terms. The institutional investors who drove the BlackRock IBIT outflow are making rational portfolio adjustments for their own portfolio optimization, not reacting to BTC-specific distress; and the recovery from $72,714 to $73,381 on deal news is the same rational optimization running in reverse as the macro environment improves.
