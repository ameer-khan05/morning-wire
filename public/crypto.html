Senate Republicans released a new CLARITY Act draft on July 22 that would bar covered US officials — including the President, Vice President, Congress members, and federal judges — from issuing or sponsoring digital assets "for compensation" until January 2029 and require them to divest cryptocurrency holdings or place them in a blind trust, while expressly preserving the right to hold digital assets as passive investments. Senator Warren immediately issued a statement opposing the new text, arguing the "for compensation" qualifier limits the ban's coverage in ways that do not match Democratic demands for broader restriction on officials' economic interests in crypto ventures.

The language fight is the precise second-order dispute that followed the first-order ethics acceptance. The "for compensation" qualifier matters legally: it excludes beneficial ownership in crypto funds, advisory stakes in crypto companies, and investment returns from assets held before a term begins. Democrats who demanded ethics coverage broad enough to separate officials from crypto economic interests argue the qualifier leaves those indirect interests intact. Republicans who accepted the ethics provision in principle will resist language that goes further — the "for compensation" wording was designed to preserve passive investment rights. 14 days remain to resolve it.
