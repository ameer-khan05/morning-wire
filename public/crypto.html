Bitcoin breaks its five-day slide at $76,988 as stocks post best month since 2020 — but March PCE at 3.5% cements the macro ceiling that no equity rally can remove.

Bitcoin climbed back to $76,988 Friday morning — up 1.13% from Thursday's close and breaking a five-consecutive-day declining streak — as Apple's $111.18 billion Q2 blowout and the S&P 500's first-ever close above 7,200 lifted broad risk sentiment. But the day's most important macro signal runs in the opposite direction: March PCE inflation of 3.5% year over year — the hottest consumer price read since 2022 and well above the 2.6% consensus — confirms that the oil-driven inflation regime is not cooling and that the Federal Reserve's no-cut posture is not softening under incoming chair Kevin Warsh. The 10-year Treasury yield rose to 4.52% on the PCE print, a ten-basis-point move that tightened real rates just as May trading began. Bitcoin's one-day recovery is real, but the structural macro ceiling is the same or higher than it was on April 30. The War Powers 60-day deadline — hitting May 1 — and Trump's signals of possible new Iran strikes add geopolitical uncertainty that historically suppresses risk-asset upside.

The Anchor

BTC breaks five-day slide at $76,988 — the macro ceiling remains: PCE 3.5%, 10Y at 4.52%, and War Powers uncertainty open May.

Bitcoin opened Friday at $76,988 — its first morning above the prior day's open in six sessions — as equity markets digested Apple's record Q2 result and the S&P 500's best month since 2020 in broadly positive terms. The recovery from $76,130 represents a 1.13% gain and is the first unambiguous positive overnight session for Bitcoin since the five-day decline began from the $79,461 conference peak last week. Ethereum recovered to $2,293 (▲ 0.97%). Altcoins followed: SOL at $148.30 (▲ 1.44%), XRP at $2.14 (▲ 1.42%), DOGE at $0.177 (▲ 1.72%). BTC dominance rose further to 61.14%, a continuation of the capital-consolidation pattern that emerges when institutional holders want crypto exposure but are uncertain about the macro backdrop — holding BTC as a liquid defensive position rather than rotating into higher-beta altcoins.

The PCE read that accompanied Thursday's session is the most significant single macro data point for crypto in weeks. March PCE at 3.5% year over year — up from 2.8% in February — means the first full month with Brent averaging above $100 produced exactly the consumer price acceleration the Fed feared. Core PCE at 3.2% is running more than a percentage point above the Fed's 2.0% target. The 10-year Treasury yield's rise to 4.52% on the data is the real-rate signal that matters for crypto: Bitcoin's opportunity cost versus a risk-free 4.52% Treasury has risen again, and the probability of a rate cut at Warsh's first FOMC meeting as chair has fallen further. Q1 GDP at +2.0% — healthier than the 0.5% Q4 2025 print — means the Fed cannot justify a growth-emergency cut even if it wanted to. The dual confirmation of robust growth and accelerating inflation is the worst possible configuration for rate-cut expectations: stagflation fears (no growth, high inflation) at least carry a growth-emergency cut argument; strong growth with high inflation removes even that.

BTC breaks its five-day morning decline at $76,988 — Apple's record Q2 and S&P's best month since 2020 provide the risk-on catalyst, but dominance rising to 61.14% signals continued caution.

Bitcoin's recovery to $76,988 Friday morning is the first positive overnight session since the five-day decline began from the $79,461 conference peak. The proximate catalyst is equity-market sentiment: Apple's $111.18 billion Q2 result — the best in the company's history — and the S&P 500's first-ever close above 7,200 produced a broadly risk-on overnight environment that lifted BTC modestly. But the structure of Friday's recovery contains a specific signal about market confidence: BTC dominance rising to 61.14% while altcoins recover more slowly (SOL +1.44%, XRP +1.42%, DOGE +1.72%) versus BTC's +1.13% is unusual. In a fully risk-on recovery, altcoins outperform BTC because they have more beta. The fact that dominance is rising even during BTC's recovery day suggests institutional holders are using the equity-market bounce to add BTC specifically — not to rotate into higher-risk altcoins — which implies continued caution about the macro backdrop rather than a return to the risk-seeking posture that characterized the conference week. BTC dominance at 61.14% is its highest in six weeks and is running above the level that typically precedes either a broad altcoin rally (when dominance begins to fall) or a continued BTC-specific strength period that reflects defensive positioning.

March PCE at 3.5% — the hottest since 2022, and the first Brent-above-$100 month in print. The macro ceiling just got measurably higher.

Thursday's March PCE inflation report is the most consequential single macro data point for crypto since the Iran conflict began: 3.5% year-over-year consumer price growth — versus a 2.6% consensus — is the first empirical confirmation that the Brent-above-$100 regime has translated into headline consumer inflation at a pace that the Federal Reserve cannot ignore. Core PCE at 3.2% is more than a percentage point above the Fed's 2.0% mandate. The Q1 2026 PCE for the full quarter came in at 4.5% — the highest since Q3 2022 — meaning the oil shock's consumer price impact accelerated through the quarter rather than being front-loaded in January. For Bitcoin, the March PCE read is the single most important non-BTC-specific data point of the week, more important even than Apple's $111 billion Q2 or the S&P 500's best-month-since-2020 record: it quantifies the depth of the inflation problem that prevents Fed rate cuts, and it confirms that the macro regime suppressing BTC will persist through at least the next two FOMC meetings. The 10-year Treasury's rise to 4.52% on the PCE print — a ten-basis-point move in a single session — reflects markets revising their rate-cut probability downward in real time. For a Bitcoin investor calculating the real-rate opportunity cost of holding BTC versus a 4.52% risk-free Treasury, Thursday's data made the choice harder.

War Powers 60-day deadline hits May 1 — White House claims ceasefire halts clock, Senate contested. Trump signals "short powerful" strikes. Geopolitical uncertainty spikes for crypto.

The Iran conflict's War Powers Resolution 60-day deadline — after which Congress must authorize or end the conflict — fell on May 1, the same morning crypto markets opened Friday. The White House is claiming that the conflict's ceasefire status halts the clock, an argument Senate Democrats are actively contesting. The Senate had already rejected a war powers resolution earlier in the week. Simultaneously, Trump said Washington "might need" to restart active military operations, with CENTCOM having briefed a wave of "short and powerful" strikes on Iranian infrastructure. The convergence of the War Powers deadline and the strike signals creates a specific uncertainty spike for crypto: a scenario in which new active US military strikes resume against Iran would almost certainly send Brent back toward or above the $126 four-year high it touched on Thursday, adding a further inflationary shock on top of the 3.5% March PCE already in the data. For Bitcoin, the War Powers uncertainty is an asymmetric risk: resolution through Congress would add some diplomatic structure and could modestly lift risk sentiment; resumption of strikes would deliver an oil price spike that tightens the macro ceiling further. Markets are not yet pricing the strike scenario, but Trump's statement makes it a possibility that the crypto market's risk management must account for over the Friday session and weekend.

S&P 500's first-ever close above 7,200 lifts crypto sentiment modestly — but April's best-month-since-2020 equity performance didn't lift Bitcoin out of its five-day decline.

Thursday's S&P 500 close at 7,209.01 — the first-ever above 7,200 — is unambiguously positive for broad risk sentiment and contributes to Friday morning's BTC recovery. But the relationship between equity market records and crypto price direction in the current macro regime is weaker than the historical correlation would suggest. Specifically: April was the best month for equities since 2020, with the S&P 500 up approximately 10% over the month. During the same period, Bitcoin declined from its mid-April recovery highs near $83,000 to Thursday's $76,130 — a divergence that reflects the specific macro headwind operating on crypto (real-rate ceiling) that doesn't apply to equities in the same way. Equities benefit from nominal earnings growth that partially offsets the rate headwind; Bitcoin, as a non-yielding asset, faces the full real-rate burden without the nominal earnings offset. The S&P 500 record is good news for the risk-on environment that crypto benefits from marginally, but it does not address the structural macro factor that has suppressed BTC's recovery for five weeks. Friday's 1.13% BTC recovery in the context of the S&P's best month since 2020 illustrates the asymmetry clearly.

Quick hits

  • Bitcoin's April performance — from approximately $83,000 mid-month to $76,988 Friday morning — represents a decline of roughly 7% from peak to current, against the S&P 500's best monthly performance since 2020 (approximately +10%). The divergence measures the exact magnitude of the macro headwind specific to crypto in the current regime: equities are benefiting from nominal AI earnings growth that offsets the rate headwind; Bitcoin is absorbing the full real-rate burden without the nominal earnings offset. That gap — roughly 17 percentage points of performance divergence between the S&P 500 and BTC in April — is the "macro tax" the current regime imposes on a non-yielding risk asset versus a nominal-earnings-generating equity index.
  • The March PCE of 3.5% year over year implies that if the Fed's PCE target is 2.0%, the current inflation rate is 1.5 percentage points above target — and accelerating, not decelerating. For context: every 0.5 percentage point of PCE above the Fed's 2.0% target that persists for a full year represents approximately $100 billion in excess consumer purchasing-power erosion relative to the Fed's baseline. At 3.5% sustained, the annual purchasing power cost of the oil-driven inflation is approximately $300 billion — a transfer from US consumers to energy producers that the Fed cannot control directly but must respond to through the rate regime. For Bitcoin, the direct implication is simple: the Fed cannot cut until PCE is below 2.5% on a sustained basis, and 3.5% is nowhere near that threshold.
  • Coinbase (COIN) at $301.90 (▲ 1.38% Friday) is recovering alongside BTC, but the equity's recovery is more directly correlated with trading volume expectations than with BTC price levels. A BTC recovery from $76,130 to $76,988 — less than 1.2% — is unlikely to generate a significant increase in retail trading volume on the platform. COIN's 1.38% recovery likely reflects the broader equity market's strong day lifting tech-adjacent stocks rather than a specific crypto volume catalyst. The true COIN trading volume signal will be visible in Friday's late-session data: if COIN holds its gains through the session, it reflects genuine retail engagement; if it gives back gains as the equity-market lift fades, it reflects the absence of organic crypto volume growth.
  • The Q1 2026 GDP at +2.0% combined with Q1 PCE at +4.5% is technically not stagflation (stagflation requires negative or near-zero growth), but it is the closest the US economy has come to that designation since the 1970s-style shocks that defined the term. For crypto, the stagflation-adjacent environment is specifically toxic because it eliminates the emergency-cut option: in a true recession, even with inflation, central banks have historically cut rates because the growth emergency outweighs the inflation concern. At +2.0% GDP growth, there is no growth emergency — the Fed has no pretext to prioritize growth over inflation even if it wanted to. The only catalyst for crypto's macro ceiling to lift is either a genuine GDP contraction (bad for all risk assets) or a genuine PCE deceleration (which requires Brent below $90 sustained).
  • The War Powers Resolution deadline's interaction with Trump's strike signals creates a specific option-value dynamic for Friday's crypto trading. If Trump resolves the War Powers ambiguity by the end of the trading day — either by formally seeking Congressional authorization (diplomatically signal-rich) or by announcing a new strike (oil-spike signal-rich) — the crypto market's current pricing will be wrong in one direction or the other. The ambiguity is priced as stable-to-mildly-positive (BTC up 1.13%); a strike announcement would likely send BTC back below $76,000 within hours; a diplomatic Congressional resolution would likely push BTC above $77,500. The option value of watching the War Powers outcome is unusually high for a Friday morning.
  • Strategy's (formerly MicroStrategy) 818,334 BTC treasury, marked to Friday's $76,988, represents an unrealized portfolio value of approximately $62.9 billion against an average acquisition cost of approximately $75,528 per BTC. At Thursday's trough of $76,130, the unrealized gain above acquisition cost was approximately $494 million on the full treasury — a razor-thin positive margin that illustrates how close the five-day decline brought Strategy to its treasury "underwater" threshold. At $76,988, the cushion is approximately $1.19 billion — better, but structurally thin relative to the $8.83 billion treasury value cushion that existed at the conference-week $79,461 peak. The convertible debt structure that underpins Strategy's treasury model has specific triggers; whether any of those triggers approach the current price range is the most closely watched structural question in institutional crypto markets.
