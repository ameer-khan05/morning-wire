The CLARITY Act enters its final Senate floor window Monday July 13 — the Senate returning from recess with CoinDesk reporting a new bill draft expected "this week" — as the three remaining disputes that have blocked floor consideration must be resolved before any vote: ethics provisions addressing former President Trump's approximately $1.4 billion in cryptocurrency-related income (Democrats want disclosure language), law enforcement rules the National District Attorneys Association says would impair criminal investigations, and whether exchanges can pay yield on stablecoin balances — the dispute in which Coinbase's $1.35 billion annual USDC rewards revenue and the American Bankers Association's institutional opposition are on opposing sides of the same legislative language. The bill needs seven Senate Democrats, and the August recess creates a hard deadline of approximately August 7.

The stablecoin yield dispute is the CLARITY Act's most structurally consequential unresolved question because it determines whether stablecoins become interest-bearing dollar instruments accessible to anyone with a smartphone — directly competing with bank savings products — or remain payment-only instruments that preserve banks' savings-product moat. Coinbase's $1.35 billion in annual USDC rewards is the revenue at stake for crypto; the banking industry's concern is orders of magnitude larger, representing the structural threat to their deposit base. The dispute's resolution this week sets the direction of the $150+ billion stablecoin market for the next decade. Passage with yield permitted is structurally bullish for ETH (stablecoin infrastructure) and DeFi. Passage with yield prohibited still provides regulatory certainty but removes a key institutional adoption driver.
