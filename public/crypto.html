The Anchor

BTC $62,400. ETH $1,651. Chips are selling off globally. But WTI is below $74 and the 10-year just fell 4 basis points. The near-term headwind and the medium-term tailwind are both getting larger on the same Tuesday morning.

Tuesday's BTC and ETH moves are explained almost entirely by the global chip selloff. KOSPI fell 9.99% overnight; Nasdaq futures dropped 2.6%; Micron, Samsung, and SK Hynix each fell 10–12%. Crypto's correlation with tech in risk-off environments is well-established — when the equity market is selling generalized technology exposure, BTC and ETH sell with it, regardless of their fundamentals. The chip selloff's proximate cause (profit-taking after AI infrastructure stocks ran hard on AI demand expectations) is also a signal that the market is beginning to price the difference between AI demand and AI infrastructure demand — the same question Micron's Wednesday earnings will answer directly.

The Iran oil sanctions waiver is operating on a different timeline from Tuesday's price action. WTI at $73.72 is a 60-day story: with Iranian crude back in global supply through August 21, the June and July CPI energy components will show disinflation that the FOMC will read in August. The UST 10Y falling 4 basis points to 4.47% today is the first crack in the 4.53% ceiling that Warsh's June 18 press conference established. If WTI holds near $73–74 through the month, the opportunity cost argument for holding BTC weakens — not because BTC's absolute value case changes, but because the rate environment that had been a persistent headwind is beginning a credible easing path. Tuesday's drop is the near-term sell; the medium-term setup is improving.

Bitcoin fell to $62,400 (▼4.31%) and Ethereum to $1,651 (▼7.04%) Tuesday as the global chip and AI infrastructure selloff that began in Asia — South Korea's KOSPI down 9.99%, Samsung and SK Hynix each losing 12%, Nasdaq futures down 2.6% — transmitted into crypto through risk-off sentiment. ETH's larger decline (-7.04% vs BTC's -4.31%) reflects its higher beta to tech-adjacent risk-off moves; the same asymmetry produced ETH's relative Monday outperformance (▲2.66% vs BTC's ▲1.72%). DeFi markets remained stable through Tuesday's selloff, with no cascade liquidations and collateral positions intact at $62,400 — confirmation that the current price level is not triggering structural market stress. Week-over-week, BTC at $62,400 is still approximately 2% above the pre-Iran deal baseline of $61,170, indicating the nuclear deal's geopolitical premium has not been fully unwound by Tuesday's selloff.

The chip selloff's transmission to crypto operates through two channels. The direct channel is risk-off correlation: when institutional portfolios sell technology exposure broadly, crypto sells as a technology-adjacent risk asset. The indirect channel is narrative: the chip selloff's proximate cause — doubt about whether AI memory demand justifies extended valuation — is the same question that underpins AI infrastructure investment, which is one of crypto's bull-case narratives. If AI infrastructure investment decelerates, the demand for decentralized computation and data infrastructure that some crypto networks provide also decelerates. This indirect channel is speculative and longer-dated, but it contributes to the same-direction price move. Wednesday's Micron earnings will answer the direct AI memory demand question; the answer will reverberate in crypto as much as in equities.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent announced a 60-day waiver on Iran oil sanctions Tuesday, covering Iranian crude oil production, transportation, and sale alongside banking transactions, shipping, insurance, and maritime logistics — legal cover for the full Iranian oil supply chain through August 21. WTI crude fell 2.68% to $73.72 and Brent fell 2.44% to $76.90 as oil traders priced the additional supply entering global markets. The 10-year Treasury yield fell 4 basis points to 4.47% — its first sustained decline since Warsh's June 18 press conference set 4.53% as the hawkish post-FOMC level — as flight-to-safety bond flows absorbed some of the equity selling generated by the chip rout.

For crypto, the oil sanctions waiver is a medium-term positive operating on a 60-90 day horizon. The mechanism: WTI at $73.72 versus the pre-deal level of ~$84 contributes meaningfully to June's CPI energy component. June CPI data will be published in mid-July; if it shows the energy disinflation the oil price path supports, the July print will provide the FOMC with the first concrete CPI evidence of the oil-to-inflation transmission the deal promised. A favorable July CPI makes the case for Warsh's September hold — rather than the hike that nine FOMC members projected in the June dot plot — substantially stronger. Each basis point decline in the UST 10Y (today ▼4 bp to 4.47%) directly reduces the opportunity cost argument for holding non-yielding BTC. Tuesday's sell is the near-term noise; the rate trajectory the oil waiver enables is the signal that matters for crypto's medium-term direction.

FHFA Crypto Mortgage Directive · Fannie + Freddie · Structural Demand · Implementation Advancing FHFA FHFA Director Pulte directive: Fannie Mae + Freddie Mac to count cryptocurrency as qualifying mortgage asset · implementation: both GSEs advancing internal preparation · combined GSE portfolio: ~$7T conventional mortgage market · demand mechanism: crypto holders no longer must liquidate before mortgage qualification → removes conversion pressure → sustained holding incentive → structural demand · specific asset list: pending in implementation guidance (BTC confirmed, ETH status pending) · daily relevance: Tuesday selloff does not affect implementation timeline · Micron earnings Wednesday: key read on AI memory demand → will influence whether chip selloff is valuation or demand correction → crypto follows

The FHFA's directive requiring Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac to accept cryptocurrency as a qualifying asset for mortgage applications continues advancing through both government-sponsored enterprises' internal implementation processes, providing a structural demand positive for crypto that Tuesday's global tech selloff does not affect. The directive eliminates the pre-mortgage crypto liquidation incentive across the ~$7 trillion conventional US mortgage market — the aggregate holding incentive created when millions of crypto holders no longer face implicit pressure to convert to cash for mortgage qualification. The specific asset list (confirming which cryptocurrencies qualify) and the haircut methodology (how crypto's volatility is discounted relative to cash) remain pending in implementation guidance from Fannie and Freddie.

Tuesday's price action serves as a useful test of the FHFA directive's demand mechanism. The selloff demonstrates that the structural holding incentive the directive creates operates on a different timescale from daily price volatility — holders deciding whether to liquidate crypto before a mortgage application are making decisions on 30-90-day timescales, not intraday moves. Wednesday's Micron earnings are the next material event for both the chip selloff narrative and crypto: if Micron's AI memory demand data is strong, the chip selloff is a valuation correction and the AI infrastructure thesis survives; if demand data is weak, the selloff is demand-driven and the AI narrative that has been part of crypto's 2026 bull case faces a more fundamental challenge. Watch for Micron's commentary on hyperscaler AI memory orders specifically.

Quick hits

  • The FHFA implementation's key pending decision is the haircut applied to crypto holdings versus cash in mortgage qualification. Crypto's volatility means a $1 BTC balance is not the same risk exposure as $1 in a savings account from the lender's perspective. If Fannie and Freddie apply a 50% haircut (counting $1 of crypto as $0.50 of qualifying assets), the demand incentive is halved. If they apply a 25% haircut, it is stronger. The haircut methodology will be the most watched component of the implementation guidance because it determines the real-world incentive magnitude for the ~30 million Americans who hold meaningful crypto positions. Watch Fannie and Freddie's implementation guidance for the haircut language specifically.
  • ETH at $1,651 is the same level ETH traded at in early June before the Iran deal geopolitical premium began accumulating. Tuesday's selloff has effectively unwound ETH's geopolitical premium entirely — ETH is back to pre-deal territory. BTC at $62,400 retains approximately 2% of the pre-deal geopolitical premium ($61,170 base). The divergence reflects ETH's higher beta amplifying the selloff past the premium level, while BTC's lower volatility preserved some of the accumulated gain. If the Swiss talks continue producing positive readouts through the week, both assets should recover above Tuesday's levels as risk-on sentiment returns.
