The CLARITY Act Senate floor debate opened Monday, July 20 — but the odds of passage dropped to 40% on Polymarket after Senators Murphy, Van Hollen, and Merkley held a press conference formally opposing the merged Senate draft, which omits the ethics disclosure provision they had named as the price of their floor votes; a merged bill from the Banking and Agriculture Committees was released omitting Democratic demands on disclosure, bringing three public no-votes before debate even opened, with two floor windows remaining before the August 7 recess deadline and Senator Lummis warning that failure could defer crypto market structure law to 2030 or the end of the 119th Congress entirely.

Today's floor opening is a materially different event than Sunday's 48% Polymarket odds suggested. Three Senators holding a press conference to formally oppose a bill before its floor debate begins is not a negotiating signal — it is a public commitment requiring explicit reversal. The merged draft omitting the ethics provision means floor debate opens with the core Democratic precondition unmet. Forty percent passage odds reflect the gap: 10% of the probability has been transferred from pass to fail in the 24 hours since Sunday, and it moved on specific news rather than sentiment drift.

The CLARITY Act's Senate floor timeline had always been compressed: a July 20 floor opening, multi-day debate, and final vote in the July 22–24 window before the August 7 recess deadline. That timeline assumed an ethics compromise would be reached before or shortly after floor debate opened. The merged Senate draft released earlier this week collapsed that assumption: it combines the Banking and Agriculture Committee versions but deliberately omits the ethics provision — Trump administration crypto income disclosure requirements — that Senator Kirsten Gillibrand and at least six other Democrats had named as a floor-vote precondition. Murphy, Van Hollen, and Merkley formalizing their opposition at a joint press conference turns the ethics gap from a known obstacle into a public commitment: without the provision, their no votes are on record before the gavel falls.

Benzinga and Crypto Adventure both reported that Senate Majority Leader Thune has pledged a floor vote before the August recess, with July 20 as the target date — and Senator Lummis has made the stakes explicit: the CLARITY Act has two floor windows left (weeks of July 20 and July 27) before the recess. Missing both means September, when the legislative calendar fills with FY2027 appropriations and debt ceiling negotiations, reducing CLARITY's priority, and potentially the end of the 119th Congress in January 2027. Lummis has said that failure could push market structure legislation to 2030 at best. BTC and ETH both hold at pre-vote levels despite the odds drop, suggesting markets are not yet pricing failure as the base case — but 40% is no longer a market-confident signal.

Quick hits

  • The ethics disclosure dispute is a specific, solvable problem — which is why 40% passage odds still implies a live possibility, not a foregone failure. The disagreement is over scope: Democrats want mandatory disclosure of crypto income for Trump administration officials; the merged draft omits this. A floor amendment with narrowed language — covering, say, senior financial officials rather than all administration staff — could potentially satisfy enough Democrats to reach cloture without requiring the White House to accept full disclosure. The question is whether a workable narrowing exists that both sides can accept under floor debate time pressure. Senator Gillibrand's language on this has been more specific than Murphy's, which suggests the compromise space exists even if it is narrow.
  • Stablecoin yield surviving the merged draft is underreported given the ETH price action. The ethics provision is what Murphy, Van Hollen, and Merkley have publicly named as their condition — but the stablecoin yield clause is what ETH's 7% five-day outperformance from last week was pricing. The merged bill keeping yield while dropping ethics means the economic substance of what ETH needed is intact if passage occurs; only the political condition for passage is in dispute. If an ethics compromise is reached and the bill passes, ETH holders who have been pricing the stablecoin yield clause will find the bet paid off. If the bill fails, the yield clause's survival in the merged draft becomes irrelevant.
