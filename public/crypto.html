The CLARITY Act faces its final legislative window with the Senate returning July 13 and CoinDesk reporting that a new draft of the bill — the version that must resolve the three remaining disputes and attract the votes needed for cloture — could emerge "as soon as next week," creating a compressed sequence from draft release to floor action to a vote before the August recess; the three unresolved disputes are ethics provisions regarding former President Trump's approximately $1.4 billion in cryptocurrency-related income (which Democrats want addressed through disclosure language), law enforcement rules the National District Attorneys Association says would impair criminal investigations, and whether exchanges can pay yield on stablecoin balances — the dispute in which the American Bankers Association's opposition and Coinbase's $1.35 billion annual USDC rewards revenue are on opposing sides of the same language. Cryptoslate reported that delivery of the bill "now depends on seven Senate Democrats."

The stablecoin yield dispute is the CLARITY Act's most consequential unresolved question because it determines the stablecoin market's fundamental product category. If stablecoin balances can earn yield — which Coinbase's USDC rewards program already implements and which the bill language either permits or prohibits — stablecoins become interest-bearing dollar instruments accessible to anyone with a smartphone, competing directly with bank savings products. If stablecoin yield is prohibited, stablecoins remain payment instruments and the banking system's savings-product moat is protected. The banking industry's $1.35 billion annual concern is straightforward; the broader market implication of either outcome is orders of magnitude larger. The dispute's resolution in the next two to three weeks will set the structural direction of the $150+ billion stablecoin market for the next decade.

Gyeonggi Province: Stablecoin Testing August · Zero-Knowledge Proofs · Reserve Verification · Payment Transparency · Public Fund Management · Government ZK-Stablecoin Infrastructure · First Live Government Deployment ZK Gyeonggi Province: South Korea's largest province · population: ~13.5M · stablecoin testing: begins August 2026 · technology: zero-knowledge proofs (ZK) + reserve verification · application 1: payment transparency · application 2: public fund management · ZK reserve verification: confirms stablecoin backing without exposing transaction-level data (solves regulatory opacity objection) · global significance: first provincial government ZK-verified stablecoin deployment · ETH $1,800 ▲2.86% Saturday: CLARITY Act stablecoin yield dispute context

South Korea's Gyeonggi Province — the country's largest province with approximately 13.5 million residents — announced stablecoin testing beginning in August using zero-knowledge proofs for reserve verification, aiming to improve payment transparency and public fund management through a cryptographic approach that confirms stablecoin backing without exposing transaction-level data to third parties; the deployment represents the first known provincial government use of ZK-proof-verified stablecoin infrastructure at a live public-sector scale, moving the technology from academic papers and private pilots into government treasury and payment operations. Ethereum recovered to $1,800 Saturday morning (▲2.86%), reflecting both the broader crypto market's geopolitical recovery and the stablecoin narrative's increasing prominence as the CLARITY Act approaches its Senate floor window.

Gyeonggi Province's ZK-stablecoin deployment is more significant for the global regulatory debate than its local scope suggests. The core obstacle to stablecoin adoption in most regulatory frameworks is the opacity of issuer-disclosed reserves — regulators cannot verify that a stablecoin issuer actually holds the reserves it claims to hold without either trusting the issuer's audit (which has failed historically) or conducting intrusive surveillance (which conflicts with user privacy). ZK proofs solve this cleanly: a ZK reserve proof confirms that reserves exist and meet the required ratio without revealing individual holdings, transaction counterparties, or fund flows. A South Korean province deploying this in August for public fund management gives regulators in the US, EU, UK, and elsewhere a live case study to cite when writing the next generation of stablecoin rules. Gyeonggi Province testing ZK reserve verification in August is the stablecoin regulatory infrastructure story of 2026.
