The Anchor

BTC $64,200. ETH $1,723. WTI below $70. Micron +18%. May PCE at 8:30 AM. Thursday's crypto is a decisive reversal: the AI narrative that Tuesday's selloff challenged has been confirmed in the most unambiguous way possible, and the rate story is moving in the right direction.

Micron's Q3 blowout did more than lift a semiconductor stock — it answered the question that had been suppressing risk appetite across assets since Tuesday. The chip selloff arose from uncertainty about AI infrastructure demand: whether the AI memory supercycle was real, durable, and priced correctly at the levels Micron had been trading. Micron's answer was definitive across every dimension: revenue beat by 15.7%, EPS beat by 21%, Q4 guidance beat by 14.7%, and the company disclosed $100 billion in contracted minimum revenue and $22 billion in upfront customer cash. When a company's customers are prepaying for product they won't receive for quarters, demand is not uncertain. That uncertainty resolution is what is driving Thursday's crypto recovery: BTC and ETH declined Tuesday because the AI narrative was in question; they are recovering Thursday because the question was answered.

WTI at $70.42 — below $70 for the first time since February — is the structural input that matters most for the medium-term crypto outlook through the rate mechanism. The 2-day WTI decline from pre-waiver levels above $76.90 to $70.42 represents a cumulative 8.4% oil price drop that will register directly in June CPI energy calculations. June CPI (published mid-July) will be the FOMC's first comprehensive reading of the Iran deal's oil supply impact. At $70.42, the energy contribution to June CPI is unambiguously disinflationary relative to May's baseline. May PCE at 8:30 AM is the day's immediate rate test: if the headline is in line or below expectations (0.5% month-over-month), the FOMC hold case for September is strengthened; a surprise to the upside would reintroduce rate pressure into the Micron-driven risk-on environment.

ETH's outperformance (▲3.49% versus BTC's ▲2.59%) reflects the beta asymmetry at work in reverse: the same higher sensitivity to risk sentiment that drove ETH harder in Tuesday's decline now produces a proportionally larger recovery when the macro narrative resolves decisively. DeFi markets remained stable, with no cascade liquidations — confirmation that the $62,000–64,000 range was not a structural stress level for leveraged on-chain positions.

The Micron earnings' significance for crypto extends beyond the immediate risk-on sentiment. The $100 billion in minimum contracted AI memory revenue and the $22 billion in upfront customer cash indicate that AI infrastructure capital expenditure is not slowing — it is accelerating and being locked in ahead of demand. This matters for crypto through the institutional allocation channel: institutional investors who have been building AI-adjacent crypto exposure (mining infrastructure, AI-linked token ecosystems, broader tech/risk allocation) had Tuesday's selloff as a potential reason to reduce. Micron's blowout removes that reason and validates the thesis. BTC at $64,200 is above the pre-Iran-deal baseline of approximately $61,170, meaning the geopolitical premium has been fully restored alongside the AI narrative confidence.

WTI crude fell to $70.42 Thursday — below $70 for the first time since late February — as Iranian tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz increased, building supply volume into global markets despite Iran's simultaneous protest that the new Hormuz routing protocol is "unacceptable and dangerous." Brent dropped to $74.16 (▼2.24%). The three-session cumulative decline from pre-waiver levels above $76.90 to $70.42 represents an 8.4% oil price drop in 72 hours — one of the fastest oil price declines since the 60-day sanctions waiver unlocked the full Iranian supply chain, covering oil production, banking, shipping, and maritime insurance simultaneously. From the pre-Iran-deal baseline of approximately $84, WTI has fallen 16.2% total.

For crypto, WTI at $70.42 matters through the rate mechanism more than the oil market itself. The oil-to-CPI transmission: WTI $70.42 → gasoline prices → June CPI energy component (published mid-July) → FOMC's August and September assessment → rate decision. The June CPI energy contribution at $70.42 will be substantially more disinflationary than what FOMC members modeled in June's dot plot — which was assembled with oil near $84. Nine FOMC members projected a September rate hike at that dot plot. Each dollar lower in WTI weakens the inflation case that justified those projections. The sanctions waiver provides supply certainty through August 21, covering both the June and July CPI prints the FOMC will read before September's decision — meaning the oil disinflation tailwind has a clear forward visibility window that the market has not fully priced.

The Iran protest is over Lebanon: the Islamabad Memorandum's first clause requires Israel to withdraw from southern Lebanon, and Israeli forces remain, giving Iran a compliance grievance it is leveraging through Hormuz access restrictions. May PCE — the Federal Reserve's preferred inflation measure — releases at 8:30 AM ET Thursday alongside the final Q1 GDP estimate and May durable goods orders, making the early morning session data-dense before markets open. Economists expect the May headline PCE to rise 0.5% month-over-month, up slightly from April's 0.4% increase, with core PCE (excluding food and energy) expected to remain around 3.3% year-over-year. The FOMC has held its funds rate at 3.50%–3.75% through the first half of 2026, with nine members projecting a September rate hike at the June dot plot — a projection made when oil was trading near $84, not the $70.42 it sits at Thursday.

The May PCE print is the day's specific macro data point for crypto direction beyond the Micron-driven risk-on sentiment. An in-line or cool PCE print (headline at or below 0.5%, core at or below 3.3%) would reinforce the rate-hold case: combined with WTI's continued decline, it builds the narrative that inflation is moving in the right direction and September is not a hike. A hot PCE (headline above 0.5%, core above 3.3%) would reintroduce rate pressure — the FOMC's data-dependent posture means a surprise upside inflation reading at the May print could shift September projections even before the June and July CPI data arrive. For crypto, the PCE print in isolation is secondary to Micron's blowout — but if May PCE is hot, it will temper some of Thursday's risk-on recovery and introduce renewed uncertainty about whether the H2 rate tailwind is real.

Quick hits

  • ETH's outperformance Thursday (▲3.49% vs BTC's ▲2.59%) is not a structural shift in the BTC-ETH relative value — it is the beta asymmetry at work. In the same way that a higher-beta asset falls more in risk-off environments (ETH fell 7% on Tuesday vs BTC's 4%), it recovers more when risk-on returns. The signal embedded in ETH outperforming BTC Thursday is not "ETH is having a breakout moment" but "risk sentiment is genuinely improving broadly, and the higher-risk-sensitivity asset is confirming it." If risk sentiment deteriorates again, ETH will lead to the downside again by the same proportion.
  • The UST 10-year yield's rise to 4.57% (▲7 basis points Thursday) is the correct response to Micron's blowout — not a contradiction. When a bellwether company reports record growth numbers and raises guidance sharply above consensus, bond yields rise because the economic growth implied by those numbers means less urgency for rate cuts. This is "good news is good news" in the growth-inflation balance the FOMC is navigating: Micron's blowout is evidence the AI infrastructure investment wave is real and durable, which supports growth, which gives the Fed less reason to cut. Bond yields rising on strong growth data while equity futures surge is the normal, healthy correlation — it is not a signal that rate hike risk is increasing. May PCE at 8:30 will be the actual rate signal.
