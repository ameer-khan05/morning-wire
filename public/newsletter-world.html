Lead Story

The morning after: a truce in Beirut, a war still alive

A ten-day ceasefire between Israel and Hezbollah took effect at first light. In Beirut, residents fired celebratory rounds into the air as the hour changed. The pause followed the first direct Israeli–Lebanese diplomatic talks in decades, held in Washington earlier this week under heavy US facilitation. For the 48-day war between Israel and the Iran-backed militant group, it is the first sustained pause.

And yet the wider conflict goes on. Iran has kept the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed to commercial shipping through a mix of mines, drone swarms and small-boat swarms; a US naval blockade of Iranian ports remains in force. The US military has widened its remit, now empowering forces around the world to intercept any vessel tied to Tehran or suspected of supplying its government. Crude is still above $90.

At noon GMT, the leaders of the UK and France will virtually convene 40 heads of state to coordinate a diplomatic and military plan for reopening Hormuz and consolidating the ceasefire. It is one of the largest ad-hoc diplomatic meetings of the year — and one of the most consequential.

Sources: CNN · CBS News

Middle East

Celebratory gunfire greets Lebanon–Israel truce in Beirut

The 10-day pause announced by President Trump took effect before dawn. Hezbollah leadership has said it will abide by the ceasefire; Israel's defense minister told reporters the IDF will "respond in full" to any breach.

Source: CNN

Hormuz stays shut despite diplomatic progress

Tehran has signaled a willingness to de-escalate but has not lifted the effective blockade of its own strait. The waterway carries roughly a fifth of global oil and LNG supplies.

Source: CBS News

US broadens interdiction authority globally

American forces worldwide can now stop any ship tied to Iran or suspected of carrying supplies to Tehran. The change has already triggered two interceptions in the Arabian Sea this week.

Source: CBS News

Ukraine & Russia

March was Russia's deadliest month since the invasion began

Ukraine tallied 35,351 Russian casualties in March — a new monthly high. Drones caused 96% of the losses. The pace of Russian advances has slowed to its lowest rate in two years.

Orthodox Easter ceasefire collapses in mutual recriminations
