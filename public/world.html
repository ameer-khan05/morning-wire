Day 91: US and Iranian negotiators reached a tentative agreement Thursday to extend the ceasefire 60 days and reopen the Strait of Hormuz — but the deal is on President Trump's desk unsigned, VP Vance said Friday the US is "not there yet," and Iran's supreme leader has not confirmed. The US simultaneously sanctioned eight Iranian oil tankers and threatened Oman. WTI fell to $87.20 on deal optimism.

The Iran conflict reached its most consequential diplomatic moment on Day 91: negotiators from Washington and Tehran have agreed on a framework, the text is reportedly on Trump's desk, and prediction markets have placed $213 million in bets on a permanent peace deal. Yet the word "tentative" is doing heavy diplomatic work. Trump has described the choice as a "solid 50/50" between accepting the deal and resuming full military operations, a characterization that is simultaneously honest about the decision's difficulty and destabilizing for any expectation of an imminent announcement. VP Vance's "not there yet" caution, delivered Friday morning, walked back Thursday evening's optimism without canceling it. The deal's specific terms — a 60-day ceasefire extension, Hormuz reopening to unrestricted navigation, Iran clearing sea mines within 30 days, and the US lifting its naval blockade in exchange for commercial traffic resumption — are the most concrete framework either side has publicly described since the April 8 ceasefire. The unresolved issue, as it has been throughout the negotiations, is Iran's nuclear program: the proposed memorandum starts 60 days of talks on the nuclear file but does not resolve it. Whether that sequencing — relief now, hard questions later — is a deal or a delay is the interpretive question that Trump's "50/50" language refuses to resolve.

The Anchor

The deal text is on Trump's desk. That is further than any US-Iran framework has gotten in 91 days. But Trump's "solid 50/50" characterization and Vance's "not there yet" are not diplomatic formalities — they are genuine expressions of a decision the president has not yet made. The next 24-48 hours will determine whether Day 91 is the last day before a deal or the day the window closed.

The structure of the tentative framework reported by NBC News, Al Jazeera, and Radio Free Europe/Radio Liberty is specific enough to analyze in detail. The memorandum of understanding would: lift constrictions on the Strait of Hormuz and restore unrestricted navigation by commercial vessels; end the US naval blockade if commercial traffic resumes; require Iran to remove sea mines from the Strait within 30 days; and start a 60-day negotiation period specifically focused on Iran's nuclear program — including the fate of Iran's stockpile of highly enriched uranium. The critical element is the sequencing: the Hormuz opening happens at or near signing, the mine clearing happens within 30 days, and the nuclear talks happen during the 60-day window. The US does not get nuclear concessions before the Strait reopens; Iran does not get nuclear sanctions relief before demonstrating good faith on maritime access. This sequencing — which differs from the Pakistan-mediated framework that collapsed on May 20 by moving the nuclear talks into a post-Hormuz window rather than making them a precondition — is the structural compromise that brought the text to Trump's desk. The question is whether Trump finds the nuclear sequencing adequate leverage, or whether he believes a pre-signed nuclear commitment is achievable with a few more days of military pressure.

The simultaneous US sanctions on eight Iranian oil tankers — announced by the State Department and Treasury on Thursday evening while the deal text was reportedly being reviewed — reflects the administration's dual-track strategy in its starkest form. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent's statement — "We will not allow the Iranian government to increase its oil revenue for the purpose of reconstituting its armed forces" — is the economic pressure track continuing regardless of diplomatic progress. The Oman threat — Bessent warned Oman that it would face sanctions if it helps Iran collect transit fees for Hormuz passage — directly targets the mediation infrastructure that has facilitated the negotiations. These are not actions designed to reassure a deal partner; they are the maximum-pressure maintenance actions of an administration that believes its leverage is at peak and must be sustained until the final signature, not relaxed in the closing hours. Whether Iran's government interprets simultaneous sanctions as good-faith negotiating or as a reason to slow down the signing process will be evident in the next 24-48 hours.

US and Iranian negotiators reached a tentative agreement Thursday on a 60-day ceasefire extension that would reopen the Strait of Hormuz to unrestricted commercial navigation — the deal text is reportedly on President Trump's desk awaiting his signature, but VP Vance said Friday morning the US is "not there yet, but very close." Iran's state media said the text had not been finalized, and Iranian officials said the supreme leader had not confirmed the agreement. The proposed memorandum would restore Hormuz navigation at or near signing, require Iran to clear sea mines within 30 days, end the US naval blockade when commercial traffic resumes, and start 60 days of nuclear talks.

The tentative deal's structure reveals the negotiating logic that produced it. The fundamental impasse in the Pakistan-mediated talks that collapsed May 20 was sequencing: the US wanted nuclear concessions before lifting the Hormuz blockade; Iran wanted Hormuz lifted before any nuclear commitment. The tentative framework resolves this by proposing simultaneous first-step actions — Hormuz opens as the deal is signed, the US blockade lifts when commercial traffic resumes — and deferring the nuclear questions to the 60-day period that follows. This sequencing gives Iran the immediate economic relief (oil revenue from Hormuz passage) that its government needs to demonstrate domestic benefit from the deal, while preserving US leverage for the nuclear negotiations by keeping the possibility of blockade reimposition if Iran does not engage constructively in nuclear talks. The 30-day mine-clearing requirement is an Iranian operational obligation that provides the US a verifiable near-term compliance benchmark: if Iran begins mine clearing, the deal is real; if it doesn't, the US has clear grounds to exit. Whether Trump accepts this framework or demands more on the nuclear front before signing is the open question. His "solid 50/50" language suggests genuine decision uncertainty, not diplomatic theater.

Vice President JD Vance said Friday morning that the US and Iran are "not there yet" on a deal to reopen the Strait of Hormuz and extend the ceasefire — but "we're very close," he added. President Trump separately described his pending decision as a "solid 50/50" between accepting the diplomatic agreement and resuming full military operations against Iran. The White House's deliberate caution about the deal's imminence, even as the text reportedly sits on Trump's desk, reflects the administration's negotiating posture: releasing optimism without releasing leverage.

The Vance-Trump language combination is analytically significant for what it communicates to both sides simultaneously. "Very close" tells Iran's government that the deal is real enough to sustain — it is not dead, not withdrawn, not rejected. "Not there yet" tells Iran's hardline IRGC faction that there is still time for the US to walk away, keeping the maximum-pressure logic alive even in the closing hours. Trump's "solid 50/50" is the most unusual formulation: a sitting president publicly characterizing a major diplomatic decision as a coin flip is not standard diplomatic language. It is either genuinely accurate — reflecting a real internal administration debate about whether the current nuclear sequencing is sufficient — or it is a pressure tactic designed to convince Iran's leadership that the deal's conclusion cannot be assumed. The political context is relevant: Trump's Republican critics have called the deal framework insufficiently hawkish on the nuclear file, while Democrats have argued the administration is prolonging the conflict for political timing purposes. Trump's "50/50" language threads both criticisms by suggesting the president is genuinely weighing the hardest tradeoff rather than rushing to a predetermined outcome.

The State Department and Treasury Department announced new sanctions on eight Iranian oil tankers Thursday evening — while the deal text was simultaneously being reviewed by the president — sanctioning the Marshall Islands-flagged Flora, the Comoros-flagged Hauncayo, the Panama-flagged Ill Gap, and five other vessels involved in transporting Iranian crude oil. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent stated: "We will not allow the Iranian government to increase its oil revenue for the purpose of reconstituting its armed forces." Separately, Bessent threatened Oman with sanctions if it helps Iran collect transit fees for Hormuz passage, and announced steps to shut down Iranian airlines' access to landing slots, refueling, and ticket sales internationally.

The tanker sanctions and Oman threat represent the administration's deliberate choice to maintain maximum economic pressure during the deal's closing hours rather than signal good faith through a sanctions pause. This approach reflects a specific theory of the negotiation: that Iran's decision to sign comes from economic distress ($75-80 million per day in hard currency shortfall), and any reduction in that pressure before signing reduces Iran's incentive to accept the current framework rather than bargain for better terms. The Oman threat is strategically significant because Oman has been one of the primary diplomatic channels for US-Iran back-channel communications throughout the negotiations — along with Qatar and Turkey. Threatening Oman with sanctions for facilitating Hormuz fee collection could disrupt the mediation channel at precisely the moment when it is most needed for final deal logistics. Whether the Oman threat is intended as leverage against Iran's demands (telling Iran it cannot count on Oman as an intermediary if it delays) or as a genuine policy action is ambiguous. The US State Department's simultaneous sanctions announcement and the deal text's reportedly being on Trump's desk are either deliberately coordinated maximum-pressure endgame tactics or evidence of internal administration inconsistency between the diplomatic and Treasury tracks.

Quick hits

  • The 60-day nuclear talks window in the proposed deal framework is not a deadline — it is a negotiating period with no specified consequence if talks fail. This is by design: the US cannot commit to lifting all Iran sanctions before the nuclear talks conclude, and Iran cannot commit to dismantling its enrichment infrastructure before receiving sanctions relief. The 60-day window creates a structured diplomatic process with political accountability (both governments have publicly committed to engaging) without creating the brittle "take it or leave it" deadline structure that collapsed the 2015 JCPOA's renewal talks. The specific nuclear issues that 60-day talks must address are: the fate of Iran's stockpile of highly enriched uranium (70%+ purity material that is above the 3.67% JCPOA limit); the number of centrifuges Iran may operate; verification and monitoring protocols; and the timeline for sanctions relief in exchange for nuclear compliance steps. The 2015 JCPOA took approximately two years of intensive negotiation to produce these parameters. Expecting a comprehensive nuclear deal in 60 days is unrealistic — which is precisely why the current framework defers it. The 60-day period is designed to produce a "framework framework" — an agreement on the categories of issues, the verification mechanisms, and the relief sequencing — not a complete nuclear deal. If that lower bar is achievable in 60 days, the Hormuz reopening and oil price relief it produces will have given both governments the political space to continue for a longer comprehensive negotiation.
  • Iran's supreme leader's silence on the tentative deal framework is the most consequential uncertainty the framework faces on the Iranian side. Supreme Leader Khamenei's position on the nuclear file has been consistent throughout the conflict: Iran will not accept a deal that eliminates its enrichment capability, and will not accept verification protocols that give the US access to military-adjacent facilities. The proposed 60-day nuclear talks window does not require Iran to pre-accept these constraints — it only requires Iran to begin talks. Khamenei's silence could mean: he has approved the Pezeshkian government's negotiating mandate and is choosing not to publicly endorse a deal that the IRGC hardliners oppose; he is genuinely undecided and is waiting to see Trump's signature before committing; or he is opposed and his silence is the Iranian form of "not there yet." The internet restoration directive that Pezeshkian issued on May 26 remains the clearest behavioral signal that the elected government believes a deal is imminent — governments do not restore connectivity as a preparatory step for resumed conflict. But the elected government's view and the supreme leader's view are not identical in the Islamic Republic's governance structure. Pezeshkian's mandate is to negotiate; Khamenei's authority is to ratify or veto. The deal is not done until the supreme leader signals approval.

  • The Lebanon ceasefire's fourth round of talks (June 2-3) and the Pentagon security track (May 29) provide a secondary diplomatic signal about the Iran deal's proximity. Lebanon's ceasefire framework has been explicitly linked by Iran's government to any Iran-US deal structure — Iran has demanded that Lebanon's security arrangement be addressed as part of any comprehensive regional framework. The May 29 Pentagon security track meeting is happening on the same day as VP Vance's "not there yet" statement and the deal text's reported delivery to Trump's desk. The timing suggests US diplomatic and military planners are sequencing Lebanon alongside Iran: if the Iran deal closes in the next 48-72 hours, the June 2-3 Lebanon political talks would occur in a post-Iran-deal environment, giving them substantially greater latitude to address the Hezbollah disarmament and Lebanese Army deployment questions that have been the sticking points in those negotiations. The regional architecture being assembled — Iran ceasefire, Lebanon security arrangement, Gulf states normalization framework — is designed to be mutually reinforcing: each element provides political cover and security guarantees for the others. Whether the Iran deal's unsigned status delays the Lebanon talks' prospects or is independent of them will be visible in the May 29 Pentagon track's outcome.
  • The Iran sanctions on oil tankers announced Thursday while the deal text was on Trump's desk reflects the Treasury's operational independence in implementing congressionally authorized sanctions, which does not require presidential approval for each individual designation. This is not an administration sending mixed signals — it is the administrative machinery of sanctions enforcement continuing to operate on its mandate while diplomatic decisions are made at the presidential level. The eight tankers designated — including vessels flagged in Marshall Islands, Comoros, and Panama — are part of Iran's shadow fleet, the approximately 100-150 vessels that have been transporting Iranian crude to China, India, and other buyers at discounted prices since the conflict began. These vessels typically change names and flag registrations to avoid sanctions, creating a perpetual enforcement challenge for the Treasury's OFAC. The eight new designations represent a regular maintenance of sanctions pressure, not a sudden escalation. The Oman threat is more strategically significant and arguably does require presidential-level coordination: threatening a GCC mediation partner with sanctions while that partner is facilitating negotiations is a diplomatic action with potential consequences for the negotiation architecture. If the Oman statement was made without coordination with the State Department's diplomatic track, it represents a Treasury-State coordination failure at a critical moment. If it was coordinated, it is the administration's deliberate message to Oman — and through Oman, to Iran — that the US will not allow deal mechanics to be exploited for Iranian economic benefit before nuclear compliance is demonstrated.
  • Trump's "solid 50/50" language about the Iran deal decision deserves analysis as a diplomatic communication strategy distinct from his usual declarative style. Trump's characteristic public negotiating posture is confident assertion: "best deal," "winning," "totally dominating." The "solid 50/50" framing is unusual because it publicly acknowledges genuine indecision, which creates specific effects in the negotiating environment. For Iran's government, a 50/50 Trump is more dangerous than a confident Trump: a president who has already decided to take a deal cannot walk away; a president who is genuinely weighing the alternatives can. For Trump's domestic political audience, the 50/50 framing manages expectations in both directions — if he signs the deal, it was a hard-won decision not a capitulation; if he rejects it and resumes operations, it was a principled choice after genuinely considering the alternative. For the oil market, 50/50 from the president's own lips should logically reduce deal probability from the ~65% that WTI is pricing to ~50%, implying WTI should be trading closer to $88-90 rather than $87. The market appears to be discounting Trump's stated 50/50 — pricing the deal probability higher than the president's own characterization — which may reflect the market's experience that Trump's stated positions and eventual actions have often diverged in his favor for deal-making over confrontation when economic signals (WTI, S&P records) are pointing toward the deal outcome.
