The trip had been postponed Thursday when Iran's Lebanon condition — requiring ceasefire guarantees before nuclear talks — blocked Vance's original travel plans. Vance left Saturday and arrived Sunday, telling reporters he hoped to "make progress on the nuclear issue and the Lebanon ceasefire issue" — framing Lebanon as an ongoing agenda item in Switzerland rather than a precondition that had been resolved beforehand.

The talks' resumption on Day 4 closes a gap that the weekend's markets and crypto markets had been trading against since Thursday evening's postponement announcement. The mechanism that allowed Vance to travel despite Iran's Lebanon condition remaining unresolved in public terms was almost certainly private diplomatic assurances — either US commitments about Lebanon monitoring arrangements, discreet signals about the consequences for Iran's economic interest in the MOU's sanctions relief if the nuclear talks stalled, or a combination of both. The Lebanon question is now explicitly on the Swiss agenda rather than being resolved beforehand, which means the talks beginning Sunday are more complex than a straightforward nuclear negotiation: they are simultaneously a nuclear framework session and a Lebanon ceasefire negotiation, conducted by the same parties under the same 60-day clock.

Iran declared the Strait of Hormuz closed again on Saturday, warning vessels to stay away from the critical shipping route and citing alleged ceasefire violations as justification. The United States flatly denied the closure — and VP Vance publicly contradicted the claim with specific numbers: "We actually got 16 million barrels of oil out of the Strait of Hormuz yesterday — that is a record going back to even before the conflict started." Vance then departed for Switzerland that same Saturday despite Tehran's declaration, treating Iran's closure claim as a diplomatic pressure move rather than a physical military act — which his transit data supported.

The Hormuz declaration sequence reveals the fault line within the deal's implementation: Iran has the rhetorical ability to declare the strait closed and create market uncertainty even when the physical reality is that tanker traffic is at record levels. Saturday's episode showed both the power and the limit of that leverage. The power: oil prices and crypto experienced a whipsaw as markets briefly priced the closure claim as real. The limit: US rebuttal data and Vance's departure removed the pricing uncertainty within hours. The episode establishes a pattern that may recur during the 56 remaining days: Iranian hardliners using Hormuz status as periodic pressure leverage, the US responding with transit data and diplomatic counter-movement. Markets will need to internalize the distinction between rhetorical Hormuz closures and physical ones — Saturday's episode is the template for how the US has decided to handle the difference.
