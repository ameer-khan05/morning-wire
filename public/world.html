Day 70: Iran proposes a 5-year nuclear moratorium; the United States demands 20 years; three diplomatic sources tell PBS NewsHour and the Times of Israel that 12–15 years is the likely landing zone — the single remaining gap between the one-page framework and a signed deal. Friday morning, US CENTCOM intercepted Iranian attacks on Navy destroyers in the Strait of Hormuz.

The Iran deal has entered its final negotiating phase and its most dangerous simultaneous day. The duration of the nuclear enrichment moratorium — the last unresolved substantive term in the one-page, 14-point framework — is the gap separating both parties from a signed agreement. Iran's position, transmitted through Pakistani mediators, is five years: long enough to demonstrate compliance, short enough for any Iranian government to survive domestically. The US position, stated by Trump in Fox News interview Wednesday, is twenty years: long enough to outlast the current Iranian government and extend into any successor administration's term. Three diplomatic sources cited by PBS NewsHour and the Times of Israel on Thursday place the likely negotiated outcome at 12 to 15 years — consistent with the 2015 JCPOA's 15-year constraint timeline, the last international framework Iran formally accepted. While that gap was being discussed in diplomatic channels, US CENTCOM announced Friday morning that American forces had intercepted Iranian speedboat attacks on two US Navy destroyers in the Strait of Hormuz — the first kinetic incident since Trump paused Project Freedom four days ago. Day 70 is simultaneously the closest the parties have been to a signed deal and the day the conflict's violence resumed.

The Anchor

The 12–15 year landing zone is the most specific signal yet of where the Iran deal closes — or doesn't. The gap is now a number, not a concept. The CENTCOM Hormuz intercept Friday morning is the variable that could collapse the negotiating calendar before the number is resolved.

The moratorium duration gap is the Iran deal's last genuine obstacle. The one-page framework's other terms — nuclear enrichment pause during the moratorium period, UN inspection access, US sanctions relief, frozen fund release, Hormuz commercial shipping resumption — have been reported as agreed in principle by both sides. What remains is how long the moratorium lasts. The five-year Iranian position reflects a specific domestic political constraint: five years is short enough that Khamenei can frame it as a temporary confidence-building measure rather than a permanent concession on Iran's nuclear sovereignty, and short enough that the current government's political coalition survives the deal. The twenty-year US demand reflects the Trump administration's stated objective of outlasting the current Iranian leadership structure — a demand that, taken literally, asks Iran to accept constraints that no currently living Iranian political figure will see expire. The 12–15 year range cited by PBS NewsHour and the Times of Israel sources represents the zone where both constraints are theoretically satisfiable: long enough that the US can present it domestically as a genuine strategic constraint on Iran's nuclear program; short enough that Iran's leadership can present it as a time-limited confidence-building measure with a defined endpoint. The JCPOA's 15-year framework — which the Trump administration itself abandoned in 2018 — is the obvious reference point: both Iran and the US have history with a 15-year nuclear constraint architecture, and both know what it looks like in practice. The question is whether Trump can accept a framework that resembles what his own administration walked away from eight years ago, and whether Iran's leadership can accept terms that the US has a documented history of abandoning unilaterally.

The CENTCOM Hormuz intercept Friday morning is the new variable that the moratorium duration discussion must now absorb. US Central Command announced that American naval forces intercepted Iranian speedboat attacks on USS Cole (DDG-67) and USS Gravely (DDG-107) in the Strait of Hormuz at approximately 0340 local time Friday. The intercept involved defensive countermeasures; no US casualties were reported; one Iranian speedboat was disabled. The incident is the first kinetic exchange since Trump paused Project Freedom on Monday — the operational pause that the White House framed as a good-faith diplomatic signal giving Iran space to respond to the one-page framework. Iran's attack on US Navy vessels during the pause period is a significant complication: it either represents an unauthorized IRGC operation conducted without the knowledge or authorization of Iran's negotiating team (a command-and-control fragmentation scenario), or it represents a deliberate Iranian signal that the IRGC is not bound by the civilian government's negotiating posture (a hardline veto scenario). Which interpretation is correct determines whether the attack kills the deal or merely delays it. The White House has not yet commented publicly on the intercept as of Friday morning Eastern time.

Iran's 5-year moratorium offer versus the US 20-year demand is the Iran deal's last open question. Three diplomatic sources tell PBS NewsHour and the Times of Israel that 12–15 years is where the negotiation closes — consistent with the 2015 JCPOA framework both parties have history with.

The nuclear moratorium duration gap is the final substantive obstacle in Iran-US negotiations because both sides have publicly staked political capital on their respective numbers. Trump's Fox News interview Wednesday was explicit: "I want a long deal, not a short deal — twenty years at minimum." Iran's foreign ministry and Pezeshkian's government have signaled equally explicitly through Pakistani intermediaries that five years is their political ceiling — the maximum duration they can accept without it becoming a de facto permanent concession that the IRGC and Supreme Leader's office would veto. The 12–15 year zone is where each side's political constraint can theoretically be satisfied with the right framing: the US presents it as a "15-year minimum" that goes beyond what Iran offered; Iran presents it as a "confidence-building measure" with a defined endpoint, not a permanent elimination of its nuclear program. The 2015 JCPOA is the explicit reference architecture for both parties: the original deal imposed 15-year constraints on Iran's enrichment capacity, and both sides have institutional memory of what that framework looked like in practice. Iran accepted 15 years in 2015; accepting 12–15 years in 2026 is politically defensible for Pezeshkian's government as a return to the JCPOA's structure rather than a capitulation to a new US demand. The Trump administration's domestic framing challenge is more acute: having withdrawn from the JCPOA in 2018 specifically because "it doesn't go far enough," accepting a framework that matches or falls short of the JCPOA's duration requires the administration to argue that the 2026 deal is substantively better than what it condemned — a higher bar when the duration is 12–15 rather than the demanded 20.

One Iranian speedboat disabled; no US casualties. White House silent as of Friday morning EST.

The CENTCOM Hormuz intercept Friday morning is the most consequential single event of Day 70 because it occurred during the Project Freedom pause — the US operational concession that was explicitly framed by the White House as a good-faith diplomatic signal giving Iran space to respond to the one-page framework. Iran attacking US Navy vessels during the pause has two possible interpretations, each with different implications for the deal's survival. Interpretation one: IRGC unauthorized operation. Under this reading, the IRGC conducted the Hormuz attack without authorization from Pezeshkian's civilian government or the Supreme Leader's office specifically in the context of the deal negotiations — a command-and-control fragmentation in which the IRGC's operational commanders acted independently of the political track. This interpretation is consistent with prior IRGC behavior during the conflict: the IRGC has consistently operated with significant tactical autonomy, and there is precedent for IRGC operations that complicated diplomatic tracks without being ordered by political leadership. Under this reading, the attack is a serious complication but not a deal-killer: Pezeshkian's government can credibly disavow it to Pakistani mediators, and the White House can choose to treat it as an IRGC rogue action rather than a government decision. Interpretation two: IRGC hardline veto. Under this reading, the attack was authorized — directly or implicitly — by the Supreme Leader's office as a signal that the IRGC will not accept any deal that Pezeshkian's government negotiates without IRGC buy-in. This interpretation means the deal is structurally blocked from the Iranian side regardless of what the civilian negotiating team agrees to, because the IRGC's operational posture is not controlled by the same political actors conducting the negotiations. The White House's silence Friday morning is itself a signal: the administration is likely making the interpretation decision internally before any public statement commits the US to a position that closes off one of the two scenarios.

Lebanon ceasefire renewal talks are confirmed for the May 14–15 window — the same week as the Trump-Xi Beijing summit. The US is attempting to manage both simultaneously. The IDF's continuing operations north of the Litani are the structural obstacle to any Lebanon extension; Hezbollah's rearmament question is the political obstacle.

The confirmation of Lebanon ceasefire renewal talks for May 14–15 is positive news in the narrow sense that there is a confirmed diplomatic process rather than an expiry without a framework. But the structural conditions for a Lebanon extension have not improved since the April extension: the IDF is still operating north of the Litani River, which the ceasefire's original geographic constraints placed off-limits; Hezbollah has used the ceasefire period to rearm from Iranian supply lines that the Hormuz conflict did not interrupt; and the political framework for a Lebanon extension requires alignment between the US, France, Saudi Arabia, and Lebanon's government — four parties whose Lebanon priorities have not converged on a shared framework. The ceasefire's April extension was brokered in the context of the Iran deal process: the Iran nuclear track and the Lebanon track were presented to the region as a package, with the Iran deal providing the incentive structure for Hezbollah (an Iranian proxy) to accept extension terms it would otherwise resist. Day 70's CENTCOM Hormuz intercept complicates that package: if the Iran deal is in doubt because of IRGC action, Hezbollah's incentive to accept Lebanon extension terms connected to an Iran deal framework weakens simultaneously. The US is attempting to run three simultaneous diplomatic tracks — Iran moratorium duration negotiation, Lebanon ceasefire renewal, and Trump-Xi summit preparation — in the same week, with the same diplomatic bandwidth. The Lebanon renewal is the track most at risk of receiving insufficient attention.

The summit's geopolitical outcome will determine whether the week's diplomatic calendar is a breakthrough or a collision.

The Trump-Xi Beijing summit's May 14–15 timing is not coincidental: both parties selected the date knowing it falls on the same day as Lebanon's ceasefire expiry, the window for Iran's response to the one-page framework, and the BLS jobs report's aftermath in US markets. The summit's stated agenda — trade, AI, supply chain — sits inside a week of maximum geopolitical complexity in which the US is simultaneously attempting to close an Iran nuclear deal, renew the Lebanon ceasefire, and conduct the first in-person Trump-Xi summit of the second administration. The White House's pre-summit posture is confrontational on AI: the administration has formally accused China of "industrial-scale AI theft" of US technology and personnel in a statement released through the National Security Council Wednesday, citing specific incidents involving DeepSeek R2 development and semiconductor IP. China's preemptive response — blocking Meta's commercial AI products and the Manus AI agent platform from operating in China — is a reciprocal signaling move that establishes Beijing's willingness to restrict US technology access as a bargaining chip. The summit's geopolitical context beyond AI: the tariff regime imposed in April is still in effect, supply chain restructuring from the conflict period has accelerated Chinese manufacturing capacity in categories adjacent to sanctioned goods, and China's financial support for Iran during the conflict — through oil purchases conducted outside the SWIFT system — has provided Tehran with hard currency that partially offset the US sanctions pressure. Whether Trump raises the Iran oil purchases in the Xi meeting, and whether Xi offers any China role in the Iran deal framework in exchange for tariff relief, are the summit's most consequential unpublicized agenda items.

The Iran deal's oil market thesis: a signed moratorium restores Hormuz transit, WTI normalizes toward $80, and the employment pressure from oil-economy states partially eases within two quarters.

The April jobs report's +62,000 number is the Iran deal's most important economic argument: the conflict's Hormuz disruption has been the primary driver of the oil price elevation that has suppressed consumer and small business spending, constrained the Fed's room to cut, and driven the stagflation dynamic that the April data confirms. The causal chain from Hormuz to employment is specific: elevated WTI above $90 raises transportation, manufacturing, and agricultural input costs; those elevated costs reduce real margins; reduced margins compress hiring in the oil-price-sensitive sectors (logistics, manufacturing, food production) that dominate the +62K miss's distribution. A signed Iran deal does not immediately fix the April number — it is historical — but it changes the forward outlook in a way that the Fed, the Treasury, and the White House all need. The oil normalization math: WTI at $94.81 with an open-Hormuz thesis implies a potential $15–20 per barrel decline toward the $75–80 consensus post-deal range. That decline, if realized over 4–8 weeks following a signed deal, would reduce CPI by an estimated 0.3–0.5 percentage points — sufficient for the Fed to move on rate cuts by July under Warsh's stated framework of data-dependent easing. The jobs miss, the oil price, the Iran deal, and the Fed path are a single connected system in the current macro environment. The deal's economics are as important to the White House's domestic political calculus as its security objectives.

Quick hits

  • The JCPOA reference is unavoidable in the 12–15 year moratorium duration discussion because both parties have recent, explicit, and documented history with a 15-year nuclear constraint architecture. The 2015 deal's most important constraint — the 15-year limitation on Iran's low-enriched uranium stockpile — was the product of 18 months of negotiation between Iran, the US, UK, France, Germany, Russia, and China. Both Iranian and US negotiating teams from that period are consulted participants in the current talks. The institutional memory of what a 15-year framework looks like in practice — the inspection mechanism, the enrichment centrifuge limits, the stockpile caps — exists on both sides and provides a working blueprint that the current negotiators can adapt without rebuilding from scratch. The Trump administration's challenge in accepting a 12–15 year framework is the 2018 withdrawal: if 15 years was insufficient in 2018, accepting a 12–15 year deal in 2026 requires either an argument that the 2026 deal is better in other dimensions (more intrusive inspections, harder snapback provisions, connected to Hormuz economics) or an implicit acknowledgment that the 2018 withdrawal was a strategic error. The administration's framing capacity — to present the 2026 deal as stronger than the JCPOA despite a shorter or equal moratorium duration — is the domestic political test it must pass to survive the deal politically in the United States.
  • The CENTCOM Hormuz intercept's timing — at 0340 local Friday, approximately 36 hours after Trump's Wednesday "one week" deadline statement — raises a specific interpretation question. If the IRGC planned the attack before Trump's Wednesday statement and simply executed it on schedule regardless of the diplomatic development, it suggests a planning cycle that is independent of real-time diplomatic signals: the IRGC does not retask operations based on daily diplomatic news. If the attack was planned after Trump's Wednesday statement specifically as a response to the "one week" deadline framing — an IRGC message that it will not accept time-limited ultimatums from Trump — it suggests the IRGC is operating as an active participant in signaling the terms under which it would accept a deal, not a passive executor of whatever the civilian government negotiates. The distinction matters for the deal's durability: a deal that the civilian government negotiates without IRGC buy-in on the specific terms — especially the moratorium duration — is a deal the IRGC can undermine operationally at any point during its term, as the Hormuz intercept demonstrates it has the capacity and willingness to do.
  • Lebanon's May 14–15 renewal window landing on the same day as the Trump-Xi summit creates a specific State Department prioritization problem. The individuals responsible for Lebanon renewal negotiations — the Special Envoy for Lebanon, the senior NSC official for the Levant, and the Deputy Secretary of State — are also the individuals who would be in Beijing for the Trump-Xi summit or providing the interagency preparation for it. Running a high-stakes bilateral summit and a multilateral ceasefire renewal simultaneously with the same senior diplomat cohort requires one of the two tracks to receive delegated handling from more junior officials — which for Lebanon renewal means the substantive decisions are being made by officials who are one or two levels below the seniority that the Lebanese government, the IDF, and Hezbollah's indirect counterparts require to take the framework seriously. The structural fix — finalizing Lebanon's extension before May 12, so it is resolved before the summit week — requires the parties to agree in the next four days. The IDF's Litani operations are the obstacle that cannot be resolved in four days.
  • China's preemptive blocking of Meta and Manus AI before the Trump-Xi summit is a negotiating posture that mirrors exactly what the US has done with Huawei, TikTok, and Chinese semiconductor companies: restrict access to domestic markets as leverage in bilateral technology negotiations. The symmetry is not accidental — China's technology policy community has studied the US approach to technology restriction and adapted the toolkit for Chinese use. The specific choice of Meta (whose commercial AI products are expanding in enterprise markets) and Manus (an autonomous AI agent built on Chinese models by a Chinese team but commercially targeting US and European enterprise customers) is calibrated to create a negotiating cost for the US without blocking the technology categories — semiconductors, foundational model training — where Chinese domestic capacity is already constrained by US export controls. Beijing's position is: if the US wants access to Chinese enterprise AI markets for its companies, the tariff and export control regime needs to move. The summit is the venue for testing whether that position translates into a negotiated outcome or a continued escalation.
  • The April +62K jobs print's distribution matters more than the headline for the Iran deal's economic argument. The employment weakness in April is concentrated in oil-price-sensitive sectors: transportation and warehousing (−18K), manufacturing (−12K), food services (−9K), and construction (−7K, driven by residential starts impacted by elevated material costs). Government employment was flat; healthcare and professional services provided positive offset. The sectoral distribution confirms the conflict-cost hypothesis: it is not a broad labor market softening but a targeted weakening in the cost-exposed sectors that connect directly to the Hormuz disruption's supply chain and energy price effects. This distribution makes the Iran deal's economic case internally consistent: the same sectors that are weak in April are the sectors that benefit most directly from a WTI normalization. A deal signed in May, with oil normalization beginning in June, would show up in July and August employment data — within a political calendar relevant to both the midterm environment and Warsh's stated framework for rate-cut conditions.
  • The Trump-Xi summit's Iran dimension — specifically, China's oil purchases from Iran during the conflict period — is the unpublicized agenda item with the most strategic weight. China has been purchasing Iranian oil at a significant discount (estimated $15–20 per barrel below market) throughout the conflict, using transactions structured outside the SWIFT system through Chinese state banks. These purchases have provided Iran with the hard currency that partially offset US sanctions pressure and extended Iran's economic capacity to sustain the conflict beyond what pure sanctions would have allowed. The White House has two possible approaches to this in the summit: raise it explicitly as a sanctions violation and demand China cease the purchases (confrontational posture that risks the summit's trade agenda), or offer to formalize a role for China in the Iran deal implementation framework — inspections, frozen fund escrow, sanctions compliance monitoring — in exchange for China ending the parallel oil purchases (constructive posture that brings China into the deal's architecture). The second approach would be a significant strategic innovation: a US-China cooperative framework on Iran would be the first major bilateral security cooperation between the administrations, and would provide the deal with a multilateral enforcement mechanism that the original JCPOA had but the 2026 one-page framework currently lacks.
