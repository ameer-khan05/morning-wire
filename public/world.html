Iran launches missiles and drones at the UAE — first attack on a US Gulf ally since the April 8 ceasefire. US Navy sinks seven Iranian speedboats. Trump announces Project Freedom. Iran says Project Freedom is a ceasefire violation. The truce is on the brink of collapse. Day 67.

The Iran-US ceasefire, which had held for 27 days without a direct attack on a US ally, reached its most critical test Monday when Iran launched 12 ballistic missiles, three cruise missiles, and four drones at the United Arab Emirates — a US Gulf partner and host to approximately 3,500 US military personnel. UAE air defense systems intercepted the projectiles; one Iranian drone struck the Fujairah Oil Industry Zone, sparking a major fire and injuring three Indian nationals. The US military responded by sinking seven Iranian speedboats operating in the Strait of Hormuz under President Trump's new "Project Freedom" initiative — an armed US Navy escort mission for commercial vessels transiting the strait, announced Sunday. Trump warned Monday from the White House that Iran will be "blown off the face of the earth" if it targets US ships or the vessels they are escorting. Iran's foreign minister Abbas Araghchi, posting on X, declared Project Freedom itself a violation of the ceasefire: "Events in Hormuz make clear that there's no military solution to a political crisis." The ceasefire is described by multiple outlets as being on the brink of collapse — the most severe characterization since the truce began April 8. Lebanon's May 14 ceasefire expiry is now nine days away, with the IDF conducting operations north of the Litani River and no renewal framework in place, creating a dual-front countdown as the Iran-UAE exchange dominates Day 67's news cycle.

The Anchor

Iran's UAE attack and Project Freedom's launch have produced the conflict's first direct US-Iran military exchange since the ceasefire — and Iran calling the US escort mission a violation is the diplomatic signal that no mutual understanding of ceasefire terms exists.

Monday's events represent a qualitative escalation beyond anything that has occurred since the April 8 ceasefire: for the first time, Iran has directly attacked a US Gulf ally (the UAE), and for the first time, US forces have engaged Iranian military assets (seven speedboats) in the Strait of Hormuz under a named US military operation. The "Project Freedom" designation — given to Trump's Sunday announcement of the Hormuz escort mission — is significant beyond its operational content: naming the operation "Project Freedom" frames the US position as one of asserting the principle of freedom of navigation against Iranian sovereign claims, which is a political statement as much as a military one. Iran's counterclaim — that Project Freedom violates the ceasefire by attempting to force open a strait Iran considers its sovereign territorial waters — defines the terms of the dispute with the same clarity: the ceasefire, in Iran's reading, covers the suspension of air strikes against Iranian territory, not the US right to operate its navy through Iranian-claimed maritime zones. The gap between the US reading (ceasefire covers air strikes only; US retains all existing naval rights) and Iran's reading (ceasefire covers any US military activity in the region that Iran characterizes as hostile) is the legal and political abyss that Monday's exchange has exposed.

The physical events of Monday are documented: Iran launched projectiles; UAE intercepted most of them; one drone struck the Fujairah Oil Industry Zone, a major petroleum storage and processing facility in the UAE's easternmost emirate, sparking a fire that injured three Indian nationals and prompted Dubai airport to temporarily disrupt flights due to airspace concerns. The US Navy, operating in the Strait of Hormuz under Project Freedom, engaged seven Iranian speedboats that approached US vessels and commercial ships being escorted; the speedboats were sunk. A South Korean cargo vessel sustained unspecified damage in the broader exchange. Trump spoke from the White House: "They will be blown off the face of the earth" — referring to Iran — if US ships are targeted. No US personnel casualties have been confirmed from Monday's exchange. Iran has described its actions as a response to the Project Freedom "provocation" rather than as a new offensive campaign, suggesting Iran is framing Monday's strikes as reactive within its own narrative framework even as they represent a clear breach of the ceasefire's practical expectations.

Iran's Monday attack on the UAE: 12 ballistic missiles, 3 cruise missiles, 4 drones — UAE intercepts most; one drone strikes Fujairah Oil Industry Zone, sparking fire and injuring three Indian nationals. Dubai Airport disrupts flights over airspace concerns. First direct Iran strike on a US Gulf ally since the ceasefire.

Iran's Monday attack on the UAE is the most complex single-target missile and drone barrage of the 67-day conflict. The projectile count — 12 ballistic missiles, three cruise missiles, and four drones — represents a scaled-up version of the attack typology Iran has used against Israeli targets and US naval assets, now directed at a UAE civilian and industrial target for the first time since the April 8 ceasefire. UAE's air defense system, which includes Patriot batteries, the THAAD system provided by the US, and UAE's own Baynunah systems, intercepted the majority of inbound projectiles; the UAE Ministry of Defense confirmed engagements against 15 missiles and four drones. One Iranian drone successfully penetrated UAE air defenses and struck the Fujairah Oil Industry Zone — a major petroleum storage, refining, and bunkering hub on the UAE's eastern coast that handles a significant fraction of the oil supply that previously transited Hormuz before the blockade. The Fujairah strike sparked a major fire that took several hours to control, injured three Indian nationals working at the facility, and prompted precautionary airspace closures over the area that disrupted Dubai International Airport — the world's busiest international airport — with dozens of flights rerouted or delayed. No UAE civilian deaths have been confirmed from Monday's attack.

Trump's announcement of "Project Freedom" on Sunday — the US military will escort commercial vessels through the Strait of Hormuz — immediately changed the conflict's operational parameters. Under Project Freedom, US Navy assets are positioned within the strait to physically escort commercial ships that are attempting to transit; Iran's response to the first escorted convoy was to launch cruise missiles at the vessels and dispatch speedboats to intercept them. The US Navy sank seven Iranian speedboats in the engagement; the CNBC report confirms US forces "blew up" six small Iranian boats (with a seventh sunk separately, per CBS News). A South Korean cargo vessel sustained unspecified damage from the broader exchange — the first direct confirmation that a third-party commercial vessel has been hit in a US-protected transit. Trump's warning — "they will be blown off the face of the earth" if Iran targets US ships — is the most explicit personal military threat Trump has issued against Iran since the conflict began. Iran's response, through foreign minister Araghchi's X post, reframes Monday's events as Iran reacting to US provocation rather than Iran initiating a new offensive: "Events in Hormuz make clear that there's no military solution to a political crisis." The exchange is the first direct US-Iran military engagement since the April 8 ceasefire — a threshold crossing that, depending on whether it is contained or repeated, determines whether the conflict has resumed or whether Monday was a single-session escalation event.

The ceasefire's formal status as of Tuesday morning: technically in force but materially violated by Monday's events on both sides. Iran struck UAE (a US ally); US sank Iranian speedboats (Iranian military assets); Iran fired cruise missiles at US-escorted commercial vessels. The ceasefire framework agreed April 8 — covering the suspension of large-scale US and Israeli air strikes against Iranian territory in exchange for a halt in Iranian offensive military operations — is now under question for both parties. Iran's position, as stated by Foreign Minister Araghchi, is that the US launched Project Freedom in violation of the spirit of the ceasefire, and that Iran's Monday actions were reactive rather than offensive. The US position, per Trump's White House statements, is that the US retains all rights to operate its navy in international waters including the Strait of Hormuz under any ceasefire framework, and that Iran's attacks constitute a breach. The legal reading of the April 8 ceasefire text — the specifics of which were never publicly disclosed — is the contested question that both sides are now interpreting to justify their actions. The Pakistan mediation track, which transmitted Iran's 14-point peace proposal through the diplomatic channel, has not yet publicly addressed Monday's events; its continued functioning is a necessary condition for any de-escalation pathway. Iran's FM's statement that "there's no military solution to a political crisis" suggests Iran has not fully abandoned the diplomatic channel even while conducting Monday's attacks.

The Fujairah strike targets this alternative supply route directly — if the Fujairah hub is disrupted, the alternative to Hormuz becomes less reliable, raising both the strategic value of Project Freedom's escort mission and the cost of any oil that does move through the region. Quick hits

  • The Fujairah Oil Industry Zone's strategic significance in the current conflict context cannot be overstated. Before the Hormuz blockade, Fujairah handled approximately 3-4 million barrels per day of oil bunkering and transshipment — a fraction of the 21 million barrels that transited Hormuz daily. Since the blockade, Fujairah's role expanded substantially as UAE pipelines from Abu Dhabi's onshore fields became the primary bypass route for UAE oil exports. Iran's targeting of Fujairah — the alternative to Hormuz — is the operational logic of a party that wants to remove every viable shipping alternative and force all negotiations through the Hormuz access question. If Fujairah is disabled, the only operational path for Persian Gulf oil is through Iranian-controlled Hormuz under Iranian toll conditions, which is precisely the outcome Iran's Hormuz sovereignty law and IRGC toll collection are designed to institutionalize.
  • UAE's air defense performance — engaging 15 missiles and four drones, successfully intercepting all but one — demonstrates the effectiveness of the layered air defense system the UAE has built since the Yemen Houthi drone attacks of 2019-2021. The UAE's THAAD system (US-provided), Patriot batteries, and domestic Baynunah systems created the multi-altitude interception capability that caught the large ballistic missiles at high altitude, the cruise missiles at medium altitude, and most drones at low altitude. The one drone that penetrated to the Fujairah facility is the exception that demonstrates Iran's investment in low-altitude, terrain-hugging drone profiles that can exploit gaps in radar coverage — the same vulnerability that Iran-backed Houthi drones have exploited against Saudi Arabia. The penetration of Fujairah's specific air defense coverage is likely an operational lesson Iran will apply in subsequent attacks if the conflict continues.
  • The UAE's response to Monday's attack — calling it "treacherous Iranian aggression" and reserving the right to respond — is consistent with its established pattern from prior Houthi attacks that Iran backed: strong condemnatory language, immediate coordination with the US, and reliance on its security partnership rather than independent military action. The UAE's 3,500 US personnel presence — at Al Dhafra Air Base, primarily — gives the US a direct stake in UAE security that goes beyond alliance politics: US military personnel are physically exposed to Iranian attack in the UAE, which is why Trump's "blown off the face of the earth" threat is calibrated around US ship targeting rather than UAE territory targeting (the latter is an attack on an ally; the former is an attack on the United States directly).
  • The South Korean vessel damaged in Monday's Hormuz exchange represents a specific diplomatic complication. South Korea — which hosts approximately $7 billion in frozen Iranian assets from prior oil payment arrangements — is simultaneously a US ally, an Iran creditor, a major Persian Gulf oil importer, and now a party whose commercial vessel has been damaged in a US-Iran military exchange in international waters. The South Korean government's response to the vessel damage will be watched closely: if Seoul formally protests to either the US or Iran, it creates a new diplomatic vector in the conflict involving a country with unique leverage (the frozen assets) and unique exposure (the vessel damage).
  • The Lebanon May 14 ceasefire expiry's convergence with the Iran-UAE exchange creates a specific problem for the US diplomatic capacity. The Trump administration brokered both the Iran ceasefire (April 8) and the Lebanon ceasefire (April 16), and both were extended once. Managing simultaneous renewals — or managing simultaneous collapses — of both ceasefires while also managing Project Freedom's operational parameters in the Strait of Hormuz is a tripling of the diplomatic bandwidth required from a team that has been operating continuously since the conflict began February 28. The State Department, NSC, and DoD are now coordinating on three simultaneous crisis-level items (Hormuz, Lebanon, Iran negotiations) against the backdrop of a domestic calendar (April jobs report, Warsh Fed transition) that would individually constitute a full week of administration attention. The convergence is not by design; it is what 67 days of managed escalation looks like when every managed crisis simultaneously approaches its next threshold.
  • Iran's internet blackout — now 67 days old, affecting 90 million people — means the Iranian public is receiving a specific government-curated version of Monday's events: Iran defending itself against US naval aggression in its sovereign waters. The regime's narrative — Project Freedom as provocation, Monday's attacks as self-defense, the IRGC as protecting Iranian sovereignty — is the only version of events available to 90 million Iranians. The implications for the 14-point peace proposal: the Iranian public, if and when the proposal becomes public knowledge, will encounter it in a context where the regime has established that the US is the aggressor. A proposal that involves lifting the US naval blockade will be framed domestically as Iran receiving validation rather than making concessions — which is the only domestic political framing under which Pezeshkian's government can accept such terms.
