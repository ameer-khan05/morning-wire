The Iran ceasefire is in open crisis: an IRGC drone struck a Singapore-flagged cargo ship in the Strait of Hormuz on June 26, Trump called it a "foolish violation" of the MOU, the US military struck Iranian missile and radar sites in retaliation, and the IRGC fired missiles and drones at US-linked targets in the early hours of June 28 — with Kuwait and Bahrain reporting incoming projectiles and condemning the attacks as "heinous."

The escalation sequence transforms what had been a fraught but functioning ceasefire framework into an active military exchange at the world's most important energy chokepoint. The Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding signed June 17 is now Day 11, with 49 days remaining to its August 17 expiry, and the Strait of Hormuz — the deal's economic anchor, kept open under a 60-day sanctions waiver to allow Iranian crude to flow — is now the site of IRGC drone attacks, US retaliatory strikes, and IRGC counter-strikes in a 48-hour cycle. A simultaneous dispute over IAEA nuclear inspector access adds an inspection dimension to negotiations already complicated by the enrichment gap and Lebanon withdrawal. And Iran's World Cup team — competing in a US-hosted tournament — plays on as the two countries exchange overnight strikes.

The Anchor

Day 11. 49 days to August 17. IRGC drone hits Hormuz. US strikes back. IRGC fires back. Kuwait and Bahrain: incoming projectiles. IAEA vs Iran: inspector dispute. The deal signed eleven days ago is under active military stress. The Hormuz supply assurance was its most durable mechanism — and it is now the mechanism under direct attack.

The Hormuz escalation is the most dangerous single moment in the MOU's first eleven days. The ceasefire framework that the US and Iran signed June 17 rested on three interlocking economic and security incentives: a 60-day Iran sanctions waiver keeping oil flowing, a Lebanese ceasefire clause requiring Israeli withdrawal, and a nuclear negotiation window allowing the four working groups in Switzerland to close the enrichment, inspection, and reconstruction gaps. The Hormuz waiver was the arrangement's economic anchor — it gave Iran immediate, tangible revenue and gave the US the disinflationary oil-price argument that held the September FOMC rate-hike case at bay. An IRGC drone striking a cargo ship is not a signal of a deal under stress; it is the IRGC directly attacking the mechanism that makes the deal economically viable. US Central Command's retaliatory strikes on Iranian missile and radar infrastructure are calibrated to punish the attack without triggering a full-scale resumption of hostilities — but the IRGC's counter-strikes in the early hours of June 28 show that the retaliatory cycle did not stop at a single US response.

The critical question for the next 49 days is whether this is a limited retaliatory exchange — a cycle of attacks and counter-attacks that the diplomatic framework survives — or the beginning of a breakdown that makes the August 17 permanent deal deadline unreachable. The four working groups in Switzerland must now continue negotiations while the parties are exchanging military strikes at Hormuz. The IAEA inspector access dispute — where the IAEA director general confirmed the deal grants inspectors access to Iranian nuclear sites while Iranian officials insist no new inspection arrangements were agreed — adds a simultaneous diplomatic front. With Lebanon violence still complicating the clause 1 Israeli withdrawal, the MOU is managing three simultaneous problem areas (enrichment, Lebanon, inspections) plus an active military exchange at Hormuz that directly threatens the economic foundation all three problem areas depend on for the incentive to stay at the table.

The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps launched four one-way attack drones at ships in the Strait of Hormuz on Friday, June 26 — one striking the upper deck of a Singapore-flagged cargo ship, with the other three shot down — in an attack Trump described as a "foolish violation" of the Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding that the US and Iran signed June 17. US Central Command struck Iranian missile and drone storage facilities and coastal radar installations the same day in retaliation, characterizing the response as "powerful" and directed at Iran's "dangerous behavior." The IRGC then fired missiles and drones at US-linked sites in the early hours of Sunday, June 28, between 2 and 3 a.m. local time; Kuwait and Bahrain reported incoming projectiles from the exchange and condemned the attacks as "heinous." The cargo ship was able to continue its transit, per Trump.

The 48-hour cycle — IRGC drone attack, US retaliatory strikes, IRGC counter-strikes — constitutes active military exchange, not the diplomatic protest posture Iran had maintained through MOU Day 10. The distinction matters for the ceasefire framework's durability: diplomatic protests (such as Iran's ongoing characterization of Hormuz routing as "unacceptable") are negotiable friction points that the working groups can address over time. Military strikes on cargo ships and retaliatory exchanges between US and Iranian forces create a different kind of pressure: they risk escalating past the point where the diplomatic incentive structure — the economic benefits of the sanctions waiver, the WTI disinflation, the Iranian crude revenue — can hold the parties at the table. Trump's framing as a "foolish violation" rather than a trigger for resumed hostilities suggests the US is attempting to maintain the MOU framework while punishing the IRGC action, but the IRGC's counter-strikes in the early hours of June 28 show that Iran's Revolutionary Guards are not treating the US retaliatory strikes as the end of the cycle.

IAEA Head Confirms Deal Grants Inspector Access · Iran Disputes · US Says Iran Agreed · Conflicting Accounts · Nuclear Sites Under Reconstruction IAEA IAEA director general (Jun 26): US-Iran peace deal grants UN nuclear inspectors access to Iran's nuclear sites → confirmed publicly in response to conflicting accounts · Trump (prior): Iran agreed to allow nuclear inspections · Iran: "no new plans for UN inspectors to visit sites damaged in strikes" → contradicts US + IAEA characterization · Iranian position: bombed nuclear sites are under reconstruction → "operationally premature" for inspection → separate from routine inspection under existing IAEA agreements · US position: inspection access in the agreement → verification architecture for permanent deal depends on this · dispute mechanism: conflicting accounts to mediators in Switzerland · significance: adds inspection dimension to negotiations already managing enrichment gap + Lebanon withdrawal + Hormuz military exchange

The International Atomic Energy Agency's director general confirmed on June 26 that the US-Iran peace deal grants IAEA inspectors access to Iran's nuclear sites — a confirmation that directly contradicts Iran's position that no new inspection arrangements were agreed. Trump had previously stated that Iran agreed to allow nuclear inspections; Iran's official response was that there were "no new plans" for UN inspectors to visit sites damaged in US and Israeli strikes. The IAEA head's public statement — made in response to the conflicting accounts — represents the most authoritative external confirmation that the deal's text or agreed interpretation includes inspection access, but Iran's rejection of that interpretation leaves the dispute unresolved. Iranian officials told mediators in Switzerland that bombed nuclear sites are currently under reconstruction and not subject to inspection in their current state — an argument that treats the inspection dispute as a timing question rather than a fundamental disagreement about what was agreed.

The practical stakes are significant. Any permanent agreement that follows the 60-day MOU window will require a verifiable inspection and monitoring architecture — the lesson from the 2015 JCPOA is that enrichment limits without intrusive monitoring are not credible arms control. If Iran is unwilling to allow IAEA inspectors to visit bombed nuclear sites, the US cannot verify whether those sites have been rendered permanently incapable of enrichment or whether they are being reconstructed with enrichment capability intact. The dispute adds a third simultaneous problem area — in addition to the enrichment gap (where Trump's "low-level enrichment OK" walk-back opened a compromise band) and Lebanon withdrawal — to negotiations that have 49 days to produce a permanent agreement. The Hormuz military exchange now adds a fourth dimension: parties exchanging strikes while trying to negotiate at the working group level. The four working groups in Switzerland have no precedent for that challenge.

The 48-hour cycle that unfolded from Friday through Sunday morning — IRGC drone attack, US CENTCOM retaliation, IRGC counter-strikes — represents an escalation dynamic that the MOU framework must now absorb or not survive. RFE/RL reported Sunday that Trump has threatened to "complete the job" while the IRGC retaliates for US strikes; the framing "complete the job" echoes the language used to justify the June bombing of Iranian nuclear facilities before the MOU was signed. The working groups in Switzerland must now negotiate across the enrichment gap, Lebanon withdrawal, and IAEA inspection dispute while IRGC forces and US Central Command exchange strikes in the Persian Gulf. Neither the US nor Iran has formally declared the MOU void; both continue to claim adherence to the framework while conducting military operations that the other characterizes as ceasefire violations.

The MOU's durability factors still exist: Iranian crude continues to generate revenue, the sanctions waiver's economic benefits have not been formally withdrawn, Pakistan and Qatar remain engaged as mediators, and the working groups' meetings in Switzerland have not been suspended. But the fragility factors are growing: the IRGC operates with significant operational independence from the Iranian government's diplomatic negotiators, meaning Tehran's working group representatives may not have full authority over the Guards' Hormuz operations; each exchange in the 48-hour retaliatory cycle raises the risk that the next strike occurs at a level — a drone that kills US personnel, a US strike that kills IRGC senior commanders — that neither government can characterize as a limited response to a ceasefire violation. Trump's "foolish" framing of the drone attack was a deliberate de-escalation signal; the IRGC's counter-strikes suggest the signal was not received or was deliberately rejected. The next 48 hours will determine whether the cycle continues to escalate or finds a level both sides can present as a settled exchange that leaves the MOU intact.

Quick hits

  • The IRGC's operational independence from Iran's government creates a structural accountability problem for ceasefire diplomacy. Iranian government negotiators in Switzerland can agree to terms that the IRGC's Quds Force and naval commanders then breach through direct action — without those government negotiators necessarily being informed in advance or being able to prevent the breach. This is not a new dynamic in Iranian political-military structure, but it is newly critical: if the IRGC can unilaterally conduct drone attacks that Trump characterizes as ceasefire violations, and if neither the working groups nor the Iranian government can reliably prevent or respond to IRGC action, then any agreement reached in Switzerland has limited practical durability against IRGC operational decisions. The accountability gap is the structural weakness of the negotiation framework.
  • Trump's warning that he may "complete the job" — language that echoes the June bombing campaign justification — is the single most consequential signal from the weekend's escalation. If the IRGC counter-strikes on June 28 are followed by a further US strike on a higher-value target (senior IRGC commanders, additional nuclear-adjacent sites, assets deeper inside Iran), the MOU framework would be under existential pressure. The 49 days remaining are not a buffer against escalation; they are the window within which a single disproportionate strike or miscalculation could end the diplomatic track entirely. Watch what the working groups in Switzerland do on Monday morning — whether they convene, adjourn, or suspend is the first concrete signal of whether the diplomatic track is holding.
