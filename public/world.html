The Strait is the intersection of all the world's active crises: it is where the Iran war, the oil market, the nuclear negotiation, and the US's diplomatic bandwidth all converge in a single chokepoint over 24 hours.

US Central Command struck dozens of Iranian targets overnight in what officials described as "a series of powerful strikes" targeting the military infrastructure the IRGC used to attack three commercial vessels in the Strait of Hormuz on Tuesday — the Qatari LNG tanker Al Rekayat (fire in engine room, at risk of explosion), the Saudi-flagged supertanker Wedyan, and a third vessel. Both ships were damaged; Al Rekayat's fire risk made it the most dangerous single incident in the Strait since the initial ceasefire was reached. CENTCOM's strikes targeted air defense batteries, radar sites, anti-ship missile sites, and IRGC small boats — the precise operational infrastructure for another tanker attack. The US Treasury simultaneously revoked the sanctions waiver that had allowed Iran to export oil, reinstating the full economic pressure that had been partially suspended to facilitate the Doha diplomatic track.

The strategic logic is unmistakable: Iran attacked commercial vessels in the Strait, the US struck the military assets that enabled the attack, and the Treasury ended the economic concession that was the most tangible benefit Iran received from the ceasefire. From the US perspective, Iran broke the ceasefire first; the US response is calibrated to restore deterrence without escalating to the broader conflict the ceasefire was meant to end. From Iran's perspective — to whatever degree Mojtaba Khamenei's government has a coordinated perspective given his undisclosed location — the tanker attacks may represent a signal, a factional action, or a deliberate test of US resolve in the final days before the Mashhad burial that reshapes domestic Iranian politics. The body arrives in Mashhad July 9; the burial happens July 9; Doha resumes July 9. Everything converges in 24 hours.

The question the market, Doha negotiators, and US allies are all trying to answer is the same: was this authorized by Mojtaba Khamenei from his undisclosed location, or did IRGC commanders act outside the diplomatic track? If Mojtaba authorized it, Doha on July 9 has no mandate. Ali Khamenei's body transits Najaf, Iraq today — passing through the Shrine of Imam Hussein in Karbala before returning to Iran for burial at the Imam Reza shrine in Mashhad on July 9 — in a funeral procession that converges with the scheduled resumption of Doha nuclear talks on the same day, creating the most diplomatically compressed 24-hour window in the Iran crisis since February's assassination. Mojtaba Khamenei, who was reportedly wounded in that same attack that killed his father, mother, and wife, has not been seen publicly since February 28; his whereabouts remain unknown; and the mandate that Iran's chief nuclear negotiator Ghalibaf will carry to Doha on July 9 was authorized by a leader whose physical condition and current political authority cannot be independently verified by the US team, Qatari mediators, or any outside party.

The Hormuz tanker attacks overnight add a new layer of uncertainty to the Doha resumption: if the IRGC attacked commercial vessels without Mojtaba's authorization, it signals that Iran's internal command structure is fractured in ways that make the nuclear negotiation unpredictable regardless of what Ghalibaf agrees to at the table. If the attacks were authorized by Mojtaba, it signals that the diplomatic track is being abandoned from the top — and Doha on July 9 becomes a formality or a deception. Qatari mediators described "positive progress" from pre-funeral technical talks; that characterization was made before the tanker attacks. Whether Qatar revises its assessment before July 9 is the first indicator of whether the Doha session proceeds as a genuine negotiation or as a diplomatic holding action while the military exchange resolves.
