The Anchor

Trump rejects Iran's latest proposal — "can't agree to terms," threatens military action — as Lebanon's ceasefire disintegrates with IDF killing 30-plus in a day.

Iran's submission of a new peace proposal through Pakistan on Thursday evening — the latest in a series of proposals mediated by Islamabad since the ceasefire was established in early April — produced the most direct presidential rejection yet. Trump's Friday statement was unambiguous: "They want to make a deal, but I'm not satisfied with it." He described the Iranian terms as containing things he "can't agree to" and used his most forceful rhetorical threat of the conflict to date — "blast them away" — to characterize his response if negotiations fail entirely. Axios, citing officials familiar with the proposals, reported that the sticking points involve freedom of navigation through the Strait of Hormuz, Iran's nuclear enrichment program, and the conditions for sanctions relief — the three core issues that have made the Pakistan-mediated track difficult to conclude since the ceasefire began. Trump noted there is "tremendous discord" among Iran's leaders, complicating the process of obtaining a unified Iranian position on any concession; the absent supreme leader (Mojtaba Khamenei has not appeared publicly since the conflict's second week) is the specific institutional gap that Trump's "discord" observation references. Pakistan's role as mediator, which produced the original ceasefire and the current proposal track, was publicly acknowledged by both Trump and Iranian officials — one of the few points of shared diplomatic framing in an otherwise adversarial public discourse.

Lebanon's ceasefire collapsed in practical terms on Friday while remaining technically in force. Israeli strikes killed more than 30 people in southern Lebanon — the IDF's largest single-day casualty toll in the ceasefire period — prompting IDF Chief of Staff Lt. Gen. Eyal Zamir to declare publicly that "there is no ceasefire" in the south, citing continuous Hezbollah activity. Hezbollah, for its part, counts more than 500 alleged IDF violations of the ceasefire agreement and characterizes its own continued operations as "legitimate response to the enemy's persistent violations." The mutual-accusation dynamic has been present since the ceasefire took effect on April 16-17, but Friday's casualty toll elevates it from background attrition to acute escalation. The ceasefire, extended for three weeks by Trump's announcement in late April, expires May 14 — 12 days away — without any diplomatic framework for renewal and without any public indication that the underlying Hezbollah-Israel dynamic has changed enough to support a further extension. The combination of Trump's Iran deal rejection and Lebanon's accelerating ceasefire collapse on the same day is the most significant simultaneous diplomatic and military deterioration of the conflict since Day 1.

Trump · Iran Proposal · Rejected "Not Satisfied" Iran submits via Pakistan · Trump "can't agree to terms" · "blast them away" · Hormuz, nuclear, sanctions sticking points

Iran submits new peace proposal through Pakistan — Trump publicly rejects it: "can't agree to terms," threatens to "blast them away." The Hormuz, nuclear, and sanctions sticking points remain unresolved.

Iran's latest proposal — submitted to Pakistani mediators Thursday evening and transmitted to the US — represented a meaningful effort to advance the negotiation: Pakistani officials confirmed it as "an updated proposal" reflecting Iranian movement on at least some of the prior framework's contested terms. Trump's public Friday morning rejection, without entering substantive engagement with the proposal's specifics, is therefore a deliberate signal rather than a negotiating tactic: it is designed to communicate that the US is not close to a deal and that military pressure remains a live option. The reporting on the sticking points — by Axios and Al Jazeera — identifies three core issues: freedom of navigation through the Strait of Hormuz (Iran wants explicit US guarantees; the US wants Hormuz opening as a precondition for any agreement); Iran's nuclear enrichment program (the US is demanding significant rollback; Iran is demanding recognition of enrichment rights); and the conditions for sanctions relief (Iran wants front-loaded relief; the US wants behavior-conditioned phases). None of these three have moved substantively from the positions that existed at the ceasefire's establishment in April. Trump's "tremendous discord" observation about Iran's leadership is accurate and adds a specific constraint: without supreme leader authorization, no Iranian negotiator can make the core concessions the US is demanding. Mojtaba Khamenei's continued absence from public view means there is no supreme leader to authorize those concessions.

Lebanon · 30+ Killed Friday · Collapsing "No Ceasefire" IDF strikes kill 30+ Friday · deadliest ceasefire day · IDF chief "no ceasefire in south" · Hezbollah: 500+ violations · May 14

Lebanon's ceasefire is collapsing in practice — IDF strikes killed 30-plus in a single day on Friday, the deadliest ceasefire-period day yet. The IDF chief declared "there is no ceasefire" in the south. May 14 is 12 days away.

Lebanon's National News Agency reported that Israeli strikes on Friday killed more than 30 people across southern Lebanon — the highest single-day death toll since the ceasefire took effect on April 16-17. IDF Chief of Staff Lt. Gen. Eyal Zamir, visiting troops stationed in southern Lebanon, stated publicly and unambiguously: "There is no ceasefire" in the south. The IDF characterizes the continued operations as responses to Hezbollah's sustained attacks, which it says have not stopped since the ceasefire was signed. Hezbollah, in parallel, rejects allegations that it has violated the ceasefire, characterizing its operations as "legitimate responses to the enemy's persistent violations" — which it counts at more than 500 incidents. The mutual rejection of the ceasefire's validity while the ceasefire technically remains in force is the operational definition of a ceasefire collapse in slow motion. The US, which brokered the original ceasefire and its three-week extension in late April, has not publicly responded to Friday's death toll or the IDF chief's "no ceasefire" statement. The silence is itself a signal: the Trump administration is managing two simultaneous conflict tracks (Iran negotiations, Lebanon ceasefire) and has chosen to absorb the Lebanon deterioration rather than confront it while the Iran deal rejection is the primary diplomatic focus.

Pakistan · Mediator · Ceasefire Context Mediator Pakistan transmitted Iran's proposal · both sides acknowledge Pakistan's role · two-phase deal structure · Hormuz the core

Pakistan's mediation remains the only functioning diplomatic track — it arranged the original ceasefire, transmitted Iran's latest proposal, and is acknowledged by both sides. But the sticking points have not moved since April 8.

Pakistan's role as the mediator between the US and Iran — which produced the ceasefire on April 8 and has sustained the diplomatic track since — remains the conflict's only functioning diplomatic infrastructure. Pakistani officials confirmed they received Iran's latest proposal on Thursday evening and transmitted it to US counterparts. Both Trump (in his "blast them away" statement) and Iranian officials separately acknowledged Pakistan's central mediating role, which is itself a form of diplomatic signaling: both parties are keeping the Pakistan channel open even as the public postures are maximally adversarial. The original ceasefire that Pakistan arranged on April 8 was structured as a two-phase deal — a temporary ceasefire to allow negotiations, followed by a longer-term agreement addressing Hormuz, nuclear, and sanctions. The current negotiation is theoretically in the second phase, but the phase-one ceasefire's fragility (Iran's continued blockade of Hormuz, the Lebanon deterioration) is creating pressure on the phase-two negotiations before they have produced any substantive agreement. The "tremendous discord" Trump cited among Iran's leaders is Pakistan's most significant structural challenge as mediator: it is negotiating on behalf of the US with an Iranian leadership that may lack the internal authority to authorize the core concessions that a final deal requires. The supreme leader's continued public absence is the binding constraint on the diplomatic track, not the quality of Pakistan's mediation.

Lebanon · May 14 · 12 Days 12 Days May 14 Lebanon ceasefire expiry · no renewal framework · IDF: "no ceasefire" · US carriers in Hormuz · dual-theater risk

Lebanon's ceasefire expires May 14 — 12 days away — with no renewal framework, active IDF operations declared by the IDF chief himself, and US carriers committed to the Hormuz blockade unable to easily redeploy.

The Lebanon ceasefire's May 14 expiry is now the nearest hard calendar deadline in the entire conflict — and unlike the War Powers Resolution deadline (which had a legal argument available to the White House), the Lebanon ceasefire is a contractual military date with no equivalent clock-stopping mechanism. At Friday's trajectory — 30-plus killed in a single day, the IDF chief declaring "no ceasefire" — the May 14 date arrives not as a renewal opportunity but as the formalization of an already-collapsed arrangement. The strategic implication for the US is acute: the three-carrier formation committed to the Hormuz blockade cannot simultaneously address a full Lebanon re-escalation. If Lebanon moves from ceasefire-in-name-only to full active combat before May 14, the administration faces the dual-theater choice it has been trying to avoid: maintain the Hormuz blockade at full strength or redeploy resources to address Lebanon. The ceasefire's practical collapse while legally still "in force" is the worst-case scenario for the US's diplomatic leverage: it degrades the Lebanon situation without triggering the legal and resource implications of a formal conflict resumption. The administration's silence on Friday's death toll suggests it has not yet chosen how to respond to the May 14 deadline.

Iran · Internet Blackout · Day 64 64 Days 90M people offline · longest for nation over 50M · economic cost accumulating · information asymmetry continues

Iran's internet blackout enters Day 64 — 90 million people offline, the longest national internet shutdown for a country of this size in the modern era. Economic and information costs are compounding daily.

Iran's national internet blackout enters its 64th consecutive day on Saturday — 90 million people cut off from the global internet in what has become the longest sustained national internet shutdown for a country with a population over 50 million in the modern era of global connectivity. The comparison: Myanmar's post-coup restrictions in 2021 were partial and affected a smaller population; North Korea's isolation is structural rather than sudden. Iran's shutdown affects a country with approximately 80% pre-conflict internet penetration, a sophisticated user base that relied on international connectivity for banking, education, commerce, and communication, and an economy that had significant international digital exposure before the conflict. The economic cost of 64 days of complete isolation is not yet fully measured, but early estimates suggest monthly GDP impact in the range of 0.5-1.0% of annual output. The information-control dimension remains the Iranian government's primary motivation: 64 days without unfiltered information access means the Iranian public has had no independent view of the conflict's military progress, the blockade's economic impact, or the diplomatic developments that the regime is managing. Trump's "tremendous discord" observation about Iran's leadership may partially reflect the information-isolation's effects: leaders making decisions in an information environment they are also controlling face a specific distortion in their assessment of public sentiment and international pressure.

Global Food Crisis · Hormuz · UN 90% Drop Hormuz shipping -90% · global food price spike · fertilizer disruption · UN: 24 food-insecure countries at risk

Continuing: the Hormuz shipping collapse — down 90% since the conflict began — is driving a global food price shock, as fertilizer and grain shipments through the Strait remain disrupted. UN warns 24 countries face acute risk.

Shipping traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has declined by more than 90% since the conflict began on February 28, according to tracking data compiled in analysis of the economic ripple effects of the US-Iran war. The disruption is not limited to oil: the Strait handles approximately 20% of global liquefied natural gas (LNG) shipments and is a critical route for fertilizer exports from Gulf producers — particularly urea and ammonia from Qatar, which supplies approximately 15% of global urea trade. The fertilizer disruption is producing a global food price shock that is distinct from the oil-price channel: urea prices have risen approximately 40% since the conflict began, and the planting-season timing means that higher fertilizer costs are being absorbed by farmers making 2026 growing-season decisions right now, with the harvest impact arriving in Q3 and Q4 2026. UN Food and Agriculture Organization analysis identifies 24 countries with acute food-insecurity vulnerability to the current fertilizer and food-price shock — predominantly in sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and the Horn of Africa — where import dependence and fiscal constraints combine to create a direct line between the Hormuz disruption and food security outcomes. Trump's "blast them away" and deal rejection statements make a near-term Hormuz reopening less likely, not more — which extends the global food-price shock timeline into Q3 2026 and beyond.

Quick hits

  • Trump's "blast them away" statement is his most explicit military threat of the conflict's 64-day history, surpassing the "short and powerful strikes" CENTCOM-briefing signals of Day 63 in rhetorical directness. The escalation in language is deliberate and follows a pattern: the "couple of people know the talks status" diplomatic ambiguity (Day 63) has not produced Iranian movement, so the administration is moving to the next rhetorical pressure level. But the gap between "blast them away" rhetoric and operational military action remains wide: the US has a ceasefire in place, a Pakistan mediation track it acknowledges, and a Lebanon situation that would be made worse by resumed Iran strikes. The rhetoric is the negotiating leverage tool; the military option is real but not imminent on current evidence.
  • Iran's "tremendous discord" among leaders — Trump's observation, which Iranian domestic reporting partially corroborates — is the diplomatic constraint that matters most for the Pakistan mediation track. Pezeshkian, as Iran's elected president, can propose terms. He cannot authorize the core concessions on Hormuz access and nuclear enrichment without supreme leader approval. Mojtaba Khamenei's 64-day absence from public view means either that the supreme leader is incapacitated (in which case the institutional authority for major concessions is unclear), institutionally delegating (in which case to whom and with what scope is unclear), or strategically absent (maintaining plausible deniability for whatever terms are eventually agreed). All three scenarios create the same negotiating dynamic: Iran's proposals cannot include the supreme-leader-level concessions the US needs, because there is no functioning supreme leader to authorize them.
  • Lebanon's ceasefire collapsing in practice while technically remaining in force creates a specific legal and political exposure for the US. The Trump administration brokered the ceasefire and its three-week extension; it is therefore the guarantor of the arrangement. The IDF chief's public declaration that "there is no ceasefire" in the south — while the ceasefire is technically still the legal framework in place — puts the US in the position of either enforcing the ceasefire it brokered (which means confronting Israel) or tacitly accepting its collapse (which means absorbing the diplomatic cost of having arranged an agreement that neither party honored). The silence from the White House on Friday's death toll is the operational expression of the second option. The May 14 deadline will force a choice: formally renew (and enforce) or let it expire.
  • The Hormuz 90% shipping collapse is producing a global food crisis that is running ahead of the diplomatic timeline at which it might be addressed. The 2026 planting season decisions being made right now — based on fertilizer prices that are 40% above pre-conflict levels — will determine harvest volumes in Q3 and Q4 2026. By the time any Iran deal resolves the Hormuz disruption (which may be months away at current negotiating pace), the harvest decisions will have already been made under high-cost conditions. The food crisis is therefore partially independent of the conflict's diplomatic resolution: even if Hormuz reopens in June, the fertilizer price shock absorbed in March-May 2026 is already embedded in 2026 harvest costs and food price trajectories through the year's end.
  • Trump's CBS News interview line — "I'm not satisfied" — was matched in the same week by his CBS News interlocutor's question about the 64-day internet blackout affecting 90 million Iranian civilians. Trump's characterization of the Iran leadership as having "tremendous discord" reflects the actual situation, but it also reveals a gap in the administration's diplomatic theory of change: if the goal is to pressure Iranian leaders into making concessions, and the mechanism is economic and military pressure, but the leaders are in "tremendous discord" and cannot collectively authorize concessions, then adding pressure may not produce agreement — it may produce internal fragmentation that makes agreement even harder to reach. The Pakistan mediation track is designed to work around this by creating a structured framework within which even a fractured Iranian leadership can signal agreement incrementally. Trump's public rejection before engaging substantively with the proposal's specifics undermined that incremental approach.
  • The global food price shock from the Hormuz fertilizer disruption has a specific political dimension in countries that are also US allies: India, the world's largest importer of fertilizers and a country that depends heavily on Gulf urea, has been managing a direct economic cost from the US-Iran conflict that it had no role in generating. India's diplomatic navigation — maintaining relations with both the US and Iran, purchasing discounted Iranian oil through indirect channels, and hosting Pakistani mediation discussions — reflects a country managing the simultaneous pressure of being a US partner and facing a US-originated food price shock. The India-Pakistan-Iran diplomatic triangle is one of the most complex and underreported dimensions of the conflict's regional impact.
