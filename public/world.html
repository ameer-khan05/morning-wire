Both sides are simultaneously signaling maximum constraint — which is the most dangerous diplomatic configuration for the 45 days remaining.

Iran Parliament Speaker Qalibaf: "Not Negotiating At All" · Won't Enter Talks Until US Implements MOU · Hardliner Pressure · Domestic Political Management Qalibaf Qalibaf: parliament speaker + Iran's chief negotiator · statement: "currently not negotiating with the United States at all" · condition: Iran won't enter further negotiations until US implements all MOU clauses · domestic context: IRGC-aligned hardliners opposed MOU from Jun 17 signing · "negotiation" framing = politically costly · technical talks in Doha: continue (Qatar + Pakistan mediators) · dual-track: public rejection (domestic) + technical compliance (US/mediators) · oil: WTI ▼1.70% on Hormuz murkiness

Iran's parliamentary speaker and chief negotiator Mohammad Baqer Qalibaf declared Thursday that Tehran "is currently not negotiating with the United States at all" and that Iran would not enter further negotiations until Washington implements all clauses of the June 17 Islamabad Memorandum — the sharpest public repudiation of the negotiation framing by a senior Iranian official since the MOU was signed. Qalibaf's statement requires parsing against the continued reality of indirect technical talks between US and Iranian delegations in Doha, which RFE/RL reported remain active under Qatari and Pakistani mediation. The two positions are not contradictory: Qalibaf's "not negotiating" declaration targets a domestic audience — IRGC-aligned hardliners who read US-Iran engagement as a concession and who require public distance from the "negotiation" framing that Western media applies to Doha contacts — while the technical talks in Doha address US and mediator requirements for visible MOU implementation progress.

The domestic political pressure on Iran's executive to publicly reject the negotiation framing is real and has intensified since the MOU signing. The IRGC, which has demonstrated operational independence from the foreign ministry's diplomatic commitments in previous ceasefire periods, has publicly questioned the MOU's terms. Iranian hardliner media coverage has characterized the ceasefire as a concession extracted under military pressure rather than a diplomatic achievement. Qalibaf — who straddles the parliament speaker and chief negotiator roles — is managing this domestic narrative by publicly denying negotiations exist while the technical-level work continues through indirect channels. The question is how long the dual-track can be sustained: as the MOU's August 17 deadline approaches, the US will need formal senior-level engagement to reach the final deal framework that the June 21 road map envisions, and Qalibaf's "not negotiating" posture leaves little visible space for that escalation without a domestic political cost in Tehran.

VP Vance: US Prepared to Use Force Against Iran if Diplomacy Fails · Clearest Force Signal Since Ceasefire · MOU Deadline Credibility · Stakes Around Aug 17 Vance VP JD Vance: White House prepared to use force against Iran if diplomacy fails · clearest US force signal since Jun 17 ceasefire · raises stakes around 60-day MOU window expiring Aug 17 · context: MOU halted open hostilities but core disputes unresolved · nuclear enrichment, Hormuz governance, sanctions not resolved · prior Vance statement (CBS News): four objectives achieved — nuclear inspectors, Hormuz mechanism, Lebanon deconfliction, future negotiations framework · current: diplomacy stalling → force signal re-introduced

Vice President JD Vance signaled Thursday that the White House is prepared to use force against Iran if diplomacy fails — the clearest US force threat since the June 17 ceasefire — raising the stakes around the 60-day MOU window that expires August 17 and injecting a deadline-credibility dimension that the administration had largely left ambiguous through the ceasefire's first two weeks. CBS News had earlier reported Vance describing four objectives achieved in the negotiations: allowing nuclear inspectors to resume, building a mechanism to open the Strait of Hormuz, establishing a regional ceasefire deconfliction framework for Lebanon, and creating a process for future negotiations. But with Qalibaf declaring Iran "not negotiating at all" and core disputes remaining unresolved on Day 15, Vance's force signal reasserts that the MOU is a time-bounded window with consequences, not a permanent status quo.

Vance's force signal serves a specific diplomatic function: it complicates Iran's ability to run out the 60-day clock through technical compliance without senior-level engagement on the core issues. If the MOU window expires on August 17 with no formal progress on nuclear enrichment, Hormuz governance, and sanctions relief, the US faces a choice between accepting a failed ceasefire (returning to the pre-MOU confrontation environment) or using force to re-establish leverage. By publicly stating the US is prepared for the latter, Vance is reducing Iran's incentive to treat August 17 as a neutral deadline with no downside. The signal is also calibrated to the nuclear inspections issue: Vance's CBS statement credited the MOU with allowing nuclear inspectors to resume, and Iran's continued compliance with inspections is one of the measurable MOU deliverables that the US can point to as progress — but inspections compliance alone is insufficient if enrichment levels and HEU stockpile status remain unaddressed by August 17.

Doha Technical Talks Continue · Qatar + Pakistan Mediators · Tentative June 21 Road Map: Nuclear Inspectors + Hormuz Mechanism + Lebanon + Future Negotiations Road Map Doha: indirect technical talks continue · Qatar + Pakistan serving as mediators · separate from Qalibaf's "not negotiating" public statement (dual-track) · Jun 21 road map: US + Iran agreed tentative framework for final deal · included: nuclear inspectors resuming · Hormuz mechanism · Lebanon-Israel deconfliction · future negotiations structure · NPR: mediators confirmed road map · unresolved: nuclear enrichment levels, HEU stockpile status, formal Hormuz governance, sanctions relief · 45 days to translate road map into binding agreement

Indirect technical talks between US and Iranian delegations continued Thursday in Doha, with Qatar and Pakistan serving as mediators — the same channel that has carried the implementation track of the Islamabad Memorandum since June 17 despite Qalibaf's public declaration that Tehran is "not negotiating at all." NPR reported in late June that US and Iranian negotiators had reached a tentative agreement on a "road map" for a final deal, with mediators confirming the framework included four elements: resumption of nuclear inspector access; a mechanism to open the Strait of Hormuz; a Lebanon-Israel ceasefire deconfliction framework; and a structured process for future negotiations on the core unresolved issues. Vance described the same achievements in a CBS News interview, characterizing them as the four objectives accomplished in the ceasefire window to date.

The road map's existence is significant precisely because it is tentative and partial. A road map defines the destination (a final deal) and the agenda (nuclear, Hormuz, Lebanon, future talks structure) without resolving any of the substantive disputes within those categories. Iran and the US have agreed to work toward a framework on nuclear enrichment and HEU stockpiles — but they have not agreed on what that framework says. They have agreed to build a Hormuz mechanism — but they have not agreed on what role Iran plays in it, the central dispute that Oman's proposal is trying to bridge. The road map's value is that both sides have formally acknowledged the four categories require resolution; its limitation is that the resolution of each category involves concessions neither side has yet agreed to make. The 45 days remaining to August 17 are the window for converting the road map's agenda into binding commitments — and Vance's force signal and Qalibaf's "not negotiating" statement are the constraints on both sides' ability to make those conversions without domestic political cost.

Quick hits

  • The most important unresolved issue in the Doha technical talks is nuclear enrichment — specifically, the level at which Iran's uranium enrichment will be capped under any final deal and what happens to the highly enriched uranium stockpile Iran accumulated during the conflict period. Iran currently enriches uranium to up to 60% purity; the 2015 JCPOA limited enrichment to 3.67%. The gap between those levels is the core nuclear dispute, and neither the MOU's text nor the June 21 road map specifies where the agreed enrichment cap will sit. A final deal that allows significantly higher enrichment than the JCPOA will face serious US congressional and Israeli opposition; a deal that requires a return to JCPOA levels will face IRGC opposition in Tehran. The nuclear enrichment question may be the single issue that determines whether the August 17 window produces a deal or a failed ceasefire.
  • Qalibaf's "not negotiating" statement introduces a procedural trap for the administration. The US position is that indirect technical talks in Doha constitute implementation of the MOU — which is what the MOU commits both sides to doing. If Iran's chief negotiator publicly declares there are no negotiations, the administration faces a framing problem: it is either conducting negotiations Iran says don't exist (making it look like the US is the one demanding engagement), or it accepts Qalibaf's framing and has nothing to show for 15 days of MOU implementation. The administration's best response is to emphasize measurable MOU deliverables — nuclear inspector access, Hormuz shipping — rather than arguing about whether "technical talks" constitute "negotiations." Vance's force signal is calibrated to this framing: it asserts consequences regardless of what Qalibaf calls the current process.
