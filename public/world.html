The stand-down's announcement Monday morning is the clearest signal since June 17 that both governments still want the MOU to survive. Trump's framing — announcing the Qatar meeting after Tehran "requested talks" — gives the US political credit for responding to an Iranian diplomatic overture rather than escalating further. Iran's parallel announcement that $6 billion in frozen Qatar accounts is being released signals that the sanctions-relief economic mechanism is still flowing, giving Iran's government a concrete deliverable to point to even as the IRGC's recent behavior has complicated the diplomatic picture. The ceasefire negotiations' relocation from Switzerland to Doha — with maritime security first on the agenda before nuclear enrichment and inspections — reflects the practical reality that the Hormuz question is now the active crisis and must be addressed before the working groups can return to the nuclear agenda.

The Iranian cyber surge tells a parallel story. A senior Israeli security official reported Monday that Iranian cyberattacks targeting Israel have reached approximately 4,800 hostile cyber incidents in June — compared to roughly 1,600 in June 2025 and roughly 1,600 in a typical month before the MOU — a tripling in attack volume that coincides precisely with the ceasefire period. The pattern is consistent with Iran redirecting aggressive activity from kinetic operations (which are visible and attributable and risk the MOU) to cyber operations (which are less visible, more deniable, and do not directly threaten the Hormuz economic mechanism). If that interpretation is correct, the stand-down holds the Hormuz front while Iran maintains pressure on Israel through a channel that the MOU does not explicitly address. The 48 days remaining to the August 17 deadline must now navigate enrichment, inspections, Lebanon, Hormuz maritime security, and an undeclared cyber front against Israel — simultaneously.

The United States and Iran agreed to a mutual stand-down Monday morning, ending the retaliatory exchange cycle that had run from Friday's IRGC drone strike on a Hormuz cargo ship through US CENTCOM retaliatory strikes Friday afternoon and the IRGC's counter-strikes against US-linked targets in the early hours of Sunday. Trump announced a meeting in Qatar after Tehran "requested talks," with both sides characterizing the halt to attacks as a precondition for returning to the negotiating table. Ceasefire sessions previously scheduled in Switzerland — where four working groups have been addressing Iran's nuclear enrichment gap, IAEA inspector access, Lebanon withdrawal, and reconstruction — have been relocated to Doha, Qatar, with the June 30 session focused first on maritime security and the Hormuz situation before returning to the nuclear agenda. RFE/RL reported Monday that "both sides will stand down for now" ahead of the technical talks.

The stand-down announcement arrives after Trump had threatened over the weekend to "complete the job" — language that echoes the pre-MOU bombing campaign justification and that, if acted on, would have been the MOU's most direct public repudiation by the US side. The decision to characterize Tehran's outreach as a request for talks rather than a capitulation under threat reflects a diplomatic framing choice: the US can present itself as responding to an Iranian overture, while Iran can present Tuesday's Doha session as a dialogue it sought rather than terms it accepted under duress. Both characterizations are partially true. The test of the stand-down is not the framing but the IRGC's behavior in the 24 hours before and during the Doha session — whether the Guards' operational commands treat the government's stand-down agreement as binding on their Hormuz units.

A US official confirmed Monday that trade vessels can "move freely" in the Strait of Hormuz, restoring the shipping security assurance that the IRGC's drone attack on a Singapore-flagged cargo ship on Friday had directly threatened. The Hormuz reopening is not simply a diplomatic signal — it is the MOU's economic mechanism restored: the 60-day sanctions waiver allowing Iranian crude to flow through the strait, and international shipping to move without IRGC interference, is the deal's primary commercial deliverable for both Iran (crude revenue) and the US (disinflationary oil prices). WTI at $70.18 and Brent at $73.80 reflect a market that now treats the supply disruption risk as temporarily contained rather than ongoing. Iran's president simultaneously announced that $6 billion of Iran's $12 billion in frozen assets held in Qatari accounts has been released and is returning to Iran — a partial sanctions relief flow that provides Tehran with a tangible MOU deliverable even as the Hormuz stand-down has required Iran to publicly restrain its IRGC from further attacks.

The $6 billion release figure — half of the $12 billion total in Qatar — suggests the sanctions-relief mechanism is moving in installments rather than as a lump sum, consistent with a framework that maintains US leverage over subsequent releases as Iran fulfills its ceasefire commitments. Iran's position on Hormuz "control" remained contested even after the stand-down announcement: Al Jazeera reported Monday that Tehran continues to insist on its right to control Hormuz passage, a position that contradicts the US characterization of the strait as an international waterway under free navigation rights. The contrast between Iran's public position and the practical reality of ships moving freely through the strait — confirmed by the US official — reflects the dual-track nature of the MOU: practical compliance with the economic mechanism running alongside political rhetoric that each side uses for domestic audiences. A US-Iran maritime hotline is expected to be established to coordinate commercial shipping traffic, reducing the risk of another incident stemming from miscommunication between IRGC naval units and passing commercial vessels.

Iranian Cyberattacks on Israel Surge 3× · 4,800 Incidents in June vs 1,600 Last Year · Kinetic → Cyber Redirection 4,800 Senior Israeli official Jun 29: 4,800 Iranian cyber incidents this month vs 1,600 last June (3×) · surge coincides with MOU period (Jun 17–present) · pattern: IRGC redirecting from kinetic (risks MOU) to cyber (deniable, not covered by MOU text) · MOU has no working group for cyber operations against Israel

A senior Israeli security official announced Monday that Iranian cyberattacks targeting Israel have reached approximately 4,800 hostile cyber incidents in June — compared to roughly 1,600 in June 2025, a tripling of attack volume in the 12 months since, and a figure that has surged precisely during the MOU's first 12 days. The 3× increase in Iranian cyber activity against Israel coinciding with the ceasefire framework's active period is consistent with a deliberate IRGC strategy of redirecting aggressive activity from kinetic Hormuz operations — which are visible, attributable, and directly threaten the MOU's economic mechanism — to cyber operations, which are more deniable and are not explicitly addressed in the MOU's text. The June 28–29 IRGC drone and missile attacks on Hormuz and US-linked targets demonstrated that the IRGC is willing to conduct kinetic operations that risk the MOU when it judges the provocation sufficient; the cyber surge suggests the Guards are maintaining pressure on Israel through a channel they assess as below the MOU's threshold.

The cyber surge creates a complication for the Doha talks that the stand-down announcement does not resolve. The MOU framework's working groups address enrichment, nuclear inspections, Lebanon military withdrawal, and now Hormuz maritime security — there is no working group for Iranian cyberattacks on Israel, and no mechanism in the current framework to address a 3× surge in hostile cyber activity that falls below the kinetic threshold while the Hormuz stand-down holds. Israel's response options are constrained by the same MOU framework that it has publicly supported: Israeli kinetic retaliation against Iran for cyberattacks would threaten the ceasefire that the US is actively managing and that Israeli officials have acknowledged serves Israeli security interests in the Lebanon clause. Watch whether the Israeli security establishment raises the cyber surge at the Doha session — or through separate channels with US counterparts — as a test of whether the MOU can address non-kinetic hostile activity without a dedicated working group mechanism.
