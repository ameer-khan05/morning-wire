The most significant development in the Iran nuclear track this week is not the funeral pause — it is Vice President Vance's confirmation that IAEA nuclear inspectors have resumed access to Iran's facilities. The resumption of inspector access was one of the four deliverables that Vance described as achieved through the MOU process: nuclear inspectors resuming, a mechanism to open the Strait of Hormuz, a Lebanon-Israel ceasefire deconfliction framework, and a process for future negotiations. Confirming that the first of those deliverables — inspector access — is operational provides the administration with concrete, verifiable evidence that the MOU framework is producing results on the nuclear track even as the senior-level diplomatic engagement has been publicly denied by Iran's parliamentary speaker Qalibaf and interrupted by the funeral.

The funeral pause runs July 4–9, encompassing the Independence Day holiday on the American side as well. It is a natural diplomatic gap that neither side needs to explain or justify. When talks resume after July 9, the landscape will be defined by the same core issues that have not moved in 16 days of MOU implementation: nuclear enrichment levels (Iran currently at 60% purity versus the 3.67% the 2015 JCPOA set), the Hormuz governance mechanism (whose specific terms remain undefined), and sanctions relief (which the US has not indicated it will grant beyond the temporary oil sanctions pause). With 39 days remaining to August 17, the pace of progress on those issues needs to accelerate significantly from what the first 16 days produced. The funeral pause, while natural, is 9 days of the remaining 39 — and each day that passes without substantive movement on the core issues increases the probability that the window expires without a binding agreement.

Iran's nuclear diplomacy in Doha has been suspended ahead of a multi-day state funeral for Supreme Leader Ayatollah Khamenei — killed in US-Israeli strikes in late February 2026 — with Qatari and Pakistani mediators adjourning the indirect technical talks through at least July 9. CNN reported that the next round of negotiations is scheduled for "the earliest possible time" after funeral processions planned for July 4 through July 9. The pause arrives on MOU Day 16, with 39 days remaining to the August 17 window that the 60-day Islamabad Memorandum established for resolving the core disputes between Washington and Tehran on nuclear, Hormuz, and sanctions issues.

The week's most substantive Iran nuclear development came before the funeral pause was announced: Vice President Vance confirmed in PBS NewsHour reporting that IAEA nuclear inspectors have resumed access to Iran's facilities under the MOU framework — the first of the four deliverables Vance has described as achieved through the ceasefire window (alongside a Hormuz mechanism, Lebanon deconfliction framework, and future negotiations process). Inspector access resumption is measurable and verifiable in a way that the other three deliverables are not: the IAEA's Department of Safeguards has established procedures and reporting timelines, and the resumption of inspections means the agency is collecting data on Iran's nuclear activities that was unavailable during the conflict period. What the inspectors find regarding enrichment levels and HEU stockpile status is the most consequential nuclear intelligence the administration will receive before the August 17 deadline — and that data will define how much pressure both sides face to reach a binding agreement before the window expires.

DRC + Uganda Ebola: 1,406 Confirmed Cases · 438 Deaths · Third-Largest Bundibugyo Outbreak on Record · No Approved Vaccine or Treatment · WHO Situation Report 9 Ebola 2026 Ebola outbreak: Bundibugyo virus · DRC (primary) + Uganda (secondary) · 1,406 confirmed cases + 438 deaths as of Jul 2 · third-largest Bundibugyo outbreak on record · declared outbreaks: both countries May 15, 2026 · Bundibugyo: no approved vaccine (unlike Zaire strain, which has rVSV-ZEBOV) · no specific treatment · WHO Situation Report 9: Jul 1, 2026 · case fatality rate: ~31% based on reported cases · health system context: DRC eastern provinces historically conflict-affected · response: WHO + CDC active · vaccine candidates: in development, testing phase

The 2026 Ebola disease outbreak caused by the Bundibugyo virus in the Democratic Republic of Congo and Uganda reached 1,406 confirmed cases and 438 deaths as of July 2, according to WHO Situation Report 9 released July 1 — making this outbreak the third-largest involving the Bundibugyo strain on record. Both countries declared outbreaks on May 15, 2026, after laboratory confirmation of Bundibugyo virus infection, a strain that is distinctly different from the more commonly discussed Zaire Ebola strain and for which no approved vaccine or specific antiviral treatment currently exists. The Zaire strain has the rVSV-ZEBOV (Ervebo) vaccine, which was critical to controlling the 2018–2020 DRC outbreak; the Bundibugyo strain has no equivalent approved protective measure, which substantially complicates the response.

The DRC's eastern provinces, where the current outbreak is centered, have experienced chronic instability from armed group activity that has historically complicated Ebola outbreak response in the same region — access to affected communities for contact tracing, vaccination (where experimental candidates are available), and case isolation is harder when humanitarian access is contested by armed actors. WHO and CDC are active in the response; vaccine candidates for the Bundibugyo strain are in development and testing, but the absence of an approved vaccine means the current response relies on traditional outbreak control measures: case isolation, contact tracing, infection prevention and control in health facilities, and safe burial practices. At 1,406 confirmed cases, the outbreak is the third-largest Bundibugyo event on record, approaching the scale at which external resources and international emergency response declarations typically become the decisive factor in outbreak trajectory. The 31% case fatality rate based on reported cases — 438 deaths from 1,406 confirmed cases — reflects the severity of the Bundibugyo strain when specific treatment is unavailable.

Disappointing economic results under Colombia's left-leaning government have created meaningful momentum for right-wing candidates ahead of the next election cycle, according to Deutsche Bank Research's July geopolitical calendar — part of a broader regional pattern across Latin America in which the left-of-center governments that swept into power between 2021 and 2024 are confronting voter backlash driven primarily by economic underperformance rather than ideological realignment. The pattern mirrors post-left-boom corrections seen across the region's history: the same economic frustrations that drove voters toward left-of-center candidates — inequality, commodity price volatility, institutional weakness — are now driving them toward alternatives as the incumbents struggle to deliver structural change within the constraints of global economic conditions they did not cause but cannot escape.

In Southeast Asia, Myanmar is re-emerging as an arena of geopolitical competition between China and India, with dueling state visits from both powers revealing how the country's military government is managing competing relationships. Myanmar's junta has alternated between Beijing and New Delhi for infrastructure investment, energy deals, and diplomatic cover — a positioning strategy that extracts maximum concessions from both without committing to either. For India, deeper engagement with Myanmar is driven by the need to secure overland trade and energy connectivity to Southeast Asia without routing through China-controlled corridors. For China, Myanmar is a critical link in the Belt and Road Initiative's western corridor and a strategic buffer against Indian influence on its southern flank. The competition for Myanmar's alignment is intensifying at a moment when the junta's domestic position — managing ethnic armed organization challenges and an ongoing civil war — makes external engagement with either power a source of economic and military support that neither side can afford to see captured exclusively by the other.

Quick hits

  • The DRC Ebola outbreak's trajectory — 1,406 confirmed cases in 48 days since May 15 — represents an average of roughly 29 new confirmed cases per day. For comparison, the 2018–2020 DRC Zaire outbreak that became the second-largest on record peaked at roughly 25 new cases per day before the rVSV-ZEBOV vaccine's expanded use began breaking transmission chains. The Bundibugyo outbreak's daily case count is running slightly above that peak pace without an equivalent vaccine, which is the primary reason it is the third-largest Bundibugyo event on record at 48 days. If the response fails to break transmission chains through the traditional non-vaccine measures — contact tracing, case isolation, safe burials — the case count trajectory suggests the fourth-largest title is achievable before a vaccine candidate could complete emergency authorization and deployment. The absence of a Bundibugyo vaccine is not a knowledge gap that the response can substitute for; it is a structural vulnerability in the Ebola outbreak response architecture for this specific strain.
  • Colombia's rightward political shift matters for global markets primarily through its oil sector implications. Colombia is a significant oil producer and exporter; left-of-center governments in the region have historically imposed more restrictive conditions on foreign oil investment, nationalization risk premiums, and export tax structures. A right-of-center successor government would likely reverse or moderate those policies — reducing foreign investment risk premiums in Colombia's oil sector and potentially increasing production trajectory at a moment when OPEC+ is managing output for price support. The political cycle timeline determines when these policy changes materialize; the election cycle's pace, and who actually wins, will define whether Colombia's oil sector becomes a supply-positive or supply-neutral factor in H2 2026 energy markets.
