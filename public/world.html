The 52nd G7 summit in Évian-les-Bains concluded Wednesday with all seven leaders formally endorsing the US-Iran MOU — welcoming it as "a historic opportunity to prevent Iran from acquiring any nuclear weapon" — while calling for an immediate ceasefire in Lebanon and Hezbollah disarmament, as the formal Geneva signing remains confirmed for June 19 and Canada's Mark Carney declares the deal "a game changer."

The G7 endorsement in Évian is diplomatically significant beyond its symbolic value. The summit — held in Évian-les-Bains, on the French shore of Lake Geneva, across the water from Thursday's signing venue — was deliberately sequenced as the final multilateral endorsement before the treaty ceremony. By securing G7 backing in the hours before the Geneva signing, the deal gains formal support from every major Western democracy: the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, France, Germany, Italy, and Japan. That support converts the Iran MOU from a bilateral US-Iran arrangement that future administrations or Congresses might seek to reverse into a commitment endorsed by the G7's institutional framework — which includes treaty obligations, diplomatic relationships, and the European Hormuz mission that is now operationally backed by France, Germany, Britain, and Italy. The G7's Lebanon demand is the other significant element: calling explicitly for an "immediate, robust ceasefire" in Lebanon and Hezbollah disarmament — and for Lebanon's monopoly of arms and sovereignty — the G7 has now unified around a position on Lebanon that neither Israel nor Hezbollah has accepted. Whether that unified demand creates diplomatic leverage on Israel's Lebanon position in the 60-day nuclear negotiating window is the near-term diplomatic question. The 60-day clock begins at the Geneva signing on Thursday; everything that happens today — the G7 statement, the Fed decision, market movements, Lebanon's posture — is context for what will be one of the most consequential 60-day diplomatic negotiations in recent history.

The Anchor

The G7 backed the deal. The G7 demanded Lebanon ceasefire. Both happened in the same statement. The deal is now multilateral. The Lebanon pressure is now G7-unified. Geneva is in two days. The 60-day clock is about to start.

The G7 joint statement from Évian-les-Bains is the most complete diplomatic framing of the Iran deal that has existed since Sunday's announcement. G7 leaders described the US-Iran agreement as a historic opportunity to prevent Iran from acquiring a nuclear weapon, pledging readiness to contribute to its implementation. Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney told CNN that the agreement is "a game changer," adding Canada's explicitly enthusiastic endorsement to the more formal language of the joint statement. The G7 statement also referenced the agreement's terms — the ceasefire, the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, financial relief for Iran, and Iran's reiteration that it will never produce a nuclear weapon — as the framework the G7 supports, signaling that the G7 will use its collective diplomatic weight to maintain the deal's momentum through the 60-day nuclear negotiating window.

The Lebanon demand in the G7 statement breaks new ground: the alliance has now formally and collectively called for Hezbollah disarmament. Previous Western statements on Lebanon had been national positions (the US and France separately calling for ceasefire) or soft diplomatic language in multilateral forums. The G7's joint statement represents a unified Western position that adds international pressure to the Lebanon-Hezbollah front as it enters the 60-day window. Israel has not responded to the G7 Lebanon demand and has maintained its position that it retains freedom of action against Hezbollah regardless of the US-Iran MOU. The question for the 60-day nuclear negotiations is whether Iran's negotiating team will make progress on Lebanon-Hezbollah a condition for nuclear progress — and whether the G7's unified Lebanon demand gives Western mediators a lever to move Israel's position that was not available in prior negotiations.

All seven G7 leaders issued a joint statement at the close of the 52nd summit in Évian-les-Bains, France, formally welcoming the US-Iran agreement as "a historic opportunity to prevent Iran from acquiring any nuclear weapon and tackling threats related to its regional and ballistic activities." The statement acknowledged the deal as having been "secured under the strong leadership of President Trump with the support of mediating countries" and pledged G7 readiness to contribute to its implementation. Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney, speaking to CNN on the sidelines of the summit, described the agreement as "a game changer" and expressed Canada's strong support for the deal's implementation. The summit was held in Évian-les-Bains on the French shore of Lake Geneva — across the water from the signing venue in Geneva where the MOU will be formally executed Thursday.

The G7's collective endorsement converts the Iran MOU from a US-bilateral arrangement into a deal with the formal backing of the world's seven largest democratic economies. The timing — the Évian summit concluding Wednesday, the Geneva signing Thursday — is a deliberate sequencing that gives the deal's durability a multilateral foundation before the 60-day nuclear negotiating clock begins. Each of the four European G7 members (France, Germany, Britain, and Italy) has also separately pledged naval assets to the Hormuz mine-clearance and escort mission, meaning that the G7 statement is backed by operational military commitments, not just diplomatic language. That combination — G7 statement plus operational European naval commitment — makes the deal's implementation infrastructure more durable than any purely bilateral US-Iran agreement could be.

The G7 joint statement from Évian included an explicit call for an "immediate, robust ceasefire" in Lebanon and expressed support for Lebanese leadership's efforts to achieve the disarmament of Hezbollah and the Lebanese government's monopoly on arms, along with Lebanon's territorial integrity, sovereignty, and "appropriate international security guarantees." The Lebanon clause is the most unified Western statement on Hezbollah disarmament since the Lebanon-Hezbollah conflict began and represents a G7-level diplomatic demand that goes beyond the language any individual Western government had previously put forward on the Lebanon question. Israel has not acknowledged or responded to the G7 Lebanon demand; Israeli officials have consistently maintained that the Lebanon-Hezbollah operations are a separate bilateral issue from the US-Iran MOU.

The Lebanon clause in the G7 statement sets up the most difficult diplomatic challenge in the 60-day nuclear window: Iranian negotiators are under domestic pressure to address the Lebanon-Hezbollah front as a condition for nuclear progress, while the G7 has now given that demand its collective imprimatur. Whether Western mediators can use the G7 Lebanon demand as a lever to move Israel's position — or whether Israel's refusal to engage on Lebanon remains a fixed constraint — will shape whether the 60-day nuclear talks can focus on enrichment and verification or become an expanded negotiation about regional military postures that is too wide to conclude in 60 days. The G7's Hezbollah disarmament demand and its Lebanon sovereignty language give Iranian negotiators a Western-backed argument for raising Lebanon's status in the nuclear talks without it appearing as a purely bilateral Iran demand.

The Geneva MOU signing is confirmed for Thursday, June 19 — two days from now — with US Vice President JD Vance and Iran's parliamentary speaker and chief negotiator Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf signed to represent their respective governments. The ceremony converts Sunday's Truth Social announcement and the subsequently confirmed MOU text into a formally signed sovereign treaty, providing the legal and diplomatic weight that the announcement alone did not carry. The G7 endorsement from Évian — concluded Wednesday, the day before the signing — creates the multilateral context for the ceremony: the formal signing arrives with seven major democracies having already declared their support and the European Hormuz mission operationally committed to the deal's implementation.

The 60-day nuclear negotiating window begins at the Geneva signing ceremony. Iran enters the window with three economic wins already secured in the signed MOU: sanctions relief on Iranian oil exports, frozen asset releases, and US naval withdrawal from Iranian port perimeters within 30 days. Those economic wins, already in the signed document, give Iran's negotiating team a stronger domestic political position than they had in any prior US-Iran nuclear negotiation — the IRGC and Iranian hardliners can point to tangible results already achieved, reducing the internal pressure to maximize demands in the nuclear talks that has historically derailed US-Iran agreements. The 60-day window must address uranium enrichment levels, IAEA verification arrangements, and Iran's enriched uranium stockpile — technically complex issues that took years to negotiate in the JCPOA context, but with a mutually reinforced incentive structure in 2026 that did not exist in 2015.

Israel-Lebanon: No Withdrawal · G7 Demand Unacknowledged · Hezbollah Axis Enters 60-Day Window Unresolved Israel: confirmed no withdrawal from Lebanon · IDF: retains full freedom of action vs. Hezbollah · Hezbollah: Iran's most significant regional proxy · G7: explicitly called for Hezbollah disarmament + Lebanon ceasefire · Israel: has not acknowledged G7 Lebanon demand · Iran domestic: IRGC + hardliners point to continued IDF-Hezbollah operations as argument Iran is conceding while allies are attacked · 60-day window pressure: Iran likely to push Lebanon-Hezbollah into nuclear talks · US position: Israel-Lebanon framework separate from US-Iran MOU · diplomatic challenge: decoupling nuclear talks from Lebanon in 60 days with G7 demand creating pressure on both sides

Israel confirmed Wednesday that it will not withdraw from Lebanon and will retain full freedom of action against Hezbollah — a position unchanged by the US-Iran MOU, the G7's Évian statement, or any other diplomatic development in the past week. Israeli defense officials maintained that Lebanon-Hezbollah operations are a separate bilateral matter from the US-Iran MOU framework, and that the G7's call for Hezbollah disarmament and Lebanese ceasefire, while noted, does not create an obligation for Israel to change its operational posture. IDF activity against Hezbollah positions in Lebanon continued through Tuesday.

Israel's refusal to engage on Lebanon is the most significant unresolved variable in the 60-day nuclear window's diplomatic architecture. Hezbollah is Iran's most strategically important regional proxy: Iran has invested decades and billions in Hezbollah's military capacity, and the ongoing Israeli campaign against Hezbollah in Lebanon is a pressure point that Iranian domestic politics cannot ignore during a negotiation in which Iran's leadership is already accepting constraints that some IRGC factions view as concessions. The G7's Lebanon demand — demanding Hezbollah disarmament, Lebanese sovereignty, and an immediate ceasefire — gives Iranian negotiators a Western-backed basis for raising Lebanon's status in the nuclear talks. Whether the 60-day window can conclude with nuclear terms while Lebanon remains unresolved is the core question that the CSIS, Al Jazeera, and diplomatic analysts have flagged as the deal's most fragile structural element.

Quick hits

  • The geographic symbolism of the G7 summit's location is not accidental. Évian-les-Bains sits on the French shore of Lake Geneva — close enough that French President Macron could, in principle, see the Swiss city across the water where VP Vance and Ghalibaf will sign Thursday. Hosting the summit immediately adjacent to the signing venue was Macron's diplomatic move: it created a natural news narrative in which G7 backing and formal treaty signing are presented as sequential steps in the same process, with France positioned as the convening power that facilitated both. France's role as G7 host and as a pledge participant in the European Hormuz mission gives Paris an unusually central position in the deal's multilateral architecture — one that Macron has been cultivating since the conflict began and that positions France as an indispensable interlocutor in the 60-day nuclear negotiations.
  • The G7 statement's language on Iran's nuclear program — "a historic opportunity to prevent Iran from acquiring any nuclear weapon" and "tackling threats related to its regional and ballistic activities" — is more comprehensive in scope than the US-Iran MOU's public terms. The MOU's confirmed elements cover the ceasefire, Hormuz reopening, sanctions relief, frozen assets, and naval withdrawal. The G7 statement frames all of those as part of a broader framework that includes Iran's regional role (proxies, ballistic missiles) and nuclear weapons prevention. By adopting this broader framing, the G7 is signaling that the 60-day nuclear window should address not just enrichment levels but the regional and ballistic dimensions that have historically been the hardest issues in US-Iran diplomacy. Whether the 60-day window can actually produce terms on ballistic missiles and regional proxy activity — in addition to enrichment and IAEA verification — is a question that the G7's language raises without answering.
  • WTI crude at $75.47 — its lowest since early March, down roughly $8.53 since the deal — is the most direct economic evidence of the Iran deal's global impact. For the world's largest oil importers — China, India, Japan, South Korea, and the EU — a sustained WTI in the $70s rather than the $80s represents significant macroeconomic benefit: lower import bills, lower inflation, higher household purchasing power, and monetary policy room to cut rates. The G7's collective backing of the deal is partly explained by this economic self-interest: every G7 government benefits from oil at $75 rather than $84, and every G7 government's central bank has more room to ease at lower oil prices. The G7 is not endorsing the US-Iran deal purely on security grounds; it is endorsing a deal that directly improves the macro environment in all seven member economies.
