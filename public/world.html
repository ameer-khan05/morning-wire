The Anchor

The 107-day US-Iran war ended Sunday night. The Strait of Hormuz reopens. The formal signing is June 19 in Geneva. What took 107 days was not winning the war — it was agreeing on the terms of not fighting it.

The sequence from Saturday to Sunday tells the story: Pakistan's PM said the final text was agreed and signing was expected within 24 hours. Trump announced signing would happen on his birthday. Iran said it wouldn't happen Sunday, citing US hesitancy. Qatar dispatched negotiators to Tehran overnight. And then, before Sunday midnight, Trump posted: "The deal with the Islamic Republic of Iran is now complete." The overnight breakthrough — after Iran's FM had publicly put responsibility on Washington — suggests the Qatar mediation addressed whatever sequencing or verification gap had prevented signing during daylight hours Sunday. Iran's deputy FM Gharibabadi confirmed the agreement immediately, removing any ambiguity about whether Iran had actually agreed or whether Trump had announced a deal Iran was still contesting.

The deal Trump announced is a framework: a 60-day ceasefire extension that commits both sides to end hostilities and open the Strait, with negotiations on the harder issues — sanctions relief, the scope of Iranian asset unfreeze, and Iran's nuclear program — to occur during that 60-day window. Trump has separately authorized the US naval blockade to end and mine-removal operations in the Strait of Hormuz to begin, both of which are operational steps that would be difficult to reverse once executed. The formal Geneva signing on June 19 — attended by VP Vance — converts the Truth Social announcement into a legally durable diplomatic instrument.

Iran's deputy foreign minister Kazem Gharibabadi confirmed the agreement within hours of the announcement. Qatar credited its partnership with Pakistan as the breakthrough mediation force that closed the final procedural gap after Iran's FM had said earlier Sunday that signing would not happen that day. The announcement came before midnight Sunday, setting up Monday's global market response.

The overnight breakthrough reversed what had appeared to be a stalemate through Sunday morning: Iran's FM had publicly attributed the delay to US hesitancy, Qatar had dispatched mediators to Tehran, and both sides had described the deal as close but not closed. The speed of the resolution — from "not Sunday" to "deal complete" in under 12 hours — suggests the gap was specific, addressable, and resolved by the Qatari intervention rather than requiring a fundamental renegotiation of deal terms. The formal signing ceremony at Geneva on June 19, with VP Vance as the US representative, converts the Truth Social announcement into a formal diplomatic instrument.

The deal Trump announced is a 60-day ceasefire framework: active hostilities end, the Strait of Hormuz begins mine removal and reopens for shipping after clearance, and both sides commit to 60 days of negotiations on the harder issues — broader Iranian sanctions relief and Iran's nuclear program — that the initial memorandum left for follow-on talks. The framework structure reflects the political reality that the fully negotiated settlement (sanctions, nuclear verification, regional proxy arrangements) is too complex to complete in the diplomatic sprint that produced the ceasefire announcement. The 60-day window converts the ceasefire into either a permanent settlement or a failed attempt.

The Hormuz reopening is the most immediately consequential operational element: mine removal takes weeks, after which global shipping through the Strait — approximately 20% of the world's traded oil — resumes normal transit. Iran's oil exports, currently subject to sanctions, will be addressed in the 60-day negotiations; a sanctions relief agreement would add Iranian production to global supply on top of the Hormuz reopening, providing additional downward pressure on oil prices and reinforcing the Fed cut path. The nuclear talks — which have been the core US-Iran tension for decades — are the 60-day window's hardest item and the most consequential for long-term regional stability.

World leaders broadly welcomed the Iran-US deal announcement as a step toward regional stability, with Al Jazeera reporting global relief at the news. The European Union and China expressed support for the Hormuz reopening, which restores the international shipping lane that carries approximately 20% of global oil trade. The most significant dissent came from Israel, which announced it will not withdraw from Lebanon despite the broader deal — a position that maintains the Israel-Hezbollah conflict axis as an independent, active flashpoint separate from the US-Iran ceasefire framework the deal establishes.

Israel's Lebanon position is diplomatically significant: the US-Iran deal does not directly govern Israel's actions against Hezbollah, and Israel has not been a formal party to the 107-day US-Iran conflict. But Hezbollah is Iran's most significant regional proxy, and Israeli strikes on Hezbollah — which will continue per Israel's announcement — create ongoing provocations that Iranian hardliners can use to argue that Iran is being asked to stand down while its allies are attacked. The 60-day negotiation window will need to address the Israel-Lebanon-Hezbollah dynamic, or it risks the regional stability the deal is designed to create.

The June 19 Geneva signing ceremony — attended by VP Vance for the United States and Iranian officials — converts Sunday's Truth Social announcement into a formal diplomatic instrument that legally commits both parties to the 60-day ceasefire framework. The ceremony matters beyond symbolism: sovereign treaty commitments made by a vice president before international witnesses carry political and legal weight that makes them significantly harder to unilaterally reverse than a presidential social media post. The Geneva signing is also the event that formally starts the 60-day negotiating clock on sanctions relief and Iran's nuclear program.

The 60-day window is where the deal's durability is determined. A successful negotiation of sanctions relief and nuclear terms — addressing Iran's uranium enrichment level, verification mechanisms, and the sequencing of asset unfreezes — produces a comprehensive settlement that permanently resolves the conflict's underlying causes. A breakdown in the 60-day talks — whether over nuclear red lines, sanctions sequencing, or the Lebanon-Israel complication — risks the deal collapsing into a temporary pause rather than a lasting resolution. The history of US-Iran diplomacy suggests that 60-day windows have failed as often as they have succeeded; the difference this time is that Hormuz mine removal is already operationally underway, making full reversal economically and physically costly for both sides.

Quick hits

  • The Qatar mediation success — closing the final gap in 12 hours after Iran's FM had publicly put the hold on Washington — validates Qatar's unusual diplomatic position: it hosts US CENTCOM and maintains formal diplomatic relations with Tehran simultaneously, giving it credibility on both sides that no other single country possesses. Qatar's record on US-Iran intermediation (prisoner releases, sanctions exemptions, nuclear side channels) means it was the natural closer once Pakistan's substantive drafting had been completed. The Qatar-Pakistan division of labor — Pakistan for text, Qatar for closure — is the most effective dual-mediation structure in recent Middle East diplomacy.
  • The deal's "60-day framework" structure is a deliberate staging of the hardest problems rather than a comprehensive settlement. The Trump administration's calculation: the Iran war has economic costs (Hormuz, oil prices, inflation) that must end now, and the nuclear/sanctions questions can be addressed over 60 days with the war off. The risk is that 60 days is insufficient for issues that have resisted resolution for decades. The precedent of the 2015 JCPOA — which took years to negotiate and then failed politically — suggests the optimism embedded in a 60-day nuclear negotiation window. The difference is that this time, the economic pain of failure (re-activating Hormuz) is immediate and mutual.
  • Israel's refusal to withdraw from Lebanon is the most consequential unresolved variable in the regional architecture the Iran-US deal creates. The US-Iran deal does not require Israeli withdrawal from Lebanon and does not directly govern Israeli operations against Hezbollah — which Israel has treated as a separate bilateral conflict throughout the 107-day US-Iran war. But the 60-day negotiation window will face pressure from Iran to address the Lebanon-Hezbollah dimension, which Iran's domestic hardliners will use to argue that Iran is making concessions while its most important regional ally is simultaneously under military pressure. Whether the 60-day talks can decouple Iran's nuclear deal from the Israel-Lebanon conflict is the diplomatic challenge that determines whether Day 107 was the beginning of a durable regional settlement or merely the end of one phase of a longer conflict.
