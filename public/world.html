The Anchor

The Lebanon truce gets three more weeks. Tehran escalates in the Strait.

President Trump announced Thursday that Israel and Lebanon agreed to extend their ceasefire by three weeks after a White House Oval Office meeting that brought together Israeli and Lebanese officials in the same room with the US President for the first time — a diplomatic milestone in itself. Trump personally hosted the delegations and afterward said on Truth Social: "The Meeting went very well!" He said he looks forward to hosting Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu and Lebanese President Joseph Aoun at the White House "in the near future," hoping both leaders can visit during the three-week window. Trump expressed optimism there is a "great chance" for a lasting peace deal between Israel and Lebanon — a more ambitious framing than the initial ceasefire brokered April 17 had suggested.

The diplomacy on the Lebanon front stands in sharp contrast to what is happening in the Strait of Hormuz. Iran's Revolutionary Guard seized two commercial vessels — the MSC Francesca and the Epaminondas — claiming they "had endangered maritime security by operating without the required authorization and by tampering with navigation systems." A third container ship was approached by an Iranian gunboat that opened fire, causing "heavy damage" to the bridge; all crew were reported safe. The Iranian government characterized the seizures as a response to the continued US naval blockade, which Tehran still considers a violation of the ceasefire. Washington rejects that framing. The US–Iran diplomatic track remains empty: no talks are scheduled, no mediator is conducting active shuttle diplomacy. Saudi Arabia's envoy remains in Tehran as the only active face-to-face diplomatic contact.

Israel–Lebanon · Extended 3 weeks Oval Office · Trump · Netanyahu · Aoun

Trump extends Israel–Lebanon ceasefire three weeks after White House Oval Office meeting.

The 10-day Israel–Lebanon ceasefire, set to expire Sunday, was extended by three weeks Thursday following a White House meeting that brought together senior Israeli and Lebanese officials in the Oval Office with President Trump, Vice President Vance, Secretary Rubio, and US ambassadors to Israel and Lebanon. Trump called it a "great chance" for a lasting peace deal and said he hopes to host Netanyahu and Lebanese President Aoun at the White House during the extension period. The meeting is the most significant direct US-brokered contact between the two sides since the ceasefire was signed April 17.

Source: CNBC · Washington Post

Iran seizes MSC Francesca and Epaminondas in Hormuz; Revolutionary Guard fires on third vessel.

Iran's Revolutionary Guard seized two commercial vessels — the MSC Francesca and the Epaminondas — in the Strait of Hormuz Thursday, claiming they "had endangered maritime security by operating without the required authorization." A third container ship was approached by an Iranian gunboat that opened fire, causing "heavy damage" to the bridge bridge. All crew members were reported safe. Iran characterized the seizures as a response to the continuing US naval blockade, which Tehran considers a ceasefire violation. The United States categorically rejects that framing.

EU · Ukraine $106B Loan approved · Hungary veto lifted

EU approves $106 billion Ukraine loan after Hungary lifts veto — oil pipeline deal breaks deadlock.

The European Union approved a 90-billion-euro ($106 billion) loan package to help Ukraine meet its economic and military needs for two years Thursday, after months of political deadlock. Hungary and Slovakia lifted their vetoes after a resolution to their oil pipeline dispute with Ukraine — Russian oil deliveries to the two countries had been halted since January when a pipeline was damaged. The EU also approved a new package of sanctions against Russia. The loans are expected to be available to Ukraine in coming weeks. The package ends the longest standoff in EU-Ukraine support since the full-scale invasion began.

Source: WFAE / NPR · Washington Times
US-Iran · Day 56 No talks Ceasefire holds · blockade intact · empty track

Day 56: US-Iran ceasefire technically holds but talks are empty — Iran's Hormuz escalation raises stakes.

The US-Iran ceasefire entered its 56th day Friday with no diplomatic talks scheduled, no active mediator, and Iran's Revolutionary Guard having just escalated in the Strait of Hormuz. Trump said Thursday that "time is not on Tehran's side" in response to the vessel seizures — framing the US position as one of patience backed by ongoing military and economic pressure. Iran's foreign minister has again characterized the US naval blockade as a ceasefire violation and conditioned any talks on the blockade's removal. Washington maintains the blockade is a lawful enforcement action.

Source: Al Jazeera · CNN
Trump · Peace framing "Best deal" Iran · Lebanon · dual-track strategy

Trump pursues dual-track: Lebanon peace deal within weeks, Iran pressure until "best deal" achieved.

Trump's statements Thursday outlined a dual strategy: accelerating toward a permanent Israel-Lebanon agreement within the three-week extension window, while maintaining pressure on Tehran until Iran submits to what he described as "the best deal" on its nuclear and regional posture. The framing suggests the Lebanon track is now explicitly decoupled from the US-Iran negotiation — progress on one does not require resolution on the other. France 24 reports that Israel and Lebanon's leadership have separately indicated willingness to push toward a longer-term framework if the three weeks produce sufficient diplomatic progress.

Source: France 24 · US News
IMF · Global Growth 3.1% 2026 outlook · shadow of war

IMF projects 3.1% global growth in 2026 — "in the shadow of war," below prepandemic averages.

The International Monetary Fund's April 2026 World Economic Outlook projects global growth at 3.1% for 2026 and 3.2% for 2027, below recent outcomes and well under prepandemic averages. The IMF explicitly frames the outlook as being conducted "in the shadow of war," citing the US-Iran conflict's impact on energy prices, supply-chain routing, and regional economic activity as the primary risk factor. The $106 billion EU-Ukraine loan approved Thursday is among the fiscal responses the IMF cited as necessary to sustain economic stability in conflict-adjacent economies. The oil premium from Hormuz is the single factor most likely to force a downward revision to the baseline.

Source: IMF · S&P Global
