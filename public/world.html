The Anchor

UN warns Hormuz stalemate risks "worst supply chain disruption since COVID" — Trump says no time frame on Iran war.

Day 60 opened Tuesday with the most direct intervention from the international community since the conflict began: UN Secretary-General Antonio Guterres warned that the continued closure of the Strait of Hormuz risks "the worst supply chain disruption since COVID-19 and the war in Ukraine," adding that the standoff could trigger a global food emergency if shipping lanes remain closed through summer. The UN statement came backed by more than sixty nations — including European Union members, Japan, South Korea, India, and most Gulf states — calling for the "urgent and unimpeded reopening" of the strait. The breadth of the international coalition is the largest unified geopolitical statement on the conflict to date, and reflects the mounting economic cost the closure is imposing on nations with no direct stake in the US-Iran nuclear dispute.

Trump reviewed Iran's Hormuz proposal Monday with his national security team — Secretary of State Rubio, National Security Advisor Waltz, and Defense Secretary Hegseth were present — and acknowledged receiving it but offered no public response. US officials confirmed to CNN that Trump is unlikely to accept a framework that defers nuclear talks indefinitely, describing it as a "Hormuz offer with nothing behind it." Iranian President Pezeshkian publicly framed the Iranian position: "Iran will not negotiate under siege — lift the blockade first." Trump, speaking Monday evening, said there is "no time frame" on the Iran war, offering no indication of when US terms might be met or modified. The absence of a counteroffer from Washington means Day 60 ends with the same structure as Day 1: the US demands nuclear concessions first; Iran demands blockade lifting first; no sequencing bridge exists. Araghchi's continued presence in Moscow deepens Iran's Russia option as the US track stalls.

UN · Guterres · Hormuz "Worst" Supply chain disruption · 60+ nations · food emergency warning · Day 60

Guterres: Hormuz stalemate risks "worst supply chain disruption since COVID and Ukraine war" — food emergency possible if closure continues.

UN Secretary-General Antonio Guterres issued a stark warning Tuesday, stating that the continued closure of the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly 20% of global oil and a significant portion of LNG and dry goods flow — risks becoming "the worst supply chain disruption since COVID-19 and the war in Ukraine." Guterres added that food security is now at direct risk if the closure continues through summer planting seasons in South Asia and Africa, which rely on fertilizer and fuel supplies that pass through Hormuz. The Guterres statement was backed by more than sixty nations — the UN Security Council had already discussed the crisis multiple times since February, but Tuesday's coordinated 60-nation statement represents the broadest international pressure campaign since the conflict began. Gulf nations, notably the UAE and Qatar, are in a particularly difficult position: they share the conflict's geographic proximity and economic disruption while formally not taking sides. The 60-nation coalition's statement specifically called for "urgent and unimpeded reopening" — language carefully chosen to avoid assigning blame to either party, but effectively targeting the naval blockade as the primary barrier to resolution.

Trump · Iran · No Time Frame Stalled Reviews proposal · nuclear non-negotiable · no counteroffer · Pakistan channel

Trump says "no time frame" on Iran war — reviews Hormuz proposal with NSC but confirms nuclear concessions are non-negotiable.

President Trump met his national security team Monday to review Iran's Hormuz peace proposal — delivered via Pakistani diplomatic channels — and offered his clearest public statement on the conflict's timeline: "There is no time frame." US officials briefed on the review told CNN that Trump is unlikely to accept a framework that defers nuclear talks indefinitely. Iran's proposal sequences the deal as: blockade lifted → Hormuz reopens → war ends → nuclear talks begin later. Trump's position, unchanged since Day 1, is: nuclear suspension first, then any discussion of blockade or war terms. The sequencing impasse is the structural barrier to a deal, and neither side has offered a bridge proposal. Pakistan's Foreign Minister Ishaq Dar, who delivered Iran's written proposal to Secretary Rubio, has offered to continue mediating — but the US position requires Iran to make the first substantive concession on nuclear enrichment before the mediator can present a viable counter. Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi separately told media Tuesday that Tehran "is considering a US request to restart negotiations" — a statement that slightly softened his prior "no talks under blockade" framing, suggesting some movement at the diplomatic margin, though not enough to close the sequencing gap.

Araghchi in Moscow deepens Iran-Russia axis — Putin's "imposed war" framing signals operational, not just diplomatic, alignment.

Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi's Monday meeting with Putin in St. Petersburg — which included Russia's military intelligence chief Igor Kostyukov — continues to reverberate as the clearest signal of the conflict's second front. Putin's framing of the conflict as an "imposed war" on Iran — language that mirrors Tehran's own state media and goes beyond Russia's prior "strategic partnership" formulation — represents a public alignment that effectively eliminates Russia's ability to serve as a neutral mediator in any future diplomatic process. Moscow's previous role in Iran negotiations (including the JCPOA process) depended on its ability to maintain relationships with both sides; Tuesday's language forecloses that positioning. Araghchi told media Tuesday that Iran "is considering a US request to restart negotiations" — briefing Putin in parallel on the diplomatic track — which suggests Iran is using the Russia axis as an insurance policy: if US talks progress, Iran has leverage; if they fail, Russia provides strategic cover. Kostyukov's presence at a meeting formally described as diplomatic signals the discussion was not limited to the Hormuz proposal's contents.

60+ nations join UN call for urgent Hormuz reopening — the broadest international pressure campaign since the conflict began.

The 60-nation coalition backing the UN call for Hormuz reopening includes every EU member state, Japan, South Korea, India, the UAE, Qatar, Bahrain, and most major trading nations with supply chains running through the strait. The coalition's statement was carefully drafted to call for "urgent and unimpeded reopening" without assigning blame to either the US-led naval blockade or Iran's missile threat posture — allowing Gulf states formally allied with the US to sign without publicly condemning Washington. For the Gulf states, the coalition represents a way to apply pressure on the US to accept a deal that removes the oil disruption threatening their own economic trajectories: the UAE and Qatar both depend on Hormuz passage for their LNG and oil exports, and the longer the closure continues, the more it erodes the Gulf's own fiscal stability. The EU's inclusion reflects the direct economic damage $111 Brent is inflicting on European economies — combined with the compounding inflation effect on ECB monetary policy — making Hormuz resolution a first-order European economic interest, not just a geopolitical preference.

Lebanon · Ceasefire · Day 3 Continuing IDF operations east Lebanon · Hezbollah retaliatory fire · May 14 expiry

Lebanon ceasefire continues as IDF maintains operations in east Lebanon — May 14 expiry creates a hard deadline with no framework for extension.

The Israel-Lebanon ceasefire extension continues Tuesday, with Israeli forces maintaining operations in east Lebanon targeting what the IDF describes as Hezbollah weapons infrastructure and command nodes. Hezbollah has continued retaliatory fire at Israeli positions. The three-week extension announced April 24 expires approximately May 14 — a hard deadline that coincides with the period when Iran's Hormuz negotiations will either have produced a de-escalation framework or collapsed entirely. Analysts note the Lebanon front serves as a pressure valve: if Iran-US talks progress, Hezbollah operations will likely be moderated as part of a broader regional de-escalation; if talks collapse, the Lebanon theater may re-escalate as Iran's proxy pressure instrument. The May 14 deadline means the Israel-Lebanon situation will resolve — in one direction or another — within the same window as the FOMC meeting on May 7 and the April PCE print on May 1. Netanyahu's government has maintained its "full freedom of action" clause in the ceasefire language, reserving the right to strike if Hezbollah violates the extension — a clause that Hezbollah's parliamentary bloc has called "meaningless" given continued IDF operations.

Brent breaks $111 on Day 60 — the highest since the closure began, as Goldman's $90 late-2026 target grows harder to model.

Brent crude reached $111.50 per barrel Tuesday morning — its highest since the Hormuz closure began in late February — as Day 60 passed without a deal and the 60-nation UN coalition produced no immediate diplomatic breakthrough. WTI crossed $99.77, approaching $100 for the first time in this cycle. Goldman Sachs's $90 Brent late-2026 target, which assumes a Hormuz resolution within the model's time window, is increasingly a longer-dated call: the Goldman model does not specify when resolution occurs, but at Day 60, the timeline is clearly extending past their prior assumption. The IMF has projected global growth slowing to 3.1% in 2026 under continued conflict conditions — a figure that will be revised lower at its June update if $100+ Brent persists through May. Iran's export workarounds — via Iraq, Turkey, and pre-existing China contracts — have allowed Tehran to sustain economic operations well into Day 60 without the financial desperation that might force nuclear concessions. The oil premium may outlast markets' patience.

Quick hits

  • The 60-nation UN coalition is notable for who signed it: India, which has carefully maintained a non-aligned posture throughout the conflict and has been buying Iranian oil through pre-existing contracts, joined the call for Hormuz reopening — signaling that even Iran's best remaining trade partner has reached a threshold where the disruption outweighs the diplomatic cost of the public statement.
  • Trump's "no time frame" statement is the clearest signal yet that the White House is not operating under a political calendar pressure on Iran. The midterms are November 2026; the conventional wisdom has been that a prolonged conflict would drive domestic energy price pressure that creates political cost. "No time frame" suggests the administration believes that cost is manageable — or that the nuclear concession objective is worth the economic drag regardless of timeline.
  • Araghchi's statement that Iran "is considering a US request to restart negotiations" is the softest diplomatic signal from Tehran since Day 1 — the first time the phrase "considering" has been used rather than outright rejection. It does not constitute an acceptance, but it shifts Iran's public posture from "we will not negotiate under siege" to "we are evaluating the conditions for dialogue" — a distinction that diplomatic observers will parse carefully for the next 48 hours.
  • The UN Guterres food emergency warning specifically references South Asian and African food security — nations that import heavily from Hormuz-dependent supply chains and have no economic buffer against $111 Brent and disrupted fertilizer flows. The food security dimension transforms the conflict's international cost from an oil-price inconvenience for rich nations into a potential humanitarian crisis for the most vulnerable economies — raising the political cost of US inaction on the international stage to a new level.
  • Russia's utility as Iran's strategic insurance is now actively constraining any potential US-mediated resolution: Putin's "imposed war" framing closes Russia's role as a neutral broker and adds the implicit message that any US military escalation in the Gulf will be met with Russian military support for Iran — not stated explicitly, but clearly implied by Kostyukov's presence at the meeting. The US cannot escalate the blockade's military intensity without factoring in Russia's new posture.
  • The Lebanon ceasefire's May 14 expiry creates a linked pressure point: if the Iran-US talks remain deadlocked into May, Israel faces the choice of extending a ceasefire that is functionally a low-intensity war or recommencing full operations against Hezbollah — at the same moment the FOMC meets, PCE data arrives, and Big Tech earnings are digested. The confluence of deadlines in the two weeks of May is not coincidental; it reflects the structural tension of multiple unresolved conflicts running on parallel tracks toward the same calendar window.
