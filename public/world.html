World · Deep Dive · US 4th Airstrike Wave · Iran Declares Hormuz Closed · Trump: Ceasefire Over · Oil Surges 4%

The US launched a fourth wave of airstrikes on Iran Monday — hitting dozens of targets and deploying one-way attack drones at sea for the first time — as Iran's IRGC declared the Strait of Hormuz "closed until further notice" and retaliated with fresh strikes on US military facilities in Bahrain, claimed radar systems in Oman, and additional strikes on Kuwait and Jordan, while Trump told reporters the memorandum of understanding was "over" and oil surged 4% to $79 a barrel.

Sunday's six-state Iranian missile barrage has become Monday's direct US-Iran escalation ladder: a fourth airstrike round, a unilateral Hormuz closure declaration, and the first confirmed use of sea-launched one-way attack drones by US forces. Trump's statement that the MOU is "over" removes the diplomatic framework that had been the only nominal constraint on both sides' escalation behavior. The IRGC's claim that the Strait is closed — disputed by US CENTCOM but not operationally resolved — is the supply shock that sends oil markets into crisis-premium pricing. The August 17 nuclear deadline is 35 days away, the Doha diplomatic channel is broken, and the conflict is accelerating in both military and economic scope.

The fourth US airstrike wave launched Monday represents a qualitative escalation from the first three rounds: US Central Command said it struck "dozens" of additional Iranian targets and used one-way attack drones at sea for the first time in the conflict — a weapons system that had not previously been deployed in the Hormuz theater. Iran's IRGC responded by declaring the Strait of Hormuz "closed until further notice" — a unilateral closure of the waterway through which approximately 25% of the world's seaborne oil trade and 20% of global LNG supply transits — and firing again at Bahrain's US military facilities, claiming to have destroyed radar systems in Oman, and striking Kuwait and Jordan for the second day running. US Central Command disputed the closure claim and said it was conducting freedom-of-navigation operations to keep the waterway open. The practical distinction between a claimed closure and a contested one is what oil markets are now pricing: Brent surged 4.12% to $79.14, WTI 4.20% to $74.41.

Trump told reporters Monday that the memorandum of understanding between the US and Iran was "over" — formally terminating the diplomatic framework that had, in theory, created space for the Doha process. The MOU's collapse removes the last nominal constraint on both sides' escalation calculus: without a framework, the US has no obligation to pause between airstrike rounds, and Iran has no framework it is notionally violating by striking Gulf allies or declaring Hormuz closed. Al Jazeera reported that the flare-up "threatened to derail talks between the US and Iran to end the war" — though the Trump statement makes clear those talks are not ongoing from the US side. The nuclear deadline of August 17 sits 35 days away. There is currently no diplomatic infrastructure capable of reaching it.

The distinction between "claimed closed" and "operationally closed" is what the next 24-48 hours will determine. Iran's IRGC can announce a closure; US CENTCOM can launch freedom-of-navigation operations to dispute it. Whether commercial shipping actually transits the Strait in the coming days — or whether insurers, operators, and charterers halt sailings regardless of military escorts — determines the real-world supply impact. If the Lloyd's War Risk market prices Hormuz transits as uninsurable, the closure becomes economically real even if US naval forces maintain physical access. The one-way drone deployment at sea is the day's most consequential military development: it demonstrates that the US is escalating its own weapons mix, not just its strike tempo.

Iran's IRGC targeted US military facilities in Bahrain on Monday — the home of the US Navy's 5th Fleet headquarters at Naval Support Activity Bahrain — while claiming to have destroyed radar systems in Oman and conducting fresh strikes against Kuwait and Jordan for the second consecutive day, as Al Jazeera reported a third consecutive day of Iranian retaliatory attacks against US allies in the Gulf following the US fourth airstrike wave, confirming a pattern in which each exchange between US and Iranian forces produces Iranian military action at expanded geographic scope and against higher-value targets. The National reported Bahrain and Kuwait were attacked again in the overnight hours Monday.

Iran targeting the US 5th Fleet's home base in Bahrain is the most direct strike on American naval command infrastructure in the conflict to date. The 5th Fleet oversees US naval operations in the Persian Gulf, Red Sea, Arabian Sea, and parts of the Indian Ocean — the same geographic theater where the Hormuz crisis is unfolding. Hitting Bahrain alongside the Hormuz closure declaration is a coordinated signal: Iran is simultaneously attempting to close the waterway and degrade the US naval infrastructure responsible for keeping it open. Whether the Bahrain strikes actually damaged 5th Fleet facilities is not confirmed by US military statements as of Monday morning; the IRGC's Oman radar-system claim is similarly unverified. But the targeting decisions themselves are the signal — Iran's retaliation is now explicitly aimed at US military command infrastructure across the Gulf, not just shipping.

President Trump told reporters Monday that the memorandum of understanding between the US and Iran was "over" — formally abandoning the diplomatic framework that had created nominal space for the Doha ceasefire process — as CNN reported the ceasefire "disintegrating" and Al Jazeera said the conflict was "threatening to derail" any remaining Iran-US talks, with the nuclear MOU deadline of August 17 now 35 days away and no viable diplomatic channel remaining: the Doha process depended on Gulf state intermediaries, and the countries that had been facilitating those negotiations — Qatar, Kuwait, Jordan, Bahrain — were struck by Iran on Sunday and Monday, making it politically impossible for any Gulf government to host Iran-US negotiations in the immediate aftermath of receiving Iranian missiles.

The MOU's collapse creates a structural problem beyond the immediate military exchange: the memorandum was the mechanism through which the US was pursuing a formal Iranian commitment on Hormuz access — the demand Washington issued Saturday that Iran has now answered with missiles and a closure declaration rather than words. Without the MOU framework, the US has no formal demands and Iran has no formal commitments. The August 17 nuclear deadline, originally the focus of the MOU process, is now a calendar marker without a corresponding diplomatic track. Saudi Arabia, Egypt, and the UAE renewed calls for ceasefire in statements Monday — but calls for ceasefire without a framework for talks are not a diplomatic mechanism; they are expressions of regional anxiety about a conflict that has no visible off-ramp. The 35 days remaining are not enough to build a new diplomatic framework from scratch.

Quick hits

  • The one-way attack drone deployment at sea is the military development that most directly responds to Iran's Hormuz closure claim. One-way attack drones — loitering munitions that approach and destroy targets without a return flight — can be deployed from surface vessels or submarines at range and are difficult to intercept in high-saturation attack patterns. Their first use in the Hormuz theater signals that CENTCOM is escalating its own weapons mix, not just strike frequency. If Iran deploys additional Hormuz-blocking measures — mining, small-boat swarms, anti-ship missile batteries — the sea-drone deployment suggests the US has positioned assets specifically to degrade those capabilities. The weapons escalation is occurring simultaneously with the political escalation of Trump's MOU declaration, meaning both the military and diplomatic frameworks of the conflict entered new phases on Monday morning.
  • The nuclear August 17 deadline is simultaneously the most important and least discussed aspect of the current escalation. The MOU that Trump declared "over" Monday was the same document that contained the deadline. Without the MOU framework, the deadline has no legal or diplomatic standing — it becomes a calendar date without a corresponding obligation. But the underlying nuclear file — Iran's uranium enrichment progress, the IAEA inspection access dispute, and the question of whether Iran is approaching weapons-grade material — does not become less urgent because the political framework collapsed. Iran's nuclear program continues regardless of the MOU's status. Whether the US treats the August 17 date as a trigger for a different type of military action — not airstrike rounds against conventional military targets but strikes against nuclear facilities — is the question that the MOU's collapse leaves entirely unresolved.
