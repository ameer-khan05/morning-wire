The Anchor

Iran put the delay on the United States. The US put the signing on Iran's timeline. Qatar sent mediators to Tehran. Day 106 is not Day 105. The "24-hour" window passed. The deal is still alive — just not signed.

The sequence of Saturday and Sunday's events is the story: Pakistan's PM Shehbaz Sharif said Saturday morning that the "final, agreed upon text" was reached and electronic signing was expected within 24 hours. Trump posted Saturday that the deal would be signed on June 14 — his 80th birthday — a framing that reflected both optimism and political narrative crafting around a historic announcement. Iran's FM Abbas Araghchi had said the deal had "never been closer" and confirmed core terms (Iran's indefinite never-nuclear commitment and US asset unfreeze). That was Saturday. Sunday morning, Iran's FM spokesman Esmaeil Baqaei said the deal would not be signed Sunday, attributing the delay to "hesitancy on the other side" — a phrase designed to put responsibility on Washington rather than Tehran. "Even if it's not tomorrow," Baqaei said, "it could happen in the coming days."

The gap between Pakistan's "24 hours" and Iran's "not Sunday" is not evidence that the deal has collapsed — it is evidence that the final procedural steps are more complicated than Pakistan's statement suggested. The Qatari mediators dispatched to Tehran overnight represent a third layer of mediation on top of Pakistan's role: Qatar has historically been the US's intermediary with Iran on hostage releases, sanctions exemptions, and nuclear negotiation side channels. Qatar's involvement suggests the gap is real and specific enough to require active mediation, but also that both sides believe it is closeable. What exactly is causing the delay — and whether Qatari mediation can close it Sunday evening — is the only question that matters for Monday's global markets.

The IDF struck what it described as a Hezbollah command center in a south Beirut apartment early Sunday — killing at least one and injuring four — after three Hezbollah drones struck northern Israeli territory. The strike is the most significant Israeli military action in Beirut in weeks and occurs simultaneously with the US-Iran peace process at its most sensitive juncture, creating a domestic political complication for Iranian leaders as they navigate the final steps of signing the Islamabad Memorandum. Iran's formal diplomatic position has consistently maintained that the Lebanon-Israel front is separate from the US-Iran conflict and should not be treated as a linkage condition for the Islamabad process.

Hezbollah is Iran's most significant regional proxy, and Israeli strikes on Hezbollah during the peace signing window create the optics of multi-front Iranian concessions. Iranian hardliners who opposed the Islamabad negotiations throughout the 106-day conflict can use the Beirut strikes to argue Iran is conceding while its allies are struck — precisely the framing that has derailed prior near-deals. The IRGC's factional position against the deal is the domestic approval hurdle that Qatari mediation needs to address alongside the US-side procedural gap.

Qatar's dispatch of negotiators to Tehran overnight represents a third mediation layer on top of Pakistan's breakthrough — Qatar has historically served as the primary back-channel between the US and Iran on hostage releases, sanctions exemptions, and nuclear negotiation side channels. Doha hosts US CENTCOM and maintains formal diplomatic relations with Tehran simultaneously, giving Qatar the dual credibility that makes it the preferred intermediary when US-Iran communication breaks down or requires a trusted third party to bridge specific gaps. The Qatar deployment suggests the remaining gap is specific and addressable — broadly, both sides know what they want — but requires a mediator to help structure the sequencing and verify each side's commitments in a way that both find acceptable.

Pakistan's role was substantive — drafting and agreeing the text. Qatar's role is procedural — ensuring the signing sequence works for both sides' domestic needs. If Qatar's mediation succeeds Sunday evening, the deal could be signed electronically before Monday's market open, producing the resolution trade global markets have been pricing toward for three months. If not, the next natural opportunity is Monday itself.

Both Iran's and the US's public statements are consistent on the most important point: the core terms of the Islamabad Memorandum — Iran's indefinite commitment to never develop nuclear weapons, the US unfreezing of confiscated Iranian assets, and the Hormuz 30-day reopening clock — are agreed and not in dispute. Iran's FM Araghchi said Saturday the deal had "never been closer" and confirmed specific terms. Iran's FM spokesman Baqaei said Sunday that the signing would not happen today — but explicitly did not say the deal had collapsed, was in serious jeopardy, or that core terms were being revisited. The word "hesitancy" attributes a procedural reluctance to Washington rather than a substantive objection by either party.

The most likely resolution path: Qatari mediators close the final procedural gap — verification sequencing, the asset unfreeze timeline, or signing ceremony logistics — and the deal is signed electronically within 24-48 hours. The Geneva ceremony with VP Vance, announced by Pakistan's PM, remains on the planning calendar. Sovereign governments do not commit vice presidents to international ceremonies until those ceremonies are nearly certain to occur; the Geneva commitment was not retracted despite the delay.
