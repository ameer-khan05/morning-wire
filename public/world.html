Iran launched simultaneous waves of missile and drone strikes against six Gulf states on Sunday morning — Qatar, the UAE, Bahrain, Kuwait, Jordan, and Oman — with the IRGC claiming it targeted Al-Udeid US Air Base in Qatar and destroyed a command-and-control center and aircraft maintenance facility, hours after the US conducted a third round of airstrikes on Iranian targets; Kuwait intercepted 4 missiles and 10 drones, Jordan shot down 8 missiles, and Saudi Arabia, Egypt, and the UAE condemned the attacks while Gulf leaders renewed calls for ceasefire.

Iran's Sunday morning strikes are the conflict's widest single-day geographic escalation — six states struck simultaneously, all US allies, several hosting major US military installations, all of which had been serving as diplomatic intermediaries between Washington and Tehran. The attack on the Doha mediators is not incidental to the diplomacy — it is its own signal: Iran has chosen military retaliation against the US's regional infrastructure over the diplomatic path that the Doha process offered. The August 17 nuclear MOU deadline is now 36 days away, the Doha framework is broken, and Iran has demonstrated a willingness to strike across the entire Gulf in a single morning.

The sequence of events on Sunday morning: US Central Command launched a third round of airstrikes on Iranian targets on Trump's orders, following an IRGC attack on a Cyprus-flagged container ship transiting the Strait of Hormuz. Iran responded by launching simultaneous waves of missile and drone strikes across six Gulf states. The IRGC's statement claimed it targeted Al-Udeid Air Base in Qatar — the largest US air base in the Middle East, hosting approximately 10,000 US military personnel — and claimed to have destroyed a command-and-control center and an aircraft maintenance facility. Qatar's air defense system engaged incoming Iranian ballistic missiles over Doha, and multiple loud explosions were reported across the capital. Kuwait's military said it intercepted 4 incoming missiles and 10 drones. Jordan's air defense shot down 8 missiles fired toward its territory. The UAE, Bahrain, and Oman also faced incoming fire, though reported interceptions were concentrated in Kuwait and Jordan.

The geographic scope of the attack reframes the conflict. Through the Strait of Hormuz escalation period, Iran had confined its military operations to the waterway and commercial shipping — a maritime harassment strategy that was harmful to global trade but contained to a specific geographic and functional scope. Sunday's strikes on six countries simultaneously, explicitly targeting US military infrastructure, represent a categorically different operation: a multi-front simultaneous attack on US force projection capability across the entire Gulf region. Saudi Arabia, Egypt, the UAE, and Qatar — nations that had been simultaneously hosting US forces, facilitating Iran-US negotiations, and calling for diplomatic de-escalation — condemned the attacks in unified statements and renewed calls for ceasefire. Iran has now struck the countries that had been mediating on its behalf, which collapses the diplomatic infrastructure the Doha process depended on.

Iran's Revolutionary Guard announced Sunday morning that it had launched simultaneous missile and drone strikes against Qatar, the UAE, Bahrain, Kuwait, Jordan, and Oman — claiming in an IRGC statement to have targeted Al-Udeid Air Base in Qatar, the largest US military installation in the Middle East with approximately 10,000 personnel, and claiming to have destroyed a command-and-control center and an aircraft maintenance facility — as Kuwait's military confirmed it intercepted 4 incoming missiles and 10 drones, Jordan's air defense announced shooting down 8 missiles, and Euronews reported multiple loud explosions over Doha in two waves as Qatar's air defense engaged Iranian ballistic missiles above the capital. The United Arab Emirates and Bahrain also faced incoming fire in what Euronews described as simultaneous "waves of major attacks" across the Gulf.

Al-Udeid Air Base in Qatar is the operational hub for US Central Command's air operations in the Middle East — the same headquarters from which the US third-round airstrikes on Iranian targets were likely coordinated. If the IRGC's claim of striking Al-Udeid's command center and aircraft maintenance facility is confirmed by US military statements or satellite imagery, it would represent the most significant direct strike on US military infrastructure in the region since the conflict began. US Central Command had not released a damage assessment as of Sunday morning. Qatar's air defense engaging Iranian ballistic missiles directly over Doha — with audible explosions over the capital of a country that hosts one of the world's largest US military bases — is the visual signal that the conflict has entered a phase where US military presence in the Gulf provides no deterrence against direct Iranian strikes.

US Central Command conducted a third round of airstrikes on Iranian targets on Trump's orders following an IRGC attack on a Cyprus-flagged container ship transiting the Strait of Hormuz — the third consecutive exchange in the escalation chain that has characterized the Iran-US military interaction since the Hormuz crisis began — as the pattern of each US strike round producing Iranian retaliation at an expanded geographic scope became explicit on Sunday: the first US rounds drew Iranian responses targeting Gulf shipping; the third US round drew Iranian responses targeting six Gulf states and US military bases directly. NPR reported the Hormuz vessel attack as the immediate trigger for the US third round, which Iran's IRGC then characterized as justification for the broadest single-day Iranian military operation in the Gulf in the conflict's history.

The escalation pattern — each US-Iran exchange producing Iranian retaliation at wider geographic scope — is the most consequential structural development in the conflict since the Hormuz crisis began. It says that Iran's response calculus is not one of proportional retaliation but of escalation ladder climbing: each US strike justifies a response that extends to the next level of geographic and target scope. If the pattern holds, a fourth round of US strikes — whether or not it occurs — would produce an Iranian response targeting something broader still. The open question is whether Sunday's six-state attack represents Iran's maximum willingness to escalate (a ceiling) or a point on an ascending trajectory. The US response will determine which answer the next 36 days before the nuclear deadline produce.

Saudi Arabia condemned what it called Iran's "repeated violations of the sovereignty of neighboring states" and warned that Sunday's attacks "threatened regional security and undermined international efforts to restore stability" — as the UAE, Qatar, Egypt, and other Gulf governments issued parallel condemnations and renewed calls for ceasefire, while Gulf leaders intensified diplomatic contacts that Arab News described as aimed at "reviving negotiations" — but the central problem for any diplomatic revival is that Iran has now struck the nations that had been facilitating the negotiations: Qatar, which hosted Doha rounds 1 and 2, is now a strike target; the UAE and Kuwait, which had been providing diplomatic cover for the process, have now intercepted Iranian missiles. The August 17 nuclear MOU deadline is 36 days away and the nuclear file has not been addressed in either prior Doha round.

The collapse of the Doha diplomatic framework through Iran's strikes on the mediator states is the Sunday story that markets will be processing alongside the oil-price move Monday. The Doha process had been the mechanism through which the US expected to extract Iran's formal Hormuz guarantee — the demand Washington issued Saturday that Iran has now answered with missiles. Without the Doha framework, and without Gulf states willing to host further rounds after being struck, the only remaining path to the August 17 nuclear deadline is either a direct US-Iran channel that does not require Gulf mediation or a military outcome that replaces the diplomatic one. The 36 days remaining to the nuclear deadline represent one of the shortest viable windows for rebuilding a diplomatic framework from the wreckage of Sunday's strikes — a timeline that was already considered nearly impossible before the attacks.

Quick hits

  • The IRGC's claim to have targeted Al-Udeid — specifically a command-and-control center and aircraft maintenance facility — is significant regardless of whether it is fully confirmed. Al-Udeid is the operational headquarters for US air power projection across the Middle East and Central Asia. A credible strike claim against its C2 infrastructure, even if partially successful, serves Iran's signaling purpose: it communicates to the US that the cost of continued airstrike escalation is the degradation of the US's regional command infrastructure, not just the disruption of commercial shipping. If the US does not confirm the Al-Udeid damage, it reduces Iran's claim to signaling theater. If the US confirms even partial damage, it validates Iran's ability to reach the most critical US military infrastructure in the region.
  • The Gulf mediator problem created by Sunday's strikes is genuinely novel. Iran-US negotiations since 2025 have relied on Gulf state facilitation — Qatar and Oman most directly, with UAE and Kuwait providing political cover. Iran has now struck all of these states in a single morning. No Gulf leader can politically host Iran-US negotiations in the immediate aftermath of having Iranian missiles intercepted over their capital cities. The diplomatic vacuum this creates is not something the US can fill by direct channel alone — Iran's domestic political constraints make direct US-Iran communication without regional intermediaries nearly impossible at the working level. The 36-day nuclear deadline window is now a diplomacy-infrastructure problem as much as a substantive negotiation problem.
