Whether that authorization — and the negotiating posture it expressed — remains operative on July 9 depends on Mojtaba's condition and on the political decisions he has made or been able to make from his undisclosed location. The gap between "positive progress in technical talks" and "political authorization to close a framework" is the gap that July 9 through August 17 must close.

New reporting Tuesday revealed that Iran's Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei was wounded in the February 28 assassination that killed his father Ali Khamenei, his mother, and his wife — providing the most significant explanation for his four consecutive days of public absence from the state funeral and establishing that Iran has been led since February by a wounded supreme leader directing the country from an undisclosed location. The severity of his wounds has not been disclosed. His whereabouts remain unknown. He has not made a public appearance since the assassination, and he was not expected to appear at any of the five-day funeral proceedings for his father.

The wounding report changes the reading of Iran's behavior since February in ways that are immediately relevant to the July 9 Doha talks. A supreme leader who is physically incapacitated does not conduct the traditional signaling and public positioning that normally accompanies a nuclear negotiation at a critical juncture — the speeches, public statements, and controlled media appearances through which a supreme leader shapes domestic and international expectations before major diplomatic moves. Mojtaba has done none of these. His absence is not a negotiating tactic; it is a clinical and security constraint. The practical implication for Doha is that Ghalibaf's mandate on July 9 was authorized by a wounded leader under conditions that neither the US team nor the Qatari mediators can independently verify. What Ghalibaf says he is authorized to do, and what Mojtaba has actually authorized from his undisclosed location, may or may not be the same thing — and there is currently no publicly available mechanism to close that gap.

The Doha nuclear talks are set to resume July 9 following confirmation that the burial of Ali Khamenei at the Imam Reza shrine in Mashhad will occur on the same day — after the body transits through Najaf, Iraq on July 8 and the Shrine of Imam Hussein in Karbala before returning to Iran. Qatari mediators described "positive progress" from technical discussions held before the funeral pause, with both the Iranian and US teams agreeing to resume as soon as possible after the funeral concludes. Parliament Speaker and chief nuclear negotiator Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf leads Iran's Doha team and was present at the Day 2 funeral ceremonies before departing for Doha preparations. The resumption on July 9 leaves 37 days before the August 17 MOU deadline.

The "positive progress" Qatari characterization of the pre-funeral talks must be read against the wounding report. Ghalibaf's team achieved technical progress in discussions before July 4 under an authorization from Mojtaba Khamenei that was established at some point between February 28 and the pre-funeral talks — during a period when the supreme leader was recovering from wounds that his government has not publicly disclosed. The outstanding issues — enrichment limits, Hormuz transit, sanctions relief — are not technical questions that Ghalibaf can resolve without explicit political authorization. Whatever Ghalibaf brings back from Doha as a proposed framework, Mojtaba must either approve or reject from his undisclosed location. The 37 days between July 9 and August 17 are not a negotiating window as much as a ratification window: the technical work is what "positive progress" describes; the political authorization to sign it is what Mojtaba's first public communication will determine.

The European Union approved a €90 billion loan to Ukraine following Viktor Orbán's removal from power, which cleared the final political obstacle to the largest peacetime fiscal transfer in EU history — covering Ukraine's "most urgent budgetary and defense industrial capacity needs" through 2026 and 2027. The Council on Foreign Relations simultaneously assessed the G7's emerging alignment on both Iran and Ukraine as "deeply fragile," describing it as built on a framework agreement that European governments privately doubt and a Ukraine negotiating window they fear the United States will squander. The Lebanon-Israel ceasefire's implementation contradiction — the IDF's commitment to a 6-mile security zone that the framework's withdrawal language requires Israel to abandon — returns to active management as the Iran funeral period that has kept all parties on maximum diplomatic caution ends July 9.

The EU's €90 billion commitment and the G7's fragile alignment are two readings of the same geopolitical moment. The EU loan de-risks Ukraine's immediate fiscal position — the largest commitment since the conflict began — while the CFR's "deeply fragile" characterization captures the strategic uncertainty underneath the visible diplomatic convergence. European governments supporting a US-negotiated Iran framework that they "privately doubt" are in the diplomatically uncomfortable position of having agreed to something they do not trust in order to maintain transatlantic solidarity. That gap — between the public alignment and the private assessment — is the structural vulnerability in the G7's Iran position. If Mojtaba Khamenei's first post-burial communication is hardline rather than conciliatory, the European partners who agreed to the current framework under private doubts will face pressure to reassess positions they are already uncertain about. The Lebanon ceasefire's re-engagement after July 9 adds a second theater where the same structural vulnerability exists: a framework agreed but not implemented, with a triggering incident risk if neither side changes its stated position.
