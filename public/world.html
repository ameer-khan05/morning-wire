The Anchor

Two sides have given contradictory accounts of the same negotiation's status. One of them will be wrong by Sunday. The markets have already bet on which.

Trump's Thursday statement was unusually specific: the US has "ended the war," both sides agreed to a "very strong memorandum of understanding" to stop the fighting, Iran committed to "never" having a nuclear weapon — which was "the whole purpose" of US demands — and Trump anticipated a signing ceremony "soon," possibly in Europe, to be attended by Vice President JD Vance. Iranian state media separately reported that the draft agreement includes a US commitment to lift oil sanctions on Iran within 30 days of signing, and Iran's pledge to reopen the Strait of Hormuz to commercial traffic within 30 days of the sanctions commitment. Those terms would satisfy Iran's two core economic demands simultaneously — the ones that have made the conflict's economic toll on Iran most severe.

Iran's government's response was structured to leave negotiating room without accepting the deal as stated. The official spokesman's "no final decision" language is not an outright rejection — it is a signal that supreme leadership has not ratified the agreement, that internal deliberation is ongoing, and that Iran reserves the right to modify terms. Tehran's accusation that Washington was "greedy" and changed the deal text suggests the dispute is over specific provisions rather than the deal's existence — a more tractable gap than a fundamental disagreement over whether to negotiate. The pattern is familiar: Iran's supreme leadership has used the "not finalized" language throughout the 104-day conflict to create space between what negotiators agree and what the supreme leader ratifies. If that pattern holds, ratification is possible by Sunday.

President Trump announced Thursday evening that the United States has "ended the war" with Iran, stating that both sides had agreed to a "very strong memorandum of understanding" to stop the fighting and that Iran had committed to never pursuing a nuclear weapon — describing the nuclear commitment as "the whole purpose" of the US military campaign since February 28. Trump cancelled further planned strikes, said a formal signing ceremony could take place "soon" — possibly in Europe — and suggested Vice President JD Vance would attend. Iranian state media simultaneously reported that the draft agreement includes a US commitment to lift oil sanctions on Iran within 30 days and Iran's pledge to reopen the Strait of Hormuz within 30 days of that sanction lift.

The substance of the reported deal — if accurate — represents a significant US concession on oil sanctions, which were among the primary economic levers the US used throughout the conflict to pressure Tehran. Iran's pledge to reopen Hormuz within 30 days of the sanction commitment would effectively tie the strait's reopening to concrete US action rather than a promise, giving Tehran a mechanism to verify US compliance before fully reopening the world's most strategically vital waterway. The sequencing of "US lifts sanctions, then Iran opens Hormuz" rather than the reverse reflects Iran's negotiating strength from the Hormuz closure threat and its diminished trust in US commitments following the May 31 Situation Room collapse.

Iran's official government spokesman directly contradicted Trump's "war ended" announcement, stating that "Iran has not reached a final decision regarding any agreement." Tehran additionally accused Washington of being "greedy" in negotiations and alleged that the US had unilaterally changed the text of the peace agreement — a specific accusation that points to a dispute over particular provisions rather than a fundamental rejection of the deal's existence. Al Jazeera's liveblog reported Iranian sources saying Trump "claims" Tehran approved a deal but that no final approval has been given from the supreme leadership level, which is required for any binding commitment in the Iranian constitutional system.

The distinction between "no final decision" and "no deal" is significant. Iran's negotiating posture throughout the 104-day conflict has consistently involved the supreme leader's office ratifying (or rejecting) what negotiators and technical teams agree at the working level — sometimes within hours, sometimes after days of deliberation. The "greedy" accusation and "changed text" allegation suggest the dispute is over specific language in the agreement rather than whether to have an agreement at all. If the changed text is correctable — if the US negotiators agree to restore language Iran accepted in an earlier version — Sunday's signing scenario remains viable. If the "greed" accusation reflects a fundamental disagreement over sanctions timing or Hormuz conditionality, the deal may not be as close as Thursday's market rally implied.

The reported draft deal terms — as circulated by Iranian state media — establish a sequential conditionality structure: the United States commits to lifting oil sanctions on Iran within 30 days of the agreement's signing, and Iran pledges to reopen the Strait of Hormuz to commercial traffic within 30 days of the US fulfilling its sanction-lift commitment. This sequencing places the initial obligation on the US side — sanctions lifted first — reflecting Iran's stated distrust of US commitments following the May 31 Situation Room collapse, when Trump added new demands after a deal was reportedly within reach. The draft also reportedly includes Iran's commitment to "never" pursue nuclear weapons, the core US demand throughout the conflict.

The economic significance of the sanctions-for-Hormuz exchange is substantial: Iranian oil exports have been effectively blocked since the conflict began, costing Tehran an estimated $4-8 billion per month in lost revenue. US oil sanctions lifted within 30 days would restore Iran's ability to export roughly 2-3 million barrels per day to global markets — particularly China and India, which have been operating under embargo pressure. Hormuz's reopening removes the global oil supply disruption that has added approximately $10-15 per barrel to WTI prices since the conflict's escalation. Both sides gain economically from the deal; the political cost is Iran accepting a non-nuclear pledge and the US accepting that it cannot maintain sanctions permanently without a military conclusion.

The ongoing US-Iran conflict has slowed global economic growth to its weakest pace since the Covid-19 pandemic — the IMF and World Bank project global GDP growth of 2.5% in 2026, down from 2.9% in 2025 — as the Hormuz transit risk premium, elevated oil prices, supply chain disruption from shipping rerouting, and the inflationary channel of commodity prices into consumer price indices have collectively constrained global economic activity for 104 days. The conflict's economic toll provides the clearest measure of what a ceasefire would unlock: analysts estimate a confirmed deal could restore 0.3-0.5 percentage points of annualized global growth, worth $800 billion to $1.2 trillion in economic activity, by removing the oil premium, restoring Hormuz transit, and enabling the Fed to resume its pre-conflict rate-cut trajectory.

The global growth downgrade has been particularly acute for energy-importing economies: Europe, Japan, India, and South Korea have all revised 2026 growth projections lower as energy costs have risen. China, which depends on Hormuz for approximately 40% of its crude oil imports, has been navigating the conflict through diplomatic channels with both the US and Iran — a diplomatic position that gives Beijing leverage as a deal facilitator if the current US-Iran negotiations stall. The ceasefire's economic dividend is global in scope, which is why so many parties beyond the US and Iran have a direct interest in Sunday's outcome.

Quick hits

  • Iran's "no final decision" language maps to a well-established pattern in the 104-day conflict: the supreme leader's office has consistently been the last to ratify or reject what negotiators agree at the working level. On May 28, Axios reported that the US and Iran had reached a deal but needed Trump's final approval — the mirror image of the current situation, where Trump has approved but Iran's supreme leader has not. The May 28 deal never got signed because Trump's Situation Room meeting on May 31 introduced new demands. The current iteration's durability depends on whether the "changed text" accusation reflects Trump's team having done the same thing again, or whether it reflects Iranian negotiators seeking better terms than what was agreed at the working level.
  • VP Vance's role at a potential signing ceremony in Europe carries diplomatic significance beyond the optics. A signing ceremony in a neutral European capital — rather than Washington or Tehran — maintains both sides' ability to frame the agreement domestically as a negotiated outcome rather than a capitulation. Iran's leadership would present the oil sanctions lift as a victory; Trump would present the never-nuclear commitment as the conflict's central achievement. The European venue signals that both sides are managing domestic political narratives as carefully as the deal's substance.
  • The global 2.5% growth projection assumes the conflict continues through end-2026. A confirmed ceasefire signed this weekend would trigger immediate upward revision of growth forecasts for the second half of 2026 — particularly in Europe and Asia-Pacific, where energy cost relief from lower oil prices and Hormuz normalization translates directly into consumer and industrial activity. The Fed's rate-cut trajectory, which has been held back by war-premium inflation in energy prices, would also resume — adding a monetary policy tailwind to the equity and bond market relief that a deal announcement would produce.
