Trump and Iran's President Pezeshkian digitally signed the 14-point US-Iran MOU on Wednesday — Trump at a dinner at the Palace of Versailles — cancelling the previously planned Geneva ceremony as both sides confirmed the agreement had entered into force, with the Strait of Hormuz reopening now underway and a 60-day ceasefire effective across all theatres. Israel is conducting "stubborn negotiations" to keep IDF troops in southern Lebanon.

The digital signing at Versailles is the culmination of five days of diplomacy that began with a Truth Social announcement and ended with both presidents putting their names on a document that has already reshaped global oil markets, financial markets, and the geopolitical risk premium embedded in asset prices worldwide. The Geneva ceremony — which had been planned for this Thursday — was formally cancelled once it became clear that both sides had already executed the MOU electronically: Trump and Vance had signed virtually during the G7 summit in Évian-les-Bains; Ghalibaf had signed for Iran's parliament. The Versailles dinner signatures from Trump and Pezeshkian elevated the document to head-of-government authority, converting a parliamentary-level agreement into a presidential one and removing any remaining ambiguity about the political commitment behind it. The 14-point MOU's terms are now in effect: the Strait of Hormuz will reopen to commercial traffic as the US and European Hormuz mission clear mines and the Iranian military stands down; the US naval blockade of Iranian ports is lifted; the 60-day ceasefire across all theatres extends to Lebanon; sanctions relief on Iranian oil exports takes effect; frozen Iranian assets begin to be released; and nuclear negotiations proceed in the second stage. The 60-day clock for nuclear talks has started. Israel's position on Lebanon — "stubborn negotiations" to maintain IDF presence south of the Litani River — is the clearest signal that the ceasefire's Lebanon provisions will face implementation pressure from a party that has not fully accepted the terms.

President Trump and Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian digitally signed the 14-point US-Iran MOU on Wednesday, with Trump signing at a dinner at the Palace of Versailles — cancelling the previously planned Geneva signing ceremony. Iran's Ministry of Foreign Affairs spokesperson confirmed the agreement had entered into force. Both sides simultaneously exchanged a hard copy of the signed document. The Geneva ceremony, which had been expected Thursday with Vice President Vance and Iran's parliamentary speaker Ghalibaf, was formally called off after the presidential-level signatures at Versailles made the ceremony redundant — the agreement was already signed, sealed, and in force by the time the dinner concluded.

The sequence of signings — electronic signatures from Trump, Vance, and Ghalibaf during the G7 in Évian, followed by the presidential dinner signatures at Versailles — reflects a diplomatic choreography that was designed to build authority incrementally: parliamentary-level execution first, then presidential endorsement. By signing at a Versailles dinner attended by other world leaders, Trump elevated the MOU from a bilateral executive agreement into an act witnessed by the international community's most powerful heads of government. The Versailles dinner context also means the agreement's execution was embedded in the G7's multilateral endorsement of the deal — the two events are inseparable as a diplomatic sequence. Iran's domestic political position is strengthened by the presidential-level signature: the IRGC and domestic hardliners who opposed the deal cannot argue that it was a parliamentary concession unsigned by the president when Pezeshkian's signature is on the document alongside Trump's.

ABC News published key takeaways from the 14-point memorandum of understanding Wednesday, providing the most complete public accounting of the agreement's terms. The MOU immediately reopens the Strait of Hormuz to commercial traffic without tolls, lifts the US naval blockade of Iranian ports, extends the ceasefire for 60 days across all theatres including Lebanon, grants sanctions relief on Iranian oil exports, and begins the release of frozen Iranian assets. The US committed to withdrawing its naval forces from positions immediately adjacent to Iranian ports within 30 days. Iran reiterated that it will not produce a nuclear weapon, and both sides agreed to begin the second stage of negotiations — covering Iran's nuclear program, enrichment levels, and IAEA verification — within the 60-day window.

The 14-point structure is notable for what it achieves in a single document: the military, economic, and diplomatic elements of a war termination agreement that typically require separate treaties and implementing agreements are bundled into one MOU that is already in force. The sanctions relief and frozen asset release, in particular, are economically significant for Iran — these are the terms that give the Iranian government and the IRGC tangible economic wins to point to domestically, reducing the internal political cost of the nuclear constraints that will be negotiated in the 60-day window. The Hormuz reopening provides the global supply benefit that was always the economic logic for pursuing the deal: WTI has declined from approximately $84 pre-deal to $75.01 as of Thursday morning, with further declines expected as commercial tanker traffic resumes and Iranian oil exports compound the supply picture.

A senior Israeli official confirmed Wednesday that Israel is conducting what the official described as "stubborn negotiations" with the United States to maintain IDF military presence in southern Lebanon south of the Litani River — despite the MOU's 60-day ceasefire provisions that extend to Lebanon. Israel's position remains that its Lebanon-Hezbollah operations are a separate bilateral matter from the US-Iran MOU framework, and IDF activity against Hezbollah positions in Lebanon has continued through the signing period. The MOU's Lebanon ceasefire provision creates a formal diplomatic tension: the signed agreement between the US and Iran includes Lebanon in the ceasefire terms, while Israel — a party to the Lebanon-Hezbollah conflict but not a signatory to the MOU — has not accepted those terms.

Israel's "stubborn negotiations" posture signals that the Lebanon question will be the most contentious implementation challenge in the 60-day nuclear window. For Iran, the ongoing IDF operations against Hezbollah — Iran's most strategically significant regional proxy — create domestic political pressure that complicates the nuclear talks: IRGC factions and Iranian hardliners can argue that Iran is making nuclear concessions while its allies are being attacked, reducing the IRGC's tolerance for generous nuclear terms. The G7's Évian statement had called explicitly for Lebanon ceasefire and Hezbollah disarmament, giving Western mediators a G7-backed argument for engaging Israel on the Lebanon front. Whether the US can persuade Israel to accept a modified Litani line arrangement that satisfies both Israel's security requirements and the MOU's ceasefire terms is the diplomatic variable that most directly affects whether the 60-day nuclear window can achieve its stated scope.

The International Energy Agency welcomed the US-Iran interim agreement and called for the Strait of Hormuz to be reopened "without conditions" — a formulation that signals the IEA's concern that implementation disputes, particularly around Israel's Lebanon position, could delay the physical reopening of the waterway beyond the timeline the global energy market needs. Several countries are actively reassessing their energy security policies in the aftermath of the Hormuz closure, which demonstrated the strategic vulnerability of routing approximately 20% of global oil supply through a single chokepoint. WTI crude stood at $75.01 Thursday morning, down approximately $9 from its pre-deal level near $84, with Brent at $78.20.

The IEA's "without conditions" formulation is diplomatically pointed: the MOU contains conditions — specifically, the ceasefire provisions, US naval withdrawal timeline, and the 30-day schedule for sanctions relief implementation — that could theoretically be used as grounds for delaying or conditionally opening the Strait. The IEA's call for an unconditional reopening is a preemptive signal to both sides that the energy market cannot absorb extended negotiations over the physical mechanics of the Hormuz reopening. The supply outlook with a clean Hormuz reopening and Iranian oil exports resuming under the MOU's sanctions waiver is more bullish than even optimistic pre-deal forecasts: the two supply channels compound to deliver more global oil than any single factor would have produced, and WTI's trajectory toward the $70s is supported by both the physical reopening and the sanctions waiver acting simultaneously.

Quick hits

  • The Versailles dinner setting is diplomatically significant beyond the symbolism. The Palace of Versailles is France's most powerful diplomatic venue — the site of the 1919 Treaty of Versailles, which ended World War I and established the post-war international order. By choosing Versailles as the location for the presidential-level signatures, France positioned itself as the convening power of a historic diplomatic moment while simultaneously hosting the G7 summit that endorsed the deal. President Macron's role in the MOU signing is part of a deliberate French diplomatic strategy: France has been the European country most actively engaged in the Iran-US mediation process, pledged naval assets to the Hormuz mission, and is now the location of the summit and dinner where the agreement was finalized. Paris emerges from the week as the EU's most consequential geopolitical player in the deal's execution, a position Macron has been cultivating since the conflict began.
  • The 60-day nuclear window's most consequential technical challenge is Iran's enriched uranium stockpile. Iran entered the MOU with a stockpile of uranium enriched to 60% — near weapons-grade, which requires 90% — and enrichment capacity significantly larger than in 2015 when the JCPOA was negotiated. Any nuclear agreement in the 60-day window must address what happens to that stockpile: transferring it to a third country (as the JCPOA contemplated), diluting it to lower enrichment levels, or leaving it in place under enhanced IAEA monitoring. Each option has domestic political costs for Iran and verification requirements for the US that will take time to negotiate. The 60-day window is tight for a stockpile agreement alone — it does not include the additional complexity of Iran's ballistic missile program and Hezbollah relationship, which the G7's Évian statement indicated should also be addressed.
  • WTI crude at $75.01 on Thursday morning represents approximately $9 of downward movement since the deal was announced — but markets have not yet fully priced the supply scenario from full Iranian export resumption. Iran's pre-war oil export capacity was approximately 3.2 million barrels per day, reduced to roughly 1.5-1.8 million barrels during the conflict and sanctions period. If Iranian exports return to near-capacity under the MOU's sanctions waiver, the additional supply entering the global market — roughly 1.4-1.7 million barrels per day — would represent a structural supply increase larger than the daily oil production of several OPEC+ members. OPEC+ has not announced its response to the MOU signing; an emergency coordination call is expected. WTI's path toward $70 or below depends on how quickly Iran's export capacity scales and whether OPEC+ responds with compensating cuts — and as of Thursday morning, neither is resolved.
