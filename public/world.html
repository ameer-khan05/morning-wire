Iran submits a 14-point peace plan — Trump says he "can't imagine" it acceptable and warns strikes could resume. The IRGC issues a deadline for the US blockade to end and declares Iran "fully prepared" for resumed conflict. The IDF expands evacuations north of Lebanon's Litani River for the first time.

Iran presented a formal 14-point peace proposal through Pakistan on Saturday — the most structured diplomatic submission since the ceasefire began in April — calling for a 30-day window to negotiate war termination in exchange for the lifting of the US naval blockade, reparations, the release of frozen assets, guarantees against future military aggression, withdrawal of US forces from Iran's periphery, and new Strait of Hormuz governance. President Trump confirmed Sunday he was briefed on the "concept of the deal" but said he "can't imagine" it would be acceptable and warned that air strikes could resume if Iran "misbehaves." The IRGC simultaneously issued a deadline for the US to end its port blockade, declaring US options are "an impossible military operation or a bad deal" and that Iran is "fully prepared" for resumed conflict — a coordinated dual-track that combines the most substantive diplomatic proposal yet with the most direct military threat yet on the same day. On Lebanon's parallel front, the IDF issued evacuation orders for towns north of the Litani River for the first time, a geographic expansion of its operational posture that signals IDF preparation for ground movement into new territory as the May 14 ceasefire expiry approaches with 11 days remaining. Lebanon's health ministry reports 73 people killed and 163 injured since its last casualty update on April 30.

The Anchor

Iran's 14-point plan and the IRGC's deadline arrived on the same Saturday — the conflict's most sophisticated diplomatic submission paired with its most direct military threat. Trump reviews the concept; the oil market prices no resolution.

Iran's Saturday dual-track communication — submitting a 14-point peace proposal via Pakistan while simultaneously having the IRGC issue a deadline for the US to end its naval blockade — is the most revealing single diplomatic move of the 65-day conflict. The 14-point proposal, as reported by NPR and Al Jazeera, calls for a 30-day window to finalize war termination terms. Its demands include the lifting of the US naval blockade, payment of reparations, release of all frozen Iranian assets, guarantees against future military aggression, withdrawal of US forces from Iran's periphery, an end to the naval blockade (repeated as a separate article), the lifting of sanctions, an end to fighting in Lebanon, and a new mechanism governing the Strait of Hormuz. The proposal's structure — 14 specific articles rather than a broad framework — represents a significant Iranian investment in the diplomatic track and a departure from the vaguer proposals that preceded it. The IRGC's simultaneous deadline declaration — characterizing US options as "an impossible military operation or a bad deal" — is the military counterweight that the regime's internal politics required: the hardline Revolutionary Guard factions cannot be seen to have allowed a diplomatic submission without a concurrent demonstration of military preparedness. The dual-track is coherent as an Iranian domestic political strategy, not a negotiating contradiction.

Trump's Sunday review of the "concept of the deal" — while stating he "can't imagine" the terms are acceptable — is the US counterpart to Iran's dual-track: engaging with the diplomatic submission at the conceptual level while maintaining the maximal public posture. Trump's statement that air strikes could resume if Iran "misbehaves" is the US military-option counterweight to the IRGC's deadline. Both sides are therefore holding open the diplomatic channel (Iran via 14 points, US via review of the concept) while simultaneously signaling military credibility (IRGC via deadline, US via Trump's strike threat). The structure of the exchange is classic coercive diplomacy: both parties need a diplomatic channel to be visible to domestic audiences and international mediators, and both need a military threat visible to the same audiences. The Pakistan mediation track — which produced the original ceasefire and has transmitted every subsequent proposal — remains the sole institutional infrastructure bridging the two sides. Pakistan's role as both diplomatic conduit and implicit guarantor of the process is the most underappreciated element of the conflict's management.

Iran's 14-point proposal: a 30-day negotiating window in exchange for US blockade lifting, reparations, frozen asset release, and Hormuz governance terms. Trump confirms he is reviewing the "concept." Secretary Rubio dismisses the proposal publicly.

Iran's 14-point peace proposal — the most detailed diplomatic submission since the conflict began February 28 — represents a significant structural shift from prior proposals. Where previous Iranian submissions were broad frameworks, the 14-point document enumerates specific demands in article form: lifting of the US naval blockade; payment of war reparations; release of all frozen Iranian assets (approximately $7 billion held in South Korean and other accounts); guarantees against future military aggression; withdrawal of US forces from Iran's "periphery" (the three-carrier formation in the region); an end to fighting in Lebanon; lifting of pre-existing sanctions; and a new mechanism for governing the Strait of Hormuz. The 30-day timeline for finalizing terms reflects Iran's stated preference for rapid resolution — a 30-day window versus the longer transition periods the US has favored in prior exchanges. Trump confirmed Sunday he was briefed on the "concept of the deal" by his national security team and said he would receive the "exact wording" shortly — a review posture, not an acceptance. Secretary Rubio publicly dismissed the proposal in comments to ABC News as not meeting US minimum requirements. The simultaneous Trump (reviewing concept) and Rubio (dismissing) signals suggest the administration is managing its internal messaging, with Rubio serving as the hawkish anchor while Trump preserves optionality for a deal if Iran can be moved further on the Hormuz and nuclear enrichment issues. The sticking points — Hormuz access guarantees, nuclear enrichment rights, sanctions relief sequencing — are the same three that have not moved since the ceasefire was established.

The IRGC issues a deadline for the US blockade to end — "an impossible military operation or a bad deal" — declaring Iran "fully prepared" for resumed conflict. The deadline and the 14-point plan arrived on the same Saturday: a coordinated dual-track, not a contradiction.

The IRGC's declaration that US options are limited to "an impossible military operation or a bad deal" — issued the same day Iran submitted its 14-point proposal — is designed to be read as a coordinated dual-track signal rather than an inconsistency. The logic is Iranian domestic politics: the IRGC hardliners who represent the Revolutionary Guard faction require a visible military posture to accept the regime's diplomatic engagement; the dual-track allows the Pezeshkian government to negotiate through Pakistan while IRGC commanders maintain their institutional credibility with their own constituency. The specific IRGC statement — "fully prepared for resumed conflict" — is the Iranian equivalent of Trump's "blast them away": a rhetorical threat designed to maintain deterrence credibility without committing to a specific action or timeline. The practical question the IRGC deadline creates is: does it have a specific date? Current reporting from CBS and CNN indicates no hard date has been publicly disclosed — the deadline is a political statement, not a calendar event. Iran's parliament is advancing a 12-article Hormuz sovereignty law that would formalize the IRGC's toll collection on Strait traffic — the IRGC deposited the first $2 million toll into Iran's Central Bank on April 23, establishing a revenue-generating status quo that the 14-point plan's "Hormuz governance" article would convert from de facto control to formal agreement.

Lebanon · Litani Evacuation · IDF Expands North of Litani IDF evacuation orders north of Litani — first time · 73 killed since April 30 · May 14 expiry 11 days · 163 injured · no renewal framework

IDF issues evacuation orders for towns north of the Litani River — the first time since the ceasefire began. Lebanon's health ministry reports 73 killed and 163 injured since April 30. The May 14 ceasefire expiry is 11 days away with no renewal framework.

The IDF's evacuation warnings for towns north of the Litani River represent the most significant geographic escalation signal in Lebanon since the ceasefire took effect in April. The Litani River — running roughly east-west across Lebanon approximately 28 kilometers north of the Israeli border — was established as the northern boundary of the IDF's declared operations zone under the ceasefire framework, with IDF forces deployed south of the Litani and evacuation orders previously limited to that zone. Issuing evacuation orders north of the Litani for the first time signals one of two things: the IDF is preparing for ground operations that will cross the Litani into territory it has not previously entered under the current ceasefire framework, or it is signaling to Hezbollah that the current ceasefire framework's geographic boundaries are no longer the IDF's operational constraint. Either interpretation carries the same implication for the May 14 ceasefire expiry: the IDF is preparing to operate in territory beyond the ceasefire's original scope, and the May 14 date is the calendar point at which the remaining ceasefire constraints formally expire. Lebanon's health ministry's updated toll — 73 killed, 163 injured since its April 30 update — represents three days of casualty accumulation in what is technically a ceasefire period. The Lebanon situation is not a ceasefire deteriorating; it is a ceasefire that exists only on paper while combat continues north and south of the Litani.

Iran Parliament · Hormuz Law · 12 Articles 12 Articles Full chamber debate · $2M first toll deposited April 23 · IRGC 5nm corridor · Qeshm-Larak · formalized toll infrastructure

Iran's parliament advances a 12-article Hormuz sovereignty law to full chamber debate — formalizing the toll collection infrastructure the IRGC has already implemented. The first $2 million toll was deposited April 23. The law converts de facto control into legislative permanence.

Iran's National Security and Foreign Policy Committee ratified a 12-article Hormuz sovereignty law on April 21, advancing it to full chamber debate in the Majles. The law is the legislative infrastructure to institutionalize what the IRGC has already implemented operationally: a five-nautical-mile shipping corridor between Qeshm Island and Larak Island, entirely within Iranian territorial waters, through which diverted vessels are routed and charged a $2 million toll. The first toll was deposited into Iran's Central Bank on April 23, establishing a revenue-generating status quo. The parliamentary law converts this de facto arrangement from an IRGC operational decision into a formal Iranian sovereign claim over Hormuz transit — a legally significant step that changes the diplomatic and commercial landscape around any future Hormuz reopening agreement. The 14-point peace proposal's "new mechanism governing the Strait of Hormuz" article is directly responsive to this parliamentary process: the Iranian proposal is implicitly offering to resolve the Hormuz governance question through international agreement rather than unilateral Iranian law — but the leverage for that offer comes from the law's advancement, which demonstrates that Iran is prepared to formalize the status quo if no agreement is reached. The Hormuz sovereignty law and the 14-point proposal are the carrot-and-stick pair of Iran's current diplomatic strategy.

Spirit Airlines · First Economic Casualty Grounded WTI $104 destroys ULCC model · 11,000 employees · 4M passenger tickets · 25 years since last major US airline shutdown · conflict economic ripple

Spirit Airlines grounds all operations — the first major US airline shutdown in 25 years and the first corporate entity to be destroyed by the Iran conflict's oil price regime. WTI at $104 eliminated Spirit's ultra-low-cost margin model.

Spirit Airlines' decision to ground all operations on Saturday makes it the first major US airline to cease operations entirely since the post-9/11 collapse of Midway Airlines in 2001 — and, more significantly, the first corporate entity in the United States to be directly destroyed by the economic consequences of the Iran-conflict oil price regime. Spirit's ultra-low-cost carrier model depended on jet fuel being priced sufficiently low to allow fares below legacy carriers while maintaining positive operating margins. WTI crude at $104 per barrel — sustained for more than two months by the Hormuz blockade's disruption of global oil supply — has eliminated that margin equation permanently, not cyclically. Spirit cannot raise fares to cover the fuel cost without losing its price-sensitive customer base; it cannot sustain losses at $104 WTI on a margin structure designed for $65-75 WTI. The shutdown affects approximately 11,000 employees and roughly 4 million passengers holding future Spirit tickets. The Spirit shutdown is the conflict's first measurable corporate casualty in the United States — an event that translates the Hormuz blockade's abstract economic consequences into the specific, visible closure of an airline with a domestic customer base. It will not be the last: other ultra-low-cost and thin-margin carriers face the same structural mathematics at $104 WTI, on a longer timeline, unless oil prices decline substantially before their fuel hedges expire.

Continuing: the Hormuz food crisis compounds — 65 days of 90% shipping disruption has now locked in fertilizer price shocks into 2026 planting season decisions, with harvest consequences arriving Q3-Q4 regardless of when the conflict resolves.

The Hormuz shipping collapse — down more than 90% since the conflict began February 28, now at 65 days — has produced a specific agricultural crisis dynamic that is now partially independent of the diplomatic timeline. Urea and ammonia fertilizer prices have risen approximately 40% since the conflict began; these are inputs for the 2026 growing season, and farming decisions based on current fertilizer prices are being made right now, in May. The planting-season commitment means that even a Hormuz resolution in June would not prevent the harvest impact from arriving in Q3 and Q4 2026 — the fertilizer cost shock is already embedded in this year's crop economics. The UN Food and Agriculture Organization identifies 24 countries with acute food-insecurity vulnerability to the current price shock, predominantly in sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and the Horn of Africa. Iran's 14-point plan contains an "end to the naval blockade" article; if the proposal progresses toward agreement, the Hormuz shipping resumption would begin to compress fertilizer prices on a 4-8 week lead time. But the 30-day negotiating window Iran proposes, followed by whatever implementation timeline is agreed, means Hormuz reopening is at earliest late June — too late to reverse the 2026 planting-season economics that are being finalized in May.

Quick hits

  • The 14-point proposal's 30-day timeline demand — Iran wants to finalize war termination terms within 30 days of agreement in principle — is at direct odds with the US preference for a longer, phased transition that separates Hormuz reopening (which would happen early) from nuclear enrichment resolution (which requires extensive verification). The 30-day timeline reflects Iran's domestic political logic: the regime's leadership cannot sustain a diplomatic process that extends indefinitely without producing visible concessions, given 65 days of internet blackout, economic pressure, and IRGC internal politics. The US's preference for a longer phased timeline reflects exactly the verification requirements the 30-day window cannot accommodate. The timeline incompatibility is as significant a sticking point as the substantive issues of Hormuz access and nuclear enrichment rights.
  • Secretary Rubio's public dismissal of the Iranian proposal — reported by ABC News — while Trump simultaneously said he is "reviewing the concept" represents the same good-cop/bad-cop dynamic the administration deployed throughout the original ceasefire negotiation. Rubio maintains the maximalist public posture, preserving the administration's leverage; Trump preserves optionality for an eventual deal by engaging at the conceptual level without endorsing any specific term. The risk of this approach is that Iran reads Rubio's dismissal as the operative US position — leading it to escalate rather than negotiate. The IRGC's deadline is the Iranian response to the previous version of this dynamic (Trump's "not satisfied" rejection of Friday's proposal), and the pattern suggests that each US rejection produces an Iranian escalation signal before the next proposal is submitted.
  • The IDF's evacuation orders north of the Litani River create a specific legal context for the May 14 ceasefire expiry. The ceasefire's geographic scope was defined by the Litani as the northern boundary of IDF operations; issuing evacuation orders north of it while the ceasefire is technically in force is either a preparation signal (we are planning to go there) or a pressure signal (we are demonstrating we can go there). Either interpretation tells Lebanon's civilian population that the territory north of the Litani — which has not been subject to formal evacuation orders since March — is now within the IDF's declared operational zone. The humanitarian implications, for a region that has already seen over a million people displaced, are significant: the north-of-Litani population is Lebanon's interior, including towns and cities that have not yet experienced the full weight of the conflict's ground phase.
  • Iran's 65-day internet blackout — affecting 90 million people, still the longest national internet shutdown for a country of this size in the modern era — has a specific relevance to the 14-point proposal. The Iranian public cannot access international reporting on the conflict's diplomatic status, the 14-point plan's contents, or Trump's response to it. The regime controls the information environment completely; what Iranians know about the proposal and the US response is what the regime chooses to tell them. This information asymmetry gives the regime significant domestic political flexibility: it can frame the 14-point submission as a reasonable peace offer that the US rejected, sustaining the narrative that the conflict is driven by US aggression rather than Iranian nuclear ambitions or Hormuz control. The blackout is not just an information control measure; it is a diplomatic leverage tool that insulates the regime from domestic accountability for the proposal's terms.
  • The Pakistan mediation track has now transmitted multiple proposals in both directions over 65 days, which itself creates a form of institutional momentum. Pakistan's role — acknowledged by both Trump and Iranian officials — means there is a functioning diplomatic channel that both parties are using even while publicly maintaining adversarial postures. The track's durability is partly a product of Pakistan's geographic and strategic position (bordering both Iran and having a historically close relationship with the US on security matters), partly a product of its non-belligerence (Pakistan is not a party to the conflict and has no direct military stake in its outcome), and partly a product of the personal relationships that Pakistani Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif and his foreign ministry have developed with both the Trump administration and the Pezeshkian government. The 14-point proposal's transmission via Pakistan is a sign that the mediation track is robust enough to handle the conflict's most detailed diplomatic exchange yet.
  • The 11 days remaining before Lebanon's May 14 ceasefire expiry, combined with the IDF's north-of-Litani evacuation orders, creates a specific countdown dynamic for the US's strategic positioning. The administration brokered both the original Lebanon ceasefire and its three-week extension; it is the guarantor of the arrangement. If the IDF is now operating — or preparing to operate — north of the Litani before May 14, the US faces the same choice it has been avoiding: confront Israel about ceasefire violations, or accept the expiry without a renewal and formalize that Lebanon has returned to active combat. The first choice risks a diplomatic rupture with Israel during an Iran negotiation; the second choice absorbs the geopolitical cost of a failed US-brokered ceasefire but removes the ambiguity that the current "ceasefire in name only" status creates. Trump's administration chose ambiguity in the Iran nuclear deal; it is likely to choose the same on Lebanon's expiry unless the IDF's north-of-Litani operations produce a civilian casualty toll that makes continued silence politically untenable.
