Day 90: A deal with Iran was "largely negotiated" according to President Trump on Saturday — then the US launched three rounds of military strikes on Iranian targets over the following four days. Iran's IRGC called the strikes an "egregious ceasefire violation" and warned retaliation is "legitimate and certain." Trump said Wednesday he won't rush the deal and Iran is "negotiating on fumes." Iran's 88-day nationwide internet blackout is beginning to lift.

The Iran conflict in Day 90 is simultaneously closer to resolution and more militarily active than at any point since the April 8 ceasefire. Trump's May 23 "largely negotiated" statement — backed by the Axios report of a specific deal framework on May 24 — represents the most concrete deal progress since the ceasefire's declaration: a 60-day extension, Hormuz reopening, Iran oil sales, and nuclear talks to follow. The WTI oil market priced this at face value: crude fell from $95-100 toward $87 in days as the deal scenario was discounted. But the US military's subsequent strikes — on missile launch sites and IRGC boats on May 25-26, and on a drone-launch ground control station near Bandar Abbas on May 27 — have complicated the narrative in two directions simultaneously. On one side: the strikes confirm that the US is maintaining military pressure that may be intended to accelerate Iranian concessions before the deal is signed. On the other: the strikes provide the IRGC's hardline faction with the "ceasefire violation" justification it needs to block or delay Pezeshkian's government from signing a deal. Trump's Wednesday statement — "I won't rush it, Iran is negotiating on fumes" — is the diplomatic language of a party that believes it has the leverage and the time to hold the current terms.

The Anchor

The US and Iran have never been closer to a deal since the conflict began — and they have never simultaneously been more militarily active in the same week. These are not contradictory facts. They are the dual-track design of a deal being concluded under fire: maximum pressure and maximum diplomacy running at the same time.

The analytical framework for understanding the May 23-28 sequence requires separating two narratives that the daily news cycle conflates: the diplomatic track (deal progress) and the military track (continuing strikes). On the diplomatic track, the evidence is the most positive since February 28: Trump said "largely negotiated" on May 23; Axios reported on May 24 that the deal includes a 60-day ceasefire extension, Strait of Hormuz reopening, permission for Iran to sell oil freely, and deferred nuclear talks; the WTI oil market — the most liquid real-time indicator of deal probability — fell $21 in a week, pricing the scenario at close to certainty. On the military track, the evidence is that the ceasefire remains what it has been since April 8: a managed reduction in intensity, not a genuine cessation. US Central Command's self-defense strikes on Iranian missile sites, boats, and a drone-launch control station are being conducted within the ceasefire's operational parameters — the US characterizes them as defensive responses to Iranian military activity near the Strait, not as escalations beyond the ceasefire framework. Iran's IRGC characterizes them as ceasefire violations. Both statements are simultaneously true within their respective legal interpretations.

The critical dynamic is how the strikes interact with the Pezeshkian government's ability to complete and sign the deal. The IRGC's "egregious ceasefire violation" statement and retaliation warning are the hardline faction's attempt to establish that the US strikes provide Iran the justification to walk away from a deal that the IRGC has opposed from the beginning. Pezeshkian's government has been working toward a deal as the economic pressure of the Hormuz blockade — $75-80 million in daily hard currency shortfall at current reduced export volumes — has become severe enough to force movement from the May 20 deadlock. The internet blackout's partial restoration — Pezeshkian directed a return to pre-December 2025 connectivity conditions on May 26 — is the most direct public signal that the government is normalizing domestic conditions in anticipation of a deal announcement. Governments do not restore internet access as a confidence-building measure in active conflict escalation; they restore it as a signal of returning normalcy. The IRGC's retaliation warning and the US ballistic missile interception near Kuwait (which CENTCOM called an "egregious ceasefire violation" by Iran on its part) are the competing signals. What happens in the next 48-72 hours — whether the IRGC's warning translates into action or the Pezeshkian government concludes the deal despite IRGC opposition — will determine whether the conflict is in its final days or faces another 30-day extension of ambiguity.

The Stories

Deal "Largely Negotiated" · 60-Day Framework · Hormuz Reopens Largely Trump May 23 "largely negotiated" · "will be announced soon" · Axios May 24 deal details · 60-day ceasefire extension · Hormuz reopens · Iran oil freely sold · nuclear talks to follow · WTI fell $21 in a week · deferred: enrichment moratorium details · verification · sanctions relief timeline

President Trump told reporters Saturday that a deal with Iran is "largely negotiated" and would be "announced soon" — the most positive diplomatic characterization the administration has offered since the April 8 ceasefire. On May 24, Axios reported the deal's specific terms: a 60-day ceasefire extension, a reopening of the Strait of Hormuz to commercial traffic, permission for Iran to sell oil freely during the period, and deferred negotiations on the nuclear enrichment moratorium and longer-term sanctions relief. WTI fell from $95-100 toward $87 in the days following Trump's statement.

The Axios report's deal framework is structurally different from the failed Pakistan-mediated framework described in the May 20 edition. The Pakistan framework attempted to negotiate the complete nuclear deal — including the enrichment moratorium duration (20-year US versus 5-year Iran gap) — before agreeing to ceasefire extension and Hormuz reopening. The "largely negotiated" framework inverts this sequence: Hormuz reopens and Iran sells oil freely for 60 days, and the nuclear negotiations take place during the 60-day window rather than as a precondition to the relief. This sequencing change is significant because it addresses Iran's core structural distrust: rather than asking Iran to make irreversible nuclear concessions (uranium delivery, facility closure) before receiving sanctions relief, the framework provides oil revenue relief immediately while nuclear talks proceed. The 60-day window creates a deadline for the nuclear framework without requiring it to be resolved before the Hormuz opening. Whether the IRGC's hardline faction will accept a deal that reopens the strait — their most powerful leverage against both the US economy and the Pezeshkian government — before the nuclear terms are fully locked in remains the central question. The May 25-27 strikes provide the IRGC's "ceasefire violation" justification for blocking the deal, if that is what the hardliners intend to do.

US Central Command conducted three rounds of military strikes on Iranian targets between May 25-27. On May 25-26, CENTCOM struck Iranian missile launch sites and IRGC small boats near the Strait of Hormuz in response to 24 hours of Iranian missile, drone, and small boat launches. On May 27, CENTCOM struck a drone-launch ground control station near Bandar Abbas, shot down four Iranian attack drones, and struck an Iranian ground control station that was about to launch a fifth drone. The US characterized all strikes as self-defense within the ceasefire's operational framework; Iran called them ceasefire violations.

The tactical logic of the US strikes — conducted while the deal is "largely negotiated" — can be read in two ways. The first reading is military: the IRGC's May 24-26 launches of missiles, drones, and boat provocations near the Strait are independent of the diplomatic track, conducted by the IRGC's operational commanders who have not been fully read into the deal negotiations and are executing standing orders to pressure US naval presence in the Gulf. The US response is the force protection mandate that applies regardless of diplomatic progress. The second reading is strategic: the strikes are deliberate US leverage actions designed to maintain military pressure on Iran as the deal's final terms are negotiated, ensuring that Iran's IRGC cannot interpret the "largely negotiated" language as American weakness or willingness to stop military pressure before the deal is signed. Both readings can be simultaneously true. The Pezeshkian government's challenge is that the IRGC's "egregious violation" characterization — and the retaliatory ballistic missile launched toward Kuwait (intercepted by Kuwaiti forces) — provides the hardline faction the justification it needs to demand a pause in deal discussions until the US "demonstrates good faith" by stopping strikes. This is the standard diplomatic maneuver for a party that wants to delay an agreement: demand a precondition that the other party cannot easily meet.

Iran's IRGC issued a formal statement characterizing the May 25-27 US strikes as "egregious ceasefire violations" and warning that the Islamic Republic considers its "right to reciprocal response to be legitimate and certain." Iran's foreign ministry said the US had continued "unlawful and unjust actions" since the ceasefire announcement. Separately, CENTCOM reported that Iran launched a ballistic missile toward Kuwait that was successfully intercepted by Kuwaiti forces — an incident CENTCOM characterized as an additional Iranian ceasefire violation.

The IRGC's "egregious violation" language and the ballistic missile launch toward Kuwait serve distinct strategic purposes for Iran's hardline faction. The "egregious violation" characterization is directed at the Pezeshkian government's domestic political audience: it establishes the IRGC's narrative that the US cannot be trusted to honor ceasefire terms, directly echoing the Iranian FM's "cannot trust the Americans" statement from the May 20 Pakistan talks. If the IRGC can sustain this narrative internally — that the US is violating the ceasefire rather than responding to Iranian provocations — it makes it politically harder for Pezeshkian to sign a deal that requires Iran to make concessions to the "ceasefire violator." The ballistic missile toward Kuwait is the operational dimension of this strategy: it demonstrates that the IRGC can escalate beyond the Hormuz region, threatening Gulf Cooperation Council states that would be affected by any deal framework, and raising the stakes for the regional parties who have been facilitating negotiations (Kuwait, Qatar, Oman). Whether the Pezeshkian government reads the IRGC's actions as IRGC independence that requires managing, or as IRGC veto power that requires accommodation, is the internal governance question that the outside world cannot observe directly. Trump's Wednesday characterization of Iran as "negotiating on fumes" suggests the US believes Pezeshkian's economic pressure — not the IRGC's political opposition — will be the decisive factor.

President Trump told his cabinet Wednesday that he will not rush the Iran deal and described Iran as "negotiating on fumes" — language that signals US confidence in the leverage position and patience for Iran's domestic economic pressure to drive the final concessions. Trump also confirmed the midterm election cycle will not impact his Iran strategy, pushing back against Democratic criticism that the strikes were politically motivated to prevent a deal before November. NPR reported both statements from the cabinet meeting.

Trump's "negotiating on fumes" characterization is the US side's economic pressure thesis stated explicitly: Iran's hard currency reserves, depleted at $75-80 million per day since February 28 (the difference between pre-conflict oil revenues of approximately $150 million per day at 1.5 million barrels per day and current reduced revenues of approximately $70-75 million per day at 600,000 barrels per day), have been drawn down enough that Iran cannot sustain the current operational posture indefinitely. At an estimated $100-120 billion in reserves before the conflict and a $75-80 million daily burn rate, Iran has approximately 40-50 months of reserves remaining before critical depletion — a timeline that extends well beyond any US domestic political calendar but that is psychologically significant when the Pezeshkian government is managing a population experiencing internet blackout and economic contraction simultaneously. The "won't rush" framing is Trump establishing that US patience exceeds Iran's economic endurance, and that the US will not make additional concessions to accelerate a deal that Iran needs more than the US does. This framing is consistent with the deal structure reported by Axios: a 60-day Hormuz reopening with nuclear talks deferred is precisely the kind of agreement that a party with patience would offer — it provides Iran immediate economic relief (oil sales) while preserving US leverage for the harder nuclear framework negotiations that follow.

Iran Internet · 88-Day Blackout Ending · Pezeshkian Directive 88 Days Iran nationwide internet blackout · 88 days since Feb 28 · 2,093+ hours · Pezeshkian May 26 directive · state TV announcement · return to pre-December 2025 conditions · regime domestic normalization signal · internal chaos over connectivity restoration · deal imminence indicator

After 88 consecutive days — 2,093+ hours — of a near-total nationwide internet blackout implemented when the conflict began on February 28, Iranian President Pezeshkian issued a directive on May 26 to restore internet access to the conditions that existed before the December 2025-January 2026 uprisings. The announcement was made by the government spokesperson on state television. The blackout, unprecedented in duration for any country, had been accompanied by escalating internal chaos within the regime over whether to restore connectivity and what political conditions to attach to the restoration.

The timing of Pezeshkian's internet restoration directive — one day after Trump's "largely negotiated" statement and two days after the Axios deal framework report — is the strongest behavioral signal available that the Pezeshkian government is preparing for a deal announcement and the domestic normalization that would follow. Authoritarian governments do not restore internet access as a concession during active military conflict escalation; they restore it when they are preparing to announce a policy outcome they want their population to receive as positive news. The "return to pre-December 2025 conditions" framing is significant: December 2025-January 2026 was a period of widespread domestic protests that the regime used the internet shutdown partially to suppress. Returning to that connectivity state — rather than fully pre-conflict connectivity — allows the government to restore economic functionality while maintaining the social media monitoring and selective censorship infrastructure it had in place before the protests began. For the market, the internet restoration is the clearest non-verbal signal from inside Iran that the Pezeshkian government believes the deal is real and imminent. Governments preparing for protracted conflict duration do not restore connectivity; governments preparing to announce deals do.

Quick hits

  • The 60-day Hormuz reopening framework reported by Axios deserves analysis of what "60 days" specifically means for the oil market, the Fed, and the nuclear negotiations. If the Strait reopens in the next 5-10 days, WTI would likely fall toward $78-83 within 2-3 weeks as the Hormuz oil premium (currently estimated at approximately $8-12 above pre-conflict baseline) is progressively unwound. At WTI $80-83 for June, the June PCE energy component would decline materially from April's levels — the year-over-year oil comparison would go from highly inflationary to neutral or slightly deflationary. This would allow the July PCE (reporting June prices) to show the first meaningful inflation moderation since the conflict began, which would reach the Fed before the September FOMC meeting and reduce the rate-hike probability from 39% toward 5-10%. The 60-day framework is therefore the mechanism by which the PCE-driven rate-hike risk resolves — if and only if the deal is completed in the next week, not if it is delayed by the IRGC for another 30 days. Every week of delay in the deal completion delays the oil price relief by a week, which delays the CPI moderation by a month, which delays the Fed's rate-hike probability reduction by a full calendar quarter.
  • The IRGC's ballistic missile launch toward Kuwait — intercepted by Kuwaiti forces and reported by CENTCOM as an additional ceasefire violation — is the most geographically significant military event since the conflict began. Previous Iranian strikes and provocations have been concentrated on the Strait of Hormuz and its immediate environs. A ballistic missile toward Kuwait represents a geographic expansion of the conflict to a Gulf Cooperation Council state that hosts significant US military assets (Camp Arifjan, Ali Al Salem Air Base, Camp Buehring) and is a major oil transshipment hub. Kuwait's government has not publicly commented on the missile interception as of Thursday morning. The incident creates three risks: if Kuwait publicly condemns the launch, it increases GCC pressure on Iran to conclude the deal; if Kuwait privately messages Iran directly, it may add a fifth mediating channel alongside Oman, Pakistan, Qatar, and Turkey; if the IRGC views the Kuwait launch as a successful escalation test, it may repeat it against other GCC states, escalating the conflict geographically in ways that would make deal completion politically harder for Pezeshkian domestically and diplomatically with the GCC.
  • Trump's "midterms won't impact my strategy" statement addresses the most prevalent Democratic criticism of the deal's timing: that the administration is rushing a deal before November to generate a political win. Trump's denial is the expected political response, but the underlying criticism identifies a real dynamic: the November 2026 midterm elections are approximately 23 weeks away, and a conflict that has driven PCE to 3.8%, 10-year yields to 4.657% at peak, and Bitcoin down 12% from early-May highs is a net economic negative for the incumbent party's congressional candidates. A deal that resolves the Iran conflict, reopens Hormuz, and sends oil from $90 back toward $75-80 would reduce PCE, lower yields, and improve consumer purchasing power in time for the November economic data that voters reference. The administration's political incentives and the deal's merits are aligned — which makes Trump's "won't rush" statement harder to assess: it could mean genuine patience, or it could mean that the administration is managing the announcement timing to maximize political impact. The market, which does not care about political timing, will price the oil impact of the deal the moment it is announced, regardless of which week in June or July the announcement occurs.
  • Iran's domestic governance dynamic — the tension between the Pezeshkian government (elected on a reform and economic normalization platform) and the IRGC (which controls significant economic interests dependent on the conflict's continuation, including the black-market oil sales to China at a discount) — is the internal constraint that the US economic pressure thesis is attempting to work through. The IRGC benefits economically from the conflict in a specific way: as the entity managing Iran's reduced oil exports to China (approximately 600,000 barrels per day at $15-20 discount to market), the IRGC controls a significant hard currency revenue stream that flows outside normal government budget channels. A deal that reopens Hormuz and allows Iran to export freely at market prices would transfer that revenue stream from IRGC-managed shadow channels back to official government accounts — reducing the IRGC's economic power while increasing Pezeshkian's. The IRGC's opposition to the deal therefore has an economic self-interest dimension that goes beyond the nuclear ideology: losing control of the black-market oil revenue channel reduces the IRGC's patronage networks, military budget autonomy, and political leverage over the civilian government. Trump's "negotiating on fumes" thesis is correct if Pezeshkian's government is the decisive actor; it is less clearly correct if the IRGC's economic interests in the conflict's continuation constitute an internal veto that Pezeshkian cannot override.
  • The Lebanon conflict provides a useful secondary diagnostic for the Iran deal's progress. Lebanon's ceasefire was extended 45 days on May 15 (fourth round of talks scheduled June 2-3, Pentagon security track May 29). Iran's insistence that Lebanon must be covered by any Iran-US ceasefire framework remains one of the deal's secondary sticking points. If the Iran-US deal framework reported by Axios is finalized, the Lebanon ceasefire extension and the Iran framework would align: the 60-day Iran framework and the 45-day Lebanon extension (expiring approximately June 29) both create a diplomatic window through late June in which comprehensive regional settlement talks can proceed. The June 2-3 Lebanon political talks and the May 29 Pentagon security track are scheduled to occur before the Iran deal announcement, providing the diplomatic infrastructure for a broader regional settlement if Iran closes the deal in the next 5-10 days. The sequencing appears deliberate: the Lebanon tracks are advancing in parallel, creating the regional framework that would make the Iran deal's strategic context coherent rather than a bilateral deal in isolation. Whether the IRGC's ballistic missile toward Kuwait disrupts this coordination — by drawing Kuwait into the Lebanese/Iran diplomatic framework in an adversarial rather than facilitative role — is the near-term risk from that incident.
  • The Iran internet restoration's specific framing — "return to pre-December 2025 conditions" — is worth parsing for what it excludes as well as what it includes. Pre-December 2025 Iran had internet access that was filtered and monitored but broadly functional for economic and social purposes: VPN use was widespread, international services (Google, Instagram, WhatsApp, Telegram) were accessible with varying levels of restriction, and domestic e-commerce, banking, and media were fully operational. "Pre-December 2025 conditions" excludes the December 2025-January 2026 protest-driven additional restrictions that the regime imposed before the February 28 conflict — restrictions that were tighter than the already-filtered norm. It also excludes the full blackout that began February 28 and has lasted 88 days. The net effect of Pezeshkian's directive: Iran's internet will return to the "normal" Islamic Republic filtering environment — better than the February-May blackout, worse than fully free internet, and specific enough to allow economic activity, international business communications, and domestic normal life to resume. For international businesses that have been unable to operate normally in Iran since February 28, the restoration is the first signal that normal commercial communications may be possible as the deal approaches. For Iranians who have been economically and informationally isolated for 88 days, the restoration is a tangible improvement that the Pezeshkian government can point to as evidence that the deal process is producing results.
