The Anchor

UAE exits OPEC effective May 1 — 57 years ends as Iran war accelerates the Gulf coalition's fracture.

The United Arab Emirates announced Tuesday it will withdraw from OPEC and OPEC+ effective May 1 — ending a membership of nearly 57 years that made the UAE one of the cartel's most economically consequential participants. The timing was not accidental. The UAE has chafed under OPEC production quotas for years, repeatedly exceeding its assigned output ceiling and clashing with Saudi Arabia over quota allocation — tensions that the Iran war's Hormuz disruption has made operationally acute. With the UAE's own export routes through Hormuz constrained since late February, and with the Saudi-dominated OPEC unwilling to acknowledge the disproportionate economic damage the closure inflicts on Gulf producers with limited alternative export infrastructure, Abu Dhabi concluded the cartel relationship had become a net constraint rather than a net benefit.

The geopolitical implications extend well beyond oil markets. The UAE's departure is a direct signal that the Gulf states' apparent unity in the face of the Iran-US conflict is fracturing at the institutional level. The UAE, historically one of Washington's most important Gulf allies, cited its criticism of "fellow Arab states for not doing enough to protect it from numerous Iranian attacks during the war" — attacks that have struck UAE territory repeatedly during the conflict's 61 days. The departure removes the UAE from the OPEC framework that Saudi Arabia uses to coordinate Gulf economic policy, potentially accelerating Abu Dhabi's pivot toward independent production increases once the Hormuz crisis resolves. For global energy markets, the loss of the UAE — with approximately 4 million barrels per day of capacity — from the production-discipline framework is structurally bullish for oil prices in the medium term and represents a lasting change in market structure that will outlast the Iran conflict.

UAE exits OPEC after 57 years — the Gulf coalition's biggest structural break since founding, accelerated by the Iran war.

The UAE's departure from OPEC is the culmination of tensions that predate the Iran conflict: Abu Dhabi has repeatedly exceeded its OPEC production quota and argued for a larger allocation that reflects its $100 billion+ post-2020 production capacity expansion, an argument Riyadh has consistently refused. The Iran war provided the exit opportunity. With Hormuz constraining UAE oil exports since late February, and with Iranian missile and drone strikes hitting UAE infrastructure repeatedly during the 61-day conflict, Abu Dhabi found itself absorbing the war's economic and security costs while remaining bound by Saudi-shaped OPEC quotas that it views as serving Saudi interests rather than Gulf unity. Bloomberg reported the UAE plans to pursue an "independent production strategy" post-OPEC that would allow it to increase output to full capacity immediately upon Hormuz reopening — a signal that the post-conflict oil market will face supply increases from UAE that OPEC could previously have constrained. For Saudi Arabia, the loss is both economic (OPEC loses a major producer from its discipline framework) and political (the UAE's departure signals that the Riyadh-Abu Dhabi relationship — long the Gulf's most consequential bilateral — has been permanently strained by the war).

Iran Day 61: Trump says "don't rush me" — Mojtaba Khamenei unseen, Iran army "still in war situation," nuclear deadlock holds.

Day 61 of the US-Iran conflict passed without any progress toward a deal. When pressed by reporters on the Iran conflict's timeline, President Trump responded with his most combative framing yet: "Don't rush me." The statement extends his prior "no time frame" posture into active resistance to the diplomatic urgency the UN, Gulf states, and 60+ nations have been applying. Iran's army described itself as "still in war situation" — an internal communication that signals Tehran is not treating the extended ceasefire as a signal of US softening, but as a pause rather than a pathway to resolution. The nuclear sequencing deadlock remains unchanged: the US requires nuclear concessions before any Hormuz discussion; Iran requires Hormuz reopening before any nuclear talks. Mojtaba Khamenei, named new supreme leader following the killing of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, has not been publicly seen or heard from since the war began — a leadership vacuum that complicates Iran's ability to make the kind of sovereign decision that a nuclear framework concession would require. CBS News described the situation as "stuck in limbo."

Gulf leaders hold emergency summit as UAE's OPEC exit signals a new era of independent Gulf policy outside Saudi Arabia's orbit.

Gulf Cooperation Council leaders held emergency talks Tuesday following the UAE's OPEC departure announcement, with the session described as "urgent" by CNN's Day 60 conflict coverage. The summit reflects the Gulf's accelerating fragmentation under the Iran war's pressure: the UAE's OPEC exit is the most visible sign, but the underlying fractures run deeper. Saudi Arabia has maintained a posture of formal neutrality in the US-Iran conflict while quietly protecting its oil revenues through alternative Hormuz-adjacent export routes; the UAE, which has faced direct Iranian strikes, has adopted a more adversarial posture toward Tehran and more explicit alignment with Washington's security guarantees. The divergence between Saudi and Emirati strategic interests — long managed within the GCC framework — has become operationally acute under the war's 61 days of continuous pressure. For Washington, a fracturing GCC is a mixed signal: the UAE's closer alignment with US security positions is useful, but Saudi Arabia's non-alignment creates a gap in the Gulf coalition that Iran can exploit diplomatically.

Mojtaba Khamenei · Leadership Unseen New supreme leader · not appeared publicly · decision capacity unclear · nuclear talks blocked

Iran's new supreme leader Mojtaba Khamenei has not been seen publicly since the war began — leadership vacuum complicates any nuclear deal.

Mojtaba Khamenei, named Iran's new supreme leader following the killing of his father Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, has not been publicly seen or heard from directly since the war began — a leadership vacuum that CBS News specifically flagged as a factor in why the Iran-US stalemate persists. Any nuclear concession that Iran's framework would require — even a partial enrichment suspension or a declared pause — would require the supreme leader's explicit authorization under Iran's constitutional structure. Without a functioning, publicly present supreme leader, Iran's negotiating posture is effectively frozen: President Pezeshkian and Foreign Minister Araghchi can receive and convey proposals, but they cannot make the sovereign concession the US demands. The Trump administration's claims that there has been "regime change" following the killing of the senior Khamenei — a claim US officials have made — adds a further complication: if Iran's new leadership structure is not fully constituted, who has the authority to accept a deal? The absence of Mojtaba from public view is the single most underreported structural barrier to a negotiated resolution of the conflict.

Three US aircraft carriers now in the Middle East — first time since 2003, signaling the blockade has escalated to maximum naval presence.

The USS George H.W. Bush arrived in the Middle East on April 24 — joining the two carriers already positioned in the region — establishing the first three-carrier US naval presence in the area since the 2003 Iraq invasion. The three-carrier formation represents the maximum sustained naval force projection the US can maintain in a single theater and signals that the blockade enforcement posture has escalated beyond what two carriers can credibly maintain against a multi-month conflict. For Iran, the three-carrier presence is a specific deterrent against any Iranian naval action against the blockade itself: the combination of carrier-based air power, destroyer escorts, and submarine support makes any Iranian attempt to break the US naval cordon militarily prohibitive. For the diplomatic track, the three carriers simultaneously complicate Iran's negotiating posture — they represent US military commitment that Trump cannot withdraw without an explicit diplomatic concession — and reinforce Trump's "no time frame" statement by demonstrating that the military position is sustainable indefinitely.

Quick hits

  • The UAE's OPEC exit is notable for what it reveals about the Gulf states' post-war planning: Abu Dhabi is positioning itself to maximize production immediately when Hormuz reopens, without OPEC quota constraints. That implies the UAE is modeling a scenario in which it benefits from being the first mover in a post-closure oil supply surge — a bet that Hormuz does eventually reopen, but on a timeline where early movers capture outsized market share in the recovery period.
  • Trump's "don't rush me" statement is the sharpest formulation of the administration's timeline posture to date. It goes beyond "no time frame" — it actively signals irritation at external pressure to accelerate. The 60+ nation UN coalition, the ECB's rate-cut complications, the UK Parliament's briefing papers — all of these are producing diplomatic noise that Trump is publicly dismissing. The statement is also domestically directed: midterm elections are November 2026, and "don't rush me" signals the White House believes the domestic political cost of the conflict is manageable on its current trajectory.
  • Mojtaba Khamenei's absence from public view is now 61 days old. In the prior Iranian constitutional structure, the supreme leader's public presence was central to regime legitimacy — Friday sermons, public addresses, judicial and military oversight all flowed through the supreme leader's visible authority. The absence creates a genuine power vacuum that Iran's formal institutional structures (Guardian Council, IRGC, presidency) are filling on a provisional basis, but cannot fill legitimately without a functioning supreme leader. A nuclear concession of any kind requires supreme leader authorization; without a publicly functional supreme leader, the concession cannot be made regardless of what Iranian negotiators say in Pakistan or Moscow.
  • The three-carrier formation in the Middle East represents approximately 220 aircraft, 3 carrier air wings, and the full escort and submarine complement of the US Fifth Fleet — a force that Iran cannot challenge conventionally. The Hormuz-blockade enforcement is not at risk militarily. What is at risk is the indefinite sustainability of the economic and human cost to the US Navy's deployment rotation, the political cost of $114 Brent domestically, and the diplomatic cost of being isolated in the UN's 60+ nation coalition. None of these are military problems, which is why three carriers don't resolve them.
  • Saudi Arabia's reaction to the UAE's OPEC departure has not been publicly stated as of Wednesday morning — a silence that is itself significant. Riyadh may privately welcome the departure as eliminating its most resistant quota violator from the formal framework; or it may view the UAE's exit as a strategic blow that weakens OPEC's remaining cohesion. The Saudi response in the next 48-72 hours — whether they make concessions to other potential departures, call an emergency OPEC meeting, or simply absorb the loss — will determine whether the UAE's departure is an isolated event or the beginning of a broader OPEC fragmentation.
  • Iran's "dual blockade" structure — the US Navy blockading Iran, while Iran's mine threat and missile capability blockades the Gulf — creates a symmetric standoff that has no historical precedent in the Hormuz strait's modern history. Neither blockade can be maintained indefinitely, but they can each be maintained for longer than the other side expected. At Day 61, both sides have demonstrated they can sustain their blockade posture. The question that will break the stalemate is not military capability but domestic political tolerance — and "don't rush me" suggests Trump believes his tolerance is longer than Tehran's.
