Day 92-93: Trump's Situation Room meeting Friday ended without a signature — he issued new social media demands requiring Iran to agree to never build a nuclear weapon, open Hormuz immediately with no tolls, and allow the destruction of its ~900-pound highly-enriched uranium stockpile. Iran's supreme leader adviser called it "betraying diplomacy for the third time." Hegseth warned the US is "more than capable" of resuming strikes. No decision as of Sunday morning.

The Iran conflict's Day 92-93 is defined by a single structural development: the deal framework that was reportedly on Trump's desk Thursday was not sufficient to produce a signature on Friday, and the president's response was to add harder conditions rather than sign or reject. Trump's new Friday demands — posted on social media after the Situation Room meeting produced no announcement — represent a shift from the tentative 60-day-talks framework to a pre-condition framework: where the tentative text proposed that Iran and the US would begin nuclear talks after Hormuz opened, Trump's new demands appear to require Iran's commitment to specific nuclear outcomes (permanent weapons prohibition, HEU stockpile destruction) before or simultaneously with the Hormuz opening. This sequencing reversal — from "relief first, nuclear talks later" to "nuclear commitment now, relief follows" — is the structural change that Iran's government rejected through the supreme leader adviser's statement. Whether Trump's new demands are the final squeeze before an Iranian concession that concludes the deal, or the conditions that make the deal fundamentally unachievable in the current diplomatic window, is the question that the next 48-72 hours will answer. Hegseth's parallel military readiness statement — the US is "more than capable" of resuming strikes, weapons stockpiles are "enough to get the job done," and Iran can "deal with the military" if it won't accept a deal — confirms that the maximum-pressure track is running at full intensity alongside the now-stalled diplomatic track. The conflict's dual-track character has never been more pronounced: the deal is closer in framework terms than it has ever been (tentative text, Hormuz framework agreed), and the military posture is more explicitly threatening than it has been since May 20's strikes. The outcome is entirely in Trump's hands — and in Iran's decision about whether the new conditions are acceptable.

Trump's Friday social media demands deserve line-by-line analysis because their specificity reveals the points of disagreement that prevented the Situation Room meeting from producing a signature. The first demand — Iran must agree to "never have a Nuclear Weapon or Bomb" — is a permanent, unconditional nuclear prohibition that goes beyond any prior US ask in the Iran nuclear context, including the 2015 JCPOA, which limited Iran's nuclear activities without permanently prohibiting weapons capability. The JCPOA's approach was to restrict enrichment levels, centrifuge numbers, and stockpile volumes — leaving Iran with civilian nuclear infrastructure — in exchange for sanctions relief. Trump's "never have a nuclear weapon" demand is categorically different: it requires Iran to accept permanent nuclear disarmament as a sovereign commitment, not a temporary restriction with sunset clauses. The second demand — Hormuz must be "immediately open, no tolls, for unrestricted shipping traffic" — addresses the Hormuz transit fee mechanism that Iran's new Maritime Toll Authority had announced. Where the tentative framework proposed a 30-day mine-clearing timeline and gradual Hormuz reopening, Trump's demand appears to require immediate opening with no fees — eliminating the revenue mechanism Iran had hoped to extract from Hormuz passage. The third demand — the ~900 pounds of highly-enriched uranium must be "DESTROYED" — is the most operationally specific condition and the most diplomatically consequential: where prior frameworks proposed monitoring, downblending, or removal of Iran's HEU stockpile, Trump demands its physical destruction, leaving Iran with no HEU infrastructure to reconstitute quickly if a subsequent US administration withdraws from whatever deal is signed.

Iran's response to the three demands — delivered through the supreme leader adviser Mojtaba Zonnour's "betraying diplomacy for the third time" statement — is a reference to three historical moments Iran's government regards as US diplomatic violations: the 2015 JCPOA withdrawal under Trump's first term, the May 20 talks collapse, and now this Friday episode. The "third time" framing signals that Iran's government is not treating Friday's demands as an opening bid to be negotiated — it is treating them as an exit from the process. Hegseth's military readiness statement — the US "more than capable" of resuming strikes, weapons stockpiles "enough to get the job done," and "if Iran doesn't want to make a great deal that ensures they don't get a nuclear weapon, they can deal with" the military — is the American maximum-pressure response to the Iranian rejection: maintaining the credible military threat that the administration believes is driving Iran toward any deal. The question for Day 93-94 is whether the mutual hardening of positions represents the final compression before resolution (both sides reaching their walk-away limits and then finding a deal between them) or the beginning of a negotiating breakdown that requires resumed military action to reset conditions.

President Trump convened a Situation Room meeting Friday — attended by Secretary of State Marco Rubio and Vice President JD Vance — to make a "final determination" on the Iran deal. The meeting ended without a public announcement. Trump then posted on social media that Iran must agree to "never have a Nuclear Weapon or Bomb," that the Strait of Hormuz must be "immediately open, no tolls, for unrestricted shipping traffic," and that Iran's ~900 pounds of highly-enriched uranium must be "DESTROYED." The demands replace the tentative 60-day-talks framework — which deferred nuclear questions to post-Hormuz negotiations — with pre-conditions that require Iranian nuclear commitments before any deal is finalized.

The structural significance of Trump's new demands is the reversal of the sequencing that made the tentative framework possible. The tentative memorandum of understanding — the text reportedly on Trump's desk Thursday — worked by giving Iran immediate economic relief (Hormuz opening, naval blockade lifted) in exchange for beginning nuclear talks within 60 days. This sequencing allowed Iran's government to show its domestic audience a tangible economic benefit from the deal without requiring the IRGC and nuclear program to accept disarmament as a precondition. Trump's Friday conditions reverse this: they require Iran to commit to permanent nuclear disarmament and HEU destruction before receiving Hormuz relief, which Iran's government cannot accept without either public commitment to nuclear capabilities it has defended for decades as a sovereign right, or the same domestic political backlash that has historically prevented Iranian governments from accepting US pre-conditions. Whether Trump's demands are intended as a final-pressure maximization strategy (designed to produce a small Iranian concession that allows both sides to claim victory) or as a genuine deal-ender (designed to produce Iranian rejection that justifies resumed military operations) will determine the conflict's next phase.

The institutional dynamics of Iran's decision-making on the deal are as consequential as the deal's substantive terms. The Islamic Republic's governance structure gives the supreme leader — not the elected president — final authority over major foreign policy decisions. Pezeshkian's government can negotiate and propose, but Khamenei must ratify. The supreme leader's continued silence since the tentative framework was reported — he has not confirmed the deal, not rejected it, and not publicly engaged with Trump's new demands — is itself a diplomatic signal whose interpretation divides analysts: it could mean Khamenei has delegated the final-stage negotiation to Pezeshkian's team and will ratify whatever they conclude; it could mean he is waiting to see whether Iran's strategic interests are fully served before endorsing; or it could mean he is opposed to the deal and his silence is the Islamic Republic's form of "not there yet." The adviser's "betraying diplomacy" formulation is the supreme leader's office's voice — not the elected government's — and its use of that language is the closest to an explicit rejection that Khamenei's circle has issued. The critical question for the next 24-48 hours is whether the adviser's statement reflects Khamenei's final position or a maximum-pressure response designed to extract the final concessions before the supreme leader endorses.

Quick hits

  • Trump's "destroy the HEU" demand — requiring the physical destruction of Iran's ~900-pound highly-enriched uranium stockpile — is the most operationally specific new condition and the one that most directly challenges Iranian sovereignty claims. Iran's HEU stockpile (enriched to 60-70%, one technical step from weapons-grade 90%+) represents approximately two to three years of enrichment work at current centrifuge capacity. Destroying it would eliminate Iran's most immediately usable nuclear breakout material and require re-enrichment from lower-purity starting material if Iran were to reconstitute its capabilities. The demand differs qualitatively from prior US nuclear conditions in two ways. First, it requires physical destruction rather than international custody (as proposed in the 2003 Libyan model, where the US removed weapons materials) or downblending to lower purity (as proposed in some prior Iran negotiations). Physical destruction on Iranian soil would require international inspectors and US or IAEA-supervised procedures that effectively require Iran to publicly demonstrate nuclear disarmament — a visibility Iran's government has historically refused. Second, the 900-pound figure is specific enough to indicate US intelligence has a precise accounting of Iran's current HEU inventory, which itself carries a message about US intelligence collection inside Iran's nuclear program. The HEU destruction demand may be the condition that determines the deal's fate more than any other: it is concrete enough to verify, symbolically significant enough to be domestically significant for both sides, and technically irreversible enough to represent genuine nuclear limitation rather than a temporary restriction that a future Iranian government could reverse.
  • The "no tolls" condition on Hormuz passage is a direct response to Iran's Maritime Toll Authority announcement — the institutional mechanism Iran established to collect transit fees from vessels using the Strait during the conflict, which was itself a revenue-generation tool and a sovereignty assertion over Hormuz passage. Trump's demand that Hormuz be open "immediately" with "no tolls for unrestricted shipping traffic" eliminates the toll mechanism and requires simultaneous rather than sequenced opening — the "immediately" language rejecting the 30-day mine-clearing timeline that the tentative framework proposed. The commercial significance of the "no tolls" condition: at the traffic volumes typical of Hormuz pre-conflict (approximately 20 million barrels per day in oil tanker traffic, plus LNG and container traffic), even a $1 per barrel toll would generate approximately $20 million per day in Iranian revenue — $7 billion per year — from a waterway Iran regards as within its sovereign territory or exclusive economic zone. The US rejection of any toll mechanism is both a practical commercial demand (keeping shipping costs low for the global economy) and a sovereignty assertion (the US does not recognize Iranian sovereign claims over Strait navigation that would justify a toll). Iran's position — that it has sovereign rights to regulate and monetize Hormuz passage — is incompatible with the US "no tolls" demand without a fundamental concession on sovereignty that Iran's government has defined as a core national interest. This demand, like the HEU destruction demand, may be designed to extract a maximum Iranian concession rather than to be immediately accepted in full.
  • The Shangri-La Dialogue's discussion of "reducing reliance on US-led security guarantees" reflects a structural shift in Asia-Pacific security thinking that the Iran conflict has accelerated. Before February 28, the dominant Asia-Pacific security assumption was that the US would maintain its forward military presence and treaty commitments (AUKUS, Quad, US-Japan and US-Korea bilateral alliances) regardless of other regional conflicts. The Iran conflict has introduced uncertainty about that assumption in two ways: first, the intensive US military commitment in the Gulf has raised questions about whether the US maintains sufficient capacity for simultaneous major theater operations (Middle East plus Asia-Pacific) at the required scale; and second, the Trump administration's "great deal" framing for the Iran conflict — which treats the conflict as a negotiable business dispute rather than a values-based security commitment — has raised questions about whether US security commitments to Asia-Pacific partners are unconditional or subject to similar deal-framing recalibration. The Shangri-La Dialogue is the venue where these questions are being asked by Asia-Pacific defense ministers, and the answers being developed will shape security architectures for the next decade: whether the region doubles down on US-led frameworks (AUKUS, Quad) or develops independent regional security mechanisms (ASEAN-led security frameworks, bilateral non-US defense arrangements) will depend substantially on how the Iran conflict concludes and what precedent it sets for US military engagement in major geopolitical disputes.
  • The Iran conflict's 92-day duration already exceeds the historical duration of several major post-WWII conflicts that are usually treated as "quick" military operations: the 1991 Gulf War ground offensive (100 hours), the 2003 Iraq invasion's major combat phase (21 days), Israel's 2006 Lebanon War (34 days), and the 2011 Libya intervention's major campaign phases. The Iran conflict's duration reflects a structural feature of the ceasefire format: unlike outright wars that end with a clear victory or defeat, the managed-intensity ceasefire format allows both sides to sustain military operations at a level below the threshold that would require full mobilization, while diplomacy continues. This format is militarily sustainable for the US for an extended period — the US military has the capacity to maintain its Gulf presence and conduct periodic strikes indefinitely — but is economically painful for Iran (oil revenue blockade) and reputationally costly for the US (credibility of maximum-pressure campaign). The 92-day duration also means the conflict has now outlasted the attention span of many international media cycles, creating a risk of normalization: global markets, AI earnings seasons, and other geopolitical events are increasingly operating as if the Iran conflict is a background condition rather than an acute crisis. Whether this normalization reflects a genuine reduction in systemic risk (the conflict is contained and manageable) or an analytical failure to price the potential for rapid escalation to full-scale conflict (if the deal fails and Trump resumes operations) is a question that WTI at $87.20 and BTC at $74,054 are not definitively answering in either direction.
  • Colombia's May 31 presidential election occurs in the context of a broader Latin American political cycle that includes Ecuador's elections earlier in 2026, Chile's constitutional process, and Brazil's ongoing Lula administration. Colombia's specific dynamic — the debate between peace-negotiation frameworks (as represented by the 2016 FARC peace deal and subsequent Petro-era ELN negotiations) and security-enforcement frameworks (as represented by candidates who argue that the peace process has increased rather than reduced armed group violence) — reflects a tension that is visible across the region. The meta-question in Colombian politics is whether the 2016 FARC peace deal represents the right model for transitional justice and conflict resolution, or whether the deal's implementation failures (continued FARC dissident violence, ELN expansion, coca cultivation increases) indicate that the peace-process framework is being exploited rather than implemented in good faith. The May 31 election will determine which political coalition has won the argument about that question — and whether Colombia's next government pursues negotiations with the ELN (following the Petro model) or suspends them in favor of security operations (following the Uribe model). The implications for the broader region: if Colombia's election produces a decisive shift toward the security-enforcement model, it may catalyze similar policy shifts in Ecuador, Peru, and other countries dealing with Colombian-connected armed group activity, reshaping the narco-trafficking and migration dynamics that affect the entire Americas region.
