The Anchor

Iran peace talks collapse for the second time — Trump kills the Pakistan framework as Araghchi heads to Oman.

The US-Iran peace process collapsed for the second time Saturday, as President Trump canceled the planned trip of Witkoff and Kushner to Islamabad at the last minute — hours after Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi himself left Pakistan for Oman without a deal. Trump announced the cancellation on social media, writing that there is "tremendous infighting and confusion within their 'leadership'" and that "Nobody knows who is in charge, including them." He added: "We have all the cards, they have none. If they want to talk, all they have to do is call." Trump cited excessive travel time for not sending envoys, noting "we're not going to spend 15 hours in airplanes all the time going back and forth to be giving a document that was not good enough." The statement's tone — dismissive of Iran's leadership structure — makes a rapid resumption of Pakistan-format talks politically difficult for both sides. Iran's FM Araghchi presented Pakistani mediators with a framework for ending the war; Trump's response was to say Iran's offer was not good enough and call off the mission.

Araghchi is now in Muscat, Oman, where he is expected to meet Omani Foreign Minister Badr Albusaidi — the only surviving back-channel between Washington and Tehran. Oman has historically served as the most effective US-Iran diplomatic conduit, brokering earlier nuclear deal conversations. The ceasefire agreed April 8 remains formally in place; the US naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz continues tightening. Day 58 opens without a scheduled negotiating session, with the next plausible diplomatic contact being Oman's shuttle diplomacy between Araghchi and Witkoff — a slower, indirect process than the direct Pakistan format. The IMF projects global growth slowing to 3.1% in 2026 in the shadow of the conflict; oil markets are waiting for any signal that the blockade might ease before repricing downward from the current Hormuz premium of $8–12 per barrel above pre-conflict Brent levels.

Trump cancels Witkoff-Kushner Pakistan trip at last minute — calls Iran talks off citing "infighting" in Tehran.

President Trump canceled the planned mission of special envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner to Islamabad on Saturday evening, hours after Iran's FM Araghchi had already left Pakistan for Oman without a deal. Trump announced the cancellation on social media, citing "tremendous infighting and confusion" in Iran's leadership and saying "Nobody knows who is in charge." He framed the move as pressure: "We have all the cards, they have none. If they want to talk, all they have to do is call." Trump also cited the 15-hour flight time as a practical objection, saying he would not send envoys to deliver a document "that was not good enough." Iran's position, as stated by Araghchi before leaving Islamabad, was that it had presented a workable framework and was waiting to see if the US was "truly serious about diplomacy."

Araghchi · Oman Muscat FM Albusaidi · Oman back-channel · sole contact

Araghchi heads to Oman after Pakistan collapse — Albusaidi talks are the last surviving US-Iran diplomatic thread.

Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi arrived in Muscat, Oman, on Saturday after negotiations in Pakistan ended without a deal and Trump canceled the US delegation's travel. Araghchi is expected to meet Omani Foreign Minister Badr Albusaidi — who has historically served as the most effective US-Iran back-channel broker. Oman brokered key contacts during the Obama-era nuclear negotiations and has maintained trusted relationships with both governments through successive US administrations. The Muscat channel is now the only surviving active diplomatic contact between the two sides. Any path to a ceasefire upgrade or blockade negotiation runs through Albusaidi. A Middle East diplomat briefed on the discussions told NBC News that Iran's position remains that it will not negotiate while under siege — conditioning any talks on removal of the blockade, a position Washington categorically rejects.

Day 58: US blockade tightening with no diplomatic path — Iran ceasefire holds formally as pressure mounts.

The ceasefire agreed on April 8 remains formally in place on Day 58, but the US naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz is tightening by design — a deliberate pressure strategy rather than a military offensive. Iran's parliamentary speaker maintained Sunday that the blockade constitutes a ceasefire violation and that talks are conditioned on its removal, a position the US categorically rejects. Al Jazeera's analysis estimates Iran can sustain the current economic pressure for anywhere from a few months to over a year, depending on oil export workarounds through Iraq and Turkey and on pre-existing China contracts. Trump's cancellation of the Pakistan talks Saturday — framed as pressure rather than disengagement — sets a new baseline: Iran must call Washington to restart talks, on US terms, without preconditions. Neither side is making that move today.

Viktor Orban · Hungary 36 years Quits parliament · Tisza supermajority · May 9

Viktor Orban quits parliament after 36 years — Tisza wins 138 of 199 seats in Hungary's landslide.

Viktor Orban announced Saturday he will not take his seat in Hungary's new parliament, ending a 36-year unbroken legislative career that began in 1990 during Hungary's transition from state socialism. Orban's Fidesz party was routed in the April 12 elections by Péter Magyar's Tisza party, which won 138 of the parliament's 199 seats — a supermajority. The new parliament convenes May 9; it will be the first in post-socialist Hungary without Orban as a sitting lawmaker. Orban said he will remain Fidesz party leader to "rebuild" the national side — a role that, historically in Hungarian politics, carries significant executive influence even without a parliamentary seat. The defeat ends 16 years of Orban's continuous rule as prime minister, a tenure that reshaped Hungary's judiciary, media, and EU relationship.

Source: Bloomberg · NBC News · Washington Post
Hungary · Tisza · Magyar 138/199 Supermajority · May 9 parliament · post-Orban era

Péter Magyar's Tisza holds 138 of 199 Hungarian seats — new parliament opens May 9 without Orban.

Péter Magyar's Tisza party holds 138 of the 199 seats in Hungary's incoming parliament — a supermajority that gives Magyar the constitutional votes to amend the Hungarian Basic Law and potentially reverse many of Orban's structural changes to the judiciary and media. The new parliament formally convenes May 9. Magyar, 38, rose from relative obscurity as a former husband of a Fidesz official to lead the first sustained political opposition to Orban in over a decade. Tisza's supermajority is the first opposition supermajority since Hungary's democratic transition. Magyar's platform included restoring judicial independence, re-engaging with EU institutions on rule-of-law disputes, and reorienting Hungary's foreign policy away from Moscow and Beijing. Chatham House notes that "Orbanism" — the governing model — may outlast Orban's parliamentary departure.

Lebanon ceasefire extension technically holds Sunday — but Friday's strikes and Hezbollah fire have not stopped.

The three-week Israel-Lebanon ceasefire extension announced Thursday remains formally in place Sunday, but its credibility deteriorated sharply after Friday's Israeli strikes on rocket-launcher sites in Yater and Kafra — which killed 6 — and Hezbollah's retaliatory fire. Hezbollah lawmakers called the extension "meaningless"; Netanyahu's office has not retracted its "full freedom of action" statement. Sunday is quiet, with no new reported strikes from either side in the past 24 hours. The ceasefire framework is functioning more as a pause than a peace — both parties continue to claim the right to respond to violations. CFR notes the three-week window Trump described as a "great chance" for lasting peace began with both sides still exchanging fire, creating structural pressure on even this minimal framework to hold.

Quick hits

  • Trump's "all the cards" framing is a deliberate pressure posture — historically, this language signals a negotiating maximum-pressure phase designed to force a counterparty concession rather than a permanent diplomatic breakdown.
  • Oman's role as the last surviving US-Iran back-channel is structurally critical: Oman is not a NATO ally, maintains diplomatic ties with Tehran, and has brokered multiple prior US-Iran contacts without taking sides — it is the only format Iran may accept without losing face.
  • Araghchi's departure from Islamabad to Muscat — rather than returning to Tehran — signals Iran is not withdrawing from diplomacy entirely; the Oman channel is being kept warm even as the Pakistan format collapses.
  • Orban's decision to stay as Fidesz party leader while exiting parliament is a historically unusual move in Hungarian politics — it gives him a base to attempt a political comeback in 2030 or to influence the new government from outside without the formal accountability of a parliamentary seat.
  • Magyar's Tisza supermajority of 138/199 seats is sufficient to amend Hungary's Basic Law (a two-thirds threshold is required) — putting Orban's most durable constitutional changes, including judicial appointments and media ownership rules, within legislative reach for reversal.
  • The Lebanon ceasefire's formal three-week extension runs approximately until May 14; both sides' willingness to continue violating it while maintaining its nominal existence suggests neither party wants the political cost of formally ending it — a structural equilibrium that keeps violence contained but ongoing.
