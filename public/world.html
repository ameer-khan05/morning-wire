The US administration demanded that Iran "publicly confirm the full opening of the Strait of Hormuz to shipping and guarantee the cessation of attacks on ships passing through it" — a formal-commitment demand that represents the sharpest US posture on the Strait since the ceasefire framework collapsed with Tuesday's tanker attacks — moving the diplomatic requirement from accepting Qatar and Pakistan's "positive progress" characterization of round 1 talks to requiring Iran to issue an explicit, public, verifiable statement that can be cited as a binding commitment. The demand arrived Saturday morning, before Doha round 2 has been scheduled, giving Iran the choice to respond before the next round or to make the formal commitment a condition of round 2's substance. Iran's response has not been reported as of Saturday morning; that response is the most consequential single diplomatic event of the weekend.

The posture shift from "positive progress" acceptance to "publicly confirm" and "guarantee" language is substantive because it changes what counts as success. Under the previous formulation, diplomatic process progress — rounds convening, mediators reporting optimism, strikes paused — was sufficient for the market to price de-escalation (oil fell $2.30 Friday). Under the new formulation, the success condition is a formal Iranian public statement that satisfies the US's language requirements. That is a harder standard that Iran has historically resisted: formal public acknowledgment of constraints on its own sovereign maritime jurisdiction touches the same domestic political vulnerabilities as nuclear concessions do. The US asking for public commitment rather than private assurance is either an indication that it has Iran's informal agreement and is formalizing it, or that it is testing Iran's willingness to formalize what it has been saying privately. The weekend will answer which reading is correct.

Russian forces launched overnight precision-weapon strikes on military-industrial complex targets in Kyiv — Pravda EN reporting the strikes as "group attacks with precision weapons" against defense industry enterprises — as the Ukrainian Air Force reported destroying 144 enemy UAVs in the 12-hour period from 8 AM to 8 PM Moscow time on July 10, while Zelensky's open letter to Putin offering direct in-person bilateral negotiations — issued after Secretary Rubio declared the US mediation effort a failure and Trump indicated he was "fully focused on Iran" — remained unanswered as Russia continued advancing on multiple fronts. The overnight strikes are Russia's military answer to a diplomatic offer: Putin is negotiating from the position of a military initiative that has no incentive to pause for bilateral talks that would freeze the current front lines.

The US diplomatic void in the Ukraine theater is now explicit rather than implied. Rubio's declaration that US mediation has failed, Trump's stated Iran focus, and the absence of any US engagement with Zelensky's Putin letter all confirm that Ukraine has, for the first time since the conflict began, lost the active US diplomatic attention that shaped its negotiating position and its allies' arms-supply decisions. European partners are filling part of the gap — EU arms supply decisions are accelerating — but the strategic calculation of when and whether to negotiate has historically required US involvement to be credible to Russia. Zelensky's bilateral offer to Putin without US backing is creative diplomacy in a constrained environment; whether it produces a response depends entirely on whether Russia decides that bilateral talks serve its interests more than military advances do, which is not the current Russian assessment.

The August 17 MOU deadline is 37 days away and the nuclear file has not been addressed in either Doha round — with round 2 expected next week, Iranian President Pezeshkian's stated position that enrichment rights are non-negotiable, and the sequencing dispute over whether Iran's frozen funds are released before or after nuclear parameters are agreed remaining unresolved after 11 years of Iran nuclear diplomacy; the diplomatic mathematics are unforgiving: if two to three substantive nuclear rounds are required to reach a framework text, each taking five to seven days, the practical nuclear framework deadline is approximately August 10 — leaving seven days for both governments to ratify the text through their respective leadership chains before the August 17 MOU date. Lebanon's IDF security zone in southern Lebanon, which has violated the ceasefire's withdrawal requirement throughout the post-ceasefire period, entered its most vulnerable phase as Khamenei's funeral period ended Thursday, removing the informal buffer that had reduced triggering-incident risk for ten days.

The nuclear file's continued deferral across two Doha rounds creates a paradox: each "positive progress" signal from Doha is simultaneously accurate (process progress on maritime and frozen funds) and insufficient (no nuclear movement). The market has priced the accurate part — oil fell, stocks rose — without fully pricing the insufficient part: 37 days is not enough time to negotiate nuclear parameters from scratch through an indirect mediation format. Either Trump's "very good meetings" language signals that the nuclear terms have been privately agreed and Doha is ratifying rather than negotiating, or the August 17 deadline is not going to be met through the nuclear channel. The market is currently pricing the optimistic reading; the diplomatic record suggests the cautious reading deserves more weight than it is receiving.
