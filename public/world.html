The state funeral of Iran's Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei — killed in US-Israeli airstrikes on February 28 — commenced in Tehran on Independence Day, with thousands of mourners gathering under red Shiite flags chanting "revenge," ceremonies running through July 9 when burial is scheduled, and the Doha nuclear talks formally paused for the week as Israel's defense minister threatened Iran's new supreme leader and Tehran warned of an immediate powerful response.

The funeral's Day 1 establishes the symbolic context for the five days to follow: mass mourning, public revenge rhetoric, and the red flags of Shiite call for vengeance, all while the US-Iran nuclear diplomacy is in a mandated pause and Israel's defense minister has explicitly threatened the life of the man who now leads Iran. The combination is the most acute escalation scenario in the post-ceasefire period: Israeli Defense Minister Katz's statement that Mojtaba Khamenei is "marked for death" — issued during the state funeral that is attributing Ayatollah Khamenei's death to those same Israeli strikes — creates conditions in which an Israeli action during the funeral period would trigger the precise response the mourning crowds are demanding. The US has invested significantly in the MOU framework and the Lebanon ceasefire; the next five days are the test of whether that investment holds against the geopolitical pressure that the funeral is generating. Doha talks resume after July 9. Forty-four days remain to August 17.

Iran's state funeral for Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei began Saturday July 4 in Tehran, with thousands of mourners entering the Grand Mosalla on the first morning of five-day ceremonies leading to burial on July 9. Mourners held red flags — the Shiite symbol of unavenged blood and a call for revenge — and chanted "revenge, revenge" as Khamenei's body was displayed publicly for the first day of what Iranian authorities expect will draw millions to Tehran and to parallel ceremonies across Iran, Iraq, Lebanon, and Pakistan. Foreign leaders arrived in Tehran for the state ceremony, making the funeral simultaneously a mass public political mobilization and an international diplomatic event in which Iran's standing in the Shiite world and beyond is being actively reinforced.

The United States formally agreed to halt nuclear talks with Iran for the duration of the funeral period — consistent with the diplomatic norm of not conducting active negotiations while a counterpart country is in formal state mourning — with the Fox News report characterizing it as the US agreeing to "halt talks for a week." Qatari and Pakistani mediators have adjourned the indirect Doha sessions, with the next round scheduled for "the earliest possible time" after July 9. The funeral pause is not a failure of the MOU framework; it is a natural diplomatic gap that both sides need. What the pause is not is a substitute for progress on the core issues — nuclear enrichment, Hormuz governance, and sanctions relief — that have not moved in 18 days of the 60-day window's implementation. When talks resume on or after July 9, 38 days will remain to August 17, and the pace of progress needed to reach a binding agreement in that time will be substantially higher than what the first 18 days produced.

Israel's Defense Minister Israel Katz stated that Mojtaba Khamenei — who succeeded Ayatollah Khamenei as Iran's supreme leader following the February 28 airstrikes — was "marked for death," according to Israeli news agency Ynet, a statement issued while Iran's state funeral for the previous supreme leader is underway and millions of mourners are publicly calling for revenge for that death. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araqchi responded with a warning that Tehran would deliver an "immediate and powerful response" to any threat against Iranian leadership. The US has not publicly issued a warning to Katz to retract or walk back the statement, creating a diplomatic gap in which the Israeli defense minister's stated intent is active during the five-day mourning period when an Israeli action would trigger the maximum political and military response from an Iranian public already mobilized by grief.

A US-brokered Lebanon-Israel ceasefire framework concluded last week calls for Hezbollah disarmament, a gradual Israeli withdrawal from southern Lebanon, and the deployment of the Lebanese army in the south — terms that the Lebanese government and Hezbollah read as requiring Israeli forces to leave. Israeli officials have simultaneously vowed to maintain a 6-mile "security zone" in southern Lebanon regardless of the framework's withdrawal language, creating an implementation contradiction that is apparent from day one of the ceasefire. Israeli attacks on Lebanon since the start of the conflict have killed over 4,000 people by Al Jazeera's reporting; the ceasefire is designed to end that killing, but the security zone provision means Israeli military presence in Lebanon continues in a different form. Whether the security zone is compatible with the withdrawal requirement — and who has the authority to enforce compliance — is the question that will define whether the Lebanon ceasefire holds or becomes the next flashpoint in the post-war regional order.

DRC Ebola Update: 1,406 Cases · 438 Deaths · Ituri Province 1,283 Cases · Uganda Clear Since Jun 5 · Bundibugyo Strain · No Approved Vaccine Ebola 2026 Bundibugyo Ebola outbreak: DRC (primary) + Uganda (secondary) · total confirmed cases: 1,406 (as of Jun 30) · total deaths: 438 · most recent update: 73 new confirmed cases + 25 new deaths since prior report · Ituri province (DRC): 1,283 cases including 366 deaths from 24 of 36 health zones · Uganda: no new cases since Jun 5 (cross-border transmission contained) · virus: Bundibugyo strain · no approved vaccine (unlike Zaire strain: rVSV-ZEBOV Ervebo) · no specific antiviral treatment · WHO + CDC active response · eastern DRC: chronic armed group activity → access challenges for contact tracing

The 2026 Ebola outbreak caused by the Bundibugyo virus in the Democratic Republic of Congo recorded 1,406 confirmed cases and 438 deaths as of June 30 — an increase of 73 new confirmed cases and 25 new deaths since the prior situation report — with the outbreak now concentrated primarily in DRC's Ituri province, where 1,283 of the total 1,406 cases and 366 of the 438 deaths have been recorded across 24 of the province's 36 health zones. Uganda has reported no new cases since June 5, indicating that cross-border transmission from DRC into Uganda has been contained at the current level of response, though the DRC outbreak's trajectory in the conflict-affected Ituri region continues without an approved vaccine or specific antiviral treatment for the Bundibugyo strain.

The Bundibugyo vaccine gap is the outbreak's defining structural vulnerability. The Zaire strain of Ebola — responsible for the 2014–2016 West Africa outbreak and the 2018–2020 DRC North Kivu outbreak — has the rVSV-ZEBOV (Ervebo) vaccine, which has proven highly effective at breaking transmission chains when deployed with ring vaccination strategies. The Bundibugyo strain, which caused the 2007–2008 and 2012 outbreaks and is now driving the 2026 event as the third-largest Bundibugyo outbreak on record, has no equivalent approved protective measure. The response therefore relies entirely on traditional outbreak control: case isolation, contact tracing, infection prevention and control in health facilities, and safe burial practices — all of which are complicated in Ituri province by the chronic armed group activity in eastern DRC that restricts humanitarian access in affected communities. WHO and CDC are active in the response; Bundibugyo vaccine candidates are in development, but none has completed the clinical trial and emergency authorization process that would make deployment in the current outbreak possible.

Quick hits

  • The Katz statement about Mojtaba Khamenei being "marked for death" is not an isolated hawkish remark — it is consistent with a pattern of Israeli statements about the post-Ayatollah Iranian leadership that have not been walked back despite US diplomatic investment in the MOU process. The US has successfully negotiated a ceasefire with Iran, a Lebanon framework, and IAEA inspector access; it has not successfully restrained Israel's stated intent toward the Iranian regime that its own strikes helped install. The diplomatic gap between the US's stated goal (a binding agreement before August 17) and Israel's stated intent (eliminating the new Iranian leadership) is the structural contradiction in the post-war US strategy in the Middle East. During the funeral period, when Iranian public sentiment is most primed for the revenge that the red flags are demanding, the practical question is whether the US will use its leverage with Israel to prevent an action that would end the MOU process — and whether that leverage is sufficient to produce the restraint the MOU requires.
  • The Lebanon ceasefire framework's security zone provision is the implementation crisis that is already built into the text of the agreement. The framework requires Israeli withdrawal; Israeli officials have publicly committed to maintaining a 6-mile buffer zone regardless. There is no way to resolve this contradiction within the framework's own language — it requires either Israel to change its stated position or the framework to be renegotiated to accommodate the security zone. Neither is politically easy: Israel has domestic political and security reasons for the security zone; Lebanon and Hezbollah have domestic political reasons not to accept it. The ceasefire holds as long as both sides don't test the contradiction directly. When one side does — either by IDF forces acting aggressively within the zone, or by Hezbollah refusing to withdraw from areas the IDF is present in — the framework's durability will be determined in real time, not in the language of the agreement.
