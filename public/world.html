Pakistan's Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif announced Saturday morning that US and Iranian negotiators have reached the "final, agreed upon text" of a peace agreement — to be called the "Islamabad Declaration" — with an electronic signing expected within 24 hours, after which a formal ceremony will be held in Geneva with Vice President JD Vance representing the United States. Pakistan's role as mediator — trusted by Iran as a Muslim-majority ally and acceptable to the US as a long-standing partner not party to the conflict — proved decisive in bridging the gap that direct US-Iran negotiations had repeatedly failed to close. Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi said the accord has "never been closer."

The distinction between this announcement and Trump's Thursday "war ended" claim is the source: Pakistan is a neutral mediating party with direct knowledge of both sides' positions, not an interested party making an optimistic assertion. Sharif's "final text" language — following his government's direct involvement in drafting the agreement — carries a different epistemic weight than any prior announcement in the 105-day conflict.

Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi confirmed Saturday that the peace deal has "never been closer," disclosing two specific deal terms: Iran is committing indefinitely to never procure or develop nuclear weapons — described by a senior US administration official as "a significant concession" from Tehran — and the US will unfreeze confiscated Iranian assets as part of the accord. The asset freeze language represents an escalation beyond what was previously reported (oil sanctions lift only), and addresses the deeper financial grievance Iranian leaders have cited throughout the 105-day conflict. Iran's indefinite non-nuclear commitment — rather than a time-limited moratorium — is the formulation the US has sought since the conflict began on February 28.

The "never procure or develop" language is notable for its scope: it covers both the development of nuclear weapons (which Iran has always officially denied pursuing) and the procurement of nuclear weapons from a third party — closing a loophole that previous Iranian nuclear commitments did not explicitly address. The verification mechanism for this commitment — whether IAEA inspections, additional protocols, or a new monitoring framework — is expected to be negotiated in follow-on technical talks after the Islamabad Declaration is signed. The indefinite timeframe without a sunset clause is the most significant departure from the 2015 JCPOA framework, which included nuclear limits that expired after 10-15 years. Iran accepting an indefinite commitment suggests that the economic terms — asset unfreeze, Hormuz normalization, sanctions relief — are sufficiently valuable to warrant a permanent nuclear renunciation.

Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif's government established back-channel communications between US and Iranian negotiators beginning in February 2026 — before the conflict's first kinetic strikes — and maintained those channels through 105 days of escalation, ceasefire attempts, and near-deals that all failed until the Islamabad process yielded final text on Saturday. The agreement's naming honors Pakistan's role in the way diplomatic tradition reserves for decisive mediators.

Pakistan's mediation success opens a broader geopolitical question: whether Islamabad has permanently established itself as the go-to broker for US-Iran disputes, and whether that role gives Pakistan new strategic leverage in its relationships with both Washington and Tehran. The Islamabad Declaration is the highest-profile diplomatic achievement in Pakistan's post-independence history, achieved under a government that has faced significant domestic economic and political challenges. The agreement's success will be a defining legacy for Shehbaz Sharif's administration regardless of what follows domestically, and Pakistan's role will be cited as a model for neutral Muslim-majority state mediation in future regional conflicts.

Two specific hurdles remain between the Islamabad Declaration's finalized text and a signed agreement: verification mechanisms for Iran's indefinite non-nuclear commitment — the IAEA's role and the inspection protocols need to be defined — and factional approval within Tehran's political system, particularly from Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps leadership that has opposed negotiations throughout the 105-day conflict. Pakistan's PM's language — "preparations underway for the signing ceremony" — implies that the supreme leader's ratification has been secured, since preparations for a ceremony with a sitting US vice president would not advance without that approval. Iran's Foreign Minister Araghchi, who has supreme leader confidence and executive authority over the foreign affairs brief, would not have made his "never been closer" statement without knowledge of the supreme leader's position.

Verification is likely deferred to follow-on technical talks rather than blocking the Islamabad Declaration's signing. The practical sequencing: sign the political commitment, then negotiate the verification framework while the asset unfreeze and Hormuz reopening proceed in parallel — ensuring both sides have economic incentives to complete verification talks rather than leaving them unresolved. The JCPOA framework provides a template, though Iran's indefinite never-nuclear scope is broader and may require a new inspection architecture.
