Brent crude crossed $100 to close at $100.69 (▲2.29%) Thursday — the first close above $100 since May — as Yemen's Houthi rebels struck two Saudi oil tankers in the Red Sea and threatened to blockade the Bab al-Mandeb Strait, which carries approximately 12–15% of global maritime trade. WTI crude closed at $92.18 (▲4.54%). The threat introduces a potential third chokepoint to the Gulf energy disruption that has driven oil prices since the Iran conflict began in late June — joining Hormuz (the eastern Gulf exit, central to the Iran-US conflict) and the existing Houthi attacks on commercial Red Sea shipping — making full oil-price normalization dependent on three separate diplomatic resolutions rather than two.

The Bab al-Mandeb Strait connects the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden and is the passage that Gulf and Saudi oil uses to reach European markets. A Bab al-Mandeb blockade would reroute tankers around Africa's Cape of Good Hope — adding approximately 10–15 days and 6,000–10,000 nautical miles to the transit, with corresponding shipping cost and insurance premium increases. Unlike a Hormuz closure, a Bab al-Mandeb blockade would affect LNG exports from Qatar and the UAE more severely, as LNG carriers are larger and less maneuverable around the Cape. Friday's oil retreat from $100 suggests the market is treating the Houthi threat as a warning signal rather than an imminent execution — but the Houthi track record over the preceding 23 days is one of threats followed by action.

President Trump stated Thursday that the United States would hold Iran directly responsible for any future Houthi attacks on shipping and threatened "major military punishment" if the group targets vessels again — escalating US deterrence posture by creating an explicit military-response link between Houthi actions and consequences for Iran. Secretary of State Marco Rubio simultaneously said the US remains open to diplomacy with Iran even as US forces have conducted nine consecutive nights of strikes in the country. The two-track approach — maximum pressure alongside an open diplomacy channel — has been consistent US policy since the conflict began, but Trump's "major military punishment" language is the most direct threat to Iran over Houthi actions since the conflict started.

The deterrence logic is straightforward: if the US links Houthi attacks to consequences for Iran, it changes Iran's calculation about whether to support, direct, or restrain Houthi operations in the Red Sea. Iran's ability to restrain the Houthis is debated — the group has shown significant operational autonomy throughout the conflict — but US policy now treats that restraint as Iran's responsibility to produce or pay consequences for. Rubio's diplomacy signal is the pressure-valve that prevents Trump's statement from triggering immediate escalation: if Iran wants to negotiate, the channel is open. If Iran cannot or will not restrain the Houthis, the Trump warning implies military action against Iranian assets. That is the pressure calculus Pakistan must navigate in mediation.

UN Secretary-General António Guterres warned Thursday that the situation in the Middle East is "getting out of control" as mediators continue to search for a diplomatic off-ramp before the crisis escalates further. Pakistan is pursuing a second mediation attempt after its first effort produced the July 4 ceasefire that held for three days before Iran struck commercial vessels on July 6–7, prompting Trump to declare the truce over. The new mediation faces a significantly more complex architecture than the first: Russia's confirmed operational role in helping Iran plan CIA facility strikes requires a diplomatic channel to Moscow that the Pakistan-led framework does not currently include. Iran's new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei has still not appeared publicly, leaving the IRGC in operational control of a military campaign that now includes expanded Houthi support.

Guterres' "getting out of control" warning is UN diplomatic language for an escalation trajectory that the Security Council's existing tools are not positioned to slow — not a description of present chaos but a warning about where the trajectory leads if unchecked. A Bab al-Mandeb blockade, Trump military punishment of Iran for Houthi attacks, and a nuclear enrichment at 84%+ purity approaching the August 17 deadline simultaneously would produce conditions that neither the July 4 ceasefire model nor any current diplomatic framework is designed to address. 24 days from the nuclear deadline is the constraint that gives the urgency an endpoint; the question is whether any of the open channels — Pakistan, Rubio's diplomacy signal, European mediation — can produce a framework before August 17 that Iran's IRGC can present to Khamenei for authorization.
