The Lede · 06:00 ET

OpenAI launches GPT-5.5. SpaceX bets on in-house chips. Iran seizes vessels in Hormuz — Brent crosses $100.

OpenAI released GPT-5.5 Thursday — its first fully retrained base model since GPT-4.5, shipping with a 1-million-token context window and $5/$30 API pricing — while SpaceX disclosed in its $1.75 trillion IPO filing plans to manufacture its own GPUs through the Terafab joint venture with xAI, Tesla, and Intel. Iran's Revolutionary Guard seized two vessels in the Strait of Hormuz and fired on a third Thursday, driving Brent crude above $100 for the first time since the conflict began and pulling equities lower: the Nasdaq fell 0.89% to 24,438 and the S&P 500 slipped 0.41% to 7,108, with IBM tumbling more than 8% and ServiceNow cratering nearly 18% after earnings. President Trump brokered a three-week extension of the Israel–Lebanon ceasefire in an Oval Office meeting Thursday — calling it a "great chance" for a lasting peace deal — even as the US–Iran diplomatic track remains empty. Bitcoin recovered to $78,553 Friday morning, up 3.75%, after Thursday's failed attempt to clear $80,000; Strategy solidified its position as the world's largest corporate Bitcoin holder after surpassing BlackRock's holdings, with Capital Group's $747 million MSTR buy driving shares up 9.4%.

The week's biggest science story: the National Institutes of Health is approximately $1 billion behind its typical grant-award timeline, with thousands of studies stalled and labs on track to shut permanently — odds of winning an NIH grant at the National Cancer Institute have fallen from 1-in-10 to 1-in-25. A separate NYU Langone study of nearly 400,000 patients finds elevated neutrophil levels on a routine blood count can predict Alzheimer's risk years before clinical symptoms appear, potentially opening an early-detection window from tests already in widespread clinical use.

Science & Health $1B NIH grants · delayed · labs closing

NIH is $1 billion behind on grants — thousands of studies stalled, labs on track to close permanently.
