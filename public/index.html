Trump formally declared a ceasefire Wednesday on Day 7 of Swiss talks, while IAEA Director-General Rafael Grossi confirmed nuclear inspectors will visit Iran's bombed enrichment sites under the interim deal — "the accord says explicitly that the nuclear activities will be supervised by the IAEA... obviously to do that we will have to inspect... this is going to happen" — ending two days of contradictory messaging in which Trump claimed Iran "fully and completely" agreed to inspections while Tehran's foreign ministry denied any such plans. The ceasefire was tested almost immediately: Iran closed the Strait of Hormuz over renewed fighting between Israel and Hezbollah in Lebanon, a reminder that the broader Lebanon architecture remains unresolved.

Grossi's statement is the most diplomatically significant development Wednesday: it resolves the inspection dispute not by repeating Trump's assertion but by citing the accord's actual text — giving the requirement independent, UN-sourced authority rather than contested US framing. For the deal's durability, that distinction matters. The Hormuz closure over Lebanon fighting underscores the deal's structural vulnerability: Iran's leverage in these negotiations runs partly through Hormuz, and as long as Lebanon remains outside the ceasefire framework, Iran retains a pressure valve that can be deployed when the talks hit friction.

Food Preservatives Linked to High BP + Heart Disease · 112,000-Person Study · Mammalian Regeneration Switchable Heart Food preservatives: 112,000+ person study → nitrites + sulfites + sorbates + benzoates → significantly higher risk of high blood pressure + heart disease · common sources: cured meats + processed cheese + wine + dried fruits + packaged baked goods · mammalian regeneration: ability not evolutionarily lost → switched off → researchers show potential to reactivate → implications for wound healing + limb regeneration · Yale aging: nearly half of adults 65+ improved physically/mentally over 3-year study · newborn neurons: navigate tight brain spaces during development

A study of more than 112,000 people found that eating foods containing common preservatives — including nitrites in cured meats, sulfites in wine and dried fruits, and benzoates in packaged foods — is significantly linked to higher risk of high blood pressure and heart disease, one of the largest epidemiological studies to connect preservative exposure to cardiovascular outcomes. Separately, scientists found that the ability to rebuild complex body parts — a capacity thought to be evolutionarily lost in mammals — may not be lost at all: it may simply be switched off, and researchers demonstrated steps toward reactivating it in mammalian tissue models.

The preservatives finding has immediate dietary guidance implications: these compounds are in hundreds of widely consumed foods, and the magnitude of the association (described as significant across a 112,000-person sample) is large enough to matter at population level. The regeneration finding is more speculative but potentially transformative: if the biological machinery for limb and tissue regeneration exists in mammals but is inactive, identifying the switch could open treatment paths for traumatic injury, severe burns, and degenerative disease that current medicine has no approach to.
