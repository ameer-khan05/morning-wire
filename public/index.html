Gum Disease Bacteria → Calcific Aortic Valve Stenosis · Inflammation + Calcium Buildup · Presented AHA Basic Cardiovascular Sciences Sessions Boston · 214,000-Person Global Dementia Study: Risk Factors Differ by Country · Neuron Cytoskeleton as Cellular Gatekeeper Heart AHA (Boston Jul 13–16): gum disease bacteria → calcific aortic valve stenosis · mechanism: inflammation + calcium buildup in aortic valve · CAVS: leading cause of valve surgery in adults 65+ · 214,000-person global dementia study: risk factors differ widely by country · challenges one-size-fits-all prevention model · neuron cytoskeleton: microscopic skeleton acts as gatekeeper controlling cellular functions · ultrasound: non-invasive joint inflammation therapy

Researchers presenting at the American Heart Association's Basic Cardiovascular Sciences Scientific Sessions in Boston (July 13–16) found that bacteria linked to periodontal disease trigger inflammation and calcium buildup in the aortic valve, driving calcific aortic valve stenosis — a leading cause of valve replacement surgery in adults over 65 — establishing for the first time a cellular-level mechanism linking oral health to heart valve disease; and a separate analysis of more than 214,000 people found that dementia risk factors differ significantly across countries, with the findings challenging the standard one-size-fits-all prevention framework and calling for country-specific prevention strategies as global dementia cases are projected to triple by 2050.

The gum disease to aortic valve pathway is clinically significant because CAVS has been treated largely as age-related mechanical deterioration — interventions are surgical (TAVR or open replacement) rather than biological. A bacterial-inflammation mechanism means CAVS has a modifiable upstream cause: treating or preventing periodontal disease could reduce CAVS incidence before it becomes a surgical problem. The AHA presentation establishes the mechanism at the cellular level with sufficient precision to design interventional trials. The 214,000-person dementia study's country-specific finding challenges the 12-factor Lancet Commission framework widely adopted as the global standard — population-level European data may be inappropriately applied to contexts where different risk factor profiles dominate.
