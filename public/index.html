The Lede · 07:00 ET

Alphabet smashes estimates with Google Cloud up 63% — answering the OpenAI miss. Meta falls 9% after hours. Trump tells Iran to "just give up" as Brent briefly touches $126.

Wednesday's Big Tech earnings slate delivered the most important verdict of the AI cycle: Google Cloud grew 63% to $20.0 billion in Q1 2026, with Alphabet's total revenue hitting $109.9 billion on a $107.1 billion estimate and net income rising 81% — GOOGL surging 8.4% in after-hours. Amazon's AWS revenue reached $37.59 billion, beating the $36.64 billion estimate and accelerating from $29.27 billion a year ago, pushing AMZN up 2% after hours. The two results together answered last Tuesday's OpenAI miss report as directly as possible: the AI compute demand that ChatGPT wasn't capturing did not evaporate — it migrated to Google's infrastructure and Amazon's cloud. The complicating signals: Meta fell 9% after hours on user growth disappointment, with the company explicitly citing "internet disruptions in Iran" and Russia's WhatsApp restriction as material headwinds and raising its 2026 capex guidance to $125–$145 billion; Microsoft fell 2% despite Azure accelerating to 40% growth, as investors focused on full-year capex raised to $190 billion and capacity-constraint language from CEO Satya Nadella.

The Federal Reserve held rates at 3.5%–3.75% — as universally expected — but the meeting produced an extraordinary development: Chair Jerome Powell announced at his press conference that he will remain on the Federal Reserve Board of Governors after Kevin Warsh takes over the chairmanship, citing Trump's "legal attacks" as having left him "no choice." The FOMC vote split 8-4 — the most divided since October 1992 — with Stephen Miran preferring a cut and three other governors opposing an easing bias in the statement. Warsh is now the incoming chair of a board that still includes Powell, a structurally unprecedented dynamic. Thursday brings the data to follow: US Q1 GDP preliminary (Atlanta Fed GDPNow: 1.2%), March PCE inflation — the first full month at Brent above $100 — and earnings from Apple, Eli Lilly (already reported: beat, raised full-year guidance to $82–$85 billion, +7%), Mastercard, and Caterpillar.

Oil dominated the overnight session in a way that no equity move could. Brent crude briefly hit $126.41 per barrel Thursday morning — its highest price in four years — as President Trump declared the US naval blockade of Iranian ports a "success" on Day 62 of the conflict and urged Tehran to "just give up." Iran's parliament speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf dismissed the blockade as "ineffective," while the Iranian military said its restraint had been "intended to give diplomacy a chance." Brent pared gains back toward $116 as trading volumes thinned, but US gasoline prices hit their highest level since 2022 — a domestic political pressure point that is arriving precisely as the White House insists there is no urgency to resolve the Hormuz standoff. The European Central Bank held its deposit rate at 2.0% Thursday, with markets now pricing a potential first ECB hike as soon as June — adding a second central bank's tightening signal to the global macro overlay on top of the Fed's hold.

In science, NASA's Curiosity rover has made the most significant organic chemistry discovery in Mars exploration history: seven previously unidentified organic molecules — including a nitrogen-bearing compound structurally analogous to DNA precursors — preserved for 3.5 billion years in clay-bearing sandstone at Gale Crater. Published Tuesday in Nature Communications, the findings came from a first-of-its-kind experiment using a chemical-dissolution technique that unlocked molecular signatures no prior Mars analysis had reached. More than 20 organic compounds were detected in total. Scientists cannot yet determine whether the origin is biological, geological, or cosmological — but the scale and diversity of what Curiosity found has accelerated scientific consensus that early Mars was remarkably habitable.
