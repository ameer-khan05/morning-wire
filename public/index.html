Fungal Networks: 110 Quadrillion km · 4B Tons CO2/yr Into Soils · Mammalian Regeneration May Be Switched Off 4B tons Fungal underground networks: 110 quadrillion km · moves ~4 billion tons CO2 into soils/yr · largest natural carbon sink · mycorrhizal fungi: symbiotic with ~75% of terrestrial plants · mammalian regeneration: ability to rebuild complex body parts may be "switched off" not evolutionarily lost · probiotics: mental health benefits for older adults with depression · small clinical trial · PFAS "forever chemicals": broken down by UV-generated hydrogen radicals · no added chemicals · biodegradable protein beads from dairy/tofu waste: capture CO2 from atmosphere

Scientists mapped the global extent of underground fungal networks — finding they span 110 quadrillion kilometers and move approximately 4 billion tons of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere into soils annually, making mycorrhizal fungi the largest natural carbon sequestration mechanism on Earth by a wide margin. In a separate finding, scientists demonstrated that the mammalian ability to regenerate complex body parts may simply be switched off rather than evolutionarily lost, opening a new research frontier in regenerative medicine. A small clinical trial found probiotics offer meaningful mental health benefits for older adults with depression, and researchers showed that UV-generated hydrogen radicals can destroy PFAS "forever chemicals" without added reagents.

The fungal network finding reframes global carbon accounting: 4 billion tons of CO2 per year is more than any engineered carbon capture system currently deployed or in near-term development, and the mycorrhizal network already exists globally in symbiosis with roughly 75% of all terrestrial plant species. It is, in effect, the largest carbon capture infrastructure in existence — one that operates through natural biological processes and is threatened by agricultural practices that disrupt underground fungal networks. The regeneration finding is the longer-range story: if the molecular switches that enable complex limb and organ regrowth in other species exist in mammals in a dormant state, pharmacological or genetic approaches to reactivate them become a plausible research target rather than science fiction.
