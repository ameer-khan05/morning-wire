Pakistan's Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif announced Saturday morning that the United States and Iran have reached the "final, agreed upon text" of a peace agreement — to be called the "Islamabad Declaration" in recognition of Pakistan's mediating role — with an electronic signing expected within 24 hours and a formal ceremony anticipated in Geneva with Vice President Vance attending.

Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi confirmed the accord has "never been closer," and a senior US administration official described Iran's commitment to "never procure or develop nuclear weapons" as "a significant concession." The deal also features the unfreezing of confiscated Iranian assets, according to Iran's foreign minister — a harder deliverable than the oil sanctions lift previously reported, and one that addresses Iran's deepest financial grievance from the 104-day conflict. If signed within the 24-hour window Pakistan described, Day 106 opens with the Strait of Hormuz beginning its 30-day reopening clock and global oil markets pricing out the full conflict premium.

SpaceX's SPCX surged 19.3% to close at $161 on its first trading day Friday — from a $135 IPO price — lifting its implied market capitalization to approximately $2.12 trillion and confirming that institutional demand for the combined SpaceX-xAI entity exceeded even aggressive pre-IPO estimates. Markets extended the week's rally: the Dow closed at 51,202 (+353 pts) and the S&P at 7,431 (+0.5%) as SPCX's debut and near-certain Iran deal resolution drove equities to new post-conflict highs. Oil continued sliding: WTI closed at $84.29 and Brent at $87.27 as Hormuz normalization was priced with near certainty. In science, Stanford published a study in Science showing that blocking the aging protein 15-PGDH restores knee cartilage in old mice and human tissue — a potential path to eliminating knee and hip replacement surgeries for the one-in-five adults who develop osteoarthritis.

Stanford 15-PGDH Blocked · Cartilage Regrows in Old Mice + Human Tissue · Published in Science 15-PGDH Stanford Medicine: blocking 15-PGDH protein → knee cartilage regrowth in old mice · human tissue response: new functional cartilage · 15-PGDH = gerozyme (aging protein class, first ID'd 2023) · published Science Jun 12 · oral version in clinical trials (muscle weakness) · potential: eliminate knee + hip replacement surgeries · 1-in-5 US adults have osteoarthritis · fentanyl vaccine: human trials begin · 98% blockade in animal models · strength training 90-120 min/week: ▼death risk

Stanford Medicine researchers published in Science Friday that blocking 15-PGDH — an aging-related protein classified as a "gerozyme" — restored lost knee cartilage in old mice and, critically, prompted human tissue samples from knee replacement surgeries to produce new, functional cartilage when treated. The finding raises the prospect of repairing osteoarthritis-damaged cartilage with a local injection or oral medication, potentially eliminating the need for joint replacement surgery in the one-in-five US adults who develop the condition. A fentanyl vaccine entered human clinical trials, blocking up to 98% of the drug from reaching the brain in animal models.

15-PGDH belongs to the gerozyme class of proteins — enzymes that become more abundant with age and drive declining tissue function throughout the body — first identified by the same Stanford team in 2023. An oral formulation targeting the same protein is already in clinical trials for age-related muscle weakness, meaning the cartilage finding may accelerate rapidly toward a clinical application that shares regulatory groundwork already being built.
