The Lede · 07:00 ET

Iran proposes to reopen Hormuz if the US ends the blockade. Microsoft frees OpenAI to any cloud. Bitcoin breaks $79,000 in Las Vegas.

Iran filed a new peace proposal Monday through Pakistani mediators — Tehran will reopen the Strait of Hormuz and end the war if the US lifts its naval blockade, with nuclear negotiations deferred to a later stage. President Trump convened his national security team, including Secretary of State Rubio, to review the offer, but White House officials note the proposal makes no concessions on Iran's nuclear program — the central US demand. Simultaneously, Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi flew to St. Petersburg and met Russian President Vladimir Putin, who pledged support for Iran and praised its "courage and heroism" in resisting the blockade. Russia's military intelligence chief Igor Kostyukov was also present. Day 59 ends with Iran's diplomatic axis running through Moscow as much as Muscat — and Brent crude above $108 as markets price continued Hormuz closure.

In tech: Microsoft and OpenAI announced a sweeping revision of their foundational AI partnership. Under the new terms, OpenAI can serve customers on any cloud platform — not just Azure — while Microsoft's obligation to pay a revenue share to OpenAI ends. Microsoft retains a license to OpenAI's intellectual property through 2032, but no longer exclusively; the deal resolves the structural tension created by OpenAI's $50 billion Amazon compute deal and last week's Google-Anthropic $40 billion investment. Separately, analyst Ming-Chi Kuo revealed that Qualcomm and MediaTek are co-developing smartphone processors with OpenAI for an AI-native phone targeting 2028 production; Qualcomm surged 11% to $165. Bitcoin touched $79,000 — its highest level since late March — on the opening day of the Bitcoin 2026 Conference at The Venetian in Las Vegas. Strategy (formerly MicroStrategy) disclosed it purchased 3,273 more BTC for $255 million, bringing total holdings to 818,334 coins.

In science: Stanford University researchers published a landmark paper identifying the caudal granular insular cortex (CGIC) as the brain circuit that sustains chronic pain — silencing the CGIC stopped chronic pain entirely in animal models without impairing protective acute pain signals, offering the most targeted non-opioid intervention pathway in a generation. A parallel paper from Carnegie Mellon in the Journal of Neuroscience found a companion spinal cord-to-cortex loop that also sustains chronic pain — together, the two papers represent the most significant week in chronic pain neuroscience in modern medicine. Separately, a Nature Health study found that real-world mixtures of 31 individually "safe" pesticides raise cancer risk by up to 150% through synergistic biological disruption.

MSFT · OpenAI Any Cloud Revenue share ends · IP to 2032 · QCOM ▲11%

Microsoft ends OpenAI revenue share — multicloud era begins as Qualcomm surges 11% on AI smartphone chip deal.

Microsoft and OpenAI announced the restructuring of their foundational AI partnership Monday: OpenAI can now use any cloud provider for its products, Microsoft's obligation to pay a revenue share to OpenAI ends, and Microsoft's license to OpenAI's intellectual property is extended through 2032 — no longer exclusively. The deal resolves the tension created by OpenAI's $50 billion Amazon compute deal and codifies multicloud as the default AI infrastructure architecture. OpenAI products will ship first on Azure, but Microsoft cannot block OpenAI from serving customers elsewhere. Separately, Qualcomm surged 11% after analyst Ming-Chi Kuo confirmed OpenAI is building an AI-native smartphone with Qualcomm and MediaTek as chip partners; the device targets full production in 2028. Alphabet, Meta, and Microsoft report Q1 results Wednesday April 29.

Stanford · Brain Pain Off CGIC circuit · chronic pain · silencing works · Nature

Stanford finds the brain's chronic pain switch — silencing one circuit ends chronic pain without blocking protective acute pain.

Stanford University researchers identified the caudal granular insular cortex (CGIC) as a dedicated command center for chronic pain that is neurologically distinct from the acute pain pathway. Silencing the CGIC in experimental models stopped chronic pain entirely while leaving protective acute pain responses intact — the first direct evidence that chronic pain operates on a dedicated circuit that can be specifically targeted. The research, published in Nature, identifies a new non-opioid intervention point for the 50+ million Americans living with chronic pain. A companion paper from Carnegie Mellon in the Journal of Neuroscience found a parallel spinal cord–thalamus–cortex–brainstem loop that also sustains chronic pain — together marking the most significant week in pain neuroscience in a generation.
