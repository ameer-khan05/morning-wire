The Lede · 07:00 ET

Apple's $111.18B Q2 closes April with S&P 500 first-ever above 7,200 — then PCE prints 3.5%, Trump signals Iran strikes, and May opens into a different world.

Apple reported its best second fiscal quarter in company history on Thursday after the close: $111.18 billion in revenue, up 17% year over year, with diluted EPS of $2.01 beating the $1.94 estimate and Services — Apple's highest-margin segment — reaching an all-time record of $30.98 billion. AAPL jumped 3% in premarket Friday. The result, paired with Alphabet's Cloud +63% beat the night before, drove a broad equity surge on Thursday: the Dow jumped 790 points to 49,652.14, the S&P 500 rose 1% to close above 7,200 for the first time ever at 7,209.01, and the Nasdaq gained 0.9% to 24,892.31 — capping April as the best month for stocks since 2020. Apple also authorized an additional $100 billion in share buybacks and raised its dividend to $0.27 per share.

The day's macro data complicated the celebration. US Q1 2026 GDP came in at +2.0% annualized — a solid rebound from Q4 2025's near-stagnation at 0.5% — but the quarter's PCE price index surged to 4.5%, the highest since Q3 2022, and March PCE specifically rose 3.5% year over year, up sharply from 2.8% in February and well above the 2.6% consensus. Core PCE in March hit 3.2%. The inflation data is the first full confirmation of what the Brent-above-$100 quarter costs in real consumer prices — and it lands on Kevin Warsh's desk as he prepares to assume the Fed chairmanship, deepening the no-cut regime that has defined 2026's macro backdrop. The 10-year Treasury yield rose to 4.52% on the PCE print. Microsoft, meanwhile, announced a $900 million charge for its first-ever US voluntary retirement program, offering buyouts to roughly 8,700 employees under a "Rule of 70" framework — the clearest signal yet that AI is actively reshaping Big Tech's workforce structure.

Day 63 of the Iran conflict arrived with the highest diplomatic tension since the conflict began. President Trump said Washington "might need" to restart active military operations against Iran — CENTCOM has briefed options for "short and powerful" strikes on Iranian infrastructure — while Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian described the US naval blockade as an "extension of military operations" that is "intolerable." Trump added that only he and "a couple of other people" know the current status of any talks, a statement that simultaneously deepened market uncertainty and created cover for a back-channel process. The White House is simultaneously claiming that the conflict's ceasefire status halts the 60-day War Powers Resolution deadline — which fell on May 1 — a legal argument Senate Democrats are actively contesting. Oil climbed on May 1 as the legal and diplomatic ambiguity persisted, with Brent recovering toward $117 from Thursday's $114 settle after the prior session's $126 spike.

In space, NASA's SPHEREx mission released the most sweeping map of interstellar water ice ever produced: vast "interstellar glaciers" spanning more than 600 light-years in the Cygnus X star-forming region of the Milky Way. Published in the Astrophysical Journal, the finding confirms that water, carbon dioxide, and carbon monoxide ice are galaxy-wide phenomena, coating microscopic dust grains in the molecular clouds where stars are born — and, by extension, seeding the chemistry of new solar systems with the raw materials of life at cosmic scale. Earth's oceans, the study authors note, likely trace to exactly this kind of interstellar ice reservoir.

NASA's SPHEREx maps "interstellar glaciers" — 600+ light-years of water ice in Cygnus X, confirming that the chemistry of life is distributed across the galaxy at cosmic scale.

NASA's SPHEREx infrared space observatory has mapped water, carbon dioxide, and carbon monoxide ice across a 600-light-year region of the Cygnus X star-forming cloud — the most comprehensive interstellar ice survey ever produced. Published in the Astrophysical Journal, the finding reveals "interstellar glaciers": frozen water coating dust grains in the densest molecular cloud lanes, precisely where new solar systems form. The scientific implication is sweeping: the raw materials for life's chemistry are not a planetary accident but a galaxy-wide phenomenon, delivered to new solar systems as the molecular cloud collapses. Earth's oceans trace to exactly this kind of reservoir.
