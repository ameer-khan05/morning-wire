Ozempic Reduces Violent Behavior · Mayo Clinic Kidney Discovery · Preservatives + Heart Disease GLP-1 Rutgers/Criminology: GLP-1 drugs (Ozempic, Wegovy) weaken link between impulsive tendencies and violent behavior · Mayo Clinic: kidneys have hidden backup water-conservation system independent of ADH · 112,000-person study: common food preservatives linked to higher blood pressure + cardiovascular disease risk · Ozempic: behavioral medicine now alongside metabolic · GLP-1: impulsivity + alcohol consumption pathways

A Rutgers University study published in the journal Criminology found that GLP-1 receptor agonists — the drug class that includes Ozempic and Wegovy — meaningfully weaken the statistical link between impulsive tendencies and violent behavior, opening a behavioral medicine frontier for drugs previously known for metabolic outcomes. Scientists at Mayo Clinic uncovered a previously unknown kidney water-conservation backup system that operates independently of antidiuretic hormone (ADH), rewriting a textbook model of kidney physiology. And a major study of more than 112,000 people found that common food preservatives are associated with a statistically significant elevated risk of high blood pressure and cardiovascular disease events.

The GLP-1 finding is the most immediately consequential: Ozempic and Wegovy are already prescribed to tens of millions globally, meaning a behavioral violence-reduction effect would have population-scale implications. Researchers cautioned that the study demonstrates correlation, not causation. The kidney backup system finding has implications for treating hyponatremia and dehydration in clinical settings where the standard ADH pathway is impaired.
