Friday's April jobs report arrived at +115,000 — well above the 65,000 consensus — driving the S&P 500 to a fresh record at 7,398.93 and the Nasdaq to 26,247.08, its sixth consecutive weekly gain, the longest winning streak since 2024. Then Iran's counterproposal landed Saturday, and Trump called it "TOTALLY UNACCEPTABLE" on Sunday. Oil is up $4 to near $99 Monday morning, and the peace deal that drove the week's records is, for now, in suspension.

Iran's counterproposal — delivered through Pakistani intermediaries Saturday — was not a narrowing of the moratorium duration gap described in last week's reporting. It was a set of four maximalist demands: US recognition of Iranian sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz, full compensation for war damages, release of all frozen Iranian assets and removal of all US sanctions, and a binding guarantee of no further American military action on Iranian territory. Trump's Sunday Truth Social post was direct: "I have just read the response from Iran's so-called 'Representatives.' I don't like it — TOTALLY UNACCEPTABLE!" The Iranian foreign ministry responded that the proposal was "reasonable," and Iranian state media reported that Iran had rejected the original US framework as a "surrender" document. Monday opens with WTI crude at $98.50 — up $3.88 per barrel — and Brent at $104.20, oil markets repricing the Iran deal from approximately 50% probability toward materially lower. The Strait of Hormuz remains closed to commercial shipping. The fourth round of Iran-US talks in Oman proceeds Monday, but without technical-level negotiators — high-level only, focused on whether there is a framework to negotiate or whether the tracks have diverged.

The backdrop for today's geopolitical development is a US economy that is holding better than the conflict's pessimists expected. Friday's +115,000 was the second consecutive beat: March printed +178,000, April was expected to show the oil-economy slowdown at +65,000, and instead the economy added 115,000 jobs, with gains in healthcare, transportation and warehousing, and retail. The S&P 500's six-week winning streak and the Nasdaq's record close of 26,247.08 reflect an equity market that has been pricing the dual tailwinds of a resilient economy and an approaching Iran deal — tailwinds that remain partially intact (the economy) and partially suspended (the deal). The Trump-Xi summit opens Thursday in Beijing (May 14-15), with Iran now explicitly at the top of an agenda that already included trade, AI governance, Taiwan, and rare earths. Trump has been publicly leaning on Xi to use China's influence over Tehran; Iran's maximalist Saturday proposal arrives three days before that summit, potentially as a signal to Beijing as much as to Washington.

OpenAI crossed $25 billion in annualized revenue in February 2026 — growing from $6 billion at end-2024 in 14 months — and this week completed two structural moves that define its next phase: releasing GPT-5.5 Instant as ChatGPT's new default model (52.5% fewer hallucinations on high-stakes medical, legal, and financial prompts; AIME math score up from 65.4 to 81.2; 30% more concise) and renegotiating its Microsoft exclusivity agreement to allow distribution through AWS and Google Cloud. The IPO timeline is Q4 2026, with Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, and Morgan Stanley as advisors; internal signals suggest a possible 2027 slip. Separately, Microsoft, Google, and xAI agreed to give the US NIST pre-launch access to unreleased models for cybersecurity evaluation — the first voluntary government AI testing framework in the US. In science, a landmark Finnish 10-year randomized trial found that partial meniscectomy — one of the world's most common orthopedic surgeries — worsens knee osteoarthritis progression compared with a sham procedure: 81% OA progression in the surgical group versus 70% in the sham, and nearly three times as many patients requiring additional surgery.

Meniscectomy · 10-Year RCT · Surgery Worsens OA 81% OA Partial meniscectomy Finland 10-year RCT · 81% OA progression surgical group · 70% sham group · 8 vs 3 reoperations · globally common surgery · SP8 gene limb regeneration · youthful microbiome prevents liver cancer mice

A landmark Finnish 10-year randomized trial finds that partial meniscectomy — one of the world's most common orthopedic surgeries — worsens knee osteoarthritis outcomes: 81% OA progression in the surgical group versus 70% in the sham group, and nearly three times as many patients requiring additional surgery. Also: the SP8 gene controlling limb regeneration in axolotls is present in humans; a youthful gut microbiome transplant prevents liver cancer in mice.

The Finnish study's 10-year follow-up is the longest controlled evidence yet that partial meniscectomy — trimming a damaged knee meniscus — is not merely ineffective but actively harmful: it accelerates the osteoarthritis it is nominally treating, measured by objective radiographic progression and reoperation rates. The surgery is performed millions of times annually worldwide; its use has declined in Finland based on earlier evidence, but it remains routine in many countries. The SP6/SP8 gene discovery — published in PNAS, from research in axolotls, zebrafish, and mice — identifies the genetic pathway that governs limb regeneration in animals that can do it, and confirms those same genes exist in humans. CRISPR removal of SP8 blocks limb regeneration in axolotls; a viral gene therapy delivering FGF8 (which SP8 activates) partially restored digit regrowth in mice — the first experimental evidence that human-present genes could be leveraged for mammalian regeneration.
