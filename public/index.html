GPT-5.6 Sol · Terra · Luna · Limited Preview · ARD Standard · 11 Companies · Google + Microsoft · Apache 2.0 GPT-5.6 OpenAI GPT-5.6 (Jun 26): Sol flagship $5/$30 per 1M tokens · max reasoning + ultra mode (subagents) · Terra $2.50/$15 · Luna $1/$6 · ~20 govt-approved preview partners · general availability coming weeks · ARD spec: 11 companies (Google, Microsoft, Cisco, Databricks, GitHub, GoDaddy, Hugging Face, NVIDIA, Salesforce, ServiceNow, Snowflake) · ai-catalog.json + registry search API · first use: GitHub Copilot Agent Finder · counters OpenAI/Anthropic proprietary ecosystems

OpenAI launched a limited preview of GPT-5.6 Sol, Terra, and Luna on Friday — a three-tier model family that introduces ultra mode, a reasoning architecture that deploys subagents in parallel to split and accelerate complex work — restricted to roughly 20 companies whose participation has been approved by the US government. Sol is the flagship at $5 input / $30 output per million tokens; Terra delivers competitive performance to GPT-5.5 at 2× lower cost ($2.50/$15); Luna is the highest-speed, lowest-cost option ($1/$6). All three include OpenAI's most robust safety stack to date, with heightened protections for high-risk cyber requests and repeated misuse patterns. General availability is expected in coming weeks.

On the same day, eleven industry leaders — Google, Microsoft, Cisco, Databricks, GitHub, GoDaddy, Hugging Face, NVIDIA, Salesforce, ServiceNow, and Snowflake — released the Agentic Resource Discovery (ARD) specification, an Apache 2.0–licensed open standard that enables AI agents to locate, verify, and connect with tools, APIs, Model Context Protocol servers, and other agents at runtime. Organizations publish an ai-catalog.json file listing available capabilities; registries index those catalogs and expose a plain-language search API that agents query at runtime. The spec's first implementation appears in GitHub Copilot's new Agent Finder. The ARD standard is a direct counterweight to the proprietary agent ecosystems OpenAI and Anthropic have built — a bet by eleven major companies that AI infrastructure coordination, like the web before it, should be standardized and open rather than platform-controlled.

Binance confirmed Friday it will halt all EU services July 1 — new orders, deposits, sign-ups, and staking products — after withdrawing its Greek MiCA license application on June 24 when the Greek regulator signaled it would reject the application, primarily over co-founder Changpeng Zhao's ability to pass the "fit and proper" test for owners and managers. The world's largest crypto exchange will be locked out of the 27-nation EU market from July 1 until it secures a replacement license; it now plans to apply through France, though approval is unlikely before the deadline. User funds remain safe and withdrawable. The Ethereum Foundation separately announced June 23 that it had cut 54 jobs (20% of its workforce) and reduced its 2026 operating budget by 40%, closing its ZK research lab and reorganizing into five domain-focused protocol clusters.

The Ethereum Foundation's reset toward an endowment-style model — targeting a reduction from spending roughly 15% of its treasury annually to 5% per year by 2030 — comes amid nine senior departures since January 2026 and a reorganization that includes smaller Devcon events, AI-assisted formal verification replacing some research roles, and a shift toward supporting specialized external client teams. Bitcoin, meanwhile, rebounded to $59,770 Saturday from Friday's $58,900 low — its lowest since September 2024 — with the Fear and Greed Index at 13 (Extreme Fear) and a mining difficulty adjustment scheduled for today. The US Senate on June 22 passed legislation banning the Federal Reserve from issuing a retail central bank digital currency (CBDC) through 2030, embedded in the 21st Century Road to Housing Act.

Trump told The New York Times in an interview published this week that Iran will be permitted to conduct low-level uranium enrichment — a major retreat from the zero-enrichment position the US had maintained as a hard requirement throughout the Islamabad MOU negotiations. The statement is the first presidential acknowledgment of compromise space between the US's stated zero-enrichment demand and Iran's characterization of enrichment as a non-negotiable sovereign right, and it significantly changes the publicly known parameters of what a permanent deal could look like. Ayatollah Khamenei has consistently responded that enrichment is central to Iran's sovereign nuclear program and cannot be halted, but has not characterized low-level enrichment as explicitly acceptable.

Violence broke out again in Lebanon as the MOU entered Day 10 — 50 days remain before the August 17 expiry — complicating the clause 1 requirement for Israeli forces to withdraw from southern Lebanon. A separate dispute has emerged over whether Iran agreed to allow UN inspectors to view bombed Iranian nuclear sites: US and Iranian officials gave conflicting accounts to mediators, adding an inspection access dimension to the enrichment talks. The Strait of Hormuz remains open; Iranian tankers continue to flow under the 60-day sanctions waiver, keeping WTI at $70.34 and preserving the disinflation incentive that stabilizes the deal economically even as its political foundations are tested daily.

Anti-Inflammatory Diet ▼29% Dementia Risk · Even With Alzheimer's Biomarkers · UK Biobank 84K · Focus Neurons · Stroke Findings ▼29% Swedish study: ~1,900 older adults with Alzheimer's biomarkers (p-tau217) over 8 years → lower-inflammatory diet → ▼29% dementia risk · also: nearly 1 year longer without dementia · UK Biobank: 84,342 adults + cardiometabolic diseases + anti-inflammatory diet → ▼31% dementia risk · brain imaging: larger gray matter volume · focus neurons: tiny group in ancient brain region = built-in focus filter → helps brain ignore distractions · stroke: Mediterranean diet + shingles vaccine + asundexian all found beneficial in three studies · fructose vs glucose: brain treats them differently

A Swedish study of nearly 1,900 older adults with established Alzheimer's blood biomarkers — including elevated phosphorylated tau-217 (p-tau217) — found over eight years of follow-up that those following a lower-inflammatory eating pattern had a 29% lower risk of developing dementia and lived nearly a year longer without dementia onset, compared with those eating a more pro-inflammatory diet. The finding is clinically significant because the benefit was demonstrated in people already showing biological markers of Alzheimer's disease — the population that conventional medicine typically treats as past the point of dietary prevention. A separate UK Biobank study of 84,342 adults with cardiometabolic diseases confirmed a 31% lower dementia risk associated with an anti-inflammatory diet, with brain imaging showing larger gray matter volume and reduced white matter lesions in adherent participants.

Scientists also identified a small population of neurons in an ancient brain region that functions as a built-in focus filter — helping the brain suppress distractions and sharpen attention selectively — offering a neural basis for attentional control with implications for treating focus disorders. Three separate stroke studies found the Mediterranean diet, the shingles vaccine (Shingrix), and the anticoagulant asundexian each measurably beneficial for stroke prevention or recovery, representing a convergence of dietary, immunological, and pharmacological tools against one of the world's leading causes of disability. And a glucose/fructose brain study found that while both register identically on nutrition labels, glucose strongly suppresses hunger-promoting neurons in mice while fructose has a far weaker effect — a mechanistic distinction with implications for how ultra-processed foods affect appetite regulation.
