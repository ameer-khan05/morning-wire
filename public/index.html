Semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy) Slows Biological Aging 9% in HIV RCT — DunedinPACE Epigenetic Clock · First Randomized Controlled Trial Evidence · 42% Showed Slowed Aging · 49% Increased Telomere Length · Multi-Organ Improvements · Sweeteners Change Gut Bacteria · KCL-286 Alzheimer's Candidate −9% Semaglutide: 9% reduction in biological aging speed (DunedinPACE) · randomized controlled trial · adults with HIV · 42% showed slowed aging · 49% increased telomere length · improvements: blood + brain + heart + liver + kidney · PubMed + UC study · sweeteners: 100+ drug interactions via gut bacteria changes · KCL-286: DNA repair + neuroinflammation reduction in Alzheimer's mouse model

Semaglutide — the active ingredient in Ozempic and Wegovy — slowed biological aging by 9% in a randomized controlled trial of adults with HIV, as measured by the DunedinPACE epigenetic clock, with 42% of participants showing slower biological aging, 34% showing reduced all-cause mortality markers, and 49% showing increased telomere length across blood, brain, heart, liver, and kidney measures — published in PubMed and described by the University of California as the first clinical RCT evidence that GLP-1 drugs may modify the pace of human aging; separately, a large lab study found 100-plus cases where sweeteners changed gut bacteria interactions with medications, and the spinal cord injury drug KCL-286 showed Alzheimer's promise in mice through DNA repair and neuroinflammation reduction.

A 9% reduction in biological aging speed in a controlled trial is not noise — the DunedinPACE clock measures the pace of biological change simultaneously across multiple organ systems. The HIV population was chosen because HIV accelerates biological aging, making the effect detectable in a smaller sample; whether the magnitude transfers to non-HIV populations requires follow-on trials. The drug is not approved as an anti-aging intervention, but the multi-organ improvement profile — improvements spanning six biological systems in a single trial — positions semaglutide as the first commercially available drug with controlled-trial evidence of broad aging-biology modification.
