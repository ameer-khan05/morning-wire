Day 6 of US-Iran nuclear talks in Switzerland produced a key diplomatic breakthrough: Iran agreed to allow IAEA nuclear inspectors back into the country, and a coordination mechanism for Hormuz demining was established, with VP Vance describing it as "a good day." Four formal working groups were constituted — covering Sanctions Termination, Nuclear Affairs, Reconstruction and Economic Development, and Monitoring and Implementation — providing structural machinery for the 54 remaining days of the 60-day MOU window. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent separately announced a 60-day waiver on Iran oil sanctions, immediately unlocking Iranian crude for global sale with legal cover on oil, petrochemicals, banking transactions, shipping, and maritime insurance through August 21.

The IAEA inspection agreement is the most substantively important development from Day 6. Previous nuclear deals with Iran have strained over verification — the JCPOA collapsed partly because verification disputes became irresolvable without a trusted inspection regime. Iran's agreement to allow inspectors back establishes the monitoring foundation before enrichment and stockpile numbers are on the table, which is the correct sequencing: trust-building on verification first, then hard limits. The oil sanctions waiver operates on a separate track from the nuclear talks but reinforces them economically: Iran's economy benefits from the 60-day lifting, creating a domestic constituency for the talks continuing. For oil markets, $73.72 WTI is the lowest level in months and begins to build the June energy disinflation the FOMC will track.

Vaping Likely Causes Lung + Oral Cancer · UNSW Carcinogenesis Review · OLE Molecule for Alzheimer's · Longevity Genes Cancer Vaping: UNSW Sydney + multiple universities → Carcinogenesis review → nicotine vapes "likely" cause lung + oral cancer · evidence: human biomarkers (DNA damage + oxidative stress) + mouse lung tumours + lab cellular damage · carcinogens: volatile organic compounds + metals from heating coils · long-term pop data not yet available → risk not quantifiable but early signs strong · OLE molecule: restores brain's immune cells in Alzheimer's models → reduces plaque buildup → improves memory · longevity genes: rare variants in long-lived families → may extend healthy lifespan · fentanyl vaccine: Scripps Research → stops drug before reaching brain · brain health improves at any age: 3-year study of ~4,000 adults

A major systematic review by UNSW Sydney and collaborating Australian universities, published in the journal Carcinogenesis, concluded that nicotine vapes are likely to cause lung and oral cancers — the first review to reach a "likely causation" conclusion based on converging evidence from human biomarkers showing DNA damage and oxidative stress, mouse studies that produced lung tumours, and laboratory work identifying cellular damage pathways, alongside volatile organic carcinogens and metals released by heating coils in e-cigarette devices. Separately, researchers identified a molecule called OLE that restored the brain's immune cells (microglia) to a more protective state in Alzheimer's disease models, reducing toxic amyloid plaque buildup and improving memory — a potential new treatment pathway for a disease with no disease-modifying drug.

The vaping-cancer review's public health implications are immediate: vaping regulation in most jurisdictions still treats the technology as a smoking cessation tool with manageable risk rather than an independent carcinogen. If "likely causes lung and oral cancer" becomes the scientific consensus through replication, it would justify regulatory treatment comparable to cigarettes — including marketing restrictions, flavoring bans, and age verification requirements currently applied inconsistently. The OLE Alzheimer's finding advances a different pathway from the amyloid antibody approaches (lecanemab, donanemab) approved in recent years: rather than clearing plaques directly, OLE restores the microglia clearing mechanism — attacking the problem from the cellular immune side rather than the amyloid side.
