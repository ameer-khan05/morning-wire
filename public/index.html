The Lede · 08:00 ET

Trump's Situation Room meeting Friday ended without an Iran deal signature — he posted new social media demands requiring Iran to agree to never build a nuclear weapon, open Hormuz immediately with no tolls, and allow the destruction of its 900-pound highly-enriched uranium stockpile, prompting Iran's supreme leader adviser to call it "betraying diplomacy for the third time" and Defense Secretary Hegseth to warn the US is "more than capable" of resuming strikes; yet markets closed Friday at new records — S&P 500 at 7,580.06, Nasdaq at 26,972.62, and the Dow crossing 51,000 for the first time at 51,032.46 — driven by Dell's AI server blowout of $16.1 billion in revenue (+757% year-over-year) that completed the AI infrastructure earnings trifecta with Nvidia and Snowflake.

Trump's Situation Room meeting on Friday — billed as the venue for a "final determination" on the Iran deal — produced a social media post rather than a signature. The new demands he outlined are materially harder than the tentative framework that was reportedly on his desk Thursday: where the prior text proposed 60 days of nuclear talks after Hormuz reopened, Trump's Friday conditions appear to require Iran to commit pre-deal to a permanent nuclear weapons ban and the physical destruction of its roughly 900-pound highly-enriched uranium stockpile — the most diplomatically consequential precondition either side has stated publicly in the 92-day conflict. Iran's supreme leader adviser Mojtaba Zonnour's "betraying diplomacy for the third time" formulation references the 2015 JCPOA withdrawal (Trump's first term), the collapsed May 20 talks, and now this episode — signaling that Tehran reads the new conditions as a deal-ender, not an opening bid. Hegseth's parallel statement added the military clarity: the US weapons stockpiles are "enough to get the job done," and if Iran won't accept a deal that ensures they will never get a nuclear weapon, they can "deal with" the US military. The dual-track — sanctions, drone intercepts, military readiness on one side; deal text language on the other — is now running at maximum intensity with maximum ambiguity about which track prevails.

Markets absorbed the deal stall and closed at records anyway. Dell's Q1 FY2027 report — released after Thursday's close — was the catalyst: record total revenue of $43.8 billion (+88% year-over-year), AI-Optimized Server revenue of $16.1 billion (+757% year-over-year), AI orders of $24.4 billion exceeding prior-quarter shipments by 51%, and a full-year AI server guidance raised to $60 billion. The stock surged 32% to a new all-time high. Dell's result is the enterprise hardware confirmation that Nvidia's chip-layer result (Tuesday, $81.6B Q1) and Snowflake's data-platform result (Thursday, $1.39B Q1) set up: the entire AI infrastructure supply chain — from silicon to data center rack to enterprise server — is growing at 33-757% year-over-year and accelerating. The S&P 500 reached a new record 7,580.06 (+0.22%), its ninth consecutive week of gains. The Nasdaq closed at 26,972.62 (+0.20%), capping May with an 8% monthly advance. The Dow crossed 51,000 for the first time in its history at 51,032.46 (+0.72%). The Russell 2000 declined 0.59% to 2,919.34 — the only major US index to fall on Friday — as the deal stall kept rate-hike probability from declining further, holding back the rate-sensitive small-cap sectors. Bitcoin recovered to $74,054 over the weekend from Friday's $73,381 level as the deal's non-failure kept the macro ceiling from worsening.

On the science front, a landmark study led by the Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation at the University of Washington and published in The Lancet established that chronic kidney disease now silently affects 788 million people globally — nearly doubling from 378 million in 1990 — and has become the world's ninth-leading cause of death, claiming 1.5 million lives annually. China (152 million) and India (138 million) bear the highest national burdens. The condition is often asymptomatic until kidney function has significantly declined, meaning the majority of those 788 million do not know they have it. Cambridge University researchers separately published findings from miniature brain-and-spinal-cord laboratory organoids — systems built from human cells that can send electrical signals and trigger actual muscle contractions — discovering that human neurons gradually lose their ability to regrow after damage, a finding that illuminates why nerve injuries are often permanent and identifies a potential target for regeneration research. The two findings share a common urgency: diseases that progress silently and damage that is assumed permanent may be neither inevitable nor irreversible.

Cambridge University researchers published findings from miniature brain-and-spinal-cord laboratory organoids — human cell systems that send electrical signals and trigger actual muscle contractions — discovering that human neurons gradually lose the ability to regrow after damage, a finding that illuminates why nerve injuries are often permanent.

The CKD study's doubling in 30 years tracks the global rise of its primary drivers — type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and obesity — conditions that are themselves growing in the populations with the highest CKD burden. The Cambridge organoid discovery is mechanistically significant: understanding why neurons lose regenerative capacity is the precondition for designing therapies that restore it. A separate study found sleep deprivation damages specific brain circuits responsible for social memory — making it harder to recognize familiar individuals — with caffeine shown to restore neuron communication in those pathways. Research also found melatonin supplements may help night-shift workers boost DNA repair processes, partially offsetting the health damage associated with overnight work.
