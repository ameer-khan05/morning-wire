The Lede · 07:00 ET

S&P 500 and Nasdaq retreat from records as OpenAI's miss report sinks chip stocks. Wednesday brings four Mag-7 earnings, Powell's final FOMC call, and the UAE's OPEC exit as Brent breaks $114.

Tuesday's session ended with the S&P 500 down 0.49% to 7,138.80 and the Nasdaq Composite down 0.90% to 24,663.80 — the sharpest one-day retreat since Monday's twin record closes — driven by a single news event: The Wall Street Journal reported Tuesday that OpenAI missed its own internal targets for weekly active users and revenue, with ChatGPT's billion-user annual goal slipping as Google Gemini and Anthropic captured enterprise share. Oracle fell 4%, Broadcom and AMD fell 3-4%, and Nvidia led chip stocks lower — a broad repudiation of the AI infrastructure thesis less than 24 hours before four Mag-7 companies report. S&P futures are muted Wednesday morning as investors brace for the most event-dense single trading session of 2026.

Alphabet, Meta, Microsoft, and Amazon all report Q1 earnings after the close today — a combined slate representing more than $650 billion in AI infrastructure commitments for 2026. Alphabet consensus calls for $106.9 billion in revenue with Google Cloud near 50% growth; Meta consensus calls for $55.36 billion (14-quarter beat streak intact going in); Microsoft calls for $81.4 billion with Azure growth at 37-38%; Amazon for continued AWS acceleration past last quarter's 24% growth rate. The OpenAI miss report has introduced a darker reading of the $650 billion capex figure: if OpenAI — the demand signal that justified most of that spending — cannot meet its own user targets, investors will demand concrete evidence tonight that the AI ecosystem is absorbing compute at the rate the capex cycle assumed. The Federal Reserve also announces today what is likely Jerome Powell's final interest-rate decision as chair, with markets pricing a hold at 4.25-4.50%; the Senate Banking Committee votes to advance Kevin Warsh's nomination 13-11 on party lines.

In geopolitics: the UAE announced Tuesday it will leave OPEC and OPEC+ effective May 1 — ending nearly 57 years of membership — in the most significant fracturing of the Gulf oil coalition since the cartel's founding. The move reflects the Iran war's accelerating impact on regional alignments: the UAE has chafed for years under Saudi-dictated production quotas, and the conflict's disruption to Hormuz-adjacent export routes provided the exit catalyst. Brent surged to $114.64 (▲ 3.04%) on the combined news of the UAE departure and Day 61 of the Iran stalemate. WTI held near $99.32 — the Brent-WTI spread widening to roughly $15, reflecting Hormuz disruption's differential impact on internationally traded versus domestically sourced crude. Trump said "don't rush me" when asked for a timeline on Iran, the most combative framing yet of his no-deadline posture. BTC slid to $76,888 as risk-off sentiment dominated crypto ahead of the FOMC.

In science: a clinical trial at São Paulo State University found that a daily 2,000 IU vitamin D supplement boosted breast cancer chemotherapy response rates by 79% — 43% of the vitamin D group achieved complete cancer disappearance versus 24% in the placebo group, findings published in ScienceDaily on April 28. Separately, Texas A&M researchers published a solution to the 100-year-old cracking problem in organ cryopreservation — discovering that tuning the glass transition temperature of preservation solutions eliminates the fractures that destroy organs during ultra-cold storage, bringing the idea of indefinitely banking transplant organs within engineering reach for the first time.

Vitamin D · Chemo · Cancer +79% Breast cancer complete remission · 43% vs 24% · São Paulo State · ScienceDaily

Vitamin D boosts breast cancer chemotherapy success by 79% — clinical trial shows 43% complete remission versus 24% placebo.

A clinical trial at São Paulo State University's Botucatu School of Medicine found that adding a daily 2,000 IU vitamin D supplement to standard pre-surgical chemotherapy nearly doubled the rate of complete breast cancer remission: 43% of the vitamin D group achieved complete pathological response (cancer disappearance) compared to 24% in the placebo group — a 79% relative improvement published in ScienceDaily on April 28. Most participants entered the trial vitamin D-deficient (below 20 ng/mL), suggesting the benefit works partly through correcting a widespread baseline deficiency. Separately, Texas A&M researchers solved the 100-year-old cracking problem in organ cryopreservation, opening a path to banking transplant organs indefinitely.
