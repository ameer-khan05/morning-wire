UCLA NDRG1: Why Aging Muscles Heal Slowly · 3.5x Higher in Old Cells · Repair vs Survival Trade-Off · Lacunar Stroke Rethink: Widened Arteries · KAIST: Bleeding Stopped in 1 Second 3.5x UCLA: NDRG1 protein 3.5x higher in old muscle stem cells vs young · suppresses mTOR pathway → slows cell activation + repair · blocking NDRG1: cells act young again (faster repair) · cost: fewer cells survive long-term · published Science Apr 10, 2026 · lacunar stroke: University of Edinburgh 229-patient study · finding: widened + enlarged deep brain arteries (basilar artery dolichoectasia) · 4x greater risk · not fatty plaque blockage · explains aspirin failure · KAIST: spray-on powder · alginate + gellan gum + chitosan · stops severe bleeding in 1 second by forming gel

UCLA researchers identified NDRG1 as the protein accumulating at 3.5 times higher levels in aging muscle stem cells versus young ones — acting as a cellular brake that suppresses the mTOR signaling pathway and slows repair — but found a critical trade-off: blocking NDRG1 makes old cells act young again and accelerates healing, while reducing the number of stem cells that survive for future repairs. A 229-patient University of Edinburgh study separately found that lacunar stroke — the most common stroke subtype — is most strongly linked to widening of deep brain arteries, not fatty plaque blockage, explaining why aspirin often fails in this stroke type and driving the new LACI-3 trial of vessel-targeting drugs.

South Korea's KAIST developed a spray-on hemostatic powder made from three natural ingredients — alginate from seaweed, gellan gum from bacteria, and chitosan from crustacean shells — that reacts with blood calcium ions to form a gel in approximately one second, stopping life-threatening bleeding while absorbing more than seven times its own weight in blood. Originally designed for battlefield wounds, the powder could transform emergency and disaster care; it requires clinical trials and regulatory approval before real-world deployment. The three science findings together span a discovery about a fundamental aging mechanism, a rethink of the most common stroke's cause, and a new hemostatic tool with combat and civilian applications.
