SLAMF6 · Nature Jun 9 · Hidden Cancer Immune Brake · T-Cell Exhaustion · New Therapy Target SLAMF6 Nature Jun 9 2026 · Dr. André Veillette + team · Université de Montréal · SLAMF6 immune checkpoint · T-cell exhaustion without tumor contact · explains immunotherapy resistance · antibody blockade restores T-cell function in mice · new treatment direction for non-responders · protein beads from dairy+tofu waste capture CO2 · ultra-processed foods + attention decline · largest opioid pain review: small short-lived benefits

A study published in Nature on June 9 by Dr. André Veillette and colleagues at the Université de Montréal identified SLAMF6 as a previously unknown immune checkpoint molecule that enables cancer cells to exhaust T cells — crucially, it activates directly on the T cell surface without requiring tumor-cell contact, distinguishing it from known checkpoints like PD-1 and CTLA-4. Researchers developed antibodies that block SLAMF6 and restored T-cell cancer-killing function in mouse models, offering a new therapeutic target for patients whose immunotherapy has stopped working. Separately, scientists developed biodegradable protein beads made from dairy and tofu waste that capture CO2 from the atmosphere more efficiently than many current technologies.

The SLAMF6 discovery is mechanistically significant because it operates through a self-activating pathway on T cells — meaning the immune suppression does not require the tumor to be physically present and touching the T cell. This helps explain one of oncology's persistent puzzles: why some patients respond dramatically to checkpoint inhibitors (anti-PD-1, anti-CTLA-4) while others with similar tumor profiles do not. SLAMF6 blockade offers an additional or complementary switch for the non-responder population. A separate study linked ultra-processed food consumption to poorer attention and slower mental processing speed, and the largest-ever opioid review found these drugs deliver only small, short-lived benefits for acute pain.
