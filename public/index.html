The US launched a fourth wave of airstrikes on Iran Monday — hitting dozens of targets and using one-way attack drones at sea for the first time in the conflict — as Iran's IRGC declared the Strait of Hormuz "closed until further notice," retaliated with fresh strikes on US military facilities in Bahrain, claimed destruction of radar systems in Oman, and struck Kuwait and Jordan again; Trump told reporters the memorandum of understanding was "over," CNN reported the ceasefire "disintegrating," and Al Jazeera confirmed Iran had made the attacks in its third consecutive day of retaliatory strikes against US allies in the Gulf as the August 17 nuclear deadline stands 35 days out with no diplomatic framework to reach it — the Doha process collapsed when Iran struck its Gulf mediators on Sunday.

Iran targeting Bahrain — home of US Navy 5th Fleet headquarters — is the most direct strike on American naval command infrastructure in the conflict to date, and simultaneously addresses the US's primary tool for disputing the Hormuz closure: if Iran degrades 5th Fleet's operational capacity, it removes the instrument that US CENTCOM is relying on to maintain freedom-of-navigation in contested Hormuz waters. Trump's MOU declaration removes the last formal diplomatic constraint on the escalation ladder; 35 days remain to the nuclear deadline, and there is currently no framework, no intermediary, and no diplomatic channel capable of producing a negotiated resolution in that window.

Gum Disease Bacteria May Drive Calcific Aortic Valve Stenosis · Inflammation + Calcium Buildup in Heart Valve · Tau Protein Essential for Memory Consolidation · Alzheimer's Drug Constraint · MRI Redesigned with Metamaterials · Clearer Images on Existing Scanners Oral–Heart Gum disease bacteria: may drive calcific aortic valve stenosis · mechanism: inflammation + calcium deposit in valve tissue · disease: most common age-related valvular heart disease · ~2.5% adults 75+ · no approved pharmacological treatment · modifiable risk factor: periodontal treatment (not yet clinically validated for valve disease) · tau: essential for organizing place cells + grid cells for memory consolidation · Alzheimer's constraint: tau-clearing drugs must account for memory function disruption · metamaterial MRI: clearer images on existing scanners · less time · no new hardware required · DNA chip: simultaneous DNA synthesis · superconductors: ML + quantum physics

Researchers found that bacteria linked to gum disease may drive calcific aortic valve stenosis — one of the most common age-related heart conditions, affecting roughly 2.5% of adults over 75 and with no approved pharmacological treatment for progression — by triggering inflammation and calcium buildup in the heart valve, ScienceDaily reported July 13, establishing a new mechanistic oral-cardiovascular link that points toward periodontal treatment as a potentially modifiable risk factor for valve disease; separately, tau protein was found essential for organizing the brain's memory-storing place and grid cells, adding a functional biology constraint to Alzheimer's drug approaches targeting tau clearance; and scientists redesigned MRI hardware using metamaterials to produce clearer images of difficult-to-see body areas in less time on existing scanners — immediately translatable without new equipment investment.

The gum disease–heart valve finding is the one most likely to change clinical practice: calcific aortic valve stenosis has no approved drug treatment, treating gum disease is accessible and low-cost, and if clinical trials confirm the mechanistic link reduces valve calcification progression, it creates a preventive intervention deliverable through existing dental care infrastructure. The tau finding simultaneously reframes Alzheimer's drug development: tau is not just a pathological aggregate to clear but an essential organizer of memory-encoding cells — tau-targeting therapies must therefore preserve enough functional tau to maintain memory consolidation even while clearing pathological aggregates.
