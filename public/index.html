A case report in Frontiers in Neuroscience documents an 80-year-old woman who had lived with advanced Alzheimer's for 10 years — barely able to speak, unable to walk, incontinent — and who temporarily recovered speech, mobility, and bladder control following high-dose psilocybin administration. The finding is a single case and does not establish efficacy; it establishes a signal that warrants formal trial. Separately, researchers are applying evolutionary theory to cancer to preempt drug resistance before it forms, and a fully human antibody halted aggressive prostate cancer growth in preclinical research.

The Alzheimer's psilocybin case is significant precisely because the patient's disease was advanced — the prior assumption was that psilocybin's neural network reorganization mechanism required enough residual circuit integrity to work on. An 80-year-old with 10 years of Alzheimer's at the stage described does not fit that assumption. If even partial circuit reorganization is possible in late-stage Alzheimer's, it changes both the mechanism model and the potential treatment population. The cancer evolutionary treatment story reflects a strategic shift: rather than maximizing doses to kill cancer cells rapidly (which drives resistance), adaptive therapy maintains disease control by staying ahead of the evolutionary pressure that produces resistant clones.
