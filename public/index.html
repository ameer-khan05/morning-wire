The Lede · 07:00 ET

Trump kills the Iran peace talks. Google bets $40 billion on Anthropic. Viktor Orban quits parliament as Budapest resets.

President Trump abruptly canceled the planned trip of special envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner to Islamabad on Saturday evening, announcing on social media that "Nobody knows who is in charge, including them" in Iran and that the US "has all the cards." The cancellation follows Iranian FM Abbas Araghchi's departure from Pakistan — he is now expected in Muscat, Oman, for talks with Omani FM Badr Albusaidi, the only surviving indirect diplomatic contact between Washington and Tehran. Day 58 of the US-Iran conflict opens without a scheduled negotiating track as the naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz continues tightening.

In Europe, Viktor Orban announced Saturday he will not take his seat in Hungary's new parliament — ending a 36-year unbroken legislative career since 1990. Fidesz was routed in the April 12 elections by Péter Magyar's Tisza party, which won 138 of 199 seats in a supermajority. The new parliament convenes May 9; it will be the first in post-socialist Hungary without Orban as a sitting lawmaker. Orban said he will remain Fidesz party leader to "rebuild" — historically a base for sustained political influence outside parliament. In tech: Google committed up to $40 billion in Anthropic on Friday — $10 billion immediately at a $350 billion valuation with $30 billion contingent on milestones, plus 5 gigawatts of guaranteed cloud compute. The deal is the largest AI infrastructure commitment in venture history. Looking ahead: Alphabet, Meta, and Microsoft report Tuesday April 29; Bridgewater estimates $650 billion in combined Big Tech AI capex for 2026.

Bitcoin is holding at $77,510 Sunday morning as BTC spot ETFs logged $2.1 billion in net inflows over eight straight days through April 23, pushing cumulative flows since launch to $58 billion. Morgan Stanley's MSBT — the first major-bank-issued spot Bitcoin ETF — pulled in more than $100 million in its first week at an industry-low 0.14% fee. Markets are closed Sunday; Friday's record closes are shown. In science: a new MUSC study published today in Cell Reports finds that EPA — the omega-3 in fish oil — may increase CTE risk and weaken brain repair after repetitive mild injury rather than help it. Separately, KAIST researchers revealed how graphene oxide kills antibiotic-resistant superbugs by targeting lipids unique to bacterial membranes, while leaving human cells unharmed.

Science & Health EPA Fish oil · CTE risk · MUSC · Cell Reports

Fish oil's EPA omega-3 may raise CTE risk after brain injury — MUSC study upends supplement advice.

A Medical University of South Carolina study published today in Cell Reports finds that EPA — the omega-3 fatty acid in fish oil — may increase CTE risk after repetitive mild brain injury rather than aid repair, by weakening blood vessel stability and contributing to perivascular tau buildup. DHA, fish oil's other main omega-3, did not show these effects. The finding complicates public health guidance for millions of supplement users and athletes. Separately, KAIST researchers revealed how graphene oxide kills antibiotic-resistant superbugs by targeting a lipid unique to bacterial membranes while leaving human cells intact — opening a new front in the war on drug-resistant bacteria.
