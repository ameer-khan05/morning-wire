The moral argument carries institutional weight that domestic political opposition lacks, and its delivery on Independence Day ensures it is the counterpoint that defines the celebration's historical record.

ETH Zurich PhotoPROTAC: Light Wakes Dormant Cancer Cells · Destroys Glucocorticoid Receptors · Lung/Breast/Prostate · Weizmann "Mitch" Protein: Disabling Boosts Fat Burning, Blocks Fat Cell Formation Light ETH Zurich: photoPROTAC system · target: glucocorticoid receptors in dormant cancer cells · stress hormones cause cancer cells to enter low-division sleep state → treatment-resistant · light switch (wavelength-specific): activates near tumor → degrades receptor → cell wakes up → becomes drug-vulnerable · effective vs lung cancer in lab · modular: adaptable for estrogen receptor (breast) + androgen receptor (prostate) · published PNAS May 21, 2026 · Weizmann Institute: MTCH2 "Mitch" protein · disabling in human cells: ↑ fat burning + ↑ energy use + ↓ fat cell formation · Mitch-lacking mice: leaner + more athletic + obesity-resistant · muscle mass preserved (vs GLP-1 trade-off) · EMBO Journal · inhibitor drug in development

ETH Zurich scientists published a photoPROTAC system — a light-controlled molecular degrader — that wakes cancer cells from their treatment-resistant dormant state by destroying the glucocorticoid receptors through which stress hormones trigger cellular sleep; activating the switch near the tumor forces cells back into active division where chemotherapy and targeted drugs can reach them, with lung cancer effectiveness demonstrated in the lab and the modular design applicable to breast cancer (estrogen receptors) and advanced prostate cancer (androgen receptors), published in PNAS in May 2026. Separately, researchers at the Weizmann Institute of Science identified MTCH2 — nicknamed "Mitch" — as a key fat-storage regulator: disabling it in human cells increased fat burning, raised energy expenditure, and blocked new fat cell formation, while mice lacking the protein were leaner, more athletic, and obesity-resistant with muscle mass preserved.

The Mitch finding directly addresses the trade-off that has defined current obesity pharmacology. GLP-1 receptor agonists produce weight loss but also reduce muscle mass — a clinically significant cost for patients who need metabolic improvement without muscle atrophy. The Weizmann mice lacking Mitch lost fat and preserved muscle simultaneously, suggesting the protein operates through a mitochondrial energy-efficiency mechanism rather than appetite suppression. The Weizmann lab is developing a small-molecule Mitch inhibitor in collaboration with Bina, the institute's translational research unit. Both the ETH Zurich cancer switch and the Mitch obesity finding represent basic science identifying precise molecular targets where existing treatments have either no solution (treatment-resistant cancer dormancy) or a trade-off (muscle loss in obesity treatment) that a specific intervention could resolve.
