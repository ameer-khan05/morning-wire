The Lede · 07:00 ET

The S&P 500, Nasdaq, and Dow all closed at all-time records on Wednesday as reports that the United States and Iran are close to a one-page war-ending memo crashed WTI crude 7% to $95.19 — the largest single-day oil decline of the 69-day conflict — and AMD's 19% post-earnings surge powered the technology sector.

The rally was driven by two converging forces. First, Axios reported Wednesday that the White House and Iran are close to a one-page, 14-point memorandum of understanding to end the war: Iran would commit to a moratorium on nuclear enrichment, the US would lift sanctions and release frozen Iranian funds, and both sides would reopen the Strait of Hormuz to commercial shipping. Trump paused the "Project Freedom" escort mission to create space for the deal, called the 44 hours of talks "very good," and told Fox News that Iran had one week to respond — then told reporters in the Oval Office he had no specific deadline. He simultaneously threatened to bomb Iran "at a much higher level" if no agreement is reached. Iran's foreign ministry said the US proposal was under review and that Tehran would convey its answer to Pakistani mediators. Both sides called it the closest they have been to a deal since the conflict began February 28. Second, AMD reported Q1 2026 revenue of $10.3 billion — a 38% year-over-year increase — driven by data center AI chip demand; shares surged 19%. Supermicro posted a fiscal Q3 EPS beat and gross margin expansion; shares rose 18%. The Nasdaq, heavily weighted toward both names, closed up 2.02% at 25,838.94, its highest close ever.

Anthropic surpassed OpenAI in annual recurring revenue this week — reaching $30 billion against OpenAI's roughly $24 billion — driven by enterprise AI adoption that now counts more than 1,000 businesses spending over $1 million per year on Claude. The milestone, achieved in roughly 15 months from $1 billion ARR in January 2025, was built on an 80% enterprise revenue mix. Google DeepMind UK employees voted this week to form a union in response to the company's classified Pentagon AI agreements, after more than 600 DeepMind researchers signed a petition urging CEO Sundar Pichai to refuse the contracts — the petition was still being signed when the deal was finalized. Bitcoin at $81,540 Thursday morning is testing the 200-day simple moving average at $82,228 — the level it has not closed above since October 2025's all-time high — after being rejected at $82,500 on Wednesday. A clean close above the 200-day SMA would be Bitcoin's first technical trend-reversal signal in seven months.

A 10-year randomized clinical trial with a sham surgery control — the FIDELITY study — confirmed this week that arthroscopic partial meniscectomy, one of the world's most commonly performed orthopedic procedures, provides no benefit over placebo surgery and may worsen long-term knee outcomes: patients who had the real operation showed greater osteoarthritis progression and higher re-operation rates over the decade than those who received sham surgery. Separately, Kindai University researchers published evidence that arginine, an inexpensive and widely available amino acid, significantly reduces amyloid plaque buildup in Alzheimer's animal models — offering a potential low-cost therapeutic pathway ahead of human trials. The ADP report released Wednesday showed private payrolls added 109,000 jobs in April, above expectations; the BLS April nonfarm payrolls report — the first employment data potentially capturing oil-economy effects — releases Friday at 8:30 a.m. ET with consensus estimates ranging widely from 55,000 to 165,000.

Meniscus Surgery · 10-Year Trial · No Benefit Sham = Same FIDELITY 10-yr trial · arthroscopic meniscectomy vs sham · no benefit · osteoarthritis worse · re-op rates higher · arginine reduces Alzheimer's amyloid in animals

A 10-year randomized trial confirms that arthroscopic meniscus surgery — one of the most common orthopedic procedures globally — provides no benefit over sham surgery and may worsen long-term knee outcomes. Separately, arginine, a cheap amino acid, significantly reduces amyloid plaques in Alzheimer's animal models.

The FIDELITY trial's 10-year data, reported this week, close the clinical debate that began with the trial's 2-year results: arthroscopic partial meniscectomy for degenerative meniscal tears is not only non-superior to sham surgery on pain and function outcomes — it is associated with faster osteoarthritis progression and higher rates of additional knee surgery over the decade. The sham control was rigorous: incisions, anesthesia, and arthroscope insertion without tissue removal. More than 90% of participants completed the 10-year follow-up. The finding affects one of the most frequently performed surgeries in the world — approximately 700,000 arthroscopic meniscus procedures annually in the US alone. In Alzheimer's research, Kindai University scientists found that oral arginine supplementation significantly reduced amyloid-beta plaque formation, lowered neuroinflammation markers, and improved behavioral outcomes in mouse models — establishing a low-cost, safe preclinical case for human trials in a disease where billion-dollar antibody therapies have produced only modest benefit.
