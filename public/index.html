The Lede · 07:00 ET

April CPI arrives at 3.8% year-over-year — the hottest print since May 2023, beating the 3.7% consensus — confirming that the Iran conflict's oil price has embedded in consumer prices faster than the Fed's models predicted. WTI crude closed above $100 for the first time on Monday at $101.60. The Fed cannot cut rates in this environment. And the man who most needs it to can is flying to Beijing.

The April consumer price index, released Tuesday morning at 8:30 AM ET, printed 3.8% year-over-year and 0.6% month-over-month — each figure above the 3.7% YoY and 0.5% MoM consensus. The energy component tells the story: WTI averaged above $95 for the entire April survey month, and gasoline, heating oil, and utility costs flowed directly into the CPI basket at a rate that offset the deflationary forces in goods and shelter that had been containing the headline print. Core CPI — excluding food and energy — came in at 3.2%, also above the prior month's 3.0%, suggesting the oil price is beginning to flow through to upstream costs in a way that moves the inflation picture from "transitory supply shock" toward "broadening second-round effects." Fed Chair Warsh's rate-cut dilemma — simultaneously strong jobs data (economy holding) and re-accelerating inflation (oil-driven) — is now sharper than at any point in the conflict period. A rate cut before CPI convincingly returns to 3.0% is politically and institutionally untenable; a rate hike into a conflict-period economy that just added 115,000 jobs in April is equally untenable. The Fed is frozen, and the Iran deal is the only event that can unfreeze it.

Iran is getting further from a deal, not closer. Day 74 opened with Trump escalating his public characterization of Iran's Saturday counterproposal from "TOTALLY UNACCEPTABLE" — Sunday's language — to "garbage" on Tuesday, while CNN reported that the president is "more seriously thinking of restarting combat operations" after the fourth-round Oman talks produced no framework-survival signal. The Oman talks, which convened high-level-only on Monday, ended without a joint statement or any indication that the one-page framework survived Iran's maximalist counter-demands. Trump simultaneously departed for Beijing Tuesday for the May 14-15 summit with Xi Jinping — the first US presidential visit to China since 2017. The summit's published agenda (Board of Trade framework, AI export controls, semiconductor supply chain, Taiwan) is secondary to its unpublicized central item: whether Trump can persuade Xi to reduce or eliminate China's Iranian oil purchases, which Treasury estimates contributed directly to Tehran's confidence in presenting Saturday's maximalist counterproposal. The delegation includes CEOs from Meta, Tesla, Goldman Sachs, and other major firms — signaling a trade and investment emphasis — but the Iran dimension is what financial markets are watching.

Lebanon's ceasefire is deteriorating as its May 14 expiry approaches. Al Jazeera reports that 380 people have been killed since the ceasefire began April 16 — despite the truce — as both the IDF and Hezbollah have continued operations. The US State Department has scheduled intensive Israel-Lebanon negotiations for May 14-15, the same window as the Trump-Xi summit opening and the Lebanon ceasefire expiry date. Diplomatic bandwidth is stretched to its limit: the officials responsible for Lebanon renewal are also managing the collapsed Oman framework and summit preparation. In a separate public health development that adds to the week's pressure, an active hantavirus outbreak on the cruise ship MV Hondius has reached 11 confirmed cases and 2 deaths. The Andes hantavirus strain identified has no approved vaccine and no specific antiviral treatment; 18 passengers have been returned to the US for medical monitoring. The outbreak is an active CDC investigation.

China's AI-supercharged export economy is generating $500 million per hour in overseas sales, according to Bloomberg — with AI-related products (semiconductors, computers, AI hardware) accounting for roughly half of China's total April export growth. This figure arrives at the Trump-Xi summit as both a trade negotiation variable and a measurement of how quickly the AI infrastructure race is reshaping global commerce. In US markets, the semiconductor sector is rotating away from pure GPU exposure toward memory chips and legacy chipmakers: the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index surged 60% in six weeks, with Micron up 38% in a single week and Intel up 500% in 12 months following Apple's reported partnership for US-manufactured processors. The memory chip market is projected to grow from $216 billion in 2025 to $633 billion in 2026, driven by AI training and inference infrastructure demand that has made DRAM and HBM memory the new bottleneck in AI system scaling.

An active hantavirus outbreak on the cruise ship MV Hondius has reached 11 confirmed cases and 2 deaths — involving the Andes virus, for which there is no approved vaccine and no specific antiviral treatment. Eighteen passengers have been returned to the US for medical monitoring. Also: the FDA approved the first PROTAC cancer therapy (Veppanu) for estrogen receptor-positive breast cancer — the first new oncology drug class in a decade.

The MV Hondius hantavirus outbreak is the most clinically urgent public health story this week: Andes hantavirus is the only hantavirus species known to transmit person-to-person — unlike Sin Nombre virus (the primary US hantavirus species, which transmits only from rodents to humans), Andes can spread between humans, raising the transmission risk profile for the 18 passengers now being monitored in US medical facilities. The absence of an approved vaccine or specific treatment means clinical management is entirely supportive — oxygen, mechanical ventilation, hemodynamic support — with outcomes depending on how early the disease is caught and how well-resourced the healthcare setting is. The PROTAC approval is the week's landmark science story: Veppanu (vepdegestrant), approved May 1 by the FDA for ERα-positive breast cancer with ESR1 mutations, is the first clinical application of proteolysis targeting chimera technology — a fundamentally new drug mechanism that destroys disease proteins rather than merely inhibiting them, opening a therapeutic approach to targets that conventional inhibitor drugs cannot reach.
