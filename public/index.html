The Lede · 07:00 ET

US Treasury yields hit 52-week highs Tuesday — the 10-year at 4.657% and the 30-year briefly at 5.19%, its highest level in 18 years — driving the S&P 500 to a third consecutive losing session at 7,353.61. Iran peace talks in Islamabad collapsed after 21 hours; Vice President Vance called the US position its "final and best offer" and President Trump now says the ceasefire is "on life support." Google I/O 2026 launched Gemini 3.5 Flash — the fastest and cheapest frontier-quality AI model yet — while Andrej Karpathy announced he is joining Anthropic's pre-training team.

The bond market is sending a warning that equity investors cannot ignore indefinitely. Tuesday's surge — 10-year UST yield to 4.657% (a 52-week high), 20-year to 5.189%, and the 30-year briefly above 5.19% for the first time in nearly 19 years — reflects the compounding of three forces: the Iran conflict's oil price ($108.59 WTI) continuing to run above $100 and sustaining CPI above target; fiscal concerns about the path of the US deficit as the "big beautiful bill" tax package moves through Congress; and the structural repricing of the Treasury market's duration risk as investors demand higher compensation to hold long-dated debt in an environment where the Fed is demonstrably frozen. The S&P 500's third consecutive losing session — closing at 7,353.61, down 0.67% — reflects investors' recognition that rising risk-free yields create a mechanical discount on equity valuations, particularly for the high-multiple AI technology names that have driven the market's gains since late 2025. Nvidia reports earnings today after the market close, with analysts expecting $78 billion in revenue — approximately 75% year-over-year growth; the result will set the near-term tone for the Nasdaq's AI-tech leadership.

The Iran ceasefire declared April 8 is now what Trump himself describes as being "on life support" or "massive life support," language that reflects the practical outcome of Tuesday's Pakistan-mediated talks: 21 hours of negotiations in Islamabad between US and Iranian delegations concluded without agreement, with Vice President Vance saying the US team left with an "understanding that is our final and best offer." Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi has characterized Tehran's position as rooted in fundamental distrust: "We cannot trust the Americans at all," he said, identifying the absence of trust as "the main obstacle to any diplomatic effort." The core sticking point is the nuclear enrichment moratorium: the US is demanding a 20-year pause, Iran proposed 5 years, and the gap between these positions has not narrowed in the most recent round of talks despite Pakistan's sustained mediating role. Lebanon's ceasefire was extended 45 days on May 15 — a tactical success — but at least 657 people have been killed since the truce began, and the fourth round of Israel-Lebanon political talks is not scheduled until June 2-3.

The Trump-Xi summit (May 14-15 in Beijing) produced concrete trade deliverables — China agreed to buy at least $17 billion in US agricultural goods annually through 2028, order 200 Boeing jets, and establish a "Board of Trade" and "Board of Investment" as standing bilateral economic management institutions — but did not produce progress on the Iran oil purchases that would most directly affect WTI, CPI, and the Fed's rate path. Trump characterized the summit as producing "fantastic trade deals" while confirming that tariffs were not discussed. The summit's Iran dimension produced no visible result, meaning the oil-inflation-yield chain that has been the dominant macro signal since late April remains intact and is now producing the yield levels that are driving Tuesday's equity selloff. Nvidia earnings after today's close are the week's defining equity event: at $78 billion expected revenue, a beat sustains the AI investment thesis that has kept valuations elevated; a miss or weak guidance would compound the yield-driven headwinds significantly.

A major study presented at the European Congress on Obesity (ECO 2026) confirms that significant weight loss achieved on GLP-1 medications — Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro, and Saxenda — sharply reduces the risk of sleep apnea and chronic kidney disease. People who gained weight instead had a 22% higher risk of obstructive sleep apnea and 14% higher risk of chronic kidney disease compared to those who achieved substantial weight loss. The finding is the largest observational validation of GLP-1 secondary health benefits to date.

The ECO 2026 study is significant because it quantifies the magnitude-of-weight-loss dose-response relationship for GLP-1 secondary benefits in a population large enough to generate statistically meaningful risk reduction estimates across multiple disease endpoints. Earlier evidence had established that GLP-1 agonists reduce cardiovascular events and, in specific trials, improve sleep apnea severity. The new study extends this to a broader comparison: not merely GLP-1 users versus non-users, but high-responders (significant weight loss) versus non-responders (weight gain or minimal loss) within the GLP-1 user population. This framing is clinically important because it establishes that the health benefits accrue specifically from the weight loss achieved, not merely from the drug's non-weight-loss mechanisms — which means the magnitude of weight loss is the therapeutic variable clinicians should optimize. A separate discovery published this week: scientists identified tristetraprolin (TTP), an RNA-binding protein that controls chronic inflammation, as a key factor in healthy aging — elderly mice with stabilized TTP levels were stronger, more energetic, and had healthier bones than untreated controls.
