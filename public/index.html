A major scientific review concluded that nicotine e-cigarettes are likely to cause lung and oral cancers — the strongest collective link yet established between vaping and malignancy, based on convergent biomarker, animal, and laboratory evidence. The finding requires public health guidance to revise the "reduced harm" framing that has treated the cancer question as unresolved while recommending e-cigarettes as a smoking-cessation tool; approximately 35 million Americans vape regularly. Researchers separately discovered that developing neurons physically break their own DNA to navigate through tight tissue spaces in the fetal brain — a normal developmental mechanism previously categorized as external damage — with implications for understanding neurodevelopmental disorders. A Northern Arizona University study found that the Climate TRACE emissions database undercounts vehicle CO₂ in US cities by an average of 70%, raising questions about urban climate policy accuracy.

The weekend's science converges on a common theme: prior confident models that turn out to have been missing something significant. Vaping was confidently "reduced harm"; the cancer link was always uncertain. Neuronal DNA breaks were confidently damage; they turn out to be development. Climate TRACE data was confidently the authoritative source; its vehicle emissions numbers are 70% below ground truth. The scientific process working as designed — better data revises confident prior claims — but the revision at scale carries real consequences: public health for vapers, therapeutic targets for neurological conditions, and climate policy baselines for cities.
