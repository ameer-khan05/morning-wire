Apple to Open Siri to Rival AI Assistants in iOS 27

Apple is preparing a fundamental shift in its approach to artificial intelligence integration, planning to open Siri to competing AI services in iOS 27. The move represents a major strategic pivot that signals Apple's willingness to let third-party AI assistants—including rivals to ChatGPT—operate alongside its native Siri assistant. This represents one of the most significant changes to Apple's closed ecosystem in recent memory.

The overhaul is part of a broader iOS 27 update that underscores Apple's recognition that no single AI provider can dominate every use case. Rather than forcing users into a single AI experience, Apple will allow developers to integrate competing AI assistants directly into iOS, giving users genuine choice in their AI interactions. Industry observers view this as a tacit acknowledgment that Apple's proprietary AI capabilities, while strong, benefit from complementary options that specialize in different domains.

This opening could reshape the competitive landscape in AI assistants, potentially leveling the playing field between OpenAI, Google, Meta, and other contenders while simultaneously strengthening iOS's appeal to developers and users seeking customized AI workflows.

Source: Bloomberg

Meta & YouTube Found Negligent in Landmark Social Media Addiction Trial

Juries in California and New Mexico have found Meta and YouTube liable for their roles in fostering social media addiction among minors. A Los Angeles jury awarded $6 million in damages in a landmark verdict that marks the first major courtroom victory against the tech giants over addiction claims. The verdicts establish critical legal precedent that platforms may bear responsibility for designing features that knowingly exploit psychological vulnerabilities in younger users.

The cases centered on allegations that Meta and YouTube deliberately used manipulative design techniques—infinite scroll, algorithmic feeds, and notification systems—to maximize user engagement at the expense of child welfare. The findings suggest that future litigation against these platforms may succeed based on similar arguments, potentially opening the door to broader regulatory action.
