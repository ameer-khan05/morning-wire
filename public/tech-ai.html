Altman explicitly invoked the IAEA and aviation safety bodies as governance models — international structures where a US-led institution sets the rules, controls access to the technology for compliant participants, and operates as the arbiter of safety and proliferation risk. He framed the proposal as a check on the commercial pressures that can lead labs toward unsafe competitive racing — a framing that acknowledges, implicitly, that the current competitive dynamics in AI are producing that pressure.

The governance proposal's timing is inseparable from OpenAI's competitive position. Similarweb data shows monthly visits to ChatGPT fell below a majority of the generative AI market for the first time in May — meaning more than half of the traffic to major generative AI platforms is now going to competitors, a structural shift from the near-monopoly that ChatGPT held in 2023. Anthropic's $30 billion run-rate, the Google-Broadcom TPU deal, and the Fable 5 usage momentum are the data points that define what "losing the majority" looks like in practice: Anthropic has grown 3.3x in six months, Google's own Gemini products are competing at the consumer and enterprise levels, and the enterprise market that OpenAI dominated in 2024 is now genuinely contested. A US-led governance forum that sets standards for frontier models is also a mechanism that concentrates institutional power in the labs that already have the political relationships to shape what those standards say — which favors incumbents even as they lose market share.

OpenAI CEO Sam Altman published a Financial Times op-ed on July 2 proposing a "US-led international forum" to govern artificial intelligence — a body modeled on the IAEA, aviation safety standards, and global financial regulation that would establish accepted AI standards, provide impartial analysis of capabilities and risks, and make frontier AI models available to nations and companies that participate and follow the rules. Altman framed the forum explicitly as a check on commercial competitive pressures that he acknowledged can lead labs toward unsafe racing behavior — a framing that positions OpenAI as a governance-first actor at the moment when its competitive position in the generative AI market is weakening materially: ChatGPT's share of generative AI traffic fell below a majority for the first time in May, with more than half of platform traffic now going to competitors including Google and Anthropic.

The IAEA analogy is instructive for its competitive as well as its governance implications. The IAEA was constructed after World War II to channel nuclear technology through a US-led international body that conditioned access on compliance with US-defined nonproliferation standards — a structure that simultaneously promoted peaceful nuclear use and ensured that the countries with the most advanced nuclear technology (led by the United States) set the rules governing who could access it. A US-led AI governance forum with equivalent logic would establish US-headquartered frontier AI labs as the technology providers whose models are licensed to compliant participants under US-defined standards. The countries and companies that benefit most from such a framework are those already operating inside the US regulatory perimeter and with the political relationships to shape what the standards say. Whether the proposal is genuine architectural vision or competitive positioning — or both — it arrives at a moment when the governance and competition debates in AI are converging on the same question: who decides what frontier AI is, who gets access to it, and under what conditions.

Anthropic's run-rate revenue reached $30 billion by mid-2026 — up from approximately $9 billion at the end of 2025, a 3.3x increase in roughly six months driven by accelerating demand from Claude enterprise and API customers — while the company has secured 3.5 gigawatts of TPU compute capacity from Google and Broadcom in a deal that represents the largest infrastructure commitment in Anthropic's history, expected to come online starting in 2027. One gigawatt of AI compute is roughly equivalent to the entire Anthropic compute fleet at the start of 2026; the 3.5-gigawatt deal represents a 4.5x expansion of the company's compute base within an 18-month window, pre-building the infrastructure for a version of Anthropic that is substantially larger than the current one.

The $30 billion run-rate is the commercial justification for the infrastructure bet. The pattern in AI infrastructure economics is that the compute required to train and serve frontier models scales faster than revenue unless the revenue trajectory is steep enough to pre-fund the next generation of compute before the current generation saturates. Anthropic's 3.3x revenue growth in six months is steep enough that the 3.5-gigawatt bet is a plausible extrapolation of current demand rather than a speculative overinvestment — though the caveat is that the enterprise AI cost-management wave, which has already produced Tesla's $200 weekly cap and Uber's $1,500 monthly limit, represents the first sign of demand compression at the margin. The July 7 Fable 5 credit transition puts Anthropic's premium model at $10/$50 per million input/output tokens, which will be the first direct test of whether Fable 5 usage holds at commercial pricing now that the restoration period's discounted access ends.

Anthropic's Fable 5 and Mythos 5 models transition to credit-based access for all plan tiers starting July 7 — ending the restoration period during which Pro, Max, Team, and select Enterprise subscribers could use Fable 5 at up to 50% of their weekly usage limit at no additional cost, following the model's return from a 19-day US export-control suspension on July 1. After July 7, usage above the plan's included limit requires usage credits priced at $10 per million input tokens and $50 per million output tokens, with the existing 90% prompt-caching discount preserved. The transition restores the commercial model that was suspended alongside the models themselves when the Commerce Department imposed export restrictions on June 12 following an Amazon-identified jailbreak.

The credit transition arrives on July 7 — one day after Tesla's $200-per-week AI spending cap takes effect on July 6 and the same week that enterprise cost management is the dominant AI industry operational theme. Tesla's cap, which excludes Elon Musk's Grok products but applies to Anthropic's Claude models, means that Tesla employees' access to Fable 5 is doubly constrained starting this week: by the July 7 credit transition from Anthropic's side and by the $200 weekly cap from Tesla's internal policy side. For Anthropic's enterprise revenue, the practical question is whether the large enterprise customers whose usage drives the $30 billion run-rate have negotiated access agreements that buffer them from the per-token pricing, or whether the credit transition creates the same cost-management friction that is prompting corporate spending caps across the sector. The Fable 5 restoration period ending is the first major test of whether Anthropic's premium model usage holds at commercial pricing levels.

Quick hits

  • The IAEA analogy in Altman's proposal has a precise competitive implication that goes beyond the governance rhetoric. The IAEA model concentrates power in the standards-setting body, which operates under US leadership. Labs whose models are already the reference standards — whose benchmarks, safety frameworks, and capability thresholds define what "frontier" means — benefit disproportionately from governance architecture that treats those reference points as the baseline. If Altman is proposing a forum that sets rules around frontier models defined by the models that currently exist, then OpenAI's current position — still the largest and most widely deployed, even if losing traffic share — gives it the most to protect and the most to contribute to the standard-setting process. The governance proposal is not just about AI safety; it is about who defines the terms under which the AI market operates.
  • Anthropic's trajectory from $9 billion to $30 billion in run-rate revenue in roughly six months implies an annualized growth rate that, if sustained, would bring the company to over $60 billion in run-rate by early 2027 — the timeline when the 3.5-gigawatt compute deal is expected to come online. The compute bet is sized for that revenue trajectory, not the current one. The risk in the bet is that the enterprise AI cost-management wave — Tesla's cap, Uber's limit, Meta and Amazon and Walmart all constraining usage — represents the beginning of a deceleration in the demand growth that the trajectory assumes. The Fable 5 credit transition on July 7 is the first commercial data point on whether Anthropic's premium usage holds when the discounted access ends and per-token pricing resumes at full rates.
