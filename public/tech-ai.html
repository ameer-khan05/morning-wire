Alphabet smashes $109.9B with Google Cloud up 63% — the OpenAI miss is answered. Meta and Microsoft slip on capex and user concerns.

Wednesday's Big Tech earnings night delivered the clearest verdict on the AI cycle yet: Alphabet is its biggest immediate beneficiary, and the demand OpenAI failed to retain has migrated to Google's infrastructure. Google Cloud grew 63% year-over-year to $20.0 billion — against a $460 billion contracted backlog — and Alphabet's total revenue hit $109.9 billion on a $107.1 billion estimate, with net income up 81%. GOOGL surged 8.4% in after-hours. Amazon's AWS hit $37.59 billion (beat: $36.64 billion estimate), up 28.5% from $29.27 billion a year ago, pushing AMZN up 2%. The split results: Meta fell 9% after hours on user growth disappointment and an explicit acknowledgment that the Iran war's "internet disruptions" and Russia's WhatsApp restriction are material headwinds; Microsoft fell 2% despite Azure accelerating to 40% — ahead of its 37–38% guidance — as investors focused on full-year capex raised to $190 billion and CEO Nadella's characterization that Microsoft will remain "capacity constrained through 2026."

The Anchor

Alphabet's Google Cloud surges 63% to $20B — the OpenAI miss has an answer: the demand moved to Google.

The operating question that last Tuesday's OpenAI miss report left unresolved was whether AI application-layer demand had softened across the industry or merely at OpenAI specifically. Alphabet's Q1 2026 results answer it definitively. Google Cloud generated $20.0 billion in revenue — up 63% year-over-year — against a committed backlog of $460 billion, the largest cloud infrastructure backlog Alphabet has ever disclosed. Alphabet's total consolidated revenue reached $109.9 billion, beating the Wall Street consensus of $107.1 billion, representing 22% year-over-year growth and the company's 11th consecutive quarter of double-digit revenue expansion. Net income reached $62.57 billion — up 81% from a year ago — with EPS coming in at $5.11 against a $2.62 consensus estimate, a beat of more than 95% on the bottom line. Alphabet also raised its 2026 capex guidance to $180–$190 billion, reflecting the scale of contracted Cloud demand requiring infrastructure support. GOOGL surged 8.4% in after-hours trading, recovering all of Tuesday's chip-stock-driven decline and then some.

The specific implication of Google Cloud's 63% growth cannot be overstated as a response to the OpenAI miss. OpenAI CFO Sarah Friar had cited Google Gemini's enterprise market share gains as a partial cause of ChatGPT's revenue shortfall. The mechanism is now visible in Alphabet's numbers: the enterprises that chose Gemini over ChatGPT are running their inference workloads on Google's TPU infrastructure, contributing directly to Cloud revenue. The $460 billion Cloud backlog — representing committed, contracted future revenue — is the most important forward-looking metric: it means the 63% growth is not a one-quarter event but a multi-year contracted revenue stream that validates Alphabet's $180–$190 billion 2026 capex guidance. For the broader AI infrastructure investment thesis, the Alphabet result is the strongest single-quarter rebuttal to the OpenAI miss: demand did not vanish, it redistributed to competitors with superior enterprise infrastructure, and Alphabet was the primary beneficiary.

Alphabet's $109.9B Q1 — Google Cloud +63%, EPS nearly doubles estimate, GOOGL surges 8.4%. The AI-demand thesis is intact.

Alphabet's Q1 2026 results represent the strongest single-quarter performance the company has ever delivered by multiple metrics. Revenue of $109.9 billion — against a $107.1 billion estimate — was the company's first-ever $100 billion+ quarterly print and the largest quarterly revenue ever recorded by any advertising-dependent company. Google Cloud's 63% growth to $20.0 billion is almost twice the 50% consensus entering the night, and the $460 billion committed backlog suggests it will compound at elevated rates for multiple quarters. The EPS beat — $5.11 versus a $2.62 estimate — reflects two compounding effects: Cloud's high-margin AI infrastructure revenue, and the operating leverage in Alphabet's cost structure as revenue growth outpaces headcount. Alphabet also disclosed that Google Cloud has a backlog up significantly year-over-year, representing enterprise AI deals signed and committed. The bottom-line implication for the AI infrastructure debate: enterprises are not pulling back on AI spending; they are locking it into multi-year contracted commitments at Alphabet's Cloud, which is the most durable form of demand signal the industry can produce. The OpenAI miss report — which sent chip stocks down 3-4% last Tuesday — is now retroactively readable as an OpenAI-specific execution story, not an industry-wide demand signal. Alphabet's quarter is the definitive rebuttal.

Meta · User Miss · Iran Impact ▼ 9% AH EPS $10.44 · net income $26.8B · WhatsApp Russia restricted · Iran disruptions · capex $125-145B

Meta beats on profit but falls 9% after hours — Iran war disrupts WhatsApp, Russia restricts access, and capex guidance rises again.

Meta's Q1 2026 results present a company generating record profits while its user base is being structurally constrained by geopolitics in ways that no amount of AI investment can resolve. Net income climbed to $26.8 billion ($10.44 per share) from $16.6 billion ($6.43 per share) a year ago — a 61% year-over-year earnings gain. Yet the stock fell 9% in after-hours on two disclosures that investors read as structural, not transient. First, user growth: Meta cited "internet disruptions in Iran" as a material headwind — a consequence of the ongoing US-Iran conflict that has degraded internet infrastructure in the region, directly reducing WhatsApp's daily active user count in one of its largest markets. Russia's restriction on WhatsApp access was cited separately, compounding the loss of two large user geographies simultaneously. Second, capex guidance: Meta raised its 2026 capex range to $125–$145 billion (from the prior $115–$135 billion), adding $10 billion to both ends of the range without a proportional increase in near-term revenue guidance. The market's interpretation: Meta is spending more, growing users more slowly, and the Iran-war exposure is not a one-quarter event. The nine-quarter earnings beat streak is intact on a profit basis, but the user-growth concern will likely shadow the stock through the next Iran-related catalyst — whether resolution, escalation, or the Lebanon ceasefire expiry on May 14.

Microsoft · Azure 40% · Capex $77.7B Revenue +18% · Azure +40% beat · cloud backlog $392B · capex $190B · capacity constrained · ▼ 2% AH

Microsoft's Azure accelerates to 40% — but $190B capex and "capacity constrained through 2026" weigh on the stock.

Microsoft's Q3 FY2026 results answered one of the FOMC-week's most consequential tech questions: Azure did re-accelerate — growing 40% in constant currency, ahead of the 37–38% guidance that had been the make-or-break metric entering the night. Cloud revenue reached $49.1 billion, up 26%, with a contracted backlog of $392 billion (up 51% year-over-year). Total revenue of $77.7 billion represents 18% year-over-year growth. Yet MSFT fell 2% in after-hours, and the reason is forward-looking rather than backward: CEO Satya Nadella's statement that Microsoft will remain "capacity constrained through 2026" implies that Azure's 40% growth is being limited by infrastructure supply, not demand, and that the full-year 2026 capex of $190 billion represents a race to build the infrastructure needed to serve demand that already exists but cannot be fulfilled on current capacity. For investors, this is simultaneously a bullish demand signal (so much enterprise demand exists that Microsoft cannot fulfill it) and a bearish near-term margin signal (capital expenditure is running ahead of revenue recognition). Q4 capex is projected at $40 billion, sustaining the investment pace through the year-end. The Azure 40% number vindicated the Azure re-acceleration thesis; the capacity-constraint language will be the next data point to track.

Amazon · AWS · Beat $37.59B AWS +28.5% YoY · beat $36.64B estimate · AI agent adoption · ▲ 2% AH

Amazon's AWS hits $37.59B — the cleanest AI infrastructure beat of the night, and the most direct evidence demand is real.

Amazon's Q1 2026 AWS result — $37.59 billion in revenue against a $36.64 billion estimate, up from $29.27 billion a year ago — is the cleanest single-quarter AI infrastructure demand signal in the earnings night. AWS grew 28.5% year-over-year, accelerating from prior-quarter growth and beating analyst consensus. The cleanliness of the result matters: unlike Alphabet's Cloud, which is inseparable from Gemini's consumer AI strategy, and unlike Microsoft's Azure, which is entangled with OpenAI's exclusivity shifts, AWS is purely enterprise infrastructure — companies running their own AI models on Amazon's compute, without the ChatGPT subscription dynamics that complicate application-layer demand analysis. AWS's beat is therefore the most isolated evidence that enterprise AI compute spending is robust and independent of OpenAI's consumer-facing product trajectory. Amazon shares rose 2% in after-hours. The company's $200 billion in projected FY2026 capex — directed primarily at AWS AI infrastructure and Bedrock's AI agent platform — positions AWS as the third leg of the Big Tech cloud AI capex cycle alongside Alphabet ($180–190 billion) and Microsoft ($190 billion).

Fed holds at 3.5%–3.75% — Powell announces he stays on the Board despite Warsh taking the Chair, citing Trump's legal attacks.

The Federal Reserve's April 29 meeting held rates at 3.5%–3.75% as universally expected, but the meeting's defining development was Powell's announcement at his press conference that he will remain on the Federal Reserve Board of Governors — not depart the institution entirely — after Kevin Warsh takes over the chairmanship. Powell cited Trump's "legal attacks" as having left him "no choice" but to stay. The FOMC dissent vote split 8-4 — the most divided since October 1992 — with Governor Stephen Miran preferring a 25-basis-point cut, and Governors Hammack, Kashkari, and Logan opposing an easing bias in the statement. For tech investors, the Powell-stays-on-the-Board development has a specific implication: a board with Powell as a member will be a more credible internal check on any pressure Warsh receives to cut rates prematurely, which means the higher-for-longer regime — the single largest structural headwind for AI capex's debt-financed components — is unlikely to reverse before Q4. Quick hits

  • The four companies that reported Wednesday night — Alphabet, Meta, Microsoft, Amazon — together committed approximately $655–$710 billion in AI infrastructure spending for 2026, when you add the new capex guidance from each. That figure is now larger than the entire GDP of Sweden. The question of whether this spending is justified by demand was partially answered Wednesday: Alphabet and Amazon say yes, definitively; Meta and Microsoft say yes, but later.
  • Google Cloud's $460 billion backlog is the single most important number in the AI investment landscape this week. It is larger than Alphabet's entire current annualized revenue run-rate ($109.9 billion quarterly × 4 = $439.6 billion annualized), meaning Alphabet has committed Cloud revenue exceeding one full year of total company revenue. That level of backlog can only exist if enterprises are making long-term AI infrastructure bets — not one-quarter experiments — which is the strongest possible signal that AI demand is structural.
  • Meta's Iran-war user exposure is a category of risk that no amount of AI investment can hedge. The conflict's "internet disruptions in Iran" are a consequence of the same Hormuz-driven geopolitical regime that is suppressing crypto and pressuring oil markets. If the conflict extends — Trump said "don't rush me" and then "just give up" — Meta's WhatsApp user counts in Iran will continue to deteriorate. Iran has more than 80 million people; WhatsApp penetration there was among the highest in the Middle East before the conflict began.
  • Microsoft's Azure at 40% growth, ahead of 37–38% guidance, should have produced a strong after-hours reaction but didn't — because the "capacity constrained through 2026" language from Nadella implies that Microsoft's growth ceiling for the next three quarters is infrastructure, not enterprise demand. If you are constrained by supply rather than demand, revenue growth is limited by how fast you can build data centers, not by how many customers want to buy. The $190 billion full-year capex is Microsoft's attempt to remove the supply ceiling faster than competitors can match it.
  • Amazon's AWS $37.59 billion is significant for a specific reason that goes beyond the beat: AWS is the benchmark against which all enterprise AI demand is measured in a supply-constraint environment. Microsoft is capacity constrained; Alphabet has a $460 billion backlog; AWS accelerated to 28.5% growth. If all three major cloud providers are simultaneously supply-constrained and demand-backlogged, the chip shortage thesis — that Nvidia's current production rates are the binding constraint on AI deployment — is validated across the entire enterprise cloud infrastructure market.
  • Powell remaining on the Board of Governors while Warsh chairs creates an institutionally unprecedented Fed structure: a former chair with independent authority as a governor, who was explicitly kept in place by Trump's legal pressure against his removal. Markets will closely watch whether Powell votes with the majority in Warsh's first FOMC meetings, or whether the board's 8-4 dissent split from Wednesday's meeting signals an ongoing internal tension that the FOMC statement will need to manage publicly.
