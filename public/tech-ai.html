The Anchor

SpaceX SPCX enters Nasdaq-100. $4.3B passive inflows. $26B Anthropic+Google AI revenue joins the index. Fable 5 credits begin. White House AI framework live. Tuesday is the day AI infrastructure became unavoidable for every index investor — and the day the government and AI industry agreed on a framework to prevent shutdown-by-default.

SpaceX's Nasdaq-100 inclusion under Nasdaq's fast-track rules is the execution of a framework change that Nasdaq adopted specifically because the traditional eligibility model — a company must trade for a year before Nasdaq-100 consideration — was incompatible with the scale of recent AI-infrastructure IPOs. SpaceX IPO'd on June 12 and was included in the Nasdaq-100 within 15 trading days, qualifying under the new framework's requirement that newly public mega-cap companies can be included after a seasoning period appropriate to their market capitalization rather than a fixed calendar duration. The $4.3 billion passive inflows on Day 1 are the most immediate consequence; the structural consequence is that SpaceX's AI revenue, physical infrastructure, and aerospace assets are now embedded in the benchmark that defines AI investment for most retail and institutional investors.

Fable 5's return matters as much to the enterprise community as the SpaceX inclusion matters to the capital markets community. The 18-day pause from mid-June to July 7 was not a minor disruption — enterprises that had integrated Fable 5 into production workflows had to either pause those workflows, revert to earlier models, or find alternatives for nearly three weeks. The credits-for-extended-usage model that replaces the free-tier-only access represents a maturation of the Anthropic commercial model: the government's willingness to restore access is conditioned on having a tiered access structure that makes security management more granular than the binary on/off that the mid-June shutdown required. The jailbreak severity scoring framework — co-developed by Anthropic, Amazon, Microsoft, and Google — is the regulatory tool that the government now has to assess borderline findings without defaulting to complete shutdown.

SpaceX (SPCX) officially entered the Nasdaq-100 on Tuesday under Nasdaq's newly adopted fast-track inclusion framework — which allows newly public mega-cap companies to join the index after just 15 trading days rather than the traditional one-year seasoning period — triggering an estimated $4.3 billion in mandatory passive inflows as more than $800 billion in Nasdaq-100 tracking funds rebalanced their portfolios to include SPCX at market open. SpaceX brings with it approximately $26 billion in annualized AI revenue from Anthropic and Google contracts, plus a $6.3 billion Colossus supercomputing deal with Reflection AI — a company backed by NVIDIA — paying $150 million per month through 2029, which kicked in on July 1.

SpaceX is the Nasdaq-100's first aerospace constituent — converting the tech benchmark into an aerospace-AI index. Every QQQ holder, every Nasdaq-100 ETF in a retirement account, every international fund using the index as a benchmark now holds SpaceX. A company earning $26 billion annually from Anthropic and Google compute contracts before any rocket, Starlink, or government revenue is counted is not an aerospace company that does some AI — it is an AI infrastructure company that builds rockets. The Nasdaq-100's inclusion is an implicit acknowledgment of that reclassification.

Fable 5 Access Restored · Credits Begin Jul 7 · 18-Day Security Pause Ends · Jailbreak Severity Framework · Anthropic + Amazon + Microsoft + Google Co-Developed · White House AI Governance Live Fable 5 Fable 5 (claude-fable-5): US restrictions lifted Jul 7 · access restored: Pro + Max + Team + select Enterprise · up to 50% weekly usage limit: no cost · above 50%: credits required · 18-day pause: mid-June to Jul 7 · reason for pause: borderline security finding triggered government response · jailbreak severity scoring framework: jointly developed Anthropic + Amazon + Microsoft + Google · purpose: score dangerousness of jailbreaks so government has graded tool vs. binary shutdown · White House AI action plan: voluntary frontier model assessment protocol · agencies work with companies to identify cybersecurity + national security risks while tech still in development

Anthropic's Fable 5 model (claude-fable-5) returned to full subscriber access Tuesday — available to Pro, Max, Team, and select Enterprise subscribers at no additional cost for up to 50% of their weekly usage limit, with credits required for usage exceeding that threshold — ending an 18-day security pause that began in mid-June when a borderline security finding triggered a government response that pulled the model from subscriber access. The restoration follows the joint development by Anthropic, Amazon, Microsoft, and Google of a framework for scoring how dangerous a given jailbreak is — providing the government a graded assessment tool rather than the binary on/off shutdown that made the mid-June pause commercially costly for enterprises that had integrated Fable 5 into production workflows.

The scoring framework replaces the binary shutdown mechanism that made mid-June's pause necessary. Future borderline findings can be assessed against a shared severity scale — allowing the government to restrict specific use cases, require monitoring, or impose tiered access limits rather than defaulting to full termination. The credits-for-extended-usage model governing Fable 5 access is the tiered structure that proportionate security responses require.

HPE/NVIDIA and Alteryx address the enterprise AI deployment gap at the infrastructure layer — complementing Microsoft's Frontier Company, which addresses it at the human-capital layer.
