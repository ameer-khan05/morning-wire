Anthropic and OpenAI filed confidential IPO prospectuses in the same week — Anthropic at $965 billion with $30 billion ARR, OpenAI at $852 billion with $25 billion ARR — creating a $1.8 trillion public-listing pipeline that marks the most consequential week in AI company financial history. Google launched Gemini 3.1 Ultra, Microsoft unveiled MAI-Code-1-Flash, and Nvidia put a 1-petaflop chip inside a consumer PC.

The simultaneity is not coincidence. Both Anthropic (June 1) and OpenAI (June 8) filed within the same week because the same IPO window is open and the same institutional investor appetite — which wants to own frontier AI before the public market offers it — is driving both timelines. Their combined $1.8 trillion in stated valuation at filing exceeds the market capitalization of all but four companies on earth. Anthropic's $30 billion ARR growing from $9 billion at end-2025 is a 3.3× annual run-rate expansion in roughly six months; OpenAI's $25 billion ARR growing from approximately $3.4 billion at end-2023 is a 7× expansion in two and a half years. The enterprise AI adoption cycle that those revenue trajectories reflect is now arriving in the public market simultaneously with the model and platform releases — Gemini 3.1 Ultra's 2-million-token context, Microsoft's own code model — that represent the next competitive frontier above the revenue lines both companies are printing.

The Anchor

Two frontier AI companies at a combined $1.8 trillion filed for public markets in the same week. That is the week's definitive technology finding — not a model launch or a benchmark, but the moment AI's private-market financial scale becomes a public-market fact.

Anthropic's confidential S-1 filing on June 1 came at a $965 billion valuation reflecting a recent funding round, with annualized revenue of $30 billion — up from $9 billion at the end of 2025. The company's EMEA region, which grew 9× in the past year, and its Claude enterprise contracts with Fortune 500 customers across financial services, healthcare, and technology verticals are the revenue engine. OpenAI filed on June 8 at $852 billion with $25 billion in ARR, generating approximately $2 billion in monthly revenue as of March 2026. OpenAI's revenue includes large one-off licensing agreements with enterprise clients and governments, including Stargate partnership revenues from the SoftBank-Oracle-OpenAI AI infrastructure joint venture. The week also saw SpaceX price its offering, making the three-company AI/space tech IPO pipeline valuation approximately $3.6 trillion.

The filings land against a model release backdrop that reframes the competition. Google's Gemini 3.1 Ultra — with a 2-million-token native context window working across text, image, audio, and video simultaneously, without transcription intermediaries — is described by Google as capable of creating "anything from any input, starting with video." Microsoft's MAI-Code-1-Flash is the company's first internally developed code-generation model, designed to take written descriptions and output application source code, reducing Microsoft's dependency on OpenAI-provided models while lowering per-query costs for developers. The AI contest, per analysts covering the June 2026 conference season, has moved beyond the model to the agentic runtime, developer platform, and enterprise workflow integration layer — a frame that aligns precisely with why both Anthropic and OpenAI are racing to demonstrate the largest ARR before their pricing roadshows.

Anthropic filed a confidential S-1 prospectus on June 1 at a reported $965 billion valuation — reflecting its most recent funding round — with $30 billion in annualized revenue, up from $9 billion at the end of 2025. The 3.3× ARR expansion in roughly six months reflects accelerating enterprise contract signings across financial services, healthcare, and technology verticals globally, with EMEA as the fastest-growing region (9× run-rate revenue growth in the past year). The company's plan to triple its international workforce, with London growing from 200 to 800 employees, signals a commercial scaling posture consistent with a company preparing for public-market scrutiny.

Anthropic's filing comes one week before OpenAI's, establishing Anthropic as the earlier mover in the IPO window. Its Constitutional AI governance framework and public safety documentation have served as compliance artifacts for EU AI Act obligations — a competitive advantage in European enterprise procurement that partially explains the EMEA revenue surge. The $965 billion valuation at filing is not a public offering price; it is the private-round mark that reflects what institutional investors paid most recently. The actual IPO pricing will reflect the investor roadshow's demand discovery against the ARR trajectory, margin profile, and competitive positioning vs. OpenAI and Google.

OpenAI filed its confidential IPO prospectus on June 8 — one week after Anthropic — at an $852 billion valuation with $25 billion in annualized recurring revenue, generating approximately $2 billion in monthly revenue as of March 2026. The company loses approximately $1.22 for every dollar earned in its most recent reported quarter, a burn rate driven by compute costs, model development, and talent. Revenue sources include ChatGPT subscriptions, enterprise API contracts, and large-scale licensing agreements — including revenues from the Stargate AI infrastructure joint venture with SoftBank and Oracle, which is building $500 billion in US AI infrastructure over four years.

OpenAI's $25 billion ARR at a 7× expansion from end-2023 reflects the ChatGPT consumer flywheel converting to enterprise contract growth, but the $1.22 cost per dollar of revenue means the IPO prospectus will need to demonstrate a credible path to profitability. The filing timing — immediately following Anthropic's — may be partly defensive: the same institutional investors who receive Anthropic's roadshow materials will also receive OpenAI's, and OpenAI needs to be in the pipeline before Anthropic's narrative establishes the framing for both companies.

Google Gemini 3.1 Ultra · 2M Token Context · Native Multimodal · Microsoft MAI-Code-1-Flash 2M Google Gemini 3.1 Ultra · 2 million token native context window · text + image + audio + video simultaneously · no transcription intermediaries · "create anything from any input, starting with video" · Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite: 2.5× faster · 45% faster output · $0.25 per million input tokens · Microsoft MAI-Code-1-Flash: description-to-source-code · reduces OpenAI dependency · lower developer cost

Google launched Gemini 3.1 Ultra with a 2-million-token native multimodal context window that processes text, image, audio, and video simultaneously without transcription intermediaries — a context length that enables reasoning across entire codebases, legal document sets, or feature-length films in a single query. Concurrently, Microsoft unveiled MAI-Code-1-Flash, its first internally developed code-generation model that translates written descriptions directly into application and website source code, reducing Microsoft's cost and dependency on OpenAI-provided models while giving developers a faster, cheaper code-generation alternative.

Gemini 3.1 Ultra's 2-million-token native context — without the "chunking" that shorter-context models use to process long documents — changes what AI can reason about: a query can span a full company's codebase, the complete history of a legal dispute, or all of a patient's medical records simultaneously. This is not a marginal upgrade; it changes the qualitative nature of the task AI can perform. Microsoft's MAI-Code-1-Flash is strategically significant because it is Microsoft's first model not powered by OpenAI — the beginning of a dual-track AI model strategy that reduces the financial and competitive exposure of exclusive OpenAI dependency.

Nvidia put a 1-petaflop AI chip inside a consumer personal computer — a milestone that brings data-center-class AI inference capability to the edge device layer and enables on-device AI reasoning at scales previously requiring cloud infrastructure. Oracle fell more than 10% in extended trading after revealing plans to raise an additional $20 billion through equity and debt offerings to fund AI infrastructure expansion, marking the largest single AI infrastructure capital raise by an established enterprise technology company this year — and prompting a sharp market reaction to the dilutive equity component.

Nvidia's 1-petaflop PC chip is the consumer manifestation of the AI hardware buildout: where the past two years saw AI compute concentrated in data center GPU clusters, the arrival of petaflop-class AI capability on a personal computer signals the beginning of edge AI inference at scale. Oracle's $20 billion raise reflects the other end of the same buildout: cloud AI infrastructure for OpenAI's Stargate workloads requires capital at a scale that even Oracle's existing balance sheet cannot self-fund. The market's 10% after-hours reaction is a reminder that AI infrastructure spending is valued differently as a demand signal (positive for AI theses) versus an equity offering (negative for existing shareholders in the short term).

Quick hits

  • The Anthropic-OpenAI filing sequence matters for IPO mechanics: Anthropic filed first, establishing the narrative. OpenAI filed a week later, ensuring it is in the pipeline before Anthropic can price. Institutional investors will receive both roadshow books simultaneously and will have to allocate between two competing frontier AI IPOs — a dynamic that has not existed in the technology IPO market before. The pricing outcomes will set the public-market valuation benchmark for AI companies for years.
  • Microsoft's MAI-Code-1-Flash is the first concrete step in Microsoft's long-signaled strategy to develop its own AI models alongside the OpenAI partnership. It reduces per-query inference costs for developers using Microsoft's Azure AI platform and gives Microsoft a model it fully owns. The competitive pressure this creates on OpenAI — whose primary revenue includes Azure API consumption of OpenAI models — is real but modest in the near term. The longer-term implication is a Microsoft AI stack that is not entirely OpenAI-dependent.
  • Oracle's $20 billion equity and debt raise produced the largest single-session AI infrastructure capital event by an established enterprise tech company this year. The after-hours -10% reaction reflects equity dilution concerns — not skepticism about AI infrastructure demand. Oracle's stock has been a core AI infrastructure play in 2026, and the raise signals that even strong-conviction AI infrastructure operators cannot self-fund at the pace the market is demanding. The market's negative reaction is a pricing-efficiency response, not a thesis-change signal.
