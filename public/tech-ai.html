The Anchor

Nvidia's $91 billion Q2 guidance is not just the largest quarterly revenue forecast in semiconductor history. It is the data point that answers the question the market has been asking for three months: does AI demand hold at 4.5% Treasury yields? The answer, delivered by Jensen Huang on the earnings call, is that cloud customers cannot get enough Blackwell and GPU allocations are sold out.

Nvidia's Q1 FY2027 result requires unpacking across three dimensions. The first is magnitude: $81.6 billion in quarterly revenue is approximately what Nvidia generated in all of FY2024 (approximately $60.9 billion), in a single quarter. Year-over-year growth of 85% at this scale — the company is not a startup rounding from zero — means every hyperscaler that has announced AI capex commitments is delivering on them through Nvidia purchases. Amazon, Microsoft, Google, and Meta collectively guided for $280-320 billion in 2026 capital expenditure; Nvidia's Q1 result is the real-time measurement that those commitments are being honored at the pace indicated. The second dimension is the specific Blackwell story: Data Center revenue of $75.2 billion at 92% year-over-year growth means the Blackwell architecture ramp — which had been the source of supply-chain concern through 2025 — is now fully operational and demand-constrained rather than supply-constrained. Huang's statement that customers "cannot get enough Blackwell" is the commercial version of the data: supply has caught up with manufacturing expectations, and demand continues to exceed supply. The third dimension is the Q2 guidance: $91 billion, 11.5% sequential growth, implies an annualized run-rate above $364 billion. For context, the most valuable revenue-generating company in the S&P 500 generates approximately $400-450 billion annually across all business lines. Nvidia's AI hardware business, at $91 billion per quarter if the guidance is met, would approach Apple's total annual revenue in a single fiscal year.

The EPS beat is analytically distinct from the revenue beat and equally significant. Revenue of $81.6 billion versus $78 billion expectation is a 4.6% beat. EPS of $2.14 versus $1.77 expectation is a 21% beat on the bottom line — a substantially larger percentage improvement on profitability than on revenue, driven by Blackwell's favorable gross margin profile versus prior-generation products. Blackwell's manufacturing process at TSMC achieves higher die yield than H100/H200, and the product's data center configurations (NVL72 and NVL36 rack-scale systems) command premium pricing that expands the gross margin relative to individual GPU sales. The combination of higher unit volume (more Blackwell shipped per quarter), higher pricing (per-rack versus per-GPU), and better manufacturing yield produces the EPS leverage that the 21% bottom-line beat reflects. This margin structure is what makes Nvidia's $91 billion Q2 guidance credible rather than merely aspirational: the profitability improvement runs ahead of revenue, suggesting the company's pricing power in Blackwell systems is intact and the gross margin profile will sustain or improve as Blackwell matures in production.

Nvidia reported Q1 FY2027 revenue of $81.6 billion — beating the $78 billion consensus by 4.6% and growing 85% year-over-year — with Data Center revenue hitting $75.2 billion (+92% year-over-year), EPS of $2.14 versus the $1.77 estimate, and Q2 guidance of $91 billion. CEO Jensen Huang said on the earnings call that Blackwell GPU demand is "off the charts" and cloud customers cannot secure sufficient allocations. The result resolves three months of debate about whether AI capex would hold at elevated yield levels: it is holding, and accelerating.

The Q1 result has specific implications for each of the major hyperscaler AI infrastructure buyers. Amazon (AWS), which guided for $100+ billion in 2026 capex, is among the largest Blackwell customers; Microsoft Azure's AI infrastructure commitment, backing both its own deployments and the OpenAI partnership, is similarly substantial; Google Cloud's 63% year-over-year Q1 2026 revenue growth is the downstream commercial signal that AI infrastructure investment is generating enterprise revenue. At $75.2 billion in Data Center revenue for a single quarter, Nvidia is absorbing approximately 25-27% of the combined 2026 capex guidance from the four major US hyperscalers. The Blackwell sold-out condition — which Huang confirmed has no near-term resolution: "our customers are very eager to get more" — means the $91 billion Q2 guidance is supply-constrained rather than demand-constrained. TSMC is Nvidia's sole manufacturing partner for Blackwell, and the CoWoS advanced packaging capacity that Blackwell's HBM memory configuration requires remains the binding constraint on how many units ship per quarter. The $91 billion guidance implies that TSMC's CoWoS capacity ramp is proceeding on schedule through Q2, providing the semiconductor supply-chain confirmation that Nvidia can physically build and ship the product its customers want.

OpenAI launched DeployCo — a majority-owned standalone subsidiary backed by more than $4 billion in initial capital from TPG, Goldman Sachs, McKinsey, Bain Capital, Capgemini, and 14 other institutional investors — on May 11, 2026. The subsidiary is designed to provide enterprise AI deployment infrastructure at scale, building the commercial delivery layer that sits downstream of OpenAI's model development and connects the frontier AI capability to the enterprise contracts, integration services, and managed deployment that large organizations require.

DeployCo's structure as a standalone subsidiary — majority-owned by OpenAI but with independent institutional capital — reflects a deliberate separation of the deployment business from the model research business. OpenAI's core research organization has historically been structured to prioritize capability development over commercial execution; DeployCo creates an entity that can be staffed and incentivized as a commercial deployment business with the margin structure and enterprise sales culture that large-scale software delivery requires. The $4 billion in institutional capital from management consultancies (McKinsey, Bain Capital, Capgemini) and financial institutions (Goldman Sachs) is particularly telling: these firms are simultaneously customers of enterprise AI deployments and investors in the infrastructure delivering those deployments. Their capital participation creates aligned incentives between the deployment company and the consulting and financial services sectors that are among the largest potential AI deployment markets. OpenAI at approximately $25 billion in annualized revenue has demonstrated that the model subscription business scales; DeployCo is the bet that the enterprise deployment services business scales separately and at a different margin profile. The institutional investor base is the commercial validation that the bet is credible.

Microsoft is in talks to supply Anthropic with its MAIA 200 custom AI chips — a deal that would make Anthropic the first external customer for Microsoft's homegrown silicon and represent the most significant diversification of AI chip supply away from Nvidia for a frontier AI lab since AMD's MI300X entered the market. The talks follow Microsoft's $5 billion investment in Anthropic and come alongside a separate report that Microsoft is winding down some of its Claude Code licenses due to cost concerns.

The Microsoft-Anthropic MAIA 200 chip relationship, if completed, creates an unusual alignment: Microsoft is simultaneously an investor in Anthropic, a potential chip supplier to Anthropic, and a competitor to Anthropic's Claude through its own Copilot AI products. The chip supply dimension is the most strategically significant: Anthropic currently relies primarily on Google Cloud TPUs and, to a lesser extent, Nvidia GPUs for its Claude model training and inference. Microsoft's MAIA 200 — designed specifically for large language model training and inference at hyperscaler scale — would provide Anthropic with a third silicon option, reducing its dependence on Google's infrastructure and offering Microsoft a revenue stream from Anthropic's substantial compute requirements. Microsoft's MAIA 200, first deployed internally on Azure, has not previously been offered as an external product; Anthropic as the first external customer would give Microsoft the credibility to market the chip to other AI developers. The simultaneous winding-down of some Claude Code licenses reflects a cost discipline at Microsoft that is separable from the chip supply relationship — enterprise AI tool costs are being actively managed even as the infrastructure investment continues.

A public split opened Wednesday between OpenAI and Anthropic on whether AI will cause mass job displacement. OpenAI CEO Sam Altman said he was "wrong" about earlier projections that AI would wipe out entire categories of jobs and now believes a jobs apocalypse is unlikely. Anthropic co-founder Chris Olah, speaking at the Vatican's AI ethics conference, said the opposite: "There is a real possibility that AI will displace human labor at very large scale." The two organizations are now publicly diverged on the fundamental question of what their products are doing to the labor market.

The Altman-Olah divergence is not merely rhetorical — it reflects different analytical frameworks for understanding AI's economic impact that have direct policy implications. Altman's revised view — that AI augments rather than replaces workers, that job categories change rather than disappear, and that the labor market will adapt — is the view that justifies the current policy approach of letting AI deployment proceed without employment-protection regulation. Olah's view — that the displacement is real, large-scale, and should be anticipated and mitigated — is the view that justifies calls for proactive labor market policy, universal basic income discussions, and regulation of AI deployment rates. The fact that Anthropic's most prominent researcher is publicly endorsing the displacement view while simultaneously Anthropic is deploying Claude to 276,000 KPMG professionals creates an uncomfortable internal tension: if Olah is right, the KPMG deployment is part of the labor displacement mechanism he is warning about. Altman's evolution from earlier catastrophic projections to current optimism mirrors his broader public communication strategy of de-escalating concern around AI's near-term risks — a strategy that serves OpenAI's commercial interests in an environment where regulatory scrutiny is active.

KPMG and Anthropic announced on May 19 the KPMG Digital Gateway Powered by Claude — a deployment that makes Claude available to KPMG's 276,000 professionals across 138 countries through the firm's core client delivery platform on Microsoft Azure. The deployment integrates Claude Cowork (real-time collaboration with the AI) and Managed Agents (automated multi-step workflow execution) into the platform that KPMG professionals use for audit, tax, advisory, and consulting engagements with clients globally.

The KPMG deployment is, by headcount, the largest single enterprise Claude deployment announced to date and one of the largest single enterprise AI deployments of any frontier model. Its significance extends beyond the headline count: KPMG professionals are using Claude on client-facing work — audit engagements, tax filings, regulatory compliance reviews, and strategic advisory — not merely for internal productivity. AI deployed on client-facing professional services work creates a different quality of enterprise adoption than AI deployed on internal knowledge management or HR workflows: it is embedded in the billable-hour model that drives professional services revenue, meaning KPMG's partners will need to demonstrate that Claude improves engagement quality and outcome, not merely throughput. The Microsoft Azure hosting is consistent with KPMG's existing cloud infrastructure and with Microsoft's enterprise AI strategy of positioning Azure as the delivery platform for Anthropic deployments — creating a three-way relationship between Microsoft infrastructure, Anthropic models, and KPMG's global client network that each party benefits from. For Anthropic, 276,000 professional services users constitute a substantial recurring usage base across the most analytically intensive use cases in enterprise AI: legal reasoning, financial analysis, regulatory interpretation, and cross-border compliance. The Olah-Altman jobs debate runs directly through this deployment: if KPMG's partners are using Claude on client-facing audit and advisory work, the labor displacement question is not theoretical.

Quick hits

  • Nvidia's $91 billion Q2 guidance deserves scrutiny on its supply-constraint assumptions. The binding constraint for Blackwell shipments is CoWoS advanced packaging capacity at TSMC, which determines how many Blackwell modules can be assembled per month regardless of die availability. TSMC has been expanding CoWoS capacity aggressively since 2024, and Nvidia's Q1 result confirms that expansion is on track — but the Q2 guidance implicitly assumes no incremental supply constraint emerges in the quarter. The most plausible risk is not demand softening but delivery timing: if TSMC's CoWoS yield for a specific Blackwell configuration runs below plan in any given month, quarterly revenue can miss the guidance without any underlying demand deterioration. Nvidia's management has consistently guided at the midpoint of their realistic range, and their guidance track record is among the best in large-cap semiconductors. But at $91 billion, even a 2% miss is a $1.8 billion shortfall — larger in absolute terms than most semiconductor companies' annual revenues. The guidance should be read as indicating that TSMC's packaging ramp is on track, not that Nvidia has eliminated execution risk.
  • The Microsoft-Anthropic MAIA 200 chip talks exist in a context of Microsoft's broader AI chip strategy that makes the deal more understandable. Microsoft deployed MAIA 100 in Azure data centers for internal AI inference workloads in 2024, improving its cost-per-inference for lower-complexity tasks relative to Nvidia A100/H100 chips. MAIA 200 is the next generation, designed for both training and inference at the scale Anthropic's Claude models require. Microsoft's incentive to offer MAIA to external customers is primarily about volume: custom silicon's unit economics improve dramatically with utilization, and Anthropic's compute requirements (running Claude 3.X models at production scale across the enterprise customers now deploying Claude) represent a substantial utilization base. Anthropic's incentive is infrastructure diversification and potential cost reduction: at $19 billion ARR, compute costs are Anthropic's largest operating expense, and a MAIA supply arrangement that offers competitive pricing to Nvidia alternatives would directly improve Anthropic's gross margin. The deal's complication is that Microsoft and Anthropic have a publicly complicated relationship — Microsoft is an investor in Anthropic but also competes with Claude through Copilot — meaning any chip supply arrangement must be structured to avoid information access concerns and competitive conflicts.
  • The OpenAI DeployCo structure reveals how OpenAI is solving a problem that all major AI companies face: how to scale enterprise deployment without compromising research culture. OpenAI's research organization — which has historically operated on academic norms of publication, peer review, and mission-driven research rather than commercial delivery — is structurally incompatible with the enterprise software delivery culture of SLA compliance, integration services, customer success management, and professional services. DeployCo resolves this by creating a separate entity that can be staffed, incentivized, and managed as a commercial software business. The $4 billion from institutional investors — notably McKinsey, Capgemini, and Bain Capital, all of which are consulting firms that compete with KPMG, Deloitte, and Accenture for enterprise AI services mandates — creates a conflict of interest that DeployCo's governance will need to manage. A consulting firm that is both an investor in DeployCo and a competitor to the consulting firms that DeployCo may partner with or compete against for enterprise deployment mandates requires careful relationship disclosure. The $4 billion does not constitute control — OpenAI maintains majority ownership — but the investor relationships will influence which enterprise markets DeployCo prioritizes and which partnerships it pursues.
  • The Armada deployable AI data center story — the company raised $230 million in an oversubscribed Series B at a $2 billion valuation, announced May 19 — is the AI infrastructure story that is growing in importance but receiving less coverage than the hyperscaler capex narrative. Armada's model is physically portable AI compute: data centers housed in standard shipping containers that can be deployed to remote locations, military installations, oil platforms, and geographically isolated environments where hyperscaler cloud access is unavailable or inadvisable. The military and government market is the clearest near-term demand driver: US and allied militaries require AI inference capability at the edge — in deployed environments where latency, sovereignty, and connectivity limitations preclude cloud-based AI access. Armada's $2 billion valuation at Series B reflects investor assessment that the addressable market for sovereign and edge AI compute is substantial and growing. For the broader AI infrastructure picture, Armada is a data point about where the AI compute demand goes next: after the hyperscaler cloud buildout (Nvidia's $91 billion market), the next wave is purpose-built compute at the edge, in the field, and in sovereign environments that cloud providers cannot serve. The $230 million raise in a closed Series B (oversubscribed) suggests institutional investor confidence that this market is real, near-term, and large enough to justify the infrastructure investment.
  • The Canadian Privacy Commissioner's May 6 finding that OpenAI violated Canadian privacy laws in developing ChatGPT — concluded jointly with provincial counterparts in Quebec, British Columbia, and Alberta — is the most consequential privacy enforcement action against an AI company since Italy's 2023 Garante ban on ChatGPT. The finding relates specifically to the collection of personal information without meaningful consent from Canadian internet users whose content was used in GPT training data. Canada's finding is not yet an enforcement order — it is a determination that violation occurred, with negotiated remediation as the expected next step — but it creates three risks for OpenAI: it establishes that Canadian training data collection violated Canadian law, providing a legal template for similar findings in European jurisdictions under GDPR; it potentially requires OpenAI to modify its Canadian data handling, which may require operationally significant changes to its training data pipeline; and it is the first major finding by a G7 privacy regulator that a frontier AI company's pre-training data collection was unlawful, which is precisely the outcome the AI safety and privacy communities have been warning about since 2022. OpenAI's response — engaging with the commissioner's office on remediation — is the correct commercial strategy, but the legal exposure extends well beyond Canada if the finding's reasoning is applied by European data protection authorities.
  • The Microsoft open-sourcing of Webwright — a terminal-native browser-agent framework scoring 60.1% on the Odysseys long-horizon benchmark and 86.7% on Online-Mind2Web — is worth examining as an indicator of where the agentic AI research frontier is currently positioned. These benchmark scores are state-of-the-art for browser agents, meaning agents that can autonomously navigate web interfaces, fill forms, extract information, and complete multi-step online tasks without human intervention. The 60.1% Odysseys score means that on a set of long-horizon web tasks — tasks requiring multiple sequential decisions across multiple web pages — Webwright succeeds in 60 out of 100 attempts, with the failures primarily concentrated in tasks requiring disambiguation of ambiguous instructions or recovery from unexpected website states. The practical implication: current best-in-class browser agents are reliable enough for repetitive, well-defined web tasks but not reliable enough for open-ended web research or complex multi-site workflows that involve unpredictable responses. Webwright's open-source release means the benchmark will be challenged rapidly by the research community — the gap between current state-of-the-art and the 80-85% threshold that would make agents commercially reliable for enterprise deployment is approximately 20-25 percentage points, which the field may close within 12-18 months at current research velocity.
