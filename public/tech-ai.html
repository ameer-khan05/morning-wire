The Anchor

Anthropic's $30 billion ARR — passing OpenAI's $24 billion — is the enterprise AI market's first definitive revenue leadership shift. The company that the Pentagon excluded is now the industry's revenue leader.

Anthropic's ARR crossing $30 billion while OpenAI sits at approximately $24 billion represents the most consequential competitive shift in the AI industry since OpenAI launched ChatGPT in November 2022. The reversal is structural: Anthropic's 80% enterprise revenue composition — compared to OpenAI's more consumer-heavy mix — means that Anthropic's revenue is generated primarily by large organizations making annual commitments of $1 million or more per year, a customer base that is highly stable, contract-bound, and resistant to switching. The more than 1,000 enterprise customers spending over $1 million annually on Claude doubled from 500 in February — roughly two months ago — demonstrating a growth acceleration that puts OpenAI's enterprise recovery timeline under pressure. The growth rate is the more alarming figure for OpenAI than the absolute revenue gap: Anthropic grew from $9 billion ARR at end-2025 to $30 billion in approximately four months — a 3.3x increase in one quarter — while OpenAI's growth has been constrained by its compute cost structure, missed monthly revenue targets in early 2026, and consumer market share losses to Google's Gemini.

The training efficiency dimension adds strategic depth to the revenue comparison. Anthropic is achieving $30 billion in revenue while spending approximately one-quarter of OpenAI's model training budget — a 4x capital efficiency advantage on the input cost that determines long-term competitive position in AI. This means Anthropic's path to profitability (which the company has not yet reached) is structurally more achievable than OpenAI's, whose $600 billion in accumulated compute commitments creates a fixed cost base that is difficult to grow past through enterprise sales alone. OpenAI disputes the gross/net revenue comparison — arguing that Anthropic's gross figure includes pass-through compute costs that inflate the number by approximately $8 billion — but even on OpenAI's preferred calculation, Anthropic at ~$22 billion is approaching OpenAI at ~$24 billion within its preferred framing. The Pentagon exclusion — which cost Anthropic the DoD's $1.8 billion AI program budget — has been fully absorbed by enterprise commercial revenue, with the "supply-chain risk" label apparently having no negative effect on commercial enterprise procurement decisions by financial services, healthcare, and legal industry customers who prize exactly the safety commitments that disqualified Anthropic from the Pentagon contract.

Google DeepMind · UK Union · Pentagon Backlash Union Vote DeepMind UK employees vote to unionize · 600+ petition Pichai · deal signed while petition circulating · "any lawful purpose" Pentagon clause · research strikes threatened

Google DeepMind UK employees vote to form a union over the company's classified Pentagon AI contracts — the agreements that Anthropic refused to sign. More than 600 DeepMind researchers petitioned CEO Sundar Pichai to refuse the deal. The petition was still being signed when Google finalized the contract.

The DeepMind UK union vote is the most significant internal AI company labor action since Google's 2018 Project Maven walkout, and it arrives in a fundamentally different context: this time, Google leadership did not concede. The classified Pentagon AI agreements signed by Google — alongside OpenAI, Microsoft, Amazon, Oracle, Nvidia, SpaceX, and Reflection AI — include language allowing the DoD to deploy Gemini for "any lawful purpose" within the classified IL6 and IL7 environments, a formulation that 600+ DeepMind researchers identified as incompatible with responsible AI deployment for automated targeting and surveillance applications. Alex Turner, a research scientist at Google DeepMind, posted on X: "I spent the last 2 months trying to prevent this." The union vote in the UK — where collective labor rights for knowledge workers are more established than in the US — elevates the dissent from petitioning management to a formal institutional structure that gives employees standing to negotiate over AI deployment conditions in Google's contracts. Research strikes — in which employees abstain from work on core products including the Gemini AI assistant — have been discussed as potential escalation. Google's leadership position, as disclosed to employees: the Pentagon agreement is consistent with Google's AI principles and the decision was made deliberately at the executive level. The asymmetry with Anthropic — which also has an AI principles framework but applied it to refuse the Pentagon contract — is the contested ground between Google's leadership and its employees.

Quick hits

  • The OpenAI accounting dispute over the Anthropic ARR comparison deserves a close reading. OpenAI's argument — that Anthropic's $30 billion gross figure should be reduced by approximately $8 billion in pass-through compute costs to produce a comparable net revenue figure of ~$22 billion — is technically defensible in the sense that gross revenue and net revenue are genuinely different figures. The more important question is what the compute cost structure implies for each company's economics. OpenAI's $600 billion in accumulated compute commitments means its compute costs are a fixed, long-term obligation embedded in contracts; Anthropic's compute costs are more variable, tied to actual inference usage. If you're comparing ARR as a proxy for underlying business health, OpenAI's compute commitment creates a denominator problem that Anthropic's model avoids: Anthropic's revenue growth is not encumbered by a forward compute commitment that could outrun it, which is precisely what OpenAI's CFO has been warning about internally.
  • AMD CEO Lisa Su's revision of the server CPU market growth estimate from "approximately 20% annually" (her prior guidance) to "above 35% annually" is the single most significant forward market sizing change in the AI chip sector in 2026. The specific driver she cited — agentic AI workloads — deserves attention: agentic AI requires not just GPU inference capacity (where Nvidia dominates) but substantial CPU capacity for orchestration, memory management, tool-use execution, and workflow coordination that occurs between inference steps. As AI deployments shift from single-query chat interactions to multi-step agentic workflows that run continuously in enterprise environments, the CPU-to-GPU ratio in AI infrastructure changes in AMD's favor. The $120 billion server CPU market by decade-end — if it materializes — would represent roughly a 4x expansion from today's levels and would be the biggest CPU market growth cycle since enterprise data center build-outs in the 2000s. AMD, with its EPYC server CPU line, is positioned to take substantial share of that growth against Intel.
  • The DeepMind union vote's UK-specific context matters for understanding its implications. UK labor law provides a clearer framework for knowledge worker unionization than US law, particularly the "statutory recognition" pathway that requires employers to recognize a union once it represents a majority of a bargaining unit. If DeepMind UK's union achieves statutory recognition, it would create a negotiating table at which AI deployment conditions — specifically, the conditions under which Google can commit DeepMind research to military applications — become a subject of collective bargaining rather than a management prerogative. No major AI research organization has a unionized technical workforce with bargaining rights over AI deployment decisions; DeepMind UK's union vote is the first credible step toward that scenario. The escalation pathway — from union recognition to bargaining over research deployment decisions — would be the first institutional mechanism in the AI industry that places ethical AI deployment constraints outside of management's unilateral control.
  • GPT-5.5 Instant's Gmail integration is worth examining against Anthropic's enterprise agent strategy. Anthropic's financial services agents are integrated into enterprise document management systems, compliance workflows, and internal databases — the data sources that create value for financial institutions. OpenAI's GPT-5.5 Instant is integrating with Google's Workspace ecosystem — Gmail, Drive, Docs — the data sources that create value for individual consumers and knowledge workers. The two integration strategies target different buyers: Anthropic targets enterprise IT and compliance decision-makers who approve $1M+ annual contracts; OpenAI targets individual ChatGPT Plus and Pro subscribers who pay $20-200 per month. Both strategies are valid, but they are not competing for the same customer. The revenue divergence — $30 billion Anthropic vs $24 billion OpenAI — suggests that the $1M+ enterprise contracts are currently more valuable than consumer subscriptions at scale, which has implications for how both companies should allocate product and sales investment going forward.
  • Supermicro's gross margin expansion from 6.3% to 9.9% is the operational story the market has been waiting for since the company's SEC filing delay controversy in late 2025. Supermicro's ability to deliver AI server rack systems at competitive margins — after a period when component constraints, supply chain issues, and the reputational damage of the audit firm resignation combined to produce multi-quarter margin compression — is the prerequisite for the stock to recover from its 2025 lows. The 9.9% gross margin is still below Supermicro's historical peak margins (which exceeded 13% in 2023) and below the 11-12% range management has guided toward as a medium-term target. But the direction is confirmed: costs are declining as supply chain normalizes, and the revenue trajectory remains intact as AI infrastructure spending accelerates. The 18% single-session surge is the market pricing the margin recovery confirmation, not the revenue beat — the revenue miss was known to be possible given the estimation error in the hyperscaler pull-forward thesis that had driven the estimate to $12.33 billion.
  • The Anthropic financial services agent rollout — 10 agents specifically for banks, insurers, asset managers, and fintech — is the product category that will determine whether Anthropic's $30 billion ARR growth rate sustains through 2026 and into 2027. Financial services is the highest-value AI market in the enterprise sector: the compliance, risk, and productivity value of accurate AI in finance is large enough to justify very high per-seat and per-workflow pricing, and the regulated nature of financial services creates a structural advantage for an AI company with a strong constitutional AI and safety positioning. The specific agents — pitchbook drafting, financial statement review, credit memo preparation, compliance escalation — are tasks that financial institutions currently pay expensive junior analysts, paralegals, and compliance staff to perform. If Anthropic's agents can perform these tasks at accuracy levels that satisfy the compliance and fiduciary review standards of financial regulation, the displacement value is enormous and the pricing power is correspondingly high. The 1,000+ customers at $1M+/year is the first data point confirming that enough financial institutions are betting on Anthropic's agents to put serious money down.
