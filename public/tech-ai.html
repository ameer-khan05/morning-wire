Microsoft began routing tens of thousands of weekly AI prompts from Excel and Outlook through its proprietary MAI model family as of July 7, 2026 — the first disclosed production-scale shift of Microsoft 365 Copilot traffic from OpenAI and Anthropic to Microsoft's own AI systems — as TechTimes reported that Microsoft's in-house AI has "taken over" Excel and Outlook for the Copilot requests that account for the majority of enterprise AI interactions: email thread summarization, reply drafting, and spreadsheet formatting, which Microsoft AI described as "the bread-and-butter requests" that define the practical enterprise AI experience for Microsoft's 400 million Microsoft 365 subscribers. The seven MAI models launched at Build 2026 in June include MAI-Thinking-1 as the flagship reasoning model, MAI-Code-1-Flash for software development, and multimodal models for image, voice, and transcription.

The strategic significance of the shift is not about the models' benchmark performance relative to GPT-5 or Fable 5 — it is about the structural change in the Microsoft-OpenAI commercial relationship. Microsoft's $13 billion investment in OpenAI gave it distribution rights and model access, but routing Copilot traffic to its own models in its highest-traffic products is the move that reduces the ongoing revenue transfer to OpenAI on every query those products generate. WinBuzzer reported the shift as aimed at "cutting Copilot costs," but the competitive independence dimension is equally significant: as OpenAI builds its own consumer hardware (IO Products) and potentially its own enterprise products, Microsoft has competitive incentive to reduce its operational dependence on OpenAI's inference infrastructure. The July 7 production deployment — not a test or pilot but a disclosed production-scale shift — is the signal that Microsoft's AI strategy has matured beyond API resale into vertical integration of its own model stack.

Google's Gemini 2.5 Pro with Deep Think reasoning mode scored 82.4% on GPQA Diamond and 89.8% on MMLU-Pro — surpassing both OpenAI's GPT-5.5 and Anthropic's Fable 5 on science-focused benchmarks, Fortune reported — establishing Google's frontier model as the benchmark leader on graduate-level science reasoning evaluations that the enterprise AI industry uses as its primary signal of frontier model capability, while Gemini 3.5 Pro with a 2-million-token context window remains scheduled for July 17 — the longest commercial context window available from any AI model, enabling developers to load entire codebases or document collections into a single session without retrieval systems.

GPQA Diamond is specifically designed to be difficult for current AI systems — it contains graduate-level science questions that expert human PhDs answer correctly at roughly 65% accuracy, making it one of the few remaining benchmarks where AI performance relative to expert humans is still meaningfully contested. Gemini 2.5 Pro's 82.4% score — surpassing expert human PhD accuracy — means Google's frontier model is now performing above the human-expert ceiling on science reasoning in a benchmark designed to measure exactly that capability. That surpasses GPT-5.5 and Fable 5 on the same evaluation. The competitive implications for enterprise AI buyers making model commitments are direct: the company whose models were leading GPQA Diamond one quarter ago may not be leading it the next. The Gemini 3.5 Pro July 17 launch — with the 2-million-token context window as its primary differentiation — layers a context-length advantage on top of a benchmark advantage, giving enterprise buyers two distinct Google advantages over OpenAI in the same week.

OpenAI officially launched the GPT-5.6 model family on July 9 — the flagship Sol, balanced Terra, and budget Luna — following completion of a US government review that had delayed the release, making GPT-5.6 the first major commercial AI model family disclosed to have passed a government review as a condition of launch, as the Apple trade-secret lawsuit continues in Northern California federal court and OpenAI's confidential IPO filing targeting a $730 billion September 2026 debut remains planned — a filing that must disclose the Apple litigation as a material pending lawsuit in the S-1's risk factors, meaning that Tang Tan's "actual parts" instruction, Chang Liu's laptop theft allegation, and the full scope of the hardware trade-secret claim will appear in the IPO prospectus read by every institutional investor evaluating the offering.

The US government review requirement for GPT-5.6's launch is the policy development most likely to shape the competitive landscape beyond this week. If the US government is now reviewing commercial AI model releases before launch as a standard condition, it introduces a timeline variable into AI model release cycles that benefits established companies with existing government relationships and compliance infrastructure — and imposes a significant friction on smaller labs that lack the legal and government affairs resources to navigate review processes. The review also signals that the US government has identified frontier AI releases as a category of national significance warranting pre-market oversight — a regulatory stance that, if extended to other models, would constitute the first systematic US pre-release review of commercial AI systems. OpenAI's concurrent position as the first company through that review process and the defendant in a blockbuster hardware trade-secret case captures the full paradox of the frontier AI industry's moment: government-validated on one front, legally besieged on another.

Quick hits

  • Microsoft's MAI deployment in Excel and Outlook has a revenue arithmetic implication that the company has not publicly quantified but that is straightforward to estimate. Microsoft 365 Copilot charges $30 per seat per month. A meaningful fraction of that margin has been going to OpenAI and Anthropic as inference costs on every query those products serve. Routing the highest-volume query types — email summarization, reply drafting, spreadsheet formatting — to in-house MAI models eliminates that per-query cost on tens of thousands of weekly interactions. At Microsoft's scale of enterprise customers, the inference cost saving across even a fraction of its Copilot seats represents hundreds of millions of dollars annually in improved gross margin. The shift is therefore both a competitive independence move and a margin improvement program. As MAI model quality improves and Microsoft expands the query types it routes to its own infrastructure, the structural margin improvement compounds. This is the Microsoft Azure AI story at the product layer: vertical integration of the model stack, not just the compute stack.
  • The US government review requirement for GPT-5.6 is underreported relative to its potential significance. If the US government has established a review process for frontier commercial AI model releases — even informally — it creates a precedent that could evolve into a formal pre-market review regime analogous to FDA drug approval or FCC spectrum licensing. OpenAI completing such a review before launching GPT-5.6 suggests either that the company sought review voluntarily (as a regulatory relationship investment) or that government entities with national security equities required it (as a condition for continued government contracts or classified data access). Either interpretation is significant: voluntary review signals that OpenAI is positioning itself as the government-compliant AI provider, which is a substantial competitive advantage in the federal and defense markets. Involuntary review signals that US regulators have identified frontier AI releases as a category requiring pre-market national security assessment — a constraint that applies to all frontier labs, not just OpenAI, if formalized.
