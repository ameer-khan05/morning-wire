SpaceX confirmed a $60 billion all-stock acquisition of Cursor just four trading days after its record IPO, giving Musk's xAI division more than a million enterprise users and $4 billion in ARR against Anthropic and OpenAI — as Fable 5 enters its sixth day offline with Anthropic engineers now in Washington for Commerce Department talks, and OpenAI crosses $25 billion in annualized revenue with early IPO preparatory steps underway.

The SpaceX-Cursor deal is the largest AI coding acquisition ever and the fastest capital deployment following a tech IPO in recent memory. SpaceX had secured an April option giving it the right to acquire Cursor (Anysphere) for $60 billion or pay roughly $10 billion for a partnership. Two trading days after listing at a record valuation, SpaceX exercised the full acquisition option — not the cheaper partnership path. The speed of the decision is a signal: xAI's leadership viewed the Cursor acquisition as time-sensitive, probably because Anthropic's Claude Code and OpenAI's Codex were compounding enterprise market share while xAI's organic coding tool development lagged. With Fable 5 offline and Claude Code's enterprise momentum disrupted, the competitive calculus for xAI entering the coding market right now is better than it will be once Fable 5 is restored. Cursor's $4 billion in ARR — built on more than a million paying enterprise users who have made it their primary AI coding environment — gives xAI something it could not build organically in under three years: a revenue-generating, distribution-embedded, developer-sticky AI coding platform. OpenAI's milestone of surpassing $25 billion in ARR with early IPO steps signals that the AI frontier's commercial scaling is playing out faster than even optimistic forecasts predicted, while Anthropic's approximately $19 billion ARR shows that even a company with its flagship model offline is accumulating revenue at a pace that would be considered exceptional for any software company in any prior era.

The Anchor

SpaceX bought Cursor in four days. Not a partnership — the full $60 billion acquisition. That's what it looks like when a newly public company decides it needs enterprise AI coding market share immediately, and has the capital to buy it.

SpaceX went public Friday, June 12, at a record valuation. By Tuesday, June 16, it had committed $60 billion in all-stock consideration to acquire Anysphere — the company behind Cursor, the enterprise AI coding environment that competes directly with Anthropic's Claude Code and OpenAI's Codex. The April option SpaceX had secured gave it flexibility: $10 billion for a partnership, or $60 billion for the whole company. SpaceX chose the $60 billion path on day two of post-IPO trading, with the deal expected to close in Q3 pending regulatory approval. Cursor had built roughly $2.6 billion in ARR when SpaceX secured the April option; by the time SpaceX exercised the acquisition, ARR had grown to approximately $4 billion. That growth rate — and the more than one million paying enterprise users embedded in Cursor's workflow — is what SpaceX paid $60 billion for.

The strategic logic is clear in retrospect: Elon Musk's xAI had trailed Anthropic and OpenAI in enterprise AI coding tools, a segment that is increasingly the highest-value commercial AI deployment for knowledge workers. Buying Cursor gives xAI an immediate, mature distribution channel with enterprise contracts already signed, developer habits already formed around Cursor's interface, and a revenue base that justifies the valuation on standard software multiples. The deal also arrives in a week when Anthropic's flagship models are offline: Fable 5's suspension entered its sixth day Wednesday, with Anthropic dispatching senior engineers to Washington for Commerce Department talks in what is described as a deal-seeking negotiation — but with no confirmed restoration timeline and the government maintaining that "the ball is in Anthropic's court."

SpaceX confirmed Tuesday that it would acquire Anysphere — the company behind Cursor, the enterprise AI coding environment — for $60 billion in an all-stock deal, exercising in full an April option that had given SpaceX the right to either pay $10 billion for a partnership or $60 billion for the company outright. SpaceX made the decision within two trading days of its June 12 IPO, making it among the fastest post-IPO major acquisitions in technology history. Cursor had scaled from approximately $2.6 billion to $4 billion in ARR between April and the deal announcement, with more than one million paying enterprise users embedded in the product. The deal is expected to close in Q3 2026 pending regulatory review.

The strategic case for the $60 billion path over the $10 billion partnership was xAI's need for full ownership of the revenue, the product roadmap, and the enterprise contracts — not just a distribution agreement that Cursor's independent management could modify or terminate. By acquiring Cursor outright, SpaceX-xAI controls the enterprise AI coding revenue stream directly, can integrate it with xAI's broader model infrastructure, and owns the developer relationship that makes Cursor sticky against Anthropic and OpenAI alternatives. For enterprise customers with Cursor contracts, the immediate question is whether xAI's model infrastructure will replace Cursor's current model integrations and what continuity commitments xAI will make during the transition period.

Anthropic's Fable 5 and Mythos 5 remained suspended Wednesday, entering their sixth day offline, as the company dispatched senior engineers to Washington for in-person talks with Commerce Department officials. The talks are framed by reporting as a deal-seeking negotiation, with The New Stack characterizing the government's posture as "the ball is in Anthropic's court." A rumor that circulated Tuesday suggesting a 48-hour restoration window was not confirmed by Anthropic or government officials. No restoration timeline has been announced, and the June 12 export control directive that forced the recall remains in effect.

The engineering delegation to Washington is a shift in posture from Anthropic's approach after Monday's inconclusive meeting between senior leadership and White House officials. Dispatching engineers — rather than executives — to Commerce Department talks suggests the conversations have moved from policy posture to technical remediation specifics: what the government's security concern actually requires to be addressed at the model level, and whether a technical fix is achievable on a timeline that makes continued suspension unnecessary. Whether the Commerce talks produce a defined remediation path or conclude inconclusively as Monday's White House talks did will determine whether Fable 5's restoration timeline is days or weeks. The SpaceX-Cursor deal, announced on day five of Fable 5's suspension, underscores how much the enterprise AI coding competitive landscape can shift while Anthropic's flagship is offline.

OpenAI has surpassed $25 billion in annualized revenue and is taking early steps toward an initial public offering, according to reporting from Crescendo AI and multiple industry sources. The milestone makes OpenAI the fastest software company to reach $25 billion in ARR, achieved in roughly two years from the mass-market launch of ChatGPT. Anthropic is approaching $19 billion in ARR — a figure that reflects sustained growth across the Claude model family, with Claude Opus 4.8 and Sonnet 4.6 remaining fully accessible through the Fable 5 suspension and anchoring enterprise contracts that include model-family commitments rather than single-model dependencies.

The OpenAI-Anthropic ARR gap — $25 billion versus $19 billion — reflects OpenAI's earlier commercialization, larger consumer subscriber base, and deeper enterprise integrations established before Anthropic built its own enterprise sales motion. Anthropic's revenue trajectory suggests the gap is narrowing, but the Fable 5 suspension compresses the competitive window in which Anthropic can convert Fable 5's superior benchmark performance into enterprise revenue before OpenAI and xAI (via Cursor) consolidate their positions. OpenAI's IPO preparatory steps are the financial story beneath the headline: a public listing at $25 billion ARR would imply a valuation in the hundreds of billions of dollars at standard software multiples, creating a public market data point for frontier AI company valuations that does not currently exist.

Quick hits

  • The SpaceX-Cursor deal's all-stock structure is as strategically significant as the price. SpaceX used its newly public shares — priced at a record IPO valuation — as the acquisition currency, preserving cash while converting the IPO's capital event into immediate competitive capability in the enterprise AI market. Cursor's shareholders (VCs, employees, founders) receive SpaceX stock that was not publicly tradeable until last Friday, creating a potential lock-up dynamic where Cursor's equity holders are now SpaceX shareholders with an incentive to support the combined company's post-acquisition execution. The all-stock structure also means SpaceX's enterprise AI bet is priced at its current public market valuation — the same valuation it established at IPO — rather than requiring future cash outlays that would constrain capital allocation in the aerospace and satellite divisions.
  • Fable 5's sixth day offline has now produced a measurable competitive consequence: SpaceX announced the Cursor acquisition while Anthropic's flagship model and its enterprise AI coding product were unavailable. Enterprise procurement teams evaluating AI coding tools in June have been making decisions without being able to test or deploy Fable 5, which was the highest-benchmarked AI coding model before its suspension. Some of those procurement decisions — particularly for contracts closing before Q3 budgets lock — will have already converted to OpenAI Codex or Cursor commitments before Fable 5 is restored. The longer the suspension continues, the more of the June-July enterprise sales cycle becomes irreversible without Anthropic's participation.
  • Gemini 3.5 Flash is now broadly available across Google's products and APIs, with Gemini 3.5 Pro currently in internal testing. The Flash model release arrived without major media coverage in a week dominated by Fable 5, SpaceX-Cursor, and the Iran deal — but for enterprise developers evaluating AI APIs, Gemini 3.5 Flash's speed and pricing position it as a practical option for latency-sensitive applications that neither the best-performance nor the cheapest models serve optimally. Google's strategy of releasing a speed-optimized Flash model before the flagship Pro version is consistent with its 2025 Gemini rollout pattern and reflects an effort to capture developer workflow integrations at the application layer before the full performance flagship arrives.
