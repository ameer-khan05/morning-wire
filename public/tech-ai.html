The Pentagon signs classified AI network deals with eight companies — OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Nvidia, and four others — explicitly cutting out Anthropic over its refusal to remove safety guardrails on autonomous weapons. OpenAI's CFO privately warns $600 billion in compute commitments may outrun revenue.

The Department of Defense finalized AI agreements covering IL6 and IL7 security clearance levels — the top-secret and sensitive compartmented information tiers used for classified military operations — with OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Oracle, Nvidia, SpaceX, and Reflection AI. Anthropic was explicitly excluded after refusing to grant the Pentagon unrestricted access to its Claude models for deployment in fully autonomous weapons systems and mass domestic surveillance programs; the company insisted on safety guardrails that the Trump administration's Pentagon deemed unacceptable for its classified use cases. The Anthropic exclusion is the most operationally significant consequence yet of the company's AI safety commitments, converting a philosophical debate about responsible AI deployment into a concrete contract outcome worth potentially billions in federal AI revenue. OpenAI, which won inclusion in the Pentagon deals, faces a separate financial stress point: CFO Sarah Friar has privately warned leadership — including in board-level discussions — that the company's $600 billion in accumulated compute commitments for data centers and chip contracts may not be supportable by its current revenue trajectory, having missed monthly targets in early 2026 while losing consumer market share to Google's Gemini and enterprise coding market share to Anthropic's Claude. The irony is sharp: Anthropic lost the Pentagon contract over safety guardrails and gained the enterprise revenue that OpenAI is now trying to catch up to.

The Anchor

Pentagon's eight-company AI clearance — Anthropic explicitly excluded — is the most significant federal AI contract action since the White House's executive order on AI governance. The contract boundary is the safety guardrail.

The Pentagon's classified AI agreements represent a specific and consequential federal government decision: the US military's most sensitive computing environments will be built on AI infrastructure from companies that have agreed to unrestricted deployment for autonomous weapons and surveillance use cases. OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Oracle, Nvidia, SpaceX, and Reflection AI — the eight signatories — have accepted the Pentagon's terms, which include classified network integration at IL6 (Secret) and IL7 (Top Secret / Sensitive Compartmented Information) levels. The contracts cover AI deployment for intelligence analysis, logistics optimization, targeting support, and large-scale data processing in environments that require the highest US government security classification. The revenue implication for the eight signatories is substantial: the DoD's AI budget for 2026 is approximately $1.8 billion in dedicated AI program spending, with additional AI expenditure embedded in broader systems acquisition. Nvidia's inclusion — for hardware rather than model deployment — is consistent with its role as the physical infrastructure layer for every company in the deal.

Anthropic's exclusion has a specific cause. The company insisted that its Claude models cannot be used in fully autonomous weapons systems — systems that select and engage targets without human-in-the-loop review — and in mass domestic surveillance programs that monitor US citizens without individualized suspicion. The Pentagon characterized Anthropic's conditions as a "supply-chain risk" — a formal contract term indicating the vendor's terms are incompatible with the government's operational requirements. Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei has confirmed the company's position: Claude will not be deployed in applications that make life-and-death targeting decisions without human oversight. The White House subsequently reopened discussions with Anthropic — reportedly after Anthropic unveiled its "Mythos" cybersecurity threat-identification tool, which demonstrated Claude's value in defensive security contexts without requiring the offensive autonomous weapons capabilities the Pentagon contract requires. Whether that reopened conversation produces an alternative federal AI agreement that accommodates Anthropic's guardrails remains unresolved.

Pentagon · 8 AI Contracts · IL6/IL7 8 Signed OpenAI · Google · Microsoft · Amazon · Oracle · Nvidia · SpaceX · Reflection AI · classified AI · analysis, logistics, targeting · $1.8B DoD AI budget

Pentagon signs IL6/IL7 classified AI agreements with eight companies: OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Oracle, Nvidia, SpaceX, Reflection AI. The contracts cover AI deployment in top-secret environments for intelligence analysis, logistics, and targeting support.

The Department of Defense's classified AI network agreements — signed with eight companies after an extended procurement process — represent the federal government's most consequential AI contracting action since the White House's October 2023 executive order on AI governance. The IL6 (Secret) and IL7 (Top Secret / Sensitive Compartmented Information) classification levels under which these agreements operate are the highest tiers of US government security, reserved for intelligence sources and methods, classified weapons programs, and sensitive military operations. The eight signatories have agreed to deploy AI capabilities within these classification environments: OpenAI and Google for model-based intelligence analysis and natural language processing; Microsoft and Amazon for cloud infrastructure and AI service deployment; Oracle for classified database and analytics systems; Nvidia for computing hardware infrastructure; SpaceX for satellite data processing and edge AI deployment; and Reflection AI — a smaller AI safety-focused company — for specialized reasoning applications. The DoD's AI program budget of approximately $1.8 billion for 2026 represents the direct federal spending associated with these agreements; broader AI integration into weapons systems, autonomous platforms, and intelligence pipelines will add additional spending across the defense appropriations structure. The contracts establish a formal divide in the US AI industry: companies that have accepted Pentagon classified terms versus those that have not.

Anthropic explicitly excluded from Pentagon AI contracts — the company refused to remove safety guardrails prohibiting Claude from autonomous weapons and mass surveillance. The Pentagon classified the refusal as a "supply-chain risk." White House reopens discussions after Mythos cybersecurity tool announcement.

Anthropic's exclusion from the Pentagon's classified AI agreements is the most operationally significant consequence of the company's constitutional AI commitments to date. The specific conflict: Anthropic maintains that its Claude models cannot be deployed in fully autonomous weapons systems — those that select and engage targets without human-in-the-loop oversight — and in mass domestic surveillance programs that monitor US citizens without individualized legal process. The Pentagon characterized these conditions as a "supply-chain risk" — the formal contracting term for a vendor whose terms are structurally incompatible with the government's operational requirements — and excluded Anthropic from the eight-company agreement. The exclusion carries a direct revenue cost: the DoD's $1.8 billion AI program budget, and broader classified defense AI spending, is now flowing to Anthropic's competitors. The company's $30 billion in annual recurring revenue — built predominantly on enterprise and developer contracts — makes the Pentagon exclusion manageable commercially but not strategically neutral. The White House's decision to reopen Anthropic discussions after Amodei's visit and the Mythos cybersecurity tool announcement suggests the administration sees value in Anthropic's capabilities for defensive and non-lethal applications that don't require removing the guardrails the company has declined to remove for offensive and surveillance uses.

OpenAI CFO Sarah Friar privately warns the company's $600 billion in compute commitments may outrun revenue — as OpenAI misses monthly targets in early 2026, loses consumer ground to Gemini, and loses enterprise coding ground to Anthropic. Sam Altman and Friar disagree on IPO timing.

OpenAI's internal financial situation is under acute stress that its public communications — which emphasize a "vertical wall of demand" — have not reflected. CFO Sarah Friar has warned leadership in board-level discussions that the company has accumulated approximately $600 billion in future compute spending commitments — through multi-year data center contracts and chip procurement agreements signed in anticipation of rapid revenue growth — whose trajectory may not be supported by OpenAI's current revenue rate. The specific concern: OpenAI missed its goal of one billion weekly ChatGPT active users and has missed multiple monthly revenue targets in early 2026, in a market where Google's Gemini has been gaining consumer share in the conversational AI market and Anthropic's Claude has been gaining enterprise coding and developer share — the two highest-value OpenAI market segments. The compute commitment risk is not purely financial; OpenAI's $600 billion in committed spending creates a structural dependency on large compute contracts that limits the company's operational flexibility. The IPO timing dispute — Sam Altman wants a 2026 public listing; Friar argues 2027 is more prudent given the revenue trajectory and compute cost pressure — is the strategic expression of the same disagreement: Altman believes aggressive growth will resolve the revenue gap; Friar believes the gap must be closed before the company can credibly go public at a valuation that justifies the compute investment.

Apple's $1 billion-per-year deal with Google — a custom 1.2 trillion-parameter Gemini model powering Siri and Apple Intelligence — approaches its first major public reveal at WWDC on June 8. The partnership rebuilds Siri's conversational stack on Google infrastructure.

Apple's January 12 announcement of a multi-year AI partnership with Google — valued by Bloomberg's Mark Gurman at approximately $1 billion per year, with a total multi-year value of up to $5 billion — is approaching its first major consumer-facing reveal: WWDC 2026, on June 8, where iOS 27 will demonstrate the redesigned Siri built on Google's Gemini foundation. The technical architecture of the partnership involves a custom Gemini model built specifically for Apple — at 1.2 trillion parameters, eight times larger than Apple's existing cloud AI models, using a mixture-of-experts architecture optimized for summarization, planning, and natural language understanding. Apple has rebuilt Siri's entire conversational stack on Gemini; the redesigned Siri in iOS 27 will function as a full chatbot with web search, image generation, content summarization, coding assistance, file analysis, and multi-step command execution capabilities. Apple's solution to the privacy constraint: its Private Cloud Compute framework, which processes AI queries in isolated, stateless compute nodes designed to prevent data retention or model training on user queries. The Apple-Google deal represents Google's most significant external distribution win in years — embedding Gemini into the 2.2 billion active Apple device installed base — and represents Apple's clearest acknowledgment that building frontier AI models independently is not a viable near-term strategy.

Continuing: the Pentagon AI contract divide is now the most concrete expression of the AI safety debate in commercial terms — eight companies accepted unrestricted defense deployment; one drew a line and lost the contract. The contract is the policy.

The AI safety debate that has played out in conference rooms, congressional testimony, and academic papers since 2023 now has its most operationally concrete expression: a federal contract that the US military extended to eight AI companies and withheld from one, based entirely on the question of whether that company's AI can be used in fully autonomous weapons systems without human oversight. The eight signatories — OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Oracle, Nvidia, SpaceX, Reflection AI — have not publicly addressed what specifically they committed to allowing; the classified nature of the agreements means the terms are not in the public domain. Anthropic has publicly stated what it refused: autonomous weapons deployment and mass domestic surveillance without legal process. The asymmetry reveals a structure that the AI policy debate had not previously made visible: companies that accepted defense AI terms gained billions in classified revenue access; the company that held its safety line lost the contract and preserved its principles. The question the industry will now answer through behavior over the coming months: did Anthropic lose a revenue stream or establish a brand distinction that will prove more valuable with enterprise customers who care specifically about AI governance and accountability? The White House's reopened Anthropic conversations suggest the administration itself is not certain the exclusion was the right long-term outcome.

Quick hits

  • The Pentagon's "supply-chain risk" designation for Anthropic is a formal contracting term with specific legal and procurement implications. It means that future Pentagon AI contracts — beyond the current eight-company IL6/IL7 agreements — may categorically exclude Anthropic until the designation is removed. The White House's reopened discussions are significant precisely because they represent the political pathway to removing that designation: if a new federal agreement can be structured that accommodates Anthropic's safety guardrails for specific non-autonomous, non-surveillance use cases, the supply-chain risk label becomes contestable. Anthropic's Mythos cybersecurity tool — which identifies cyber threats in classified network environments without requiring autonomous targeting capabilities — is the specific product that the White House found compelling enough to restart the conversation.
  • OpenAI's IPO timing dispute between Sam Altman (2026) and CFO Sarah Friar (2027) reflects a structural disagreement about which problem is more urgent: the revenue gap that is underfunding the $600 billion compute commitments, or the capital market access that a 2026 IPO would provide. Altman's argument for 2026: the AI market is at peak investor appetite; a 2026 IPO at peak valuation raises the capital that can bridge the revenue gap. Friar's argument for 2027: going public with a revenue-to-commitment mismatch visible in the S-1 creates a valuation discount and shareholder pressure to cut compute spending that would undermine OpenAI's long-term competitive position. The dispute is fundamentally about whether OpenAI's path to financial stability runs through the public market or through revenue growth — and which arrives first.
  • Google's Alphabet gained significantly from Monday's tech sector trading, with GOOGL rising 1.87% to $243.50, outperforming most of its megacap peers on a day when the S&P fell 0.41%. The Apple-Google Gemini deal — which places Google's Gemini model into the 2.2 billion active Apple device installed base at $1 billion per year — is increasingly being priced as a structural advantage that Alphabet has secured in the AI distribution race. For Google, the Apple deal is the equivalent of securing the default search engine position on Apple devices in 2007 — a high-value, high-margin distribution agreement that generates revenue without requiring consumer-facing marketing. The WWDC June 8 reveal is the first consumer-visible milestone for the partnership, and its reception will determine how aggressively institutional investors reprice the Apple-Google AI relationship.
  • Microsoft's 0.92% decline on Monday — despite its inclusion in the Pentagon classified AI agreements — reflects the continued absorption of the OpenAI Amazon pivot story from last week. The Pentagon contracts benefit Microsoft through Azure's role as the primary cloud infrastructure for most of the eight signatories, but the market is weighing that benefit against the OpenAI revenue risk: if OpenAI's financial pressures force it to restructure the Azure-OpenAI service agreement, Microsoft's AI revenue and Azure growth rate face a specific headwind. The classified Pentagon contracts — where OpenAI deploys on government cloud infrastructure — may partially offset that risk by deepening OpenAI's dependence on Microsoft's cleared cloud capacity (IL6/IL7 environments typically run on classified Azure, Azure Government Secret, or AWS GovCloud, all of which are Microsoft or Amazon infrastructure).
  • Nvidia's inclusion in the Pentagon classified AI agreements — and its 0.94% gain on Monday, bucking the broader market decline — reflects the market's clear-eyed reading of Nvidia's role in the AI stack. Nvidia is not a model provider or application company; it is the physical compute layer that every AI system, classified or not, requires. The Pentagon's IL6/IL7 classified networks require high-performance GPU clusters that Nvidia is uniquely positioned to supply; its inclusion in the eight-company agreement is less a competitive win than a physical necessity acknowledgment. The broader implication: Nvidia's revenue is structurally less exposed to the AI safety debate than model companies, because the GPU is agnostic to the model's ethical framework — it processes tokens regardless of whether the system running them has autonomous weapons guardrails.
  • The Reflection AI inclusion in the Pentagon's eight-company AI agreement is the most notable lesser-known item in the list. Reflection AI is a smaller AI safety-focused company whose specific capabilities in formal reasoning and verifiable AI outputs appear to have attracted Pentagon interest for classified applications where AI decisions need to be auditable and formally verifiable — a requirement that large general-purpose language models from OpenAI or Google are structurally less well-suited to satisfy. The inclusion of a safety-focused AI company alongside OpenAI and Google — while excluding Anthropic, also safety-focused — suggests the Pentagon's AI safety calculus is not simply "safety-focused companies are excluded" but rather "companies that impose safety constraints that conflict with operational requirements are excluded." Reflection AI's safety approach is apparently compatible with Pentagon requirements in a way that Anthropic's autonomous weapons guardrails are not.
