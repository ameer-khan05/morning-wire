The Anchor

China orders Meta to unwind its $2B Manus acquisition — Beijing's first retroactive block of a finalized US tech deal.

China's National Development and Reform Commission — the country's most powerful economic planning body — issued a one-line notice Monday ordering Meta to cancel its acquisition of Manus, the agentic AI startup founded by a Chinese team and acquired by Meta for approximately $2 billion in December 2025. The NDRC stated it was "prohibiting foreign investment in the startup in accordance with laws and regulations" without elaborating further. The order came four months after Meta sealed the deal and had begun integrating Manus's agent technology into Meta AI. It represents Beijing's most direct assertion of regulatory authority over a finalized US tech acquisition — using foreign investment law not to block a pending deal, but to retroactively unwind one already completed.

The Manus block has immediate implications well beyond Meta. Manus was co-founded by Chinese nationals and had its technical origins in China, which made the deal politically sensitive from the start — China's Ministry of Commerce announced a review in January, citing export-control and technology-transfer concerns. The NDRC's decision to invoke those concerns to unwind a completed transaction tests the outer boundary of Beijing's extraterritorial regulatory reach: the deal was conducted under US law, by a US public company, for a company registered internationally. Meta told CNN the acquisition "complied fully with applicable law" and that it "anticipates an appropriate resolution to the inquiry." Legal analysts note the order creates an immediate tension with WTO rules on foreign investment treatment, and that Meta faces the choice of complying — unwinding the deal and writing off the acquisition — or challenging Beijing in a legal and regulatory environment where US companies have historically had limited recourse. For AI companies with China-founded teams anywhere in their portfolios, the NDRC's action is a new risk factor with no clear precedent.

China · NDRC · Meta · Manus Unwound $2B deal blocked · 4 months post-close · tech war escalation

China's NDRC orders Meta to cancel Manus acquisition — retroactive block raises stakes for all US-China tech M&A.

The NDRC's one-line order to unwind Meta's Manus acquisition is significant not just for its outcome but for its method. Prior Chinese regulatory interventions in US tech M&A — including reviews of Qualcomm's NXP bid, Nvidia's Arm bid, and Intel's Tower deal — were all decided during the review period, before transactions closed. The Manus block is different: the deal closed, integration began, and Beijing then reversed it four months later. That retroactive posture changes the risk calculus for every US tech company considering M&A involving China-founded teams. Bloomberg reported that China is testing the limits of its regulatory reach via the Xi administration's broader technology-security framework — the same framework that blocked ASML equipment exports and restricted rare earth processing in early 2026. Meta's response will be closely watched: if it complies without legal challenge, it sets a precedent that Beijing can unwind deals after the fact. If it challenges the order, it risks its ability to operate in China at all.

OpenAI · Anthropic · ARR Race $30B Anthropic surpasses OpenAI · $25B vs $30B · growth rate divergence

OpenAI tops $25B ARR — but Anthropic surpasses it at $30B, the first time a rival has publicly exceeded OpenAI's revenue run rate.

The Information reported Monday that OpenAI has crossed $25 billion in annualized revenue — up from $20 billion at end-2025 and $6 billion in 2024, a 3× year-over-year growth rate. OpenAI CFO Sarah Friar confirmed the $20 billion 2025 figure in January; the new $25 billion figure reflects continued enterprise growth, with enterprise now comprising more than 40% of revenue and on track for parity with consumer by year-end. But the milestone arrived alongside a more striking data point: Anthropic publicly disclosed that its annualized revenue run rate crossed $30 billion in April 2026 — surpassing OpenAI, the company most synonymous with the AI revolution, for the first time. Anthropic's growth rate is steeper than OpenAI's, driven by enterprise API adoption following Google's $40 billion investment and the Microsoft-OpenAI multicloud restructuring that made Anthropic's models more accessible to Azure-adjacent enterprise accounts. For OpenAI, the revenue ranking reversal arrives at a sensitive moment — the company is reportedly taking early steps toward a public listing as soon as late 2026, and a second-place revenue position could affect valuation framing.

Google · $750M · AI Agents $750M AI agent fund · 8th-gen TPUs · cloud credits · Gemini Enterprise

Google unveils $750M AI agent fund and 8th-generation TPUs — accelerating its infrastructure edge as inference dominates AI compute.

Google announced a $750 million fund Monday to support startups and partners building AI agents — including cloud credits, engineering support, and distribution through Gemini Enterprise and Google Cloud Marketplace. The fund is a direct competitive response to Microsoft's enterprise AI ecosystem (through OpenAI) and Amazon's Bedrock agent platform: Google is subsidizing the developer surface area it needs to make Google Cloud the default AI agent infrastructure layer. Simultaneously, Google Cloud rolled out its eighth-generation Tensor Processing Units (TPUs), the latest generation of the custom inference chips that now account for the majority of AI compute spending in live production deployments. The new TPUs drive down per-query cost and power consumption at the inference layer — the cost metric that determines enterprise margin at scale, as inference has overtaken training as the dominant AI infrastructure spend. Google's $40 billion Anthropic investment and now the $750 million agent fund represent a two-pronged bet: own the frontier model relationship (Anthropic) and own the application ecosystem (agent fund).

NASA · Mars · Claude · AI 456m Perseverance · first AI-planned drives · Anthropic Claude · 2 drives completed

NASA Perseverance completes first AI-planned Mars drives using Anthropic's Claude — 456 meters covered across two autonomous routes.

NASA's Perseverance rover completed the first Mars drives ever planned by artificial intelligence — using Anthropic's Claude vision-language models to analyze terrain imagery and generate route plans, covering 456 meters across two drives. The achievement represents a fundamental shift in Mars surface operations: previously, every rover drive required human engineers at JPL to manually review imagery, plot a safe path, and upload commands — a process that could take days. Claude-planned drives reduce that cycle to hours, allowing Perseverance to cover more ground per operational period and respond more quickly to time-sensitive geological targets like fresh impact craters or seasonal surface changes. The AI planner was constrained to routes that human engineers later reviewed and approved — it did not operate fully autonomously — but the speed improvement over manual planning is already significant. For Anthropic, the NASA deployment is the most high-profile non-commercial application of Claude to date and a direct counter to the perception that large language models are primarily text generation tools: here, the model is making spatial navigation decisions on another planet.
