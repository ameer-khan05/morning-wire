Anthropic crosses $30B ARR to unseat OpenAI at the top of the global LLM revenue table — the first time any AI company has led it. The Pentagon then signs classified AI deals with seven companies and explicitly keeps Anthropic out.

Anthropic officially passed OpenAI in annualized revenue in April 2026, reaching $30 billion against OpenAI's $25 billion — the first time any competitor has led the global LLM revenue table. Counterpoint Research's Q1 2026 data gives Anthropic 31.4% of LLM market share to OpenAI's 29%, powered by a dramatically higher per-user revenue rate: $16.20 per monthly active user versus OpenAI's $2.20, from 134 million users against OpenAI's 900 million. The metric reveals two different business models — Anthropic's premium-enterprise approach generating far more revenue per customer, OpenAI's mass-market scale generating far more users — and the former has just crossed the latter for the first time. Simultaneously, the Department of Defense announced classified AI deployment agreements with eight companies — OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Amazon, NVIDIA, SpaceX, Oracle, and Reflection — while explicitly keeping Anthropic blacklisted as a "supply chain risk," citing its dispute over how the Pentagon can use its Mythos model. The White House is reportedly drafting an executive action to potentially resolve the Anthropic-Pentagon dispute.

The Anchor

Anthropic $30B ARR — first AI company to surpass OpenAI in the global LLM revenue table. 31.4% market share, $16.20 per user, 30x growth in 15 months.

Anthropic's crossing of $30 billion in annualized revenue in April 2026 is a milestone that the AI industry did not expect to arrive this quickly. When Anthropic launched Claude 2 in mid-2023, the company had effectively no commercial revenue; OpenAI had a multi-year head start, a consumer product — ChatGPT — with hundreds of millions of users, and a structural partnership with Microsoft that embedded its models into the world's most widely used enterprise productivity suite. Fifteen months of sustained enterprise adoption later, Anthropic's revenue has grown approximately 30-fold and has crossed OpenAI's. Counterpoint Research's Q1 2026 data, reported by The Register, gives Anthropic 31.4% of global LLM market revenue share against OpenAI's 29% — the first quarter in which a competitor has led the table. The structural explanation is in the per-user revenue figures: Anthropic generates $16.20 in average monthly revenue per monthly active user, versus OpenAI's $2.20 — a ratio of 7.4:1 — from 134 million monthly active users versus OpenAI's 900 million. Anthropic has carved out the premium-enterprise tier of the LLM market, where customers are paying for outputs and reliability in high-stakes professional workflows. OpenAI has the mass-market tier, where ChatGPT's 900 million monthly active users generate consumer subscriptions and lighter enterprise usage. The premium-enterprise tier has just generated more total revenue, for the first time.

The business model implication goes beyond a single data point. Anthropic's revenue structure — high value per customer, enterprise contractual commitments, API-heavy usage patterns — is significantly more durable than OpenAI's consumer-heavy model under macro pressure. Enterprise customers do not cancel AI contracts the way consumers cancel subscriptions when they tighten discretionary spending; they embed AI into workflows and renew at the same or higher rates. The premium-enterprise model also carries a higher margin profile: Anthropic's inference costs per unit of output are lower relative to the revenue per output than OpenAI's mass-market pricing, which faces permanent pressure to lower API costs to maintain competitive positioning against open-source models. SaaStr noted that Anthropic's 30x revenue growth in 15 months occurred while spending approximately four times less on model training than OpenAI — a capital efficiency metric that will become increasingly relevant as both companies approach their next financing rounds and eventual public offerings. OpenAI is separately taking early steps toward a public listing, potentially as soon as late 2026, with CFO Sarah Friar navigating an internal revenue-growth pressure environment. OpenAI's $25 billion ARR is not a failure — it is the second-highest LLM revenue figure in history; the top is now held by a competitor that was not expected to lead this race.

Anthropic · $30B · Leads LLM Table $30B 31.4% LLM share · OpenAI 29% · $16.20 vs $2.20 per user · 30x growth in 15 months · enterprise model wins

Anthropic's $30B ARR crosses OpenAI's $25B — the premium-enterprise model just outpaced the mass-market model in total LLM revenue, 15 months after Anthropic had effectively zero commercial revenue.

The speed of Anthropic's $30 billion ARR milestone — reached in approximately 15 months from near-zero commercial revenue in mid-2023 — is the most significant single business trajectory in the AI industry since OpenAI's original ChatGPT moment. The growth driver is structural: Anthropic's decision to price at the premium-enterprise tier, invest in safety research that enterprise compliance teams require, and build Claude specifically for professional workflows (law, medicine, finance, software development) rather than general consumer use created a customer base that is smaller, more contractually committed, and far higher-value per seat. The Counterpoint Q1 2026 data showing Anthropic at 31.4% LLM revenue share and OpenAI at 29% is the aggregate measure of that choice playing out at scale. The $16.20 versus $2.20 per-user revenue ratio is its expression at the unit level: every Anthropic customer generates more revenue than every OpenAI user by a factor of 7.4. The SaaStr analysis adds a capital efficiency dimension that is equally striking: Anthropic achieved its 30x revenue growth while spending approximately four times less on model training than OpenAI, suggesting a company that is generating more revenue with less compute cost — the inverse of the burn-rate-heavy expansion model that has characterized OpenAI's trajectory. OpenAI remains a massive business with 900 million monthly active users and $25 billion in annualized revenue; it is not in distress. It has simply been surpassed, unexpectedly quickly, by a company that chose a different lane.

Pentagon · AI Deals · Anthropic Blacklisted 7+1 Cos OpenAI · Google · MSFT · Amazon · NVDA · SpaceX · Oracle · Reflection in · Anthropic out · IL-6/IL-7 networks

Pentagon signs classified AI deployment agreements with 8 companies — and explicitly keeps Anthropic blacklisted as a "supply chain risk." The White House is drafting an executive action to resolve the dispute.

The Department of Defense announced classified AI deployment agreements with OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Amazon Web Services, NVIDIA, SpaceX, Oracle, and Reflection — a startup backed by NVIDIA — for deployment on the Pentagon's Impact Level 6 and Impact Level 7 classified networks, which handle the military's most sensitive data. The agreements, announced Friday, are designed to enable AI-driven data synthesis, enhanced warfighter decision-making, and situational awareness at the classified level. Anthropic, which was the first AI company to deploy models on the Pentagon's classified systems and was subsequently designated a "supply chain risk" by Defense Secretary Hegseth following a dispute over how the military can use the company's Mythos model, is explicitly excluded. The Pentagon tech chief confirmed Friday that Anthropic "is still blacklisted" under the Mythos-related designation. However, the White House is separately drafting an executive action that could create a pathway for Anthropic to re-enter the government AI market — reflecting both the administration's recognition that Anthropic's models are "too powerful to ignore" and the political pressure from Anthropic's $60 billion valuation and enterprise customer base, which includes many government-adjacent firms. No timeline for the executive action has been announced. The simultaneous exclusion of Anthropic from the Pentagon's agreements and the drafting of an executive action to potentially re-include it is itself a signal: the administration wants the leverage of the exclusion threat while maintaining access to the capability. Whether those two goals can coexist long-term without resolution is the operational question.

OpenAI · $25B ARR · IPO Steps IPO? $25B ARR · early IPO steps · potentially late 2026 · CFO Friar on revenue pressure · missed internal targets

OpenAI at $25B ARR is taking early steps toward a public listing — potentially late 2026 — while CFO Sarah Friar navigates internal revenue-growth pressure and a newly occupied second-place position in the LLM revenue table.

OpenAI's $25 billion in annualized revenue — the figure that places it second behind Anthropic in the Q1 2026 LLM revenue ranking — represents extraordinary growth in absolute terms: two years ago it had under $2 billion in annualized revenue. But the context of the moment matters: OpenAI is taking early steps toward a public listing that could come as soon as late 2026, according to reporting by Decrypt and Yahoo Finance, while simultaneously facing internal pressure from CFO Sarah Friar, who has reportedly told company leaders she is concerned that OpenAI might not be able to fund future compute contracts if revenue growth does not accelerate. The concern reflects the fundamental tension in OpenAI's business model: $25 billion in revenue sounds large, but against $20-plus billion in annual model training costs and the infrastructure commitments that underpin its Microsoft partnership, the free-cash-flow picture is more constrained than the top-line figure suggests. The Anthropic revenue reversal adds a competitive dimension to the IPO calculus: a company that goes public while in second place in its core market metric faces a different valuation narrative than one that goes public as the clear market leader. OpenAI's IPO preparation will need to address the Anthropic competitive threat directly in its S-1 narrative, and the per-user revenue gap — $2.20 versus $16.20 — is the figure that any sophisticated investor will focus on when evaluating OpenAI's enterprise pricing power.

Novo Nordisk · OpenAI · Drug Discovery Continuing Pilot programs launching · R&D · manufacturing · commercial · full integration by end-2026 · Eli Lilly/Insilico parallel

Continuing: Novo Nordisk-OpenAI partnership pilots launch across R&D, manufacturing, and commercial operations — full integration targeted by end-2026. Eli Lilly's Insilico Medicine deal running in parallel.

Novo Nordisk's partnership with OpenAI — announced April 14 — is entering its pilot program phase, with launches across the Danish drugmaker's research and development, manufacturing, supply chain, and commercial operations now underway. The partnership's stated goal: use AI to analyze complex biological and clinical datasets at a scale previously impossible, identify promising drug candidates earlier in the development cycle, and compress the timeline from discovery to patient use. OpenAI will also assist Novo in upskilling its global workforce across AI literacy. Novo CEO Mike Doustdar, citing the millions of people living with obesity and diabetes who need treatment options, framed the partnership as a response to the fundamental data scale challenge in pharma: the datasets that produce drug leads — genomics, proteomics, clinical trial outcomes — are too large for conventional analysis. Full integration across all Novo functions is targeted by the end of 2026. Running in parallel: Eli Lilly announced in March a partnership with Insilico Medicine to develop and commercialize drugs discovered using AI — a direct competitive response to Novo's OpenAI deal and a signal that the major GLP-1 competitors are converging on AI-driven drug discovery as a durable competitive strategy, not a marketing positioning.

Quick hits

  • The Anthropic revenue reversal has a specific implication for the Anthropic-Pentagon dispute that makes the White House's executive action drafting more urgent: a company that has just crossed $30 billion in annualized revenue and leads the global LLM revenue table is a company with enough enterprise leverage to make its exclusion from Pentagon contracts increasingly costly in terms of forgone capability. The Pentagon's other AI providers — including OpenAI, which is second — now have a competitor that generates more revenue per customer than they do; over time, that per-customer value metric translates into model quality investment. The administration's "supply chain risk" designation, applied when Anthropic was smaller, becomes harder to sustain as Anthropic's revenue scale grows.
  • The $16.20 versus $2.20 per-user revenue comparison requires context about what generates it. Anthropic's enterprise API customers — law firms, investment banks, healthcare systems, enterprise software vendors — are accessing Claude through API calls at professional workflow rates, often under multi-year enterprise agreements. OpenAI's $2.20 average reflects a massive base of ChatGPT Plus subscribers ($20/month) plus free-tier users who generate zero revenue, plus enterprise customers who are typically smaller or less intensive users than Anthropic's enterprise base. The denominator (total MAU including free users) suppresses OpenAI's true enterprise per-seat revenue. But even on an apples-to-apples enterprise comparison, Anthropic's pricing is at a premium — a deliberate positioning decision that has now proven commercially correct at the revenue level.
  • The Pentagon's choice to include Reflection — a NVIDIA-backed startup — alongside OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, and Amazon in its classified AI agreements is a signal worth noting. Reflection's inclusion, alongside SpaceX, reflects the Pentagon's deliberate strategy to diversify its AI supply chain beyond the hyperscalers and OpenAI — precisely the kind of supply chain diversification that makes the "supply chain risk" designation of Anthropic more pointed. The Pentagon is building redundancy into its AI vendor base; Anthropic's exclusion is therefore not just a political statement but a structural supply-chain decision that will take significant policy change to reverse.
  • Novo Nordisk's OpenAI partnership and Eli Lilly's Insilico Medicine deal arriving within weeks of each other represent the pharmaceutical industry's collective acknowledgment that AI-driven drug discovery is not a future capability but a present competitive differentiator. The specific mechanism — AI analysis of biological datasets too large for conventional human analysis — addresses the industry's most expensive bottleneck: the 10-15 year drug development timeline that produces the $2-3 billion average cost per approved drug. If AI can compress any significant portion of the pre-clinical and clinical trial phases, the first movers in AI-pharma partnerships will have a structural cost advantage in their development pipelines that compounds with each drug candidate cycle. Novo and Lilly are not announcing press releases; they are restructuring their fundamental competitive position.
  • The $725 billion Big Tech AI capex figure for 2026 has an embedded arithmetic about Nvidia's revenue: if Big Tech spends $725 billion on AI infrastructure and Nvidia captures approximately 35-40% of that as GPU and related silicon revenue, Nvidia's 2026 AI-infrastructure revenue from hyperscalers alone approaches $250-290 billion. This is before sovereign AI programs (Saudi, UAE, India, EU), enterprise AI clusters, and the Chinese domestic market (which Huawei's Ascend is addressing). The total addressable market for AI silicon in 2026 is orders of magnitude larger than the semiconductor industry's total TAM three years ago — and the capex cycle shows no deceleration signals in the guidance language of any of the four major hyperscalers.
  • Google's Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite — released this week with 2.5x faster response times, 45% faster output generation, and a $0.25 per million input token price point — is Google's competitive response to OpenAI's GPT-4o Mini pricing in the high-throughput, cost-sensitive inference market. Paid monthly active users of Gemini Enterprise grew 40% quarter over quarter in Q1 2026, the fastest enterprise adoption metric Google has disclosed for any AI product. Flash-Lite's efficiency positioning — designed for applications requiring low-latency, high-volume inference at minimal cost — directly addresses the long-tail of AI developer demand that is not served by premium models like Claude Opus or GPT-4o. Google's Cloud +63% in Q1 is the financial expression of how much of this developer demand is flowing through its infrastructure.
