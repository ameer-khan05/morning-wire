Alphabet and Tesla report Q2 2026 earnings after market close tonight — Alphabet carrying $190 billion in 2026 AI capital expenditure and Google Cloud AI revenue up 63% to $20 billion in Q1 setting a high bar, Tesla with a record 480,000 Q2 deliveries up 25% year-over-year and the question shifting from volume to profitability; the July AI model wave crested Tuesday with Google releasing Gemini 3.6 Flash, joining Anthropic's Claude Sonnet 5, OpenAI's GPT-5.6, and SpaceXAI's Grok 4.5 in the most concentrated multi-lab model-release period in the industry's history; and Google Play opened US rival app marketplaces Wednesday for the first time, the first major compliance action from Epic Games' 2024 antitrust ruling.

Tonight's Alphabet earnings are the AI investment thesis's most consequential readout of the earnings season. The question is not whether Alphabet is spending on AI — $190 billion in capex is the answer to that — but whether Cloud AI revenue is growing at a rate that justifies the commitment. Q1's 63% growth to $20 billion set a high baseline. A Q2 at 60%+ confirms the trajectory; deceleration below 50% shifts the narrative from "capex generating revenue" to "capex ahead of revenue." Those two framings produce very different Nasdaq trajectories for the remainder of 2026. Tesla's earnings run parallel: record deliveries confirm demand, but the margin structure after discounting and the Optimus commercial revenue timeline are what investors are pricing in the forward multiple.

The Alphabet earnings preview context is unusually precise. Google Cloud AI grew 63% to $20 billion in Q1 with operating margin expanding to a record 32.9% from 17.8% a year earlier — meaning the business is both growing fast and growing profitably. Tesla's situation is the inverse: 480,000 deliveries is a strong number, but analysts expect revenue of only $26.4 billion — roughly 3% below last year despite record deliveries — because per-vehicle revenue declined during discounting campaigns. The AI model wave context matters for how investors read the Alphabet number. This is the competitive and collaborative context in which Alphabet's Cloud AI revenue is being generated — enterprises are choosing between models and cloud providers simultaneously. The fact that Google is both releasing models (Gemini 3.6 Flash) and generating Cloud AI revenue (Google Cloud AI Platform) from enterprise model deployment makes Alphabet's Q2 a readout on both layers of the AI economy.

Google Play Opens Rival App Marketplaces in US (Jul 22) — Epic Antitrust Compliance · First Time Competitors Eligible via Play Distribution · AI Lobbying Q2: Anthropic $1.97M ▲26%, OpenAI $1.2M ▲18%, Meta $5.99M ▼15% · CLARITY + EU AI Act in Focus Rivals Google Play: rival stores eligible starting Jul 22 · Epic Games court order (2024) · AI lobbying Q2 2026: Meta $5.99M (▼15%, largest) · Anthropic $1.97M (▲26%) · OpenAI $1.2M (▲18%) · context: CLARITY Act, EU AI Act implementation, FTC model market investigation · Anthropic + OpenAI lobbying spend accelerating

Google Play began allowing rival app marketplaces to seek distribution through its platform in the US on July 22 — the first compliance action from Epic Games' 2024 antitrust ruling that found Google unlawfully maintained monopoly control over Android app distribution. Q2 2026 federal lobbying disclosures meanwhile show Anthropic spending $1.97 million (▲26% quarter-over-quarter) and OpenAI spending $1.2 million (▲18%), with Meta leading the sector at $5.99 million despite a 15% quarterly decline.

Google Play's opening to rival marketplaces is a structural change to mobile app distribution economics. The 30% Play Store fee — which applied to in-app purchases and subscriptions across Android — is the leverage that antitrust regulators and developers challenged. Rival stores operating on Android reduce the effective fee floor by creating competitive pressure, though Google retains significant advantages in discoverability and default device installation. The AI lobbying acceleration at Anthropic and OpenAI reflects a specific legislative calendar: CLARITY Act floor vote before August 7, EU AI Act implementation rulemaking proceeding simultaneously, and the FTC's informal investigation into AI model market concentration. Both companies are spending at higher rates than Q1, suggesting the legislative and regulatory calendar is accelerating spending decisions.

Quick hits

  • Tesla's Optimus commercial deployment status is the forward-looking number that matters most for Tesla's long-run multiple. Current vehicle revenue and FSD subscriptions are the near-term earnings drivers; Optimus robot commercial deployments — Tesla's stated goal of internal deployment in its own factories first, then external customer sales — is the business that would justify a technology company multiple rather than an automotive company multiple. Any update on Optimus headcount, internal deployment status, or first external customer commitments would move Tesla's multiple independently of the Q2 EPS number. The EPS number determines whether Tesla beats Q2; the Optimus number determines whether Tesla's forward multiple is defensible at current price levels.
  • Anthropic's 26% quarter-over-quarter lobbying increase is the most significant lobbying data point for AI because Anthropic is structurally different from OpenAI and Meta: it is a safety-focused AI company that has consistently advocated for regulation while simultaneously competing on capability. Anthropic spending more on lobbying while the CLARITY Act approaches its August 7 window suggests it believes the regulatory outcome matters enough to influence — and that its public safety-first positioning is compatible with active legislative engagement. The increase also reflects Anthropic's growing enterprise revenue base: the more revenue depends on regulatory clarity, the more valuable lobbying becomes as an investment.
