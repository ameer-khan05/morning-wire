The Anchor

WSJ: OpenAI missed internal user and revenue targets — chip stocks fall 3-4%, four Mag-7 companies report tonight.

The Wall Street Journal reported Tuesday that OpenAI missed its own internal targets for both weekly active users and revenue. The company had set an internal milestone of one billion weekly active ChatGPT users by year's end — that goal is no longer achievable on its original timeline. ChatGPT's revenue targets also slipped as Google's Gemini surged in enterprise market share and Anthropic's Claude captured a disproportionate share of the coding and enterprise API market. OpenAI CFO Sarah Friar has warned colleagues internally that if revenue growth does not accelerate, the company could face difficulty funding future compute agreements — including its $300 billion, five-year partnership with Oracle. The disclosure rattled AI infrastructure stocks broadly: Oracle fell 4%, Broadcom and AMD fell 3-4%, and Nvidia led chip stocks lower as investors began asking whether the demand signal that justified $650 billion in combined Big Tech AI capex is as strong as the capex budgets assumed.

The timing is maximally consequential: the OpenAI miss report landed the evening before the most closely watched earnings slate in the AI cycle's history. Alphabet, Meta, Microsoft, and Amazon all report Q1 earnings tonight after the close. Each transcript will be parsed not just for revenue beats but for evidence that AI demand at the application layer — the layer OpenAI was supposed to validate — is translating into compute consumption at the infrastructure layer. If Alphabet's Google Cloud growth falls short of its 50% consensus, or Microsoft's Azure guidance disappoints on the tail of the OpenAI exclusivity change, the market will treat the OpenAI miss as a leading indicator of broader demand softness. If all four beat, the miss will be reframed as an OpenAI-specific execution problem rather than a structural demand signal. Tonight provides the answer.

OpenAI · Miss · WSJ Missed Weekly users off target · revenue miss · Oracle −4% · chips −3-4% · Friar warning

OpenAI missed its own internal user and revenue targets — WSJ report sends chip stocks down 3-4%, Oracle falls 4%.

The Wall Street Journal's report that OpenAI missed internal targets for weekly active users and ChatGPT revenue is significant for three reasons that extend well beyond OpenAI's own P&L. First, the scale: OpenAI's internal one-billion weekly-user target was the demand signal that justified Oracle's $300 billion compute partnership, Softbank's investment commitments, and the downstream AI infrastructure buildout. A miss on that target implies the demand curve is less steep than the buildout assumed. Second, the mechanism: ChatGPT's revenue shortfall is partly attributed to Google Gemini gaining enterprise share — a direct sign that the AI application market is becoming competitive rather than winner-take-most, which compresses the unit economics of the entire sector. Third, the CFO warning: Sarah Friar's internal caution that compute agreement funding could be at risk if growth doesn't accelerate is the first public signal of a potential gap between OpenAI's revenue trajectory and its infrastructure spending commitments. The market's immediate response — Oracle −4%, Broadcom and AMD −3-4%, Nvidia leading chip stocks lower — reflects how directly OpenAI's demand health flows through to infrastructure valuations.

Alphabet · Q1 · Tonight $107B Google Cloud ~50% · ad resilience · CapEx $179B · first $100B+ quarter

Alphabet reports Q1 tonight — $106.9B consensus, Google Cloud near 50% growth, and a potential first $100B+ quarterly print.

Alphabet's Q1 2026 earnings report — due after the close — carries a historic milestone on the revenue line: the Street's $106.9 billion consensus would represent Google's first ever $100 billion+ quarterly revenue print, a 19% year-over-year growth rate at a moment when the company is simultaneously investing $179 billion in 2026 capex. The number that matters most to AI investors is Google Cloud: consensus expects approximately $18.4 billion in Cloud revenue, representing roughly 50% year-over-year growth. If Cloud misses that figure in an environment where the OpenAI miss has raised demand concerns, the stock will likely give back its Tuesday decline and then some. If it beats — particularly if Gemini's enterprise share gains explain part of the OpenAI user shortfall by migrating to Google's platform — the narrative flips from "AI demand softness" to "demand is there, just redistributing to Alphabet." The advertising business remains the earnings floor: Google Search's resilience through $114 Brent oil and Iran-related uncertainty is itself a data point about the nature of digital advertising's cycle independence.

Microsoft · Azure · Q3 37-38% Azure growth target · $81.4B consensus · OpenAI exclusivity ended · $357B prior wipeout

Microsoft reports Q3 tonight — Azure growth at 37-38% is the make-or-break number after a $357B market cap wipeout this cycle.

Microsoft's Q3 FY2026 earnings — consensus $81.4 billion in revenue, Azure growth at 37-38% constant currency — arrive carrying more weight than any prior Microsoft print this cycle. The company suffered what GeekWire described as a "historic $357 billion wipeout" earlier in the year when Azure growth decelerated despite massive AI infrastructure investment; tonight's report is literally described as "another chance" to restore the bull case. The specific number that will move the stock: Azure's constant-currency growth versus Microsoft's own 37-38% guidance. If Azure accelerates back above guidance — evidence that enterprise AI workloads are migrating to Azure at the pace the capex assumed — the stock recovers. If it tracks guidance but doesn't beat, the reaction will be muted. If it misses guidance for the second consecutive period, the deceleration narrative becomes structural rather than transient, with direct implications for Microsoft's stock premium relative to its $81 billion per quarter cost base. The OpenAI multicloud restructuring, announced last week, has also added an Azure-exclusivity question to the transcript: analysts will probe directly whether the loss of OpenAI as an Azure-exclusive workload has already appeared in the Q3 number.

Meta · Q1 · 14-Quarter Streak $55B Revenue $55.36B · 14-quarter beat streak · zero Sell ratings · CapEx $115-135B

Meta reports Q1 tonight — $55.36B consensus and a 14-quarter earnings beat streak intact, but zero Sell ratings leaves no margin for a miss.

Meta enters its Q1 2026 earnings report — consensus $55.36 billion in revenue, +30% year-over-year — with the most bullish analyst positioning of any Mag-7 company: zero Sell ratings across coverage. The 14-quarter earnings beat streak (no sales miss since Q2 2022) has conditioned investors to expect the beat, which means the stock reaction function is asymmetric: a beat likely produces a modest positive reaction against an already-bullish positioning; a miss against zero Sells could produce a disproportionate selloff. Three variables add complexity this quarter. First, the China-NDRC Manus acquisition block: Meta's forward guidance will be examined for any disclosure about the cost of unwinding a $2 billion acquisition and the write-off of integration work already completed. Second, AI monetization: Meta's Advantage+ AI advertising platform is central to the +30% revenue growth thesis; any commentary about AI ad revenue contribution will be closely parsed. Third, the 10% global workforce reduction announced earlier this year is its first full-quarter reflection on margins — consensus implies margin expansion, and a miss here would raise questions about whether the cuts are proceeding as planned.

Amazon · AWS · AI Engine 24%+ AWS Q4 24% growth · AI agent platform · tonight alongside ECB tomorrow

Amazon reports Q1 tonight — AWS growth past 24% is the benchmark, with its AI agent platform cited as the next infrastructure battleground.

Amazon rounds out the four-company Big Tech earnings slate reporting tonight, with AWS the focal metric: the cloud unit grew 24% year-over-year in Q4 2025, the segment's fastest expansion in 13 quarters, and analysts expect acceleration continuing in Q1 2026. AWS's AI agent platform — Bedrock — is described by 24/7 Wall St. as Amazon's "AI engine about to explode higher," citing enterprise adoption of agent-based workflows as the fastest-growing segment of cloud compute. Amazon's position in the AI earnings cycle is somewhat different from Alphabet's and Microsoft's: AWS is purely cloud infrastructure, without the earnings volatility of a consumer product or a challenged hardware segment. A strong AWS number tonight would be the cleanest evidence that cloud AI demand is robust independently of OpenAI's application-layer miss — because AWS customers are enterprises running their own models, not ChatGPT subscribers. The ECB rate decision tomorrow (April 30) adds a macro layer to Amazon's European operations commentary on the earnings call.

Quick hits

  • The OpenAI miss has a specific interpretation for each company reporting tonight: for Alphabet, it is potentially good news (Gemini gained share from ChatGPT); for Microsoft, it is a mixed signal (Azure hosted OpenAI workloads, so if OpenAI's demand is lower, Azure's AI workload growth may soften); for Meta, it is irrelevant (Meta AI is a separate stack); for Amazon, it is a potential positive (AWS Bedrock enterprise adoption is differentiated from ChatGPT consumer dynamics).
  • The $300 billion Oracle-OpenAI compute partnership — signed earlier this year — is now a live risk: if OpenAI's internal concern about "funding future compute agreements" materializes as a renegotiation, Oracle's data center expansion plans are directly affected. Oracle's 4% decline on Tuesday was investors pricing a non-zero probability of that scenario.
  • Google Cloud's 50% growth consensus is itself a consequence of the OpenAI-Gemini competition: as OpenAI misses user growth targets, Gemini gains — and those Gemini users are on Google Cloud infrastructure. A strong Google Cloud number tonight could be simultaneously bearish for OpenAI and bullish for Alphabet, a split outcome that would complicate the "AI demand softness" thesis.
  • Meta's zero-Sell-rating consensus is statistically rare. Across the 42 analyst firms covering Meta, not one has issued a Sell recommendation entering Q1 earnings. The last time a major tech company entered earnings with this kind of universal bullishness was Amazon in Q3 2021 — which missed and fell 7% in after-hours. The asymmetric risk is clear: the upside from a beat is limited by expectations already being maxed out; the downside from a miss is amplified by the same.
  • Powell's final press conference today is the last chance for the outgoing Fed chair to frame the oil-inflation dynamic on his own terms. His successor Warsh has not yet publicly addressed $114 Brent, the UAE-OPEC exit, or what he would consider an acceptable oil pass-through to core inflation before tightening further. Powell's framing will effectively set the benchmark against which Warsh's first policy statement will be evaluated.
  • The Brent-WTI spread has widened to approximately $15 per barrel — historically extreme, normally around $3-5. The spread reflects the Hormuz disruption's differential impact: Brent is the international waterborne crude benchmark, directly affected by Middle East supply disruption; WTI is the US domestic benchmark, insulated by domestic production that has ramped to record levels precisely to offset the Hormuz impact. The wide spread is itself a sign of energy market fragmentation that will outlast the immediate conflict.
