The inference economics story of 2026 is Microsoft building the cost architecture that lets it route by task — frontier for hard, MAI for commodity — rather than by vendor.

Bloomberg reported July 7 that Microsoft has begun routing a "meaningful share" of Copilot prompts in Excel and Outlook to its own internally built MAI models — tens of thousands of AI prompts per week now handled by homegrown infrastructure rather than OpenAI or Anthropic. The MAI lineup, introduced at Build 2026, spans seven models: MAI-Thinking-1 for complex reasoning, plus specialized models for coding, image generation, speech, and transcription. The efficiency case is stark: the MAI tuned model for Excel matches GPT 5.4 performance while being up to 10× more efficient, letting Microsoft price Copilot competitively while controlling its inference margin.

Meta's greenlight for custom AI chip production follows the same vertical integration logic. Microsoft has Maia; Google has TPUs; Amazon has Trainium; Apple has the M-series custom silicon. Meta joining the homegrown silicon race removes its dependence on NVIDIA A100/H100 supply-chain constraints and TSMC wafer allocation competition — and positions the company to scale AI inference at a cost structure no vendor-dependent hyperscaler can match. Combined with Meta's $125B–$145B 2026 AI capex target, the chip greenlight signals that Meta's AI infrastructure ambitions are now execution-phase, not planning-phase.

Meta greenlighted production of its own custom AI chip — joining Microsoft, Google, and Amazon in the homegrown silicon race and eliminating its dependence on NVIDIA supply constraints. The chip announcement pairs with Meta's $125B–$145B 2026 AI capex and the hire of AWS computing executive Dave Brown to oversee its AI data center network.

The logic of homegrown AI silicon is straightforward: NVIDIA H100/B200 supply is constrained, TSMC wafer allocation is competitive, and per-inference costs on vendor hardware cannot fall below vendor pricing. Microsoft's Maia, Google's TPUs, and Amazon's Trainium each solved the same problem on different timetables. Meta's greenlight — after a 2026 capex commitment of up to $145 billion — signals the infrastructure investment phase is now producing its own silicon rather than waiting in queue for it. Dave Brown's hire adds the operational management layer: scaling a homegrown chip-based data center network requires someone who has done it before, and Brown built it at AWS.

Quick hits

  • Microsoft routing commodity Copilot inference to MAI has a direct implication for OpenAI's revenue trajectory. The Microsoft relationship is OpenAI's largest commercial partnership; if the highest-volume, lowest-complexity tasks — the ones that add up fastest on an inference bill — are routed to MAI, OpenAI's per-token revenue from the Microsoft channel declines even as the partnership continues. The quality partnership holds; the volume economics do not.
  • Meta's custom chip greenlight, combined with the $125–$145B capex target and the Dave Brown hire, represents a full hyperscaler infrastructure posture: own the silicon, build the data centers, hire the executive who has scaled that exact stack before. The timeline question is production ramp — homegrown silicon requires TSMC or Samsung fabrication regardless, so wafer allocation constraints still apply. The difference is that Meta now controls design and roadmap rather than waiting in NVIDIA's queue.
