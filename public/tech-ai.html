The Anchor

Microsoft and OpenAI restructure their AI partnership — OpenAI goes multicloud, Microsoft ends its revenue share.

Microsoft and OpenAI published joint details of a restructured partnership Monday that fundamentally changes the architecture of the most significant AI investment in corporate history. Under the new terms, OpenAI can now serve all its products — including ChatGPT, API customers, and enterprise deployments — across any cloud provider. Microsoft's exclusive hold on OpenAI infrastructure is ended; Azure retains first right of refusal if OpenAI cannot find necessary capabilities elsewhere, but cannot block OpenAI from building or serving on Amazon AWS, Google Cloud, or others. Microsoft simultaneously ends its own obligation to pay a revenue share to OpenAI — a payment that had been part of the original partnership structure. OpenAI will continue paying Microsoft a 20% revenue share through 2030, now subject to a cap. Microsoft's license to OpenAI's intellectual property across AI models is extended through 2032 — but is no longer exclusive, meaning Microsoft's competitors can also license OpenAI models independently.

The restructuring is a direct consequence of the AI infrastructure landscape that emerged in Q1 2026. OpenAI's $50 billion Amazon compute deal and Google's $40 billion Anthropic investment created a situation where the exclusive Microsoft-OpenAI cloud lock-in was commercially untenable — hampering OpenAI's ability to attract enterprise customers who preferred AWS or GCP. The revamped deal resolves the structural conflict by making OpenAI's cloud relationship with Microsoft commercially preferential rather than exclusive. Microsoft shares gained slightly on the news — the exclusive edge that had made Azure the default for enterprises wanting GPT access is now formally over, but the IP license extension and revenue stream through 2030 represent durable value. For the broader AI market, the deal confirms a new phase: AI infrastructure is now a commodity layer that no single hyperscaler can lock up, and OpenAI's commercial future is multicloud by design.

MSFT · OpenAI · Revamp Any Cloud Revenue share ends · IP 2032 non-exclusive · Azure first refusal

Microsoft ends revenue share, drops OpenAI exclusivity — AI infrastructure goes multicloud as IP license extends to 2032.

The restructured Microsoft-OpenAI partnership announced Monday ends the exclusivity arrangement that had made Azure the mandatory infrastructure platform for OpenAI's products since the original 2019 investment. OpenAI can now serve products on AWS, Google Cloud, Oracle, or any provider — Azure keeps first right of refusal for capabilities OpenAI needs but only if Microsoft can and chooses to fulfill them. Microsoft simultaneously ends its revenue-share obligation to OpenAI. OpenAI continues paying Microsoft 20% of its revenue through 2030, subject to a newly negotiated cap. Microsoft's license to OpenAI's IP — the models that power ChatGPT and the API — is extended through 2032 and remains comprehensive, but exclusivity is removed. The deal gives OpenAI commercial freedom to compete for enterprise accounts that had been hesitant to bet on a single-cloud vendor, and gives Microsoft a defined end-date to the revenue flow without the obligation to maintain exclusivity indefinitely.

Qualcomm · OpenAI · Phone ▲11% AI smartphone · MediaTek · Luxshare · 2028 production

Qualcomm surges 11% on report OpenAI is building an AI smartphone — QCOM and MediaTek co-developing the chip, Luxshare manufacturing.

Analyst Ming-Chi Kuo published details Sunday night confirming that OpenAI is building an AI-native smartphone: Qualcomm and MediaTek are serving as processor co-development partners, and Luxshare — which also manufactures Apple's AirPods and iPhone components — is the exclusive system co-design and manufacturing partner. The device targets full production in 2028. Qualcomm shares surged 11% in Monday trading, adding approximately $15 billion in market cap in a single session. OpenAI declined to confirm the partnership to CNBC. The device, if it enters production on schedule, would be the first smartphone built from the ground up around AI agent operation rather than app-based interaction — a fundamental redesign of the mobile computing paradigm. For Qualcomm, the partnership returns the chipmaker to the center of the next major mobile platform cycle after years of margin pressure from Apple's shift to in-house silicon.

Kevin Warsh · Fed Chair Apr 29 Senate Banking vote · Tillis clears block · May 15 Powell end

Kevin Warsh Fed chair confirmation vote set for April 29 — Senate Banking Committee clears as Tillis drops block.

Senator Thom Tillis announced Sunday he will no longer block Kevin Warsh's confirmation as Federal Reserve chair after the Justice Department dropped its investigation of current Fed Chair Jerome Powell over building renovation cost overruns. The Senate Banking Committee — 13 Republicans and 11 Democrats — is scheduled to vote on Warsh on Wednesday April 29, the same day as Big Tech earnings. With Tillis's support, Warsh is effectively confirmed; the full Senate vote would follow, putting Warsh on track to take over before Powell's term expires May 15. The transition lands at the worst possible moment for monetary policy continuity: the FOMC meets May 7, less than a week after the April PCE inflation print on May 1, with Brent above $108 and Big Tech earnings still being digested. Warsh has publicly vowed not to be Trump's "sock puppet" on rates — a statement markets will test in real time almost immediately after confirmation.

Quick hits

  • The Microsoft-OpenAI deal's non-exclusive IP license through 2032 means that Google, Amazon, and other AI competitors can now license the same underlying OpenAI models that Microsoft previously had exclusive access to — a structural leveling of the competitive playing field that benefits every cloud provider except Azure.
  • Qualcomm's 11% single-day gain on the OpenAI smartphone report adds approximately $15 billion in market cap — roughly equivalent to the annual revenue of the entire US gaming industry — based on an unconfirmed analyst report from a single analyst, Ming-Chi Kuo, whose track record on Apple supply chain has been reliable but whose AI device reporting is newer territory.
  • OpenAI's AI-native smartphone targeting 2028 production faces a fundamental challenge: it must convince users to switch from a mature app ecosystem (iOS/Android) to an agent-based paradigm that doesn't yet have the equivalent of Google Maps, Instagram, or banking apps — a cold-start problem that even Apple's App Store took three years to solve.
  • The Kevin Warsh confirmation vote on the same day as Big Tech earnings (April 29) is not a coincidence of scheduling — it reflects the Senate calendar compression as Powell's May 15 term end approaches. The two events create the highest-stakes single trading session of 2026: four earnings reports plus a Fed chair transition, all before noon on Thursday.
  • Google Cloud competing for OpenAI enterprise business now that the Azure exclusivity is ended means the $40 billion Google-Anthropic deal and the OpenAI multicloud deal were effectively announced in the same week — Google is simultaneously investing in its main AI competitor (Anthropic) and competing to host its other main AI competitor (OpenAI). This is the most counter-intuitive week in cloud competition history.
  • Meta's $115–$135 billion 2026 capex plan, if executed, would make it the single largest investor in AI infrastructure among non-hyperscaler companies — a bet on AI agents replacing software engineers that requires continued 30%+ revenue growth to justify its cost of capital.
