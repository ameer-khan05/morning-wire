The Anchor

DeepSeek V4 claims to close the frontier gap — at $0.14 per million tokens. Intel's quarter sends stocks to records.

DeepSeek released two preview models Friday — V4 Flash and V4 Pro — exactly one year after the Chinese lab's V3 upended global AI benchmarks and rattled US market valuations. V4 Flash is a 284-billion-parameter mixture-of-experts model with 13 billion active parameters; V4 Pro has 1.6 trillion total parameters with 49 billion active. Both support 1-million-token context windows using a Hybrid Attention Architecture that DeepSeek says improves long-conversation memory. Performance benchmarks show both models nearing — but not fully matching — GPT-5.5 and Gemini 3.1 Pro on reasoning tasks, while trailing on knowledge-recall tests. The gap, per independent analysis, represents "a developmental trajectory approximately 3 to 6 months behind state-of-the-art frontier models." The pricing gap, however, is larger: Flash at $0.14/$0.28 per million input/output tokens and Pro at $1.74/$3.48 compare against OpenAI's $5/$30 for GPT-5.5. DeepSeek also integrated V4 closely with Huawei's Ascend chips, reducing dependence on US-restricted NVIDIA hardware.

Intel's Q1 quarter was the session's defining US story. The company reported stronger-than-expected CPU sales and raised full-year guidance — driven by renewed demand for its server processors alongside AI accelerator revenue, with a new foundry business contribution now visible in the income statement. Shares surged more than 25%, crossing the $90 level at which Intel peaked in the year 2000 for the first time in a quarter century. The US government's equity-adjacent stake in Intel, taken via CHIPS Act agreements in August 2025, is now worth approximately $36 billion — roughly four times the original $9 billion position. Nvidia's market cap retook $5 trillion Friday as the broader semiconductor index logged its 18th consecutive session of gains. The Nasdaq's record 24,836.60 close reversed Thursday's Iran-driven pullback entirely.

Oracle · Michigan · Stargate $16B Related Digital · Blackstone · OpenAI

Oracle's $16 billion Michigan Stargate data center closes financing — Blackstone and PIMCO lead the deal.

Related Digital and Blackstone announced they have closed financing for the $16 billion Oracle data center campus in Saline Township, Michigan — the facility known as "The Barn" for a historic red barn preserved at its entrance. The deal combines Blackstone equity (~$2 billion) with $14 billion in fixed-rate bonds sold by Bank of America to PIMCO-managed funds. The more-than-1-gigawatt campus is part of the Stargate initiative — a joint project between OpenAI, Oracle, and Related Digital — and is on schedule for delivery. It will create 2,500+ construction jobs and 450+ permanent onsite roles in Washtenaw County.

Anthropic · MCP 97M Installs · every major AI provider · agent standard

Anthropic's Model Context Protocol crosses 97 million installs — becomes the default agent-to-tool standard.

Anthropic's Model Context Protocol crossed 97 million installs in March 2026, with every major AI provider — including OpenAI, Google, and Microsoft — now shipping MCP-compatible tooling. The protocol has become the default mechanism by which AI agents connect to external tools, APIs, and data sources, ending the fragmented era in which each agentic framework maintained proprietary tool-calling conventions. For Anthropic, MCP's adoption is a strategic win independent of model revenue: it positions Claude's infrastructure layer as foundational to the entire agentic AI ecosystem.

Security experts convene in DC to set AI safety standards as Anthropic's Mythos raises the stakes.

Security professionals from NIST, OWASP, SANS, and the Coalition for AI Safety (COSAI) converged in Washington, DC, this week to debate standards for securing AI systems — as Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview model raised the baseline for what frontier security-critical AI requires. The central debate: how to establish red-teaming protocols, access controls, and incident-response playbooks for AI systems that operate autonomously in enterprise environments. A White House memo from National Cyber Director Michael Kratsios meanwhile cited evidence that foreign entities — specifically China — are running industrial-scale distillation campaigns to extract value from US-developed AI models without licensing or access.
