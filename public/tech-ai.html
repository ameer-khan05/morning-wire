The Anchor

Apple's Q2 FY2026: $111.18B revenue, Services all-time record, $100B buyback — the Mag-7 season closes on the strongest possible note.

Apple's Q2 FY2026 report — filed Thursday after the close — is the final and strongest entry in the Magnificent Seven earnings season. Revenue of $111.18 billion beat the $109.66 billion consensus, up 17% year over year. EPS of $2.01 per diluted share beat the $1.94 estimate. Services generated $30.98 billion — an all-time record for the segment, up from $26.65 billion a year ago — reflecting the continued monetization of Apple's installed base through subscriptions, app store fees, and financial services. iPhone revenue reached $56.99 billion, the highest Q2 iPhone figure ever recorded. Mac revenue was $8.40 billion; iPad $6.91 billion. Apple's board authorized $100 billion in additional share repurchases and raised the quarterly dividend to $0.27 per share from $0.26 — Apple's 14th consecutive annual dividend increase. AAPL jumped 3% in premarket Friday.

CEO Tim Cook emphasized the iPhone 17's role in the results, describing it as "the most popular lineup in our history" — a signal that the AI-driven upgrade cycle the company has been building toward with Apple Intelligence is beginning to materialize in unit economics. The Services all-time record is the more structurally significant figure: it represents Apple's revenue that grows regardless of device upgrade cycles, driven by the 2.2 billion-device active installed base. Services' 14% year-over-year growth against that base is the most durable revenue engine in consumer technology. The $100 billion buyback authorization — added to Apple's existing program — is the largest single-quarter capital return announcement in the company's history and reflects the extraordinary cash generation that $111 billion in quarterly revenue now produces. Apple's Q2 print completes a Mag-7 earnings season in which five of seven companies beat decisively on AI-related infrastructure or services metrics — Alphabet, Amazon, and now Apple — with Meta and Microsoft producing more mixed results driven by geopolitical disruption and supply-constraint language respectively.

Microsoft's first-ever US voluntary retirement — 8,700 employees, Rule of 70, $900M charge. AI is restructuring Big Tech's workforce, not just adding to it.

Microsoft's decision to offer voluntary retirement buyouts to approximately 8,700 US employees — its first such program ever — marks a qualitative shift in how the company is managing its AI transition. The program operates on a "Rule of 70" eligibility framework: US employees at the senior director level and below whose combined age and years of service total 70 or more are eligible for the buyout package. The $900 million charge reflects the cost of separating a cohort of the company's most experienced — and highest-compensated — employees. The program follows 15,000-plus layoffs in 2025 and a March 2026 hiring freeze (from which AI teams were exempt), and arrives as Microsoft spends $80 billion-plus annually on AI infrastructure. The workforce logic is explicit: a company that is capacity-constrained at its AI infrastructure level (Azure's "capacity constrained through 2026" language) does not need the same ratio of experienced general-purpose engineers to revenue that it required before AI augmented every engineering function. Voluntary retirement — framed as a benefit rather than a layoff — allows Microsoft to reset its high-end payroll more gracefully than forced exits, avoiding the reputational and legal friction of a second major layoff cycle within 12 months. Business Standard reported Friday that the program signals a new model for Big Tech workforce design in the AI era: not the elimination of human labor, but the structural realignment of which human capabilities command premium compensation.

Huawei · Ascend 950PR · $12B +60% AI chip revenue 2026 est $12B · up from $7.5B in 2025 · Ascend 950PR orders surge · US sanctions workaround

Huawei expects $12B in AI chip revenue in 2026 — up 60% — as orders for its Ascend 950PR surge among Chinese enterprises locked out of Nvidia.

Huawei is projecting AI chip revenue of approximately $12 billion in 2026, up roughly 60% from $7.5 billion in 2025, driven by surging orders for its Ascend 950PR AI accelerator from Chinese enterprises that cannot access Nvidia's H100 and A100 chips under US export controls. The Ascend 950PR — Huawei's most advanced AI training chip — has emerged as the primary domestic alternative for large-scale AI model training in China, with performance benchmarks that analysts have rated at approximately 60-70% of an H100's theoretical training throughput. The revenue acceleration reflects not just demand from existing Chinese AI companies but an expanding customer base: Chinese banks, state-owned enterprises, and regional cloud providers that previously used imported Nvidia infrastructure are now transitioning to Ascend-based clusters under government pressure to localize AI infrastructure. For the global AI chip market, Huawei's $12 billion 2026 projection is significant as a competitive data point: it represents a market that Nvidia cannot address and that Advanced Micro Devices' export-controlled China sales also cannot reach. The emergence of a viable domestic Chinese alternative to Nvidia for AI training — even at lower performance levels — has structural implications for the US AI export-control regime's long-term effectiveness at delaying Chinese AI development.

OpenAI · Smartphone · 2028 AI Device MediaTek/Qualcomm custom chip · Luxshare manufacturing · AI agents · continuous context · mass production 2028

OpenAI is building an AI-agent smartphone — custom chip with MediaTek and Qualcomm, Luxshare manufacturing, mass production targeted for 2028.

OpenAI is developing a purpose-built AI-agent smartphone, with custom silicon co-developed with MediaTek and Qualcomm and manufacturing handled by Luxshare — the Chinese electronics manufacturer that produces Apple AirPods and has expanded into full device assembly. The device is designed from the ground up around AI agents: rather than running conventional apps, it is built to use AI to complete multi-step tasks autonomously, while maintaining continuous context across sessions that standard smartphone operating systems reset. Chip specifications are expected to be finalized by Q1 2027, with mass production targeted for 2028. The product strategy represents OpenAI's most direct challenge to Apple's hardware-software integration model: Apple Intelligence adds AI to a conventional smartphone, while OpenAI's device inverts the architecture — the AI agent is the primary interface, and the hardware is designed to support it. The strategic implication: if OpenAI ships an AI-first device in 2028, Apple's smartphone franchise — which has defined consumer computing for 18 years — faces its first genuine architectural competitor since Android. The 2028 timeline also means the device will arrive as Apple's iPhone 19 or 20 generation, with Apple Intelligence at a significantly more mature stage than its current 2026 rollout.

Black Hat Asia: the time from bug discovery to working exploit has collapsed from five months to ten hours — frontier LLMs are doing the offensive security heavy lifting.

Security researchers presenting at Black Hat Asia this week documented the most consequential acceleration in the offensive security landscape in a decade: the median time from a newly discovered software vulnerability to a working, deployable exploit has collapsed from approximately five months in 2023 to ten hours in 2026 — a 12-fold compression driven primarily by frontier large language models performing the code analysis, exploit scaffolding, and payload crafting that previously required specialized human expertise. The mechanism is now well-documented: an LLM with access to a CVE description and the relevant source code or binary can generate a functional proof-of-concept exploit for many vulnerability classes within a single conversation session, a task that previously required a skilled penetration tester days to weeks of work. For enterprise security teams, the implication is direct and severe: the assumption that a disclosed vulnerability provides weeks or months of remediation runway before exploitation begins is no longer valid. The ten-hour window means that patch deployment must be nearly immediate — a standard that most enterprise environments cannot meet for complex infrastructure changes. The finding also reframes the AI safety debate in a specific operational context: the same frontier models that enterprises are deploying for productivity are simultaneously accelerating the threat landscape those enterprises face.

Quick hits

  • Apple's Services segment at $30.98 billion quarterly — annualized at approximately $124 billion — now exceeds the total annual revenue of Salesforce, the world's largest enterprise software company by revenue. Apple is, by this measure, simultaneously the world's largest consumer hardware company and a top-five enterprise software company by revenue run-rate, without having specifically targeted enterprise software as a market. The AI-driven Services growth trajectory — up 14-16% year over year consistently — suggests this comparison will be even more stark by Q4 2026.
  • Microsoft's "Rule of 70" voluntary retirement eligibility design — combined age plus years of service totaling 70 — is specifically calibrated to capture the company's most experienced senior workforce: a 50-year-old with 20 years at Microsoft qualifies; a 45-year-old with 25 years qualifies; a 60-year-old with 10 years qualifies. This cohort represents the highest-compensated, most tenured employees — precisely the population that AI can now most directly augment or replace for many coding, architecture, and project management functions. The $900 million charge reflects the separation cost for this cohort; the long-term savings from replacing their compensation with AI-augmented junior employees or automated systems is the calculation Microsoft is making explicitly.
  • The OpenAI smartphone's Luxshare manufacturing partnership is a significant supply-chain signal. Luxshare — which has expanded from AirPods to iPhone components to full device assembly — is now positioned as a potential full-device manufacturer for an AI-native hardware platform. If OpenAI's device reaches mass production in 2028, Luxshare's manufacturing capabilities will be a key competitive differentiator versus Foxconn (Apple's primary assembler), since Luxshare has demonstrated willingness to work on aggressive timelines and competitive pricing. The manufacturing partnership suggests OpenAI is serious about hardware at scale, not just a prototype or developer device.
  • Huawei's Ascend 950PR orders surging 60% in AI chip revenue tracks with a broader observable phenomenon in Chinese AI development: domestic enterprises that were previously using cloud-based Nvidia infrastructure accessed through Alibaba, Tencent, and ByteDance's cloud arms are now building private AI infrastructure using Huawei's chips, driven by data sovereignty concerns and government incentives to localize AI compute. This "insourcing" trend — Chinese enterprises moving from cloud-rented Nvidia inference to on-premise Ascend training — is structurally different from the US enterprise pattern (which is moving toward cloud-based AI inference) and reflects the specific regulatory and national-security context of Chinese AI development.
  • The Black Hat Asia exploit timeline collapse — from five months to ten hours — is particularly alarming for critical infrastructure operators, who face both the longest patch deployment cycles (weeks to months for operational technology environments) and the most severe consequences of exploitation (power grids, water systems, industrial control). The ten-hour window from disclosure to working exploit effectively eliminates the "coordinated disclosure" model that has underpinned the cybersecurity industry's responsible-disclosure framework: if an exploit is weaponizable within ten hours of a CVE publication, the 90-day standard disclosure window is not a meaningful protection for enterprises that cannot patch in ten hours. The industry response will likely involve significant changes to how vulnerabilities are disclosed and how enterprises prioritize emergency patching.
  • Apple's Q2 report completing the Mag-7 earnings season with a record means that four of the seven companies — Alphabet, Amazon, Microsoft (Azure 40%), and Apple — beat their primary AI-relevant metrics decisively. Only Meta (user miss from Iran-war geopolitical disruption) and Tesla (delivery shortfall) produced clear disappointments. The Mag-7's aggregate Q2 capex guidance — now representing more than $700 billion in combined AI infrastructure spending for 2026 across Alphabet ($180-190B), Microsoft ($190B), Amazon ($200B), and Meta ($125-145B) — will be the dominant driver of Nvidia's revenue trajectory and the data center construction cycle for the remainder of 2026 and into 2027.
