The Anchor

Jalapeño chip: OpenAI's first silicon, built in nine months with OpenAI's own models. Anthropic: Alibaba ran the largest distillation attack ever. DeepSeek V4-Pro price cut permanent. The AI infrastructure war — between custom silicon, Chinese capability extraction, and a structural pricing collapse — all snapped sharper this weekend.

The Jalapeño chip's significance is not just that OpenAI built a chip — it is that the development timeline breaks the conventional economics of semiconductor design. High-performance ASICs typically take three to four years from architecture decision to production-ready silicon; Jalapeño went from initial design to tape-out in nine months. The mechanism is AI-accelerated chip design: OpenAI's own models were used to compress parts of the design and optimization process that previously required large teams and extended timelines. The result is a chip specifically architected around OpenAI's understanding of LLM inference workloads — what it actually costs to run GPT-class models at ChatGPT, Codex, and API scale — rather than a general-purpose GPU that OpenAI adapts its workload to. Jalapeño enabling gigawatt-scale data centers with Microsoft is the near-term deployment plan; the multi-generation compute platform with Broadcom implies Jalapeño is version one of an ongoing silicon roadmap, not a one-off experiment.

The Anthropic allegation reframes the AI model distillation question from a theoretical concern to a documented industrial-scale operation. From April 22 to June 5 — six weeks — operators linked to Alibaba's Qwen AI lab created approximately 25,000 accounts, routed them through commercial proxy services to circumvent the geographic restrictions that bar Chinese entities from accessing Claude, and generated 28.8 million exchanges with the model specifically targeting its software-engineering and agentic-reasoning capabilities. Anthropic's previous February allegations against DeepSeek, Moonshot, and MiniMax were smaller in scale — and already represented the kind of systematic capability extraction that Anthropic treats as IP theft. The Alibaba campaign dwarfs that prior combined disclosure. Illicit model distillation — training a weaker model on the outputs of a stronger one — allows the Qwen lab to incorporate Claude's reasoning strengths without paying for Claude's training costs, and without either OpenAI's safety constraints or Anthropic's usage restrictions.

Jalapeño: OpenAI's First Custom AI Inference Chip · Broadcom · 9-Month ASIC Dev · LLM Inference · ~50% Cheaper Than Nvidia · Late-2026 Deploy Jalapeño Jalapeño: unveiled Jun 24 · co-developed OpenAI × Broadcom · reticle-sized ASIC = largest possible die size for a single chip (maximum compute density) · purpose: LLM inference (running trained models for ChatGPT, Codex, API users) · dev cycle: 9 months from design to tape-out (industry standard for high-perf ASICs: 3–4 years) · AI-accelerated design: OpenAI's own models used to compress design + optimization process · performance: substantially better performance per watt vs Nvidia alternatives for inference · cost: ~50% cheaper than Nvidia GPUs for inference workloads · deploy: initial end of 2026 → full production scale early 2028 · broader program: multi-generation compute platform with Broadcom → enables gigawatt-scale data centers with Microsoft + other partners

OpenAI and Broadcom unveiled Jalapeño on June 24 — OpenAI's first custom AI accelerator, a reticle-sized ASIC (the maximum single-die size achievable on current semiconductor manufacturing processes) specifically designed for LLM inference: running trained AI models in response to live user requests at ChatGPT, Codex, and API scale. The chip's development cycle of nine months from initial design to tape-out is potentially the fastest ever achieved for a high-performance custom semiconductor; the speed was enabled by co-locating OpenAI's engineering teams with Broadcom's silicon implementation experts and using OpenAI's own AI models to compress and accelerate the design optimization process that normally dominates ASIC development timelines. Early testing shows Jalapeño delivering substantially better performance per watt than current Nvidia alternatives for LLM inference workloads, at approximately half the cost. Initial deployment targets end of 2026, with full production scale in early 2028 across gigawatt-scale data centers with Microsoft and other infrastructure partners, as part of what OpenAI and Broadcom describe as a multi-generation compute platform — implying Jalapeño is version one of an ongoing hardware roadmap, not a one-time project.

The strategic logic is straightforward. OpenAI currently depends on Nvidia GPUs for inference — hardware designed for general parallel compute that OpenAI then adapts to LLM workloads. An ASIC designed specifically for LLM inference, architected around OpenAI's precise understanding of what ChatGPT and Codex actually do at compute time, can outperform a general-purpose GPU on the specific workloads it is built for while costing substantially less per inference operation. At ChatGPT's scale — hundreds of millions of users — a 50% inference cost reduction compounds enormously. The nine-month development cycle matters beyond the efficiency argument: if AI-accelerated chip design has compressed custom silicon timelines from three to four years to under one year, OpenAI can iterate on Jalapeño faster than Nvidia's conventional roadmap cadence, enabling a silicon-software co-design loop that Nvidia cannot match without rebuilding its design methodology. The partnership with Broadcom — whose custom ASIC expertise powers Google's TPUs — gives OpenAI a proven manufacturing and design-services partner for the multi-generation silicon roadmap it has now committed to.

Anthropic Accuses Alibaba Qwen of 28.8M Claude Exchanges · 25,000 Fake Accounts · Largest Distillation Allegation Ever · Senate Letter · Sanctions Bill 28.8M Anthropic letter → US Senate Banking Committee (Jun 24): operators linked to Alibaba Qwen lab · ~25,000 fraudulent accounts · commercial proxy services (circumvent geographic restrictions on Chinese entity access) · 28.8M Claude exchanges April 22–June 5, 2026 (6 weeks) · targeted capabilities: software-engineering + agentic reasoning · technique: illicit model distillation = training Qwen on Claude outputs → extracts Claude capabilities without paying training costs / without OpenAI safety constraints · scale: largest single allegation Anthropic has filed · dwarfs combined scale of Feb 2026 DeepSeek + Moonshot + MiniMax allegations · legislative: Senators Bill Hagerty (R-TN) + Andy Kim (D-NJ) → amendment to defense legislation → blacklist + sanction entities conducting distillation campaigns

Anthropic sent a letter to the US Senate Committee on Banking, Housing, and Urban Affairs on June 24 accusing Alibaba's Qwen AI lab of conducting the largest known AI model distillation campaign against any foundation model: approximately 25,000 fraudulent accounts, routed through commercial proxy services to evade the geographic access restrictions that bar Chinese entities from using Claude, generated 28.8 million unauthorized exchanges with the model between April 22 and June 5, 2026 — six weeks — systematically targeting Claude's software-engineering and agentic-reasoning capabilities. Anthropic characterized the technique as illicit model distillation: feeding a massive volume of targeted queries to Claude and training Qwen's models on Claude's outputs, thereby extracting Claude's capabilities without paying the compute and data costs of independent development. The scale of the alleged campaign dwarfs the combined total of the three Chinese AI labs — DeepSeek, Moonshot, and MiniMax — that Anthropic publicly named in February 2026 for similar activity.

Senators Bill Hagerty (R-TN) and Andy Kim (D-NJ) responded by moving to add an amendment to defense legislation that would authorize blacklisting and sanctions against entities conducting AI capability distillation attacks against US foundation models. The legislative path is now bipartisan: both senators framed the Alibaba campaign as an industrial-scale IP theft targeting a national security-relevant technology. The commercial proxy routing is the most significant operational detail in the allegation: it shows Alibaba was aware that direct access was prohibited and actively engineered workarounds, which Anthropic argues demonstrates deliberate intent rather than policy violation by careless users. For the Qwen lab, the alleged capability gain is the ability to improve its models using Claude's reasoning outputs as training data — a shortcut that could compress years of independent alignment and capability work at a fraction of the training cost. The six-week campaign suggests a structured operation, not opportunistic access.

DeepSeek V4-Pro 75% Price Cut Goes Permanent · $0.435/$0.87 per 1M Tokens · Undercuts GPT-5.5 by 5× · AI Price War Structural $0.435 DeepSeek V4-Pro: 75% promotional cut (May 2026) → made permanent June 2026 · new list price: $0.435 input / $0.87 output / $0.003625 cache hit per 1M tokens (V4-Pro only) · vs GPT-5.5: $2.50/$15 → DeepSeek 5.7× cheaper input · 17× cheaper output · vs GPT-5.6 Terra: $2.50/$15 → same gap · vs GPT-5.6 Luna: $1/$6 → still 2.3× cheaper input / 6.9× cheaper output · market impact: transforms promotional price war into structural pricing → enterprises now building cost models around permanently cheaper Chinese frontier models · context: DeepSeek V4-Pro capability: competitive with top-tier Western models on many benchmarks · access: US-based developers can access via API; enterprise deployment considerations vary

DeepSeek announced in June that it was making its May promotional 75% price cut on V4-Pro permanent, settling the model's API pricing at $0.435 per million input tokens and $0.87 per million output tokens — a list-price reduction that transforms a temporary promotion into a structural market signal. The permanent pricing undercuts OpenAI's GPT-5.5 (at $2.50/$15 per million tokens) by 5.7× on input and 17× on output, and undercuts even the GPT-5.6 Luna tier ($1/$6) by more than 2× on input. The move escalates the AI pricing war from a promotional phase — where temporary discounts are tactical competitive moves — to a structural one, where the permanent list prices for Chinese and American frontier models now diverge by an order of magnitude on the input dimension.

The pricing gap's practical implication is in enterprise budget modeling: the per-query cost of running software-engineering or agentic workflows at scale through DeepSeek V4-Pro versus GPT-5.5 now differs by 5–17× on a permanent basis. For enterprises building agentic pipelines that run thousands to millions of queries per day, a 5× permanent cost reduction on the input side changes the feasibility calculation for Claude or GPT-based workflows in ways that a 30-day promotional discount does not. DeepSeek's position — a Chinese-built frontier model with strong benchmark performance at structural price advantage — is now the clearest example of the pricing competition dynamic that Citi identified in its Friday tech sector downgrade: it is genuinely difficult to see how every participant in the AI model market sustains valuation premiums when production-quality models are available at prices an order of magnitude below the Western frontier pricing structure. The Anthropic/Alibaba distillation allegation and the DeepSeek permanent price cut land on the same weekend, illustrating the two sides of the China AI competition problem: cost-side structural pressure through legal pricing, and capability-side extraction through alleged illegal access.

Quick hits

  • Jalapeño is a multi-generation bet, not a product launch. OpenAI has committed to a silicon roadmap with Broadcom — which means inference cost, performance, and model architecture co-design all feed back into each other across generations. The first chip that takes three to four years to build is a proof of concept; the first chip that takes nine months is a platform. Watch whether Jalapeño v2's timeline compresses further, and whether OpenAI moves from inference-only ASICs to training silicon.
  • The legislative response to the Anthropic/Alibaba allegation — a defense bill amendment authorizing blacklisting and sanctions — is significant because it routes the remedy through the defense authorization process rather than trade law or IP enforcement. Defense-bill sanctions are broader and faster than civil litigation; if the amendment passes, any entity conducting AI distillation attacks against US models could face designation equivalent to an export control target. The Alibaba allegation is the first time a specific company's lab has been accused at this scale, and the first time the US Senate has moved on a legislative remedy within days of the disclosure.
