The Sacks disclosure fundamentally reframes the Fable 5 narrative. What Anthropic publicly described as an imposed government recall was, according to the PCASST co-chair, a negotiation that Anthropic had the opportunity to resolve — and chose not to. The administration offered two off-ramps: a technical remediation path (fix the jailbreak) and a cooperative de-deployment path (voluntarily pull the models, avoid the export control directive). Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei declined both, leading directly to the government-issued export control directive that forced the recall. That changes the political and legal calculus significantly: Anthropic's public statement framing the recall as an overreach is more complicated to sustain against a counternarrative that they were given a choice and refused. The technical story is unresolved — there is no confirmed timeline for Fable 5 remediation, no public description of what jailbreak-fix would satisfy the government's security concern, and no indication that the negotiation is continuing. NVIDIA and ServiceNow's Project Arc — a self-evolving desktop AI agent for knowledge workers — arrives in a week when the frontier AI model landscape has been disrupted by the recall, creating unusual market dynamics. SAP's Prior Labs acquisition signals that enterprise software companies are building frontier AI capacity in-house rather than remaining API consumers, which will reshape the model provider competitive landscape over the next 2-4 years.

The Anchor

Anthropic was offered two ways out. It took neither. Sacks says Dario Amodei declined to fix the jailbreak and declined to voluntarily de-deploy. The government directive was the consequence.

David Sacks — co-chair of the President's Council of Advisers on Science and Technology — disclosed Tuesday that the Trump administration presented Anthropic with explicit options before issuing the June 12 export control directive: the government was aware of a method to bypass Fable 5's safety guidelines, and it gave Anthropic a choice. Option one: remediate the jailbreak within a set timeframe and avoid the recall. Option two: voluntarily de-deploy Fable 5 and Mythos 5 while the issue was addressed, receiving some cooperation credit. Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei declined both. The government then issued the export control directive, which — because Anthropic cannot filter users by nationality in real time — effectively forced a full model recall for all users globally.

The disclosure complicates Anthropic's position substantially. The company's published statement framed the recall as a government overreach — arguing that applying a jailbreak-to-recall standard would halt all frontier AI deployments — but that argument was made without disclosing that Anthropic had been offered two remediation paths and refused them. Whether the argument is stronger or weaker now that the choice context is public is a question being debated in AI governance circles. As of Tuesday morning, Fable 5 remained offline with no confirmed restoration timeline. All other Anthropic models, including Claude Opus 4.8, Sonnet 4.6, and Haiku 4.5, remain fully accessible.

Anthropic's Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5 remain suspended Tuesday — entering their fifth day offline — after Monday's meeting between Anthropic senior leaders and Trump administration officials concluded without resolution. David Sacks, co-chair of the President's Council of Advisers on Science and Technology, disclosed Tuesday that the government had presented Anthropic with two options before issuing the June 12 export control directive: remediate the jailbreak vulnerability within a set timeframe, or voluntarily de-deploy both models pending remediation. Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei declined both options, and the government directive was issued as a direct result. Fable 5 remains offline with no confirmed restoration date.

The disclosure is the most significant new development in the Fable 5 saga since the recall began: it establishes that the government provided a remediation path and a cooperative exit, and that Anthropic — rather than being subjected to an unexpected unilateral action — made a choice that led directly to the mandatory recall. Anthropic's public position — that applying a jailbreak-to-recall standard would halt all frontier AI deployments — is substantively unchanged by the Sacks disclosure, but the political framing shifts: the company declined both offered paths before the recall was imposed, which changes how the governance precedent is being read inside the administration and among other frontier AI companies who are watching the standoff closely for signals about their own compliance exposure.

NVIDIA and ServiceNow expanded their enterprise AI partnership with the introduction of Project Arc — a long-running, self-evolving autonomous desktop AI agent designed for enterprise knowledge workers. Project Arc is positioned as a governed autonomous agent: it executes extended workflows without constant human check-ins, improves its own task execution based on outcomes, and maintains compliance and audit trails required by enterprise governance frameworks. The announcement arrives during an unusual week in which Anthropic's two frontier models are offline, leaving the enterprise AI competitive landscape temporarily simplified to OpenAI, Google, and NVIDIA-backed partnerships.

Project Arc's "self-evolving" architecture is the most substantive technical claim in the announcement: unlike static AI assistants that execute the same instructions the same way regardless of results, Arc is designed to adapt its approach based on what worked and what failed in prior task executions within an enterprise environment. The governance framing — built-in compliance and audit trail — is explicitly designed to address the enterprise purchasing objection that AI agents operating autonomously create accountability gaps that traditional software does not. ServiceNow's platform position, already embedded in IT, HR, and legal workflows at enterprise scale, gives Arc a distribution channel that consumer-origin AI models entering enterprise lack.

SAP agreed to acquire Prior Labs — a frontier AI research startup — for more than $1.18 billion over four years, with the stated objective of transforming the acquisition into a frontier AI lab embedded within SAP's enterprise software ecosystem. The deal is among the largest acquisitions of a frontier AI research startup by a traditional enterprise software company and signals that the largest software vendors are no longer content to remain API consumers of OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google — they are building proprietary frontier AI capacity to embed directly in their product roadmaps.

SAP's rationale is straightforward: enterprise customers who run SAP for ERP, supply chain, and financial workflows will increasingly expect AI capabilities that are deeply integrated with their data, compliant with their governance requirements, and not dependent on third-party API providers whose availability — as the Fable 5 recall demonstrated — can be disrupted by government action. Building frontier AI in-house provides SAP with model control, data sovereignty alignment, and pricing independence that API-dependent AI features do not. Oracle, Salesforce, and Microsoft Copilot have all announced similar in-house AI ambitions; SAP's Prior Labs acquisition is the largest funded commitment to date.

Cisco AI-BOM Scanner · Model Provenance Kit · "DNA Test for AI Models" · Supply Chain Security AI-BOM Cisco: released open-source AI-BOM (AI Bill of Materials) scanner · Model Provenance Kit: "DNA test for AI models" · function: verify lineage + integrity + training data origin of deployed AI models · enterprise use case: AI supply chain security audit · context: Fable 5 recall → government can pull commercial models → enterprises need to know what's in their AI stack · AI-BOM: analogous to software SBOM (Software Bill of Materials) required in federal software procurement · open-source release: available to enterprise security teams and AI governance functions · Cisco: network + security infrastructure → AI supply-chain security is natural extension

Cisco released an open-source AI-BOM (Artificial Intelligence Bill of Materials) scanner and Model Provenance Kit — described as a "DNA test for AI models" — that allows enterprises to verify the lineage, training data origin, and integrity of AI models deployed in their systems. The tool is designed to address the emerging enterprise security concern that organizations using third-party AI models have limited visibility into what is actually embedded in their AI stack, what data those models were trained on, and whether their models have been modified or compromised in transit through a software supply chain. The release arrives in a week when the Fable 5 recall demonstrated that government can pull commercial AI models on national security grounds, dramatically increasing enterprise awareness of AI deployment risk.

The AI-BOM concept is modeled on the Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) now required for federal software procurement: a structured inventory of all components in a software or AI system that enables security auditing, vulnerability tracking, and compliance documentation. Cisco's open-source release makes the tool available to any enterprise security team or AI governance function at no cost, with the strategic objective of establishing Cisco's security platform as the natural infrastructure layer for AI supply-chain visibility in enterprise environments. The Fable 5 recall context is the most immediate commercial argument for the tool: if a government can recall a commercial AI model without warning, enterprises need to know exactly which models they are running and have auditable evidence of when those models were deployed and whether they have changed.

Quick hits

  • The Sacks disclosure creates a new accountability narrative that cuts against Anthropic's public position. The company's published statement argued that "a narrow jailbreak finding should not trigger a full model recall" — an argument about the government's standard, not about the negotiation that preceded it. With the Sacks disclosure public, the argument must now also address why Anthropic declined to use the two remediation paths the government offered before issuing the directive. AI governance watchers expect Anthropic to respond directly to the Sacks disclosure; until it does, the "refused both options" framing will dominate the conversation about what actually happened and why the recall could not have been avoided.
  • The enterprise AI competitive landscape is being reshaped in real time by the Fable 5 recall. In the three weeks before the recall, Anthropic's Claude Code and Fable 5 were gaining enterprise market share against OpenAI's GPT-5.5 and Google's Gemini 3.5. With Fable 5 offline on day 5 and no restoration timeline, enterprise procurement teams evaluating AI contracts in June are making decisions without Anthropic's frontier model available for testing or deployment. A 30-90 day recall period — which is plausible given the negotiation has not restarted — is long enough for enterprise decisions to lock in to OpenAI or Google for the cycle, particularly for contracts closing before Q3 planning cycles.
  • Prior Labs — the SAP acquisition target — was founded by researchers from leading frontier AI labs and had been developing large-scale reasoning models aimed at enterprise planning, forecasting, and supply chain optimization tasks. SAP's investment of $1.18 billion over four years implies roughly $295 million per year in runway — comparable to Series B/C scale frontier AI lab funding — and reflects the expectation that building enterprise-grade frontier AI capability requires sustained capital investment over a multi-year period rather than a single large bet. The acquired lab's focus on planning and forecasting AI aligns with SAP's core ERP and supply chain business, where AI-driven planning recommendations have been the highest-value AI feature request from enterprise customers for the past three years.
