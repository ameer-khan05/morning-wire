Apple filed suit against OpenAI in Northern California Federal Court alleging that OpenAI's Chief Hardware Officer Tang Tan, a former Apple vice president who oversaw product design for wearables, sensing, and accessories, directed Apple employees still employed at Apple to bring "actual parts" from Apple facilities to job interviews at OpenAI — practices Apple's complaint describes as occurring "at every level, from members of its Technical Staff to its Chief Hardware Officer" — while separately alleging that OpenAI coached departing Apple employees on how to evade Apple's security processes, and that Chang Liu, a former Apple employee who joined OpenAI, stole a physical Apple laptop; Fortune reported that Jony Ive's IO Products firm, acquired by OpenAI for $6.4 billion to anchor the company's consumer hardware ambitions, is also named as a defendant in the complaint.

The lawsuit arrives at a structurally critical moment in the Apple-OpenAI relationship: the two companies had announced a high-profile partnership in 2024 that integrated ChatGPT into Apple's operating systems. Relations deteriorated after OpenAI announced its hardware program — and specifically after the IO Products acquisition put former Apple design chief Jony Ive inside the OpenAI enterprise with a mandate to build the AI company's physical products. Apple's allegation is that the $6.4 billion acquisition of Ive's firm was the organizing vehicle for extracting hardware expertise from Apple, with Tang Tan — already at OpenAI — providing the internal direction to candidates still employed by Apple. OpenAI denied all allegations.

OpenAI is preparing a confidential IPO filing targeting a public debut as soon as September 2026 at a private-market valuation of approximately $730 billion — which would make it the largest technology IPO in history, surpassing Alibaba's $168 billion 2014 listing by more than fourfold — arriving at a moment when the company is simultaneously defendant in Apple's blockbuster trade-secret lawsuit, completing its conversion from nonprofit to for-profit governance, and reporting annual recurring revenue reportedly approaching $25 billion, a figure that would need to support a multiple consistent with a $730 billion valuation in the face of intensifying competitive pressure from Google, Anthropic, and Meta.

The timing collision between the Apple lawsuit and the IPO filing is a disclosure problem as much as a legal one. A material trade-secret lawsuit must be disclosed in the S-1 filing — meaning that Apple's claims, Tang Tan's alleged "show and tell" instructions, and the full scope of OpenAI's alleged hardware IP extraction program will appear in the IPO's public risk factors for the first time, read by every institutional investor evaluating the offering. Whether the lawsuit is dismissed before filing, settled, or litigated through the IPO process determines the shape of that disclosure. The $730 billion target is not a guaranteed outcome — it reflects where private secondary-market trades were pricing OpenAI shares entering the week, and the lawsuit introduces a litigation discount that did not exist previously.

Google's Gemini 3.5 Pro is set to launch July 17 with a 2-million-token context window — the largest commercial context available from any AI model, enabling developers to load entire codebases, extensive document collections, or hours of conversation history into a single prompt — arriving one week before OpenAI's IPO process is expected to begin building public-market momentum, as the UN separately launched a global push for AI governance with warnings of "catastrophic harm" from unregulated AI systems and Google opened the Google Africa Applied AI Lab in Accra to support African researchers and entrepreneurs with early access to its AI technologies.

The 2-million-token context window is not merely a benchmark metric — it changes what is architecturally possible with an AI system. At 2 million tokens, an enterprise customer can provide Gemini 3.5 Pro with the entirety of a software codebase, a company's entire document history, or a complex regulatory framework with all referenced documents in a single session without chunking or retrieval systems. That capability eliminates a significant category of enterprise AI integration complexity. Google launching this the week before OpenAI's IPO narrative begins building is a calculated competitive move: enterprise buyers evaluating AI vendor commitments before OpenAI's S-1 arrives will have a 2-million-token option from Google as the reference point. The UN governance push, arriving simultaneously, signals that the regulatory environment these companies will operate in is hardening faster than any single AI lab's expansion plans assumed.

Quick hits

  • Tang Tan's specific role at Apple before joining OpenAI makes the "actual parts" allegation particularly significant. As Apple's VP for product design of wearables, sensing, and accessories, Tang Tan oversaw some of Apple's most secretive hardware development work — the teams that build the sensors, chips, and physical components that distinguish Apple's hardware from competitors. Apple's allegation is that he directed job candidates still employed at Apple to bring physical components from those programs to OpenAI interviews. If substantiated, those sessions gave OpenAI real-time access to Apple's unreleased hardware designs through people who were simultaneously Apple employees. The allegation, if proven, would be among the most direct cases of competitive hardware intelligence gathering in recent tech industry history.
  • The $730 billion IPO target's disclosure implications are under-discussed. Under SEC rules, a material pending lawsuit must appear as a risk factor in the S-1 — meaning Apple's allegations against Tang Tan, Chang Liu, and IO Products will be summarized in OpenAI's IPO prospectus and read by every institutional investor evaluating the offering. The lawsuit creates a forced disclosure moment: investors who had not focused on Apple's trade-secret claims will be required to evaluate them before committing capital. Whether the lawsuit is settled or remains pending at filing determines how the S-1 frames the litigation risk. A $730 billion valuation with an unresolved trade-secret lawsuit requires either a settlement path or a disclosure that institutional investors find non-material — neither of which is currently guaranteed.
